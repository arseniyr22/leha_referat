INTRODUCTION 

Language  is  an  open  system  that  is  characterized  by  its  flexibility  and 

dynamism.  In  the  modern  world,  there  is  an exponential growth in the volume 

of textual information, which necessitates the development of effective methods 

for its analysis. Nowadays there is a need in professional fields to determine the 

complexity  of  the  text,  for  example  in  translation  studies, teaching translation,  

marketing  and  educational   spheres.  For  professional  translators  knowledge  of 

text complexity сontributes to understanding how much time they need to spend 

on  translation,  what  types  of  reference  materials  they  should  use  during  the 

translation  process,  and  assume  the  number and quality of mistakes allowed in 

this  text.  During  translation  training,  knowledge  of  complexity  indicators  will 

make  it  possible  for  students  to  select  texts  for  study  that  correspond  to  their 

real  knowledge  and  skills.  The  informativity  of  a  text  can  be  assessed  using 

various algorithms, including: the ratio of the number of terms to the total word 

count,  the  frequency  of  word  repetition  in  previously  translated  texts,  a 

readability index, and a purely mathematical calculation of information entropy. 

In the present study  probabilistic approach according to Shannon entropy 

and algorithmic approach of Kolmogorov will be сonsidered.  

To  conduct  an  in-depth  analysis  of  sentiment  trajectories  within  the 

framework of entropy and time series, it is necessary first to analyze the tonality 

of words in the text. 

Sentiment  analysis  was  employed  in  work  to  generate  time  series 

representing  the  emotional  content  of  the  text.  These  time  series  will 

subsequently  be  used  to  quantify  textual  complexity  and  entropy.  There  are 

currently enough interdisciplinary studies on sentiment analysis and the concept 

of  entropy,  but  few  scientists  have  observed  these  concepts  in  one work. With 

the  growing  interest  in  NLP,  the  number  of  studies  devoted  to  analyzing  the 

randomness  of  time  series  based  on  textual  data  is  also  increasing.  Sentiment

          1.1  Concept of Sentiment analysis and its types 

Sentiment  analysis  is  a  field  of  natural  language  processing  (NLP)  that 

focuses  on  identifying  and  extracting  subjective  information  from  text.  The 

primary goal is to determine the attitude, emotions, or sentiments expressed by a 

writer  or  speaker  regarding  a  particular  topic,  product,  service,  or  event.  This 

sentiment  is  often  classified  as  positive,  negative,  or  neutral,  though  more 

nuanced  analyses  can  detect  specific  emotions  like  joy,  anger,  sadness,  or 

surprise.  One  of  the  important  aspects  of  text  analysis  is  the  identification   of 

sentiment  (emotional  tone),  which  finds application in a wide variety of fields, 

from  monitoring  public  opinion  to  analyzing  consumer  behavior.  The  existing 

methods of sentiment analysis, as a rule, are focused on determining the general 

text  tonality,  while  the  dynamics  of  sentiment  change  (sentiment  trajectories) 

remains insufficiently studied, despite its potential informative value.  

       One  of  the  first  works  in which the term sentiment analysis is used is the 

work of T. Nasukawa and J. Yi. The authors applied sentiment analysis to words 

from  news  articles  after  doing  all  pre-processing  steps,  including POS tagging 

(Nasukawa & Yi, 2003, p. 72–73). It is interesting to note that authors use such 

a method of analyzing sentences as shallow parsing. The method consists in the 

initial  identification  of  separate  units  of  sentences  (nouns, verbs), and then the 

phrasal  groups  they  are  part  of.  Researchers  proposed  to  single  out  positive, 

negative  and  neutral  polarity.   After obtaining the results of the shallow parser, 

authors  analyze  the  syntactic  dependencies  among  the  phrases  and  look  for

phrases  with  a  sentiment  term  that  modifies  or  is  modified  by  a  subject  term. 

However,  this  approach  did  not  work  in  examples  with  ambiguity  in  complex 

sentences,  which  relates  to“the  presence  of  two  or  more  possible  meanings 

within a single word”,   periphrasis, irony (“she is grammyless”). The reason for 

this  is  the  fact  that  domains  and  data  types  are  expanded,  and the precision of 

some  difficult  data  may  go  down  to  about  75  %.  As  the  prerequisite  of  study, 

researchers  need  to modify the current system, adding new sentiment terms for 

new domains. Moreover, the authors are going to resolve the issue with various 

discourse processing methods including anaphora resolution in the future works 

(Nasukawa & Yi, 2003, p. 75-76).

1.2 Sentiment analysis and opinion mining 

There is also a term that is often identified with the above mentioned such 

as  opinion  mining,  which  is  a  feature  of  sentiment  analysis,  as  polarity  and 

subjectivity.  Sentiment  analysis  is  a  generic concept to which the term opinion 

mining  refers.  It  is  also  known  as  aspect-based  sentiment  analysis  in  Natural 

Language  Processing  (NLP).  The  term  is  defined  as  the  process of identifying 

sentiment  aspects  in  a  text  by  focusing  on  specific  aspects,  sentiment polarity, 

and  opinion  terms  related  to  the  text  (Ezzameli  &  Mahersia,  2023).    So, 

sentiment  analysis  mainly  studies  opinions  which  express  or  imply  positive or 

negative  sentiments  (Liu,  2012).   It  is  also  known  as  aspect-based  sentiment 

analysis  in  Natural  Language  Processing  (NLP).  One  of  the  first  and  most 

famous works in opinion mining was presented in 2002 by Pang and Lee (Pang, 

Lee,  &  Vaithyanathan,  2002).  They  used  three  machine  learning algorithms of 

NaiveBayes,  MaximumEntropy  and  support  vector  machine  to  classify  film 

opinions into two groups of positive and negative (Amir & Alireza, 2017). 

The  opinions  can be classified according to their meaning and pragmatic 

functions.  The  first  type  of  opinion  that  we  will  discuss  is  regular  opinion, 

another  type  is  called  comparative  opinion.  A regular opinion is often referred 

to  simply  as  opinion  in  the  literature  and  it  has  two  main  subtypes:  direct 

opinion and indirect opinion (Liu, 2006, 2011, 2012). A direct opinion refers to 

an  opinion  expressed  directly  on  an  entity  or  an  entity  aspect,  whereas  an 

indirect opinion is an opinion that is expressed indirectly on an entity or aspect 

11 

 
of  an  entity  based  on  its  effects  on  some  other  entities.  For  example,  the 

sentence “This camera is great” describes the opinion expressed directly on the 

object aspect (camera). Indirect opinion is more difficult to recognise than direct 

opinion.  One  needs  to  know  whether  some  desirable  and  undesirable  state  is 

before  or  after  some  action  (Liu,  2006), e.g., the phrase “After taking sleeping 

pills  I  became  addicted  to  it” expresses a negative opinion on the pills, but the 

sentence “Since I have had sleep disorders, I started taking sleeping pills” does 

not express a sentiment or opinion on the pills because “sleep  disorders” (which 

is negative) happened before taking medicine.  

A  comparative  opinion  is  a  type  of  opinion  which  is  expressed  by 

comparing  multiple  targets  on  a  shared  aspect  (Wang  et  al.,  2022),  e.g.,  “This 

picture  is  more  colourful  than  the  previous  one”.  Comparative  and superlative 

adjectives are  frequently used in such sentences.  

There  is  also  another  classification  of  opinions  that  is  based  on 

expressiveness:  explicit  and  implicit  opinions.  An  explicit  opinion  is  a 

subjective  statement  that  gives  a  regular  or  comparative  opinion,  e.g.,“This 

forest  is  wonderful,”  and  “This  forest  is  more  wonderful  than  where  we  have 

been.” 

An  implicit  opinion  is  a  statement  that  only  implies  a  regular  or 

comparative  opinion,  e.g.,  “I  bought  a  cake  yesterday,  and  it  was  too  sweet”, 

and“ This MacBook works faster than previous.” 

Unfortunately,  less  work  has  been  done  on  implicit  opinions  (Zhang  & 

Liu, 2011; Liu, 2012). 

Sentiment analysis сan be defined as a natural language processing (NLP) 

technique  which  defines  positive  or  negative  evaluation  expressed  through 

language.   This  method  has  gained  great  importance  in  various popular fields, 

besides  linguistic  and  computer  sciences,  for  example,  business,  marketing, 

customer service, social media analysis, etc.

Overall,  sentiment  analysis  is  one  of  the  most  widely  known  natural 

language  processing  (NLP)  tasks.  The  analysis  of  emotions  consists  in 

determining the emotional meaning of an expression in natural language. 

         1.3 Rule-based and Machine learning sentiment analysis 

It’s  generally  known  that  sentiment  analysis  generally  falls  into  three 

categories: 

●  Rule-based algorithms  

●  Machine Learning models 

●  Lexicon-based systems 

The  three  major  approaches  to  NER  are  based  on  lexicon,  rules,  and 

machine  learning (Gudivada, Rao, & Raghavan, 2015). Rule-based approach is 

a  practical  approach  to  analyzing  text  without  training  or  using  machine 

learning  models.   This  approach  results  in  rules  based  on  which  the  text  is 

labeled as positive, negative or neutral. These rules are also known as lexicons. 

Hence,  the  Rule-based  approach  is  called  the  Lexicon-based  approach.  These 

include  TextBlob,  VADER,  and  SentiWordNet.  A  sentiment  analysis  dictionary 

contains  information  about  the  emotions  or  polarity  expressed  by  words, 

phrases,  or  concepts  (Amirkumar,  Orynbekova,  Talasbek,  Ayazbayev  & 

Cankurt,  2024).  This  approach  however  has   several  limitations.  Firstly,  the 

dictionary-based  method  is  not  able  to  handle  ambiguity. Secondly, it does not 

work  with  different  dialects  and  informal  or  slang  words,  as well as acronyms 

and  shorthand.  Besides  this, when units transfer to other languages, they suffer 

from  translation  problems  and  cultural  differences  (Kaity  &  Balakrishnan, 

2020).  

With  a  machine  learning  (ML)  approach,  an  algorithm  is  used  to  train 

software to estimate sentiment level in a block of text using words that appear in 

the text as well as the order in which they appear. Engineers and researchers use 

sentiment  analysis  to  teach  their  software  how  to  identify  emotion  in  text, 

13 

 
similarly to how we, humans, do. ML models continue to “learn” from the data 

they are given, hence the name “machine learning” (Amirkumar et al., 2024). 

Here are a few of the most commonly used classification algorithms: 

●  Linear  regression  -  calculates  how  the  X  input  (words  and  phrases), 

relates  to  the  Y  output  (polarity).  LR  will  determine  where  words  and 

phrases  fall on a polarity scale from positive to negative and everywhere 

in between (Tang et al., 2022). 

●  Naive Bayes - algorithm that uses Bayes’ theorem to categorize words in 

a  batch  of  text.  Bayes  classifier  learns  the  probability  of  each  word 

appearing  in  positive,  negative,  or  neutral  text  from  the  training  data 

(Wongkar & Angdresey, 2019) 

●  K-Nearest Neighbor (KNN) - classification system, which is based on the 

concept that similar data points stay close to each other. This model helps 

to  determine  what  words  and  phrases  are  positive,  negative,  or  neutral 

based on the data that exists around them. 

●  Support  vector  machines  -  quick  and  efficient  classification  algorithm 

used to solve two-group classification problems. 

●  Random  Forest  is  an  ensemble  learning  technique  in  which  many 

decision  trees  are  trained  and  where  each  tree  carries  a  data-supplied 

sample of observations. 

●  Deep learning (DL) - also known as an artificial neural network, which is 

considered  to  be  an  advanced  machine  learning  technique  that  links 

together  multiple  algorithms  to  mimic  human  brain  function.  Models 

related  to  Deep  learning  include  models  such  as  BERT,  RoBERTa  and 

other  Transformer-based  architectures,  that  are  used  for  text  sentiment 

analysis (Umarani, Julian, & Deepa, 2021).  

We  should  point  out  the  pros  and  cons  of  both  approaches.  The  main 

advantages of rule-based approach are: accuracy, ease of use and swiftness. But 

there are a few limitations: limited scope and restricted intelligence. 

14 

 
Machine learning systems provide the following benefits to organizations: 

adaptability,  self-learning  and  scalability.  On  the  other  hand,  they  also  require 

greater  technical  expertise  from  teams  to  develop  effective  applications. 

Moreover,  building  a  sufficiently  large  and  relevant  data  set  can  be  a 

resource-intensive and time-consuming process (Umarani et al., 2021). 

One must admit that rule-based sentiment analysis is one of the very basic 

approaches to calculating text sentiments. It only requires minimal pre-work and 

the idea is quite simple, this method does not use any machine learning to figure 

out the text sentiment. On the contrary, instead of being explicitly programmed, 

machine  learning  models,  a  subset  of  AI,  learn  from  data  to  enhance  their 

performance  in  specific  tasks.  Rule-based  systems  thereby  rely  on  predefined 

dictionaries, while AI-powered machine learning learns continuously from data.     

1.4 Sentiment Analysis’ Features 

Sentiment  analysis  is  used  in  many  professional  spheres:  social  media, 

marketing, medicine, literature. Berardinelli, Gaber and Haig (2013) highlighted 

that  analysing  students'  feedback  using  sentiment  analysis  techniques  can 

identify  the  students'  positive  or  negative  feelings,  or  even  more  reﬁned 

emotions,  that  students  have  towards  the  current  teaching.  The  authors 

represented a table where the emotion  types  were  categorised  into  Love,  Joy,  

Anger,   Frustration,   and   Neutral. The student can express his feelings in short 

expressions  or  words  (Ezzameli  &  Mahersia,  2023;  Geetha  et  al.,  2024).  Tian 

Feng  et  al.  (2009)  created  patterns  to  find   what   words  are  associated  more  

with  emotions,  they  also  give  solutions  to  relieve  the  e-learner,  for  example, 

when  he  feels  depressed  as  his  teacher  does  not   like  his  essay  (Feng  et  al., 

2009).  

Sentiment  analysis  comes  in  many  forms  -  depending  on  the  tasks  and 

objectives you pursue. Linguistic sentiment is a measurement of subjectivity as 

15 

 
either  a  positive or negative opinion in text. In the Chinese blogs, for example, 

the  positive,  negative and objective emotion scores are added in the model and 

show  their  significance  to  find  influential  users  (Shuang  et  al.,  2021). 

Traditionally   there are two types of sentiment analysis, according to its further 

usage:  fine-grained  sentiment  analysis  and  aspect-based  sentiment  analysis 

(Yan  et  al.,  2022).  As  a  measurement  of  opinions  and  affective  states,  a 

sentiment  score  generally  consists  of  two  aspects:  polarity  and intensity (Tian, 

Lai  &  Moore,  2018).   A  key  aspect  of  sentiment  analysis  is  polarity 

classification.  This  polarity  can  be  expressed as a numerical rating known as a 

sentiment  score.  Fine-grained,  or  graded,  sentiment  analysis  is  a  type  of 

sentiment  analysis  that  groups  text  into  different  emotions  and  the  level  of 

emotion  being  expressed  (Jones,  2024).  Categories  can  expand  beyond  just 

positive,  neutral  and  negative.  For  example,  one  may  choose  to  use  five 

categories,  e.g.,   negative,  neutral,  positive  and  very  positive.  In  its  most 

complete  iteration,  fine-grained  analysis  is  conducted  to  define  both  the 

sentiment  target  (topic)  and  its  intensity  at  a  phrase  or  clause  level  (Wida  & 

Setiawan, 2023).  

1.4.1 Polarity 

A basic task in sentiment analysis is classifying the polarity of a given text 

at  the  document,  sentence,  or  feature/aspect  level  -  whether  the  expressed 

opinion  in  a  document,  a  sentence  or  an  entity  feature/aspect  is  positive, 

negative,  or  neutral.  Advanced,  “beyond  polarity”  sentiment  classification 

looks,  for  instance,  at  emotional  states  such  as  enjoyment,  anger,  disgust, 

sadness,  fear,  and  surprise  (Vong   et  al.,  2019).  The  parameter  can  be  in  the 

range from -100 to 100, but most often the sentiment score is between -1 and 1, 

indicating 0-neutral sentiment.  

These are the main components of the concept of polarity: 

16 

 
●  Polarity Classification: This involves assigning a sentiment label 

(positive, negative, neutral) to a given text. For instance, the sentence “I 

love this product” has a positive polarity, while “I regret buying this item” 

has a negative polarity. 

●  Lexicon-Based Approaches: These methods utilize predefined 

dictionaries where words are associated with specific sentiment scores or 

polarities. The overall sentiment of a text is determined by aggregating 

the polarities of individual words. 

●  Machine Learning Approaches: These involve training models on labeled 

datasets to learn patterns associated with different sentiment polarities. 

Such models can capture more nuanced expressions of sentiment 

compared to lexicon-based methods (Liu, 2012; Zhang, Wang & Liu , 

2018).  

In  NLP,  polarity  is  predominantly  associated  with  sentiment  analysis,  a 

subfield  focused  on  determining  the  emotional  tone of text. Sentiment polarity 

indicates  whether  a  piece  of  text  expresses  a  positive,  negative,  or  neutral 

sentiment,  forming  the  basis  for  opinion  mining and affective computing. This 

application  is  particularly  relevant in analyzing user-generated content, such as 

product reviews, social media posts, and developer communications. 

A  notable  study,  “Polarity  Calibration  for  Opinion  Summarization” 

(Polarity  Calibration  for  Opinion  Summarization),  addresses  the  challenge  of 

ensuring  that  automated  summaries  reflect  the  sentiment  distribution  of  input 

texts.  The  paper  introduces  polarity  calibration,  a  method  to  align  summary 

polarity  with  input,  mitigating  biases  where  models  amplify  majority  opinions 

and neglect minorities (Lei et al., 2024)

1.4.2 Subjectivity 

Textual information in the world can be broadly categorized into two main 

types:  facts  and  opinions.Facts  are  objective  expressions  about  entities,  events 

and  their  properties.  Opinions  are  usually  subjective  expressions  that  describe 

people’s  sentiments,  appraisals  or  feelings  toward  entities,  events  and  their 

properties (Bing, 2010). Subjectivity as a polarity is also scored, usually from -1 

to 1.  

However, subjectivity is not a simple binary attribute but rather exists on a 

spectrum.  It  is  often signaled through a variety of linguistic markers, including 

evaluative  adjectives  ,  adverbs  and verbs. Modal verbs (e.g., “might”, “could”, 

“should”) and hedges (e.g., “sort of”, “kind of”, “apparently”) can also indicate 

subjectivity  by  expressing  uncertainty  or  limited  commitment  to  a  statement.  

The  frequent  use  of  personal  pronouns  (e.g.,  “I”,  “me”,'  “my”)  and  subjective 

intensifiers  (e.g.,  “very”,  “really”,  “extremely”)  further  contributes  to  a  more 

subjective  tone.  Furthermore,  the  concept  of  subjectification  (Traugott  & 

Dasher,  2002)  highlights  how  meanings  evolve  over  time,  with  objective 

expressions often acquiring more subjective interpretations through processes of 

grammaticalization.  The  notion  of  stance  (Du  Bois,  2007),  encompassing  the 

speaker's  attitude,  feelings,  and  judgments,  is  also  central  to  understanding 

subjectivity.  The  accurate  identification  of  subjectivity  is  crucial  for  various 

applications  in Natural Language Processing (NLP). It is a foundational step in 

sentiment  analysis  (Pang  &  Lee,  2008),  where  the  goal  is  to  determine  the 

overall sentiment expressed in a piece of text. Subjectivity detection is also vital 

for  opinion  mining  (Liu,  2012),  allowing  for  the  extraction  of  subjective 

opinions from large datasets. Moreover, it plays a key role in fact-checking and 

bias  detection  (Rashkin  et  al.,  2017),  helping  to  identify  biased  or  misleading 

information  in  news  articles and social media. As such, a robust understanding 

of  subjectivity  and  its  linguistic  manifestations  is  essential  for  advancing 

18 

 
various  NLP  tasks  and  promoting  more  informed  and  nuanced  analyses  of 

textual data. 

1.5  Entropy and the concept of the Shannon’s information theory 

Entropy  arises  exclusively  in  dissipative  systems,  like  language.  The 

concept  of  entropy  was  introduced  into  scientific  circulation  in  1865  by  R. 

Clausius  to  characterize  the  processes  of  energy  conversion  (Chumak,  2011). 

The  term  entropy  is  also  used  in  classical  mechanics  as  a  "characteristic  of 

dynamic  chaos  in  systems  with  instability  of  movement”.  In  theory  of 

information entropy means "a measure of the uncertainty of a certain situation" 

(Korotaev, 2013). The entropy of a language is a statistical function of a text in 

a particular language, or of the language itself, which determines the amount of 

information per unit of text (Schneier, 2018). In modern linguistics, this concept 

is  significant and capable of replacing the term information (Nekipelova, 2013, 

p.3).  Thus,  information  entropy  determines  the  measure  of  stochasticity  and 

randomness of the text.  

The  concept  of  entropy  provides  deep  insight  into  the  direction  of 

spontaneous  change  for  many  everyday  phenomena.  Its  introduction  by  the 

German physicist Rudolf Clausius in 1850 is a highlight of 19th-century physics 

(Drake,  2025).  Research  on  stochasticity  and  time  series  has  increased  with 

growing interest in NLP. Semantic trajectories of natural texts require competent 

application  of  the  right  approach.  For  example,  researcher  V.  Gromov  (2023) 

devotes  his  works  to  sentimental  analysis  of  literary  texts  and  notes  the  main 

tendency in entropy and complexity values in data frames.  In addition, he tried 

to  determine  the  sentiment  and  semantic  trajectory  of  a  literature  masterpiece 

using mathematical methods such as Shannon Entropy and the largest Lyapunov 

exponent (2015). The object of his research is Russian and English outstanding 

literature. 

19 

 
 
Language  is  a  kind  of  programming  code  that  allows  us  to  express  our 

thoughts  and  feelings.  When  a  person  speaks  or  writes,  he  is  a  source  of 

information. The Shannon entropy estimates the average amount of information 

(mathematical  expectation)  contained  in  the  values  of  a  random  variable. 

According  to  Shannon's  formula,  the information added by each new character 

is  the  logarithm  of  the  probability  of  its  occurrence,  the  basis  of  which  is  the 

number  of  characters  in  the  alphabet  (Gromov,  2023).  With  the  help  of 

information  theory,  it  is  possible  to  reduce  the  number  of  letters  in  words 

without  loss  of  meaning.  Then  the  brain,  based  on  a  limited  amount  of 

information from the “truncated” text, will itself complete the missing elements 

to  restore  meaning.  It  turns  out  that  all  our  languages  are  redundant  to  one 

degree  or  another,  i.e.  they  contain  sounds  or  letters  that  are  not  strictly 

necessary  to  convey  a  message.  Claude  Shannon  estimated  the  redundancy  of 

the  English  language  at  75%  information  does  not  exist  without  a  physical 

medium;  it  is  discrete  and measured in bits. Information in the narrow sense is 

Shannon entropy, that is, the degree of uncertainty and novelty in a message or 

the  average  amount  of  information  per  message  character  (Chumak,  2011). 

Therefore,  the  more  uncertain  the  data,  the  more  information  is  required  to 

describe it.                                                

The  definition  of  information  entropy  was introduced in 1948 by Claude 

Shannon.  In  information  theory  it  is  the  average  rate  at  which  information  is 

produced  by  a  stochastic  source  of  data  (Nasukawa  &  Yi,  2003).  It  quantifies 

the  amount  of  information  in  the  variable.  This  term  was adapted for different 

processes  and  can  be  used  for characterization of chaoticity, or stochasticity of 

the  processes.  The  approach  implies  that,  for  a  given  time  series,  a  user 

calculates  two  characteristics  -  entropy  and  complexity  and  then  compares the 

position of this point with the lower and upper theoretical boundaries to specify 

time-series  type.  The  main  advantage  of  this  algorithm  is  that  it  is  possible  to 

work with only one-dimensional time series.

1.6 Complexity 

Complexity  characterizes  the  behavior  of  a  system  or  model  whose 

components  interact  in  multiple  ways  and  follow  its  local  rules,  leading  in 

particular  to  non-linear  system,  randomness,  collective  dynamics,  hierarchy 

(Johnson,  2001).  This  is  where  the  term  “complex  system”  originally  comes 

from. A complex system is a system consisting of many interacting components 

(also  known  as  subsystems),  as  a  result  of  which  it  gets  new  properties  (Bar 

-Yam, 2002).  

1.6.1 Statistical Complexity 

Statistical  Complexity  is  a  measure  used  to  describe  how  structured  or 

organized  a  system  is,  while  also accounting for randomness. Unlike measures 

that  only  focus  on  how  unpredictable  a  system  is  (such  as  entropy),  statistical 

complexity  tries  to  capture  both  the  amount  of  information  and  the  degree  of 

structure  present  in  the  data  (Crutchfield  &  Young,  1989).  Generally, 

applications cover quantum systems, software‐architecture networks (modeling 

components  and  interconnections  via  state‐probabilities),  and  even  edge‐map 

scoring  in  image  processing.  In  text  complexity  analysis,  SC  can  reveal  how 

25 

 
syntactic  or  lexical  distributions  balance  predictability  and  novelty,  offering 

insights that pure entropy measures miss. 

A  complementary  notion  arises  in  computational  mechanics,  where 

Statistical  Complexity  is  the  Shannon  entropy  of  a  system’s  causal states - the 

minimal  set  of  historical  patterns  needed  to  optimally  predict  the  future.  First 

formalized  by  Crutchfield  &  Young,  SC  quantifies  memory  stored  in  the 

process’s  ε‑machine (a model that helps us understand and predict patterns in a 

data  sequence  by  identifying  the  minimal  amount  of  memory  needed  to  make 

good  predictions).  Unlike  SC’s  focus  on  probability‐distance  products,  SC 

directly  measures  the  hidden–Markov‐model  complexity  underlying  temporal 

sequences. 

In  text  analysis,  SC  can  be  used  to  assess  how  much  contextual  history 

(e.g., previous words or syntactic frames) is needed to predict upcoming words, 

reflecting deep structural complexity beyond surface‐level probabilities. 

Kolmogorov  Complexity  of  a  string  x  (which  we  will  touch  on  in more 

detail  later)  has  a different meaning and is defined as the length of the shortest 

program  on  a  universal  Turing  machine  that  outputs  x.  (Shen,  2024)  It  is  a 

fundamental notion of algorithmic randomness. However, K(x) is uncomputable 

in  general,  and  practical  estimators  rely  on  bounds  or  approximations. 

Moreover,  K  treats  all  strings uniformly, ignoring any probabilistic model over 

ensembles.  In  contrast,  Statistical  Complexity  measures  are  computable  given 

empirical  distributions  or  inferred  models.  They  explicitly  leverage  Shannon 

entropy  and  distributional  structure  (e.g.,  disequilibrium  or  causal‐state 

probabilities), making them more readily applicable to real‐world data sets. 

Lempel–Ziv  Complexity  (LZC) approximates algorithmic complexity by 

counting the number of distinct substrings (“phrases”) as a sequence is scanned 

left  to  right.  Each  time  a  new  substring  appears,  the  LZC  counter  increments, 

offering  a  fully  computable  estimate  of  string  compressibility.  While  LZC

distribution.  Statistical  Complexity,  on  the  contrary,  balances  entropy  with 

structural  measures  (e.g:  Jensen–Shannon  divergence  in  generalized  SCMs), 

capturing

1.6.2 Kolmogorov complexity 

In  theoretical  machine  learning,  statistical  complexity  is  a  notion  that 

measures  the  richness  of  a  hypothesis  space.  At  its  core,  Kolmogorov 

complexity  is  a  way  of  measuring  the  amount  of  “information”  in  a  piece  of 

data  -  typically  a  string  of  text  or  bits.  In  simple  terms,  the  Kolmogorov 

complexity  of  an  object  is  defined  as  the  length  of  the  shortest  computer 

program (written in a fixed, universal programming language) that can generate 

that object and then stop. (Li & Vitaniy, 1993) 

This concept is important for various reasons: 

●  It measures Inherent Complexity: 

Unlike  other  measures  that  depend  on  probability  distributions  (like 

Shannon  entropy),  Kolmogorov  complexity  focuses  on  the  inherent 

structure  of  the  data.  For  example,  a  string  that  repeats  a  simple pattern 

27 

 
(like  “abababab…”)  can  be  generated  by  a  very  short  program  (“print 

‘ab’ 100 times”), meaning it has low complexity. In contrast, a string that 

appears  random  (with  no  discernible  pattern)  will  require  a  program 

nearly as long as the string itself. 

●  Helps us to understanding randomness: 

In  this  framework,  randomness  is  understood  as  the  absence  of  any 

shorter  description.  If  a  string’s  shortest  description  is  nearly  the  same 

length  as  the  string,  it  is  considered  algorithmically  random  or 

incompressible. This concept helps us rigorously define what it means for 

data to be “random” or “complex.” 

●  It is universal  

Although the exact value of Kolmogorov complexity can change slightly 

depending  on  the  programming  language  or  Turing  machine  used,  the 

differences  are  bounded  by  a  constant.  This  means  the  measure  is 

universal  enough  to  compare  the  complexity  of  different  objects  in  a 

meaningful way. 

●  Theoretical and Practical Impact: 

While  Kolmogorov  complexity  itself  is  not  computable  (there  is  no 

general  algorithm  that,  given  any  string,  will  produce  its  exact 

complexity),  it  underpins  many  theoretical  results  in  computer  science 

and  mathematics.  It  also  inspires  practical methods in data compression, 

machine  learning,  and  statistical  inference  (for  instance,  through  the 

Minimum  Description  Length  principle)  (Li  &  Vitányi,  An  Introduction 

to Kolmogorov Complexity and Its Applications, 2008).  

In  NLP,  researchers  use  the  idea  of  complexity  to  compare  different 

languages, texts, or even different writing styles. For instance, by estimating the 

complexity of sentences or entire documents, one can gauge their predictability. 

28 

 
Texts  that  are  more  predictable  (e.g.,  formulaic  news  reports)  tend  to  be  more 

compressible  and  thus  have lower Kolmogorov complexity than more creative, 

less predictable texts like poetry. 

Kolmogorov  complexity  also  inspires  methods  for  clustering  and 

measuring  the  similarity  between  texts.  One  such  method  is  the  Normalized 

Compression  Distance  (NCD).  NCD  approximates  the  similarity  between  two 

texts by comparing how well they can be compressed together versus separately. 

This  technique  has  been  applied  to  tasks  like  document  clustering,  language 

classification, and even authorship attribution. 

A key paper on this approach is Cilibrasi & Vitányi (2007), “The Google 

Similarity Distance” which explores compression-based similarity measures. 

The  concept  of  entropy,  in  the  context  of  information  theory,  generally 

refers  to  the  unpredictability  or  randomness  of  a  source.  When  we  talk  about 

complexity  entropy  in  relation to Kolmogorov complexity, we are interested in 

how  the  complexity  of  a  text  (or  any  data)  changes  as  its  length  increases,  or 

how  “surprising”  the  next  piece  of  information  is  given  what  we have seen so 

far. 

In many applications (especially in NLP) - entropy is used to measure the 

average unpredictability of a text. A higher entropy value indicates that the text 

is  less  predictable,  which  usually  corresponds  to  a  higher  Kolmogorov 

complexity. Conversely, texts with lower entropy are more predictable and have 

lower complexity. 

By  analyzing  the  entropy  of  text,  researchers  can  make  decisions  about 

language models, such as determining the best way to predict the next word in a 

sentence.  This  has  direct  implications  in  applications  like  speech  recognition, 

machine  translation,  and  text  generation.  The  interplay  between  entropy  (as  a 

measure  of  average  uncertainty)  and  Kolmogorov complexity (as a measure of 

the  minimal  description  length)  provides  a  robust  theoretical  framework  for 

understanding and improving these models. (Vitányi & Li, 2000).

1.6.2 Lempel-Ziv Complexity 

Lempel-Ziv  complexity  (LZ  complexity)  is  a  fundamental  measure  in 

information  theory,  quantifying  the  complexity  of  finite  sequences  based  on 

their compressibility. Introduced by Abraham Lempel and Jacob Ziv in 1976, it 

serves  as  a  practical  approximation  to  the  theoretical  concept  of  Kolmogorov 

complexity.  This  paper  provides an in-depth examination of LZ complexity, its 

computation,  applications,  and  its  relationship  with  Kolmogorov  complexity. 

The  concept  of  sequence  complexity  is  pivotal  in  various  domains,  including 

data  compression,  randomness 

testing,  and 

information 

theory.  While 

Kolmogorov  complexity  offers  a  theoretical  foundation,  its  uncomputability 

necessitates  practical  alternatives.  LZ  complexity  emerges  as  a  computable 

measure, reflecting the repetitiveness and structure within sequences. 

LZ  complexity  quantifies  the  number  of  distinct  substrings  encountered 

as a sequence is parsed from left to right, where each new substring represents a 

novel  pattern  not  previously  observed.  Formally,  for  a  sequence  S of length n, 

the LZ complexity C(S) is the count of such unique substrings identified during 

this parsing process. An efficient algorithm exists for computing LZ complexity 

in  linear  time  -  the  algorithm  involves  iteratively  scanning  the  sequence  to 

identify  the  longest  substring  that  has  not  been  previously  encountered, 

incrementing the complexity count each time a new substring is found. 

LZ complexity has been applied in various fields: 

●  Data  Compression:  it  creates  the  basis  for  algorithms  like  LZ77  and 

LZ78,  that  are  foundational  in  such  vastly  known  compression  formats 

such as ZIP and GIF. 

●  Randomness  Testing:  Used  to  assess  the  randomness  of  sequences, 

especially in cryptography. 

●  Neuroscience:  Applied  in  the  analysis  of  EEG  (electroencephalography) 

data to detect complexity in brain signals. 

30 

 
Now, speaking of similarities: 

●  Their goal is to measure the complexity or randomness of sequences. 

●  Lower  values  in  both  indicate  that  there  is  more  regularity  or 

compressibility. 

And differences:  

●  LZ  complexity  is  computable  in  linear  time,  while  Kolmogorov’s  is 

uncomputable. 

●  Kolmogorov  complexity  depends  on  the  choice  of  universal  Turing 

machine, whereas LZ complexity is model-independent. 

●  Granularity:  Kolmogorov  complexity  provides a theoretical lower bound 

on  compressibility,  meanwhile  LZ  complexity  offers  users  a  practical 

estimate. 

Due  to  its  computability,  LZ  complexity  is  widely  used  in  practical 

applications  where  an  estimation  of  sequence  complexity  is  required. 

Kolmogorov  complexity,  while  theoretically  profound,  serves  more  as  a 

conceptual  framework  for  understanding  the  limits  of  data  compression  and 

randomness. 

In  conclusion,  Lempel-Ziv  complexity  serves  as  a  practical  and 

computable  measure  of  sequence  complexity,  closely  related  to  the  theoretical 

construct of Kolmogorov complexity. While Kolmogorov complexity provides a 

foundational  understanding  of  information  content,  LZ  complexity  enables 

real-world  applications  across  various  domains.  Understanding  both  measures 

helps  us  understand  the  concept  of  data  complexity  and  the  principles 

underlying data compression and randomness.

1.7 Lyapunov Exponent 

A Lyapunov exponent quantifies the rate at which nearby trajectories in a 

dynamical system either converge or diverge over time. Specifically, it measures 

how  small  differences  in  initial  conditions  can  amplify, indicating the system's 

sensitivity  to  initial  states.  Mathematically,  if  δZ0  represents  an  initial  small 

separation  between  two  trajectories,  the  separation  δZ(t)  at  time  t  can  be 

approximated as: |δZ(t)∣≈eλt∣δZ0| 

Here,  λ  denotes  the  Lyapunov  exponent.  A  positive  λ  suggests  that 

trajectories  diverge  exponentially, indicating chaotic behavior, while a negative 

λ implies convergence, pointing to stable behavior. (Guan, 2014) 

In systems with multiple dimensions, there exists a spectrum of Lyapunov 

exponents  corresponding  to  each  dimension  of  the  system's  phase  space.  The 

largest  of  these,  known  as  the  Maximal  Lyapunov  Exponent  or  MLE,  is 

particularly  significant  because  it  determines  the  system's  predictability.  A 

positive  MLE  is  often  taken  as  an  indication  of  chaos,  provided  other 

conditions, such as phase space compactness, are met. 

A  positive  Lyapunov  exponent  indicates  that  small  differences  grow 

exponentially  over  time,  leading  to  chaotic  behavior,  whereas  a  negative 

exponent  suggests  that  the  differences  diminish,  leading  to  predictable  and 

stable  outcomes  (Shen,  Uspensky,  &  Vereshchagin,  2017).  If  the  Lyapunov 

exponent  thereby  is  positive,  then  the  behavior  of  the  dynamic  system  is 

chaotic. If the Lyapunov exponent is negative, then the behavior of the dynamic 

system is non-chaotic.

2.3.2 Complexity 

Many  different  definitions  of  complexity  have  been  proposed  to  date, 

mainly  in  the  realm  of  physical  and  computational  sciences,  e.g.,  algorithmic 

complexity  (Solomonoff-Kolmogorov-Chaitin  complexity),  the  Lempel-Ziv

the  complexity  of  a  system  based 

in 

its  diversity, 

the 

thermodynamic  depth,  the  ε-machine  complexity,  the  physical  complexity  of 

genomes,  complexities  of  formal  grammars,  etc.  (Feldman  &  Crutchfield, 

1998).  The  key  figures  in  proposing  the  idea  of  statistical  complexity  were 

David Feldman and James Crutchfield, they put forward the idea that “the larger 

and  more  intricate the “correlations” between the system’s constituents are, the 

more  structured its underlying distribution is” (1997).   So, all existing methods 

of  defining  complexity  led  scientists  to  develop  a  general  measure  that 

quantifies  the  degree of structure present in a space. In our case high statistical 

complexity  also  indicates  ordering  of  data,  however,  high  complexity  value  is 

not  a  tendency  for  texts  analyzed  in  the  present  work.  The  following  section 

provides the fiction’s changes in the complexity characteristics presented on the 

timeline.  

2.3.3 Lyapunov Exponent 

Method of Lyapunov exponent was applied in order to determine whether 

sentiment  trajectories  tend  to  chaotic  behavior  or  the  system  tends  to  a 

deterministic  state  and  indicates  stable  behavior  of  the  system.  If  the  system 

eventually  comes  down  to  one  equilibrium  point,  then  we  can assume that the 

text  has  low  entropy  and  all  phenomena,  that  is,  events,  are  predictable.  As  it 

has  been  already  mentioned  above,  the  positive  indicator  shows  chaotic 

behaviour  of  the  system,  whereas  the  negative  indicator  represents  the 

deterministic process (Zhang et al., 2023). Among all analyzed literary works a 

thriller  novel  “The  Fourth  Protocol”  has  the  greatest  indicator  which  equals 

47 

 
 
0,021,  since  it  is  considered  the  novel  with  the  most chaotic system of events. 

This is a completely understandable fact for such a genre as thriller.  For science 

fiction,  though,  such  dynamics  usually  are  not  observed.  If  the  Lyapunov 

exponent equals zero, it means that the system is in the stationary mode.  

The  results also led us to the conclusion that the Lyapunov exponent has 

generally  increased  over  time.  Thus,  in  1974  it  was  0,002,  and  in  2023  it was 

0,003. So, the deviation between these two indicators is 0, 0016. Based on Table 

1,  we  cannot  observe  significant  differences  between  indicators,  yet  generally 

we  see  that  the  indicator  in  2023  is bigger than the indicator in 1974. Besides, 

the  Lyapunov  exponent  in  texts  without  data  preprocessing  is  smaller  than  in 

preprocessed  text.  From  this  observation  one  may  conclude  that  data 

preprocessing increased the complexity of the text by removing useless data and 

getting  rid  of  the  noise.  Therefore,  after  preprocessing  procedures  such  as 

tokenization,  lemmatization  and  lowercasing,  the  number  of  unique  tokens 

increases,  so  the  text  becomes  more  chaotic,  more  unpredictable  because  of 

more pronounced use of stylistic devices and hidden meanings.

not  only  for  reading  but  also  for  using  as  a  research  material.  The  use  of  two

of  text  tonality  and  the  second  is  the  determination of mathematical indicators 

such  as  stochasticity  and  determinism,  that  is,  Shannon  entropy and Lyapunov 

exponent. In addition, we looked at how the Lyapunov exponent is applied to a 

text  system,  which  means  its  positive  and  negative  exponents.  This  is  an 

important,  characteristic  speed  of  the  run-up  of  infinitely  close  trajectories.  A 

dynamic  system  with  the  Lyapunov  effect  is called chaotic. A negative system 

on the contrary points to a non-chaotic dynamical system. Entropy is a measure 

of  how  orderly  or  chaotic  a  system  is.  The  results  presented  above  show  high 

entropy and low complexity. The more chaos is observed, the higher the entropy 

is.  In  addition  to  Shannon  entropy,  statistical  complexity  was  included  in  the 

work  as  one  of  the  research  methods.  Concept  of  complexity  is  closely 

connected with entropy, however it quantifies the difficulty of predicting system 

behaviour based on patterns present in data. Low complexity indicates that there 

is no structure or patterns in the data. Thus, in the English texts we selected for 

analysis,  the  results  show  us  the  unstructured  randomness  of  the  data.  After 

calculating  the  entropy-complexity  measures,  the  results  showed  that  entropy 

does  not  tend  to  change  over  time,  however,  there  are  slight  differences  in 

complexity  values.  In  earlier  works  from  the  80’s  and  90’s,  the  complexity 

measures of the texts are higher compared to the two thousandth years.

CONCLUSION 

From the results it was proved that sentiment analysis is widely used not 

only in understanding customer opinions, reviews, and social media comments. 

With  the  help  of such analysis, it is possible to study an array of messages and 

other  data  and  determine  how  they  are  emotionally  colored  -  positively, 

negatively  or  neutrally.  This  process  is  also  used  as  a first step for further text 

analysis using mathematical analysis methods.  

The following sub-approaches are applied to sentiment analysis: 

●  Based on lexical rules and language norms. 

●  Based  on  sentiment  dictionaries  with  calculation  of  the  emotional 

coloring  of  each  individual  expression  -  for  example,  the  negative 

sentiment of the word “terrible” is stronger than the sentiment of the word 

“bad”. 

●  Based on machine learning and analysis of large data sets. 

It 

is  generally  agreed 

that  rule-based  systems  are  simple  and 

cost-efficient,  but  lack  adaptability  and  struggle  with  ambiguity  and  bias. 

Machine  learning  systems  are  dynamic,  adaptable,  and  can  handle  complex 

situations  better.  The  findings  suggest  that  an  optimal  strategy  for  natural 

language processing (NLP) involves a hybrid machine learning approach, where 

multiple  ML  models  are  integrated  with  traditional  NLP techniques to achieve 

superior results.  

Сhaos  is  a  situation where the behaviors of a system appear random and 

unpredictable.  To  sum  up  all  that  was  mentioned  above,  entropy  is  a  concept 

that  is  used  in  both  statistical physics and information theory, but with slightly 

different  meanings  and  contexts.  Although  both  entropies  reflect  the  idea  of 

disorder,  they  belong  to  different  fields  and  have  different  definitions  and 

applications.  Physical  entropy  describes  the  state  of  physical  systems,  while 

information  entropy  describes  the  uncertainty  of data.The latter quantifies how 

much  information  is  needed  to  accurately  determine  the  outcome  of  an  event. 

52 

 
The greater the uncertainty is observed, the higher the entropy is. High entropy 

indicates that it is difficult to predict the next symbol or event. Low entropy lets 

us  know  that  it  is  easier  to  predict.  Despite  significant  differences  between  

concepts  of  entropy  in  physics  and  information  entropy, both terms are related 

to  the  concept  of  disorder  or  randomness.  In  physics,  entropy  measures  the 

disorder  in  a  physical  system.  In  information  theory,  entropy  measures  the 

disorder (uncertainty) in information.  

Statistical  complexity  reflects  not  only  a  probabilistic  description  of  a 

model  and  information  quantity,  such  as  information  entropy,  but  also  the 

organization and interactions within a system governed by systematic rules.
