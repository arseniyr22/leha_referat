Introduction	
Advertising is usually considered a marketing strategy for promoting products and services. However, it also plays a crucial and more inclusive role in reflecting social ideals, norms, and values through imagery and language (McDonald et al., 2020). The gender phenomenon, especially the portrayal of female figures, is a key aspect of this reflection in advertisements. Previous research has investigated female portrayals in various media, such as magazines (Bolotova, 2018; Lirola, 2012; Savostkina, 2018), and the language used in female ads (Marco, 1997; Miroshnichenko, 2013). Nevertheless, insignificant attention paid to evolution of the female ideals representation within specific cultures and time periods. This research aims to address this gap by comparatively exploring the ideals constructed through female images in Soviet-era and Spanish advertising from the 1930s, as well as current advertisements (2020s) in Russia and Spain, focusing on agitational posters, social advertising and commercial advertisement of goods. By focusing on visual and stylistic devices, this paper reveals how advertising reflects social and cultural perspectives on women's identity across two different historical periods and cultures. 
This study aims to provide answers to the following research questions:
How  do linguistic and visual representation of women in the Soviet and Spanish advertising posters of the 1930s contrast with those in contemporary Russian and Spanish advertisements of the 2020s ?
What specific visual and stylistic strategies are employed to construct feminine ideals in each context?
How do the socio-political and cultural contexts of these two periods affect these portrayals?
To answer the research question, the following objectives are established:
Define such concepts as advertising discourse, multimodal approach, stylistic analysis, female representation, and influencing factors.
Analyze linguistic and visual strategies and their interaction in the advertising discourses of agitational posters, social and motivational ads, and commercial advertising of goods.
Conduct a comparative analysis of findings to assess the female representation evolution and the influence of social, political and cultural factors on the advertisements.
This research makes an attempt to understand how advertising reflects political, social, and cultural definitions of feminine ideals through verbal and non-verbal communication, creating a foundation for further research in the field of media, linguistic and cultural studies to create a more inclusive and authentic imagery of women.
This study employs a variety of analytical methods. Firstly, targeted data collection is used to gather relevant material and make conclusions based on observed information. Secondly, the collected advertisements are analyzed using Kress and van Leeuwen's (2006) framework for multimodal analysis that aims to provide a detailed understanding of how meaning is constructed through the interplay of visual and textual elements in each advertisement. Thirdly, to further explore the expressive function of the advertisements, a stylistic analysis is conducted based on Shakhovskiy's (2013) approach. This analysis  focuses on identifying various expressive means on phonetic, lexical, morphological, syntactical, phraseological, and graphical levels that are supposed to reinforce persuasive intent of advertisments and evoke emotional response. To combine both approaches of analysis, a special form is created. Finally, following the multimodal and stylistic analyses, a comparative analysis is conducted to identify similarities and differences in the representation of female figures across the two time periods and locations.
This study is relevant because advertising plays a significant role in shaping and reflecting societal perceptions of female identity, and understanding of how women are represented in advertising across different cultural and historical contexts contributes to exploration of evolving social norms and values within those societies. Influence of advertising in contemporary society and understanding the role of advertising is vital for promoting more inclusive representations of women in media.
The theoretical significance lies in its contribution to the existing knowledge of female representation, multimodal approach and stylistic analysis within advertising discourse, broadening the frameworks of discourse analysis and semiotics in the field of linguistics. Exploring the verbal and non-verbal cues used in female representation across distinct time periods and cultures provides an opportunity to understand how social, cultural and political changes may influence the choice of specific visal strategis or stylistic devices to represent the female ideals. 
As for the practical significance, this paper may inform advertisers, content makers and linguistis about how female gender is constructed and perceived, promoting more responsible, effective, and inclusive advertising strategies with the most appropriate language and imagery use. The comparative analysis clarifies how social, political and cultural changes influence media female representations, providing context for future research for market researchers, sociologists, historians, and cultural scientists.

Theoretical Part
Section 1. Multimodal analysis of discourse
1.1. The concept of discourse
The concept of discourse is a crucial concept across linguistics, philosophy, and sociology, where researchers investigate the interconnected relationships between language, identity, and social interactions. The term "discourse" derives from the French word "discours," which signifies a "process of understanding, reasoning, and thought", and originates from the Latin word "discursus", meaning "conversation". This etymology highlights discourse as a unit of expression that includes both oral and written forms (Khurmatullin, 2009). So, discourse is understood as a connected word integrated within sentences and, then, speech acts.
Nevertheless, it is essential to distinguish the concepts of text and discourse, as both of them represent a coherent text. As time has passed, the understanding of discourse has evolved, and started to exist within a more complex social context. By including the category situation in this pair, it was proposed to interpret discourse as “text and situation”, while text, accordingly, was defined as “discourse without situation” (Kurbanova & Shonazarova, 2023). Foucault (1969) conceptualized discourse as "a set of statements belonging to a single system of formation", indicating that discourse is characterized by specific discursive practices. This perspective suggests that discourse should be analyzed both from the perspective of linguistic phenomena and from a dynamic social aspect that shapes and is shaped by social norms and history. Artyunova (1990) further supports this notion by defining discourse as a coherent and logical text combined with extralinguistic factors, such as pragmatic, socio-cultural, psychological, and others. This definition emphasizes that speech is not a simple tool for communication but a purposeful social action that plays a crucial role in human interaction and the mechanisms of consciousness. This understanding positions language within a broader social framework, asserting that it is contextually bound and deeply connected with social interactions. Thus, three main aspects of discourse can be identified: the language use, the transmission of specific ideas, and interaction in comminication situations (Van Dijk, 1997). These features emphasize the involvement of linguistics, psychology and and other social sciences in the study of discourse. Building upon this foundation, Karasik (2002) advanced discourse theory by creating an analytical model with four discourse categories defined by textual features: coherence, cohesion, and various types of intertextuality: constitutive, genre-specific, semantic-pragmatic, and formal-structural that all contribute to the feature of the text,
highlighting its style, meaning and organization.
So, by integrating these perspectives and frameworks, the complexity of discourse can be understood as a rich field of study that interconnects language, social interaction, and cultural context that serves as a foundation for analysis of multimodal and advertising discourse.
1.2. The origins of multimodality in linguistics
Contemporary linguistics increasingly recognizes that language is more than just spoken or written words, integrating a broader range of factors into its analyses. A significant amount of research acknowledges the vital role of other communicative resources like visuals, sounds, gestures, and spatial arrangements in the construction of a specific meaning (Jewitt, 2009; Kress & van Leeuwen, 2001; O'Halloran, 2008). This shift has given rise to the study of multimodality, which explores how these various features of communication (modes) interact and contribute to overall discourse.
To start with, еhe term "multimodality" provides a way for examining communicative situations within a wider context, acknowledging the combined influence of diverse modes like spoken and written language, visuals, spatial arrangements, and non-verbal communication. (Bateman, 2016). Previously, Halliday (1978) introduced a systemic functional linguistics that had been a crucial foundation in understanding language as a social semiotic resource. His focus on the functional aspects of language created a base for identification of visual and linguistic features in multimodal communication. Based on it, the concept of “metafunctions” was introduced that encompasses ideational, interpersonal, and textual functions of both language and imagery: representational meanings can be conveyed through concepts or narratives, interpersonal function is reflected in engaging viewers through emotional or relational cues, and textual one organize elements for clarity and proper understanding (de Silva Joyce & Gaudin, 2007). This classification may inеroduce a foundation for a more detailed linguistic and visual elements analyses and create a deep realization of a particular meaning of the message. 
Influential contributions stemming from this perspective include Kress and van Leeuwen’s (2001, 2021) works who realized Halliday’s ideas of multimodality in a more detailed and representative way. They developed a comprehensive framework for analyzing visual images using these functional concepts, defining the resources through which visual texts can represent ideas about the world, their authors and addressees, and shape cohesion about what is represented. Recognizing that meaning-making extends beyond language to encompass a variety of semiotic resources, they developed a complex framework for analyzing visual images and other non-linguistic modes of communication. Such aspects as representational processes, interactive processes, compositional meaning, modality, and color are introduced (Kress & van Leeuwen, 2021). All of these features contribute to a comprehensive understanding of how different semiotic resources interact within a text, also emphasizing denotation, connotation, and the relationship between images and text. Thus, their approach goes beyond the identification of individual elements of multimodality but creates a foundation for their interconnectedness.
What is more, a thorough multimodal analysis requires considering both the interplay of visuals and language and conducting a distinct stylistic analysis in order to achieve a complete understanding of the identified materials. According to Shakhovskiy (2013), stylistic analysis employs a sophisticated approach for examining linguistic features across multiple levels: phonetic, morphological, lexical, phraseological, syntactical, and graphical. This exploration of linguistic features enriches understanding of how language operates within multimodal contexts of advertising discourse to create meaning and convey specific messages. In particular, Shakhovskiy's framework helps identify the persuasive strategies and emotional appeals employed in a text, as well as the values, beliefs, and assumptions that are embedded within it.
Collectively, these investigations underscore the complex framework of multimodal analysis and its capacity to reveal how communication functions across diverse semiotic resources. Uniting studies from both Kress and van Leeuwen with Shakhovskiy's stylistic analysis allows for a more profound understanding of meaning creation in a variety of communicative contexts. These scholars help establish a foundation that develops verbal understanding of language, emphasizing the necessity of including visual, spatial, and other non-verbal resources in the analysis of communication, which has led to the emergence of multimodal discourse analysis as a distinct field.
1.3. Approaches to multimodality in linguistics
The field of multimodality has grown rapidly, resulting in a diversity of approaches, each with its own emphasis and methodological preferences. There are several prominent approaches.
Firstly, based on the work of Halliday (1978), the social semiotic approach emphasizes the social and cultural context of communication. It views language and visuals not only as channels for transmitting particular information but as socially and culturally constructed resources for creating meaning. Social semiotics highlights how choices of specific means are related to social power, ideology, and cultural values. The metafunctional framework derived from this approach is essential for analyzing how different units contribute to ideational, interpersonal, and textual meanings. This approach is used widely in the study of textbooks, media, and other cultural traits, emphasizing the social impact and ideological underpinnings of multimodal texts.
Secondly, interactional multimodality influenced by Goffman (1981) on face-to-face interaction, centers on the analysis of multimodal meaning-making in conversational contexts. It focuses on how participants use gestures, facial expressions, body posture, gaze, and other bodily resources in conjunction with speech to coordinate their interaction and convey intended meaning. This approach is very closely related to conversation analysis and ethnography (Mondada, 2016). For example, in her work on interactional linguistics and multimodality, she has explored how embodiment and materiality are crucial for understanding the interactive organization of social actions. This approach often uses video recordings and close analysis of interactional data.
Thirdly, the multimodal cognitive linguistics approach bridges cognitive linguistics with multimodal analysis (Forceville & Urios-Aparisi, 2009). It uses knowledge from cognitive psychology to examine how individuals process and perceive multimodal information. This approach investigates mental representations, conceptualizations, and cognitive processes involved in understanding multimodal messages. To explore this, they developed a concept that extends the traditional understanding of stylistic techniques such as metaphor beyond language and allows for other ways to explore cognitive operations that enhance understanding of multimodal expression with the usage of visuals.
Next, critical multimodal analysis combines multimodal analysis with critical theory and seeks to examine the power relations, social inequalities, and ideological biases present in multimodal texts and interactions. Following Fairclough’s (1995) analysis of language and power, it aims to identify the ways in which linguistic and visual components dominate among particular groups and marginalize others, analyze the complex relationships between discourse and society, the reflection of the social power structures (the hierarchical organization of power and influence in a society) in language, and consider the problem of social inequality expressed in discourse (Matitsina, 2019). What is more, critical multimodal analysis often involves the study of advertising, political discourse, and other contexts where power dynamics are particularly remarkable. All approaches of CDA operate with such categories as tense, metaphor, attribute, and argumentative structure of the communicative act, but the theoretical basis and methodology of different schools of CDA have some differences.


The multidisciplinary field of multimodality, reflected in these varied approaches, connects disciplines like linguistics, semiotics, sociology, psychology, and critical theory. By examining the interplay of different modes, each approach offers a unique perspective on how meaning is created and how our understanding of the world is shaped.
1.4. Definition of the mode in the theory of multimodality
The concept of “mode” is central figure of the theory of multimodality, but its precise definition can be quite debatable and can have varying interpretations. While there is no single universally accepted definition, several authors provide their interpretations of the concept of  “mode”.
Kress (2010, p.79) defines a mode as a "socially shaped semiotic resource for realisation of discourse or making meaning". This definition emphasizes that modes are not just physical channels or media for transmitting information, instead, they are understood as cultural and social resources that are actively shaped by human intention and use. He uses examples like writing, speech, and images, and argues that each mode has its own possibilities and limitations, that determine how meanings can be made with them. This definition emphasizes the active and constructive role of modes in meaning-making. Van Leeuwen (2005) also understands a mode as a socially and culturally shaped resource for making meaning and emphasizes that it has a distinct material dimension (its “medium”) and a distinct way of structuring meaning (its “form”). He suggests a mode can be understood on three levels: its material aspect, its structured form, and its social use. Furthermore, modes may have different levels of "specificity" in relation to what they represent; for instance, words may be more specific in their meaning compared to images, while images may be more evocative. His distinction between material, form, and social use adds analytical depth to understanding the concept of “mode”. Jewitt (2009) and O'Halloran (2008) expands on this definition by categorizing modes into two main types: representational modes, which convey content or information, and interactive modes, which engage the audience in the communication process. This distinction is crucial for understanding how different modes function within a text and how they relate to one another. Thus, these definitions emphasize that a mode is not a simple medium or channel of communication, it is a culturally and socially shaped system of resources that actively contributes to the construction of meaning.
In conclusion, the study of multimodal discourse is rooted in a rich theoretical foundation that emphasizes the mutual performance of various modes of communication. By exploring the origins, approaches, and definitions of multimodality, it may provide a base for understanding how meaning is constructed in a complex multimodal aspect.
Section 2: Advertising discourse
2.1. The concept of advertising discourse and its linguistic features
Jorgensen and Phillips (2002) often understand discourse as “the general idea that language is structured according to patterns that determine the utterances of people in various spheres of social life. That is why, various types of discourses started to appear, like “scientific discourse”, “political discourse”, and “advertising discourse” (Baker & Ellece, 2011).
Advertising discourse can be defined as a specialized communicative practice that aims to promote products, services, or ideas through various linguistic and non-linguistic means, so it can be also connected with the multimodality, as it rarely relies only on text, as, for instance, visual imagery can evoke emotions, audio can set a mood, and text conveys specific information, creating a memorable effect. In addition, advertising discourse often utilizes a high degree of intertextuality, drawing on cultural references and connotations of words and images to evoke desired behaviour (Barthes, 1977). Olyanich (2011) identifies advertising discourse as a unique socio-cultural phenomenon that influences various spheres of human activity through its potential to shape public consciousness, influence behavior, and share information about goods and services. Further, advertisements often use evaluative and imperative linguistic features to manipulate audience perception and behavior (Leech, 1966). For instance, epithets in the form of the superlative adjectives, hyperbole, and direct demands.
Further contribution to the field of linguistic features of advertising discourse is made by Emodi (2011), who investigates the semantic choices and persuasive strategies used in advertising language, distinguishing between denotative and connotative meanings. It also incorporates a variety of linguistic techniques, including metaphor, wordplay, deliberate misspelling, and repetition. The research explores how advertisers manipulate these features to evoke emotions, create strong visual associations, and improve message recall through repetition and ambiguity. Additionally, Vasiloaia (2014) identifies phonetic and semantic features such as rhythm, rhyme, alliteration, assonance, graphic elements, and transliteration, which contribute to the overall persuasive effect. Another significant aspect of advertising discourse is its use of hyperbole and exaggeration. As noted by O’Shaughnessy (2003), advertisers often employ persuasive strategies, and extravagant claims to capture attention and create a sense of urgency. This linguistic feature is frequently accompanied by visual elements that reinforce the message, as seen in advertisements that showcase products in idealized settings. All these studies contribute to the complex and detailed exploration of advertising discourse and its unique features.
1.2.2 The role of color and its significance in advertising discourse
Color plays a critical role in advertising, acting as a powerful tool to evoke emotions, establish brand identity, and influence consumer behavior (Ghaderi et al., 2015). The psychology of color is deeply rooted in cultural associations and personal experiences, meaning effective color usage requires a nuanced understanding of the target audience. Specific colors often carry specific connotations. For instance, red is an intense color and has a vibrant and powerful impact on emotions, as it is used to indicate celebration, purity, passion, strength, energy, excitement, power, danger, blood, war, revolution and communalism. Additionally, this color is popularly used by product designers and advertisers to highlight objects of importance and to encourage viewers to take actions. Next, orange, as well as red, has very high visibility, so it is used to catch attention and highlight the most important elements of the design, representing fun, optimism, support and sociability. Blue is considered to reflect tranquility, trust, confidence, liberty,  wisdom, wealth, and truthfulness, whereas yellow signifies sunlight, joy, earth, optimism, intelligence, hope, feminity, and friendship. As for green, it signifies growth, nature, youth, health, and stability, and brown stands for an earthy and environmental quality and is generally coupled with green. It is used to represent calmness, nature, and tradition. Purple color is considered to be the color of humility,ceremony, mystery, sensuality, pride, while pink is used to symbolize appreciation, admiration, sympathy, health, feminity, love, joy, and innocence. Furthermore, black and white in tandem demonstrate cleanliness, fearfulness, seriousness, modernity, elegance, and professionalism, while gray is used to portray elegance, respect, wisdom, old-age, balance, and neutrality (Cerrato, 2012; Singh & Srivastava, 2011). Overall, the strategic application of color is essential for creating persuasive advertising campaigns that resonate with consumers and translate the main ideas with the proper connotations. 
1.2.3. Social and motivational advertising as a separate segment of the advertising discourse
It is interesting to know that the term “social advertising” is used only in Russian advertising discourse, whereas in other countries the term “public advertising” is used for this phenomenon (Tomilin, 2010). The Federal Law No. 38-FZ of March 13, 2006 "On Advertising" provides the definition of social advertising as representation of public and state interests that aims to achieving charitable goals. The following interpretation can be found in scientific literature that defines social advertising as a special type of non-commercial information aimed to achieve certain social goals by the state, executive authorities or non-commercial organizations (Gribok, 2008; Vainer & Gladkikh, 2012). In any case, social advertising should not be considered as a separately existing phenomenon, but within the framework of the general system of public relations.
According to Kinnick et al. (1996), social advertising employs similar linguistic strategies as commercial advertising but often emphasizes ethical appeals and social responsibility. As for main strategies utilized in social advertising, it is vital to highlight the following features: simple sentences, simple words and phrases, and persuasiveness. It can be represented in the stylistic devices used in such advertising: imperative verbs, personal pronoun “you”, exclamatory sentences, explicit complex imagery that sometimes need an analysis for understanding, and emotive language with the usage of positive or negative connotations of words that evoke strong emotions and contribute to the meaning of recommendation to do something (Tatisheva, 2019). Cheremisina (2016) also examines the linguistic features of social advertising, focusing on various parts of speech that influence audience perception. She points out that verbs in the imperative mood are crucial for calls to action, while nouns and adjectives evoke emotional responses, also the use of statistics and numbers enhances the persuasive impact of advertising messages.
Thus, the main goal of the social advertising discourse is to influence the worldview of the social addressee, possibly changing his opinions, beliefs, and social attitudes (Zvada & Kuznetsova, 2019). The effectiveness of social advertising is often evaluated through its ability to resonate with the target audience. This approach not only raises awareness but also fosters a sense of community and collective responsibility.
Another popular type of advertising is motivational. It is a form of advertising that aims to inspire and motivate individuals. They often use inspiring messages, images, and stories to encourage people to take action, pursue their goals, or adopt positive attitudes (Support, 2024). While traditional advertising focuses on features and benefits, motivational advertising connects with consumers on a deeper psychological level, leveraging theories from behavioral science and neuroscience. For instance, brands like Nike does not just sell products, but looks for innovative ways to connect with its audience through creativity like the Nike+ platform. It’s an app that invites all Nike members to engage with one another, share their stories and promote active lifestyle (Garces, 2024). Additionally, emotions play an essential role in motivational advertising. Research by Berger and Milkman (2012) found that content evoking strong emotions such as inspiration, excitement, or even fear is more likely to be shared and remembered. For example, public campaigns on health risks because of negative addictions may use fear appeals to motivate behavioral change.
As for the main features of the motivational advertising, it is quite similar with the social advertising, where a call to action, imperatives, personal pronouns “I” and “you”, epithets, metaphors, hyperbole, comparisons, personifications, repetitions, rhyming slogans, and personification (Ivanova & Ponomareva, 2021). All of these strategies provide an opportunity to express effectively the implied meanings and evoke emotional response.
In summary, social advertising represents a distinct segment of advertising discourse that prioritizes social change over commercial profit. Its linguistic features and multimodal elements work together to create persuasive messages that aim to inspire action and raise awareness of social issues, whereas motivational advertising is an effective tool that can foster deep connections with the viewers and motivate them to do or not to do specific actions,maintaining authenticity and trust.
2.3. Multimodality in advertising discourse
As it was stated earlier, multimodality refers to the use of multiple modes of communication, including visual, auditory, and textual elements, to convey meaning. In the context of advertising discourse, multimodality plays a crucial role in enhancing the persuasive impact of advertisements. According to Kress and van Leeuwen (2006), advertisements often combine images, text, and colors to create a cohesive message that appeals to various senses. This multimodal approach allows advertisers to create rich and engaging narratives that resonate with consumers. For instance, a print advertisement for a luxury product for women may feature high-quality images, elegant typography, and a sophisticated color palette to evoke feelings of prestige and desirability. Furthermore, social advertising often leverages multimodal elements to enhance its impact. As for social advertising, according to Gunter and Furnham (1992), the combination of visual and verbal communication in social advertising can create a more compelling message. For example, campaigns promoting gender inequalities may use striking visuals of harassment alongside powerful slogans to evoke a sense of urgency and responsibility.
In this context, the popularity of posters, that exemplify the multimodal nature of advertising discourse, has experienced significant surges during crucial historical and social events. The interplay between image and text in posters can create a powerful emotional response, making them an effective medium for both commercial and social advertising. For example, one such period was during the Russian Civil War (1917–1923), a time marked by the emergence of diverse social ideologies, movements, and transformations. Posters played a crucial role in this era as tools for agitation, mobilization, and unity among the people. They served as vital instruments for navigating challenging times (Symonovich, 2018). It was that period when the Soviet posters presented a unique context for analyzing women's depiction, since they were one of the main figures of promotional ideas. These portrayals emphasized women's roles in nation-building efforts by depicting them as multifaceted figures: workers, mothers, and public activists (Bolotova, 2018; Mispoulet, 2006; Savostkina, 2018). These studies highlight popular female "missions" represented on these posters and explore why such representations were prevalent and provide background for understanding the choice of visuals and language used in female representation of that era.
As for the advertising of goods, the use of multimodal elements can enhance brand recognition and build trust. According to Bakhurst (2007), advertisements that employ consistent visual and textual elements across various media can strengthen brand identity and consumer loyalty. This is evident in campaigns that use specific colors, fonts, and logos to create a cohesive brand image across print, digital, and other platforms, as the rise of digital media has further expanded the possibilities for multimodal advertising discourse. With the advent of social media and online platforms, advertisers can now incorporate interactive elements, such as videos, animations, and user-generated content, into their campaigns. As noted by McQuail (2010), this interactivity allows for greater audience engagement and participation, leading to more effective persuasive communication.
In conclusion, multimodality is a fundamental aspect of advertising discourse, allowing advertisers to create rich and engaging messages that resonate with consumers. The combination of visual and textual elements, particularly in posters and digital media, enhances the persuasive impact of advertisements and contributes to brand recognition and recall.
Section 3: Female image in the advertising discourse
3.1. Linguistic features of the female image representation in advertising discourse
The representation of female images in advertising discourse has evolved significantly over time, reflecting broader social changes and cultural shifts. The study of female representation in advertising is crucial for understanding how female representation and ideals are constructed, communicated, and transformed through visual and linguistic means. Also, van Leeuwen (1996, p. 43) argues that “representations can reallocate roles; rearrange the social relations between the participants”, depending on the type of information or ideology being conveyed. Research indicates that language extends beyond simple communication, because it is also important in constructing social realities (Foucault, 1969). In the realm of advertising, for example, domestic roles attributed to women are often perceived as innate and general, yet they signify the subordination of women and the superiority of men, who are associated with positions of power in society. However, as time passed, women roles in the society have changed.
Linguistically, specific lexical choices, syntactic structures, and phonetic elements can profoundly influence how women are depicted in advertisements. For instance, alliteration is frequently employed at the phonetic level, and at the lexical level there is the vocabulary that resonates with feminine stereotypes, appealing to themes of family, stability, vulnerability, wisdom, and beauty, often through metaphor and comparison (Monakhova et al., 2023). Also, advertisements often utilize euphemisms and hyperbolic language to enhance the desirability of female figures, thereby reinforcing traditional gender stereotypes (Goffman, 1979). This categorization is reflected visually through elements such as color schemes, the primary figure's gestures and facial expressions, and connotations, as well as verbally through the use of pronouns, persuasive language, disjunctive syntax, vocabulary, and both positive and negative adjectives. By utilizing these features, advertising discourse often categorizes women into archetypes of the ideal of beauty, the portrayal of women as passive objects, the ideal of domesticity, and the stereotype of the liberated woman (Luzón Marco, 1997). Nevertheless, other female roles of the past decades can be pointed out: women as a working force, women as a mother, women as the houskeeper, women as a public activist (Savostkina, 2018). So, the linguistic framing of female images in advertising can reflect existing cultural norms and expectations regarding femininity.
3.2. Factors affecting the representation of female images in advertising discourse
The representation of female images in advertising is influenced by a variety of factors, including cultural norms, political systems, and social dynamics (Rogatin, 2023). Each of these factors contributes to shaping the narrative surrounding femininity and influences how women are depicted in advertisements. Firstly, various cultures have their unique traditions that influence advertising styles and content. For instance, Spanish advertising tends to be more emotionally and visually engaging and memorable, while Russian advertisements often focus on rational persuasion and more patriotic themes (Agrba, 2019). These cultural differences may lead to varied portrayals of women reflected in advertising within different countries and time periods. Secondly, the political system also affects the representation of women in advertising by influencing regulatory environments and societal norms endorsed by the government. For example, during the Soviet era, advertising was heavily controlled to align with state policies rather than individual’s needs. Thirdly, social factors are also vital, as society evolves, and women are increasingly portrayed in more diverse and powerful roles in advertising (Kordrostami & Laczniak, 2022). Thus, these factors shape the diverse ways female images are represented across different contexts within the advertising discourse that also can influence the usage of specific visuals and language features.
All these stereotypes and biases appear because of the tendencies in media, various political, social and cultural contexts that influence verbal and non-verbal features in the advertising woman representation. Bulycheva (2008) states that in the pre-revolutionary period, advertising of Russian culture was saturated with national features, used plots from folk tales and traditional symbols, which contributed to its perception. During the Soviet period, advertising became imperative, reflecting government policy and ignoring the real needs of consumers. With the transition to a market economy, advertising began to adapt, providing messages that were more familiar and understandable to the audience. 
3.2.1 Cultural aspects
Cultural context plays a significant role in shaping the ideals of femininity represented in advertising. Different cultures possess distinct values, beliefs, and practices that inform their views on gender roles. For instance, research by Barthes (1977) highlights how cultural symbols embedded in advertisements can shape societal perceptions. In the Soviet Union during the 1930s, female representation in advertising often aligned with socialist ideals, portraying women as strong, independent workers contributing to the collective (Mespoulet, 2006). Conversely, Spanish advertisements from the same period reflected more traditional notions of femininity, emphasizing maternity in the combination with active political performance.
For instance, in the pre-revolutionary period, advertising of Russian culture was characterized by national features, used plots from folk tales and traditional symbols, which contributed to its perception. Both traditions strive to synthesize diverse ideas, but the Spanish tradition is more eclectic, whereas Russian philosophy focuses on historical context (Yakovleva, 2003). Providing more differences in comparison between advertising in Spain and Russia, it is defined that Spanish advertising as vivid and emotional, utilizing expressive visual and verbal means to foster a trusting dialogue with the audience. Agrba (2019) states that the style of Russian advertising tends to be more monologue-based and imperative, focusing on rational persuasion and patriotism, which can create a distance between advertisers and audiences. Spanish advertising, with its emphasis on aesthetic elements and information compression, often addresses specific individuals, while Russian advertising tends to appeal to a broader audience, including those who may not be able to solve the presented problems.
3.2.2. Political system
The political landscape significantly impacts the portrayal of women in advertising discourse. Political ideologies dictate the values that are promoted through media channels, including advertisements. The 1930s was a period of major transformation in both countries: in the Soviet Union, it marked a time of rapid industrialization, agricultural collectivization, and the shift toward an authoritarian political regime. Following the Civil War in 1922, widespread political repression also took place (Korovin, 2008). As a result, Soviet posters played a significant role in reshaping female identity, portraying women as essential contributors to the socialist state and promoting their participation in traditionally male-dominated industries and political spheres. That is why advertising was utilized as a tool for propaganda to promote state-sanctioned ideals of femininity (Zaslavskaya, 1997). In contrast, the Spanish poster reinforced existing female roles focues on family, aiming to inspire political actions within familiar values but with the appearing themes of political agitation, although the period of 1930s in Spain was also difficult because of the pre-military time of Spanish Civil War (1936-1939) (Novikov, 2007). Lately, the post-Franco era marked a shift towards more progressive representations of women in advertising, reflecting broader societal changes towards gender equality.
3.2.3. Social aspects
Social factors such as economic conditions, education levels, and technological advancements also contribute to shaping female representation in advertising discourse. The rise of consumer culture in both Russia and Spain has led to an increased focus on individualism and personal identity in advertisements (Baudrillard, 1998). This shift has allowed for more diverse portrayals of women that go beyond traditional stereotypes. Moreover, the impact of social media has transformed how women are represented in advertising. As brands seek to connect with younger audiences, they increasingly utilize influencers and user-generated content that challenge conventional beauty standards and promote authenticity (Marwick, 2015). Furthermore, the activity of feminism ideologies among cultures also contributes to more explicit representation of women. This evolution reflects a broader societal shift towards inclusivity and empowerment in the representation of women.

Together, these diverse perspectives demonstrate the complex relationship between language, culture, and cognition, underscoring the multidisciplinary nature of the field. Section 4: Methodology
The methodology of this study is complex, aiming to analyze the visual and linguistic representation of female figures in advertising materials across different historical periods and geographic locations. It is based on a collection, classification, and analysis of visual and textual data to understand how meaning is constructed and conveyed through various modes of communication. It consists of 40 samples of the advertising materials, including agitational posters, commercial advertisements of goods, as well as non-commercial social and motivational advertising of the 1930s and 2020s in the Soviet Union, Russia, and Spain. Data was sourced manually, from open online sources such as Pinterest and Google,  with the usage of targeted keywords as “women”, “poster”, “Soviet Union”, “Russia” “Spain”, “war”, “work”, “beauty”, “products”, “2020s”, and “1930s”. These materials were divided into eight distinct groups with 4-6 examples for each group based on the following criteria: time period, location, presence of female imagery, type of advertising.
The avertisements were classified according to the following aspects: geographical location, aim and time period. A list of the following groups was formed:
- Group 1: Soviet agitational posters of the 1930s;
- Group 2: Soviet commercial posters of goods of the 1930s;
- Group 3: Spanish agitational posters of the 1930s;
- Group 4: Spanish commercial posters of goods of the 1930s.
- Group 5: Russian social and motivational ads of the 2020s;
- Group 6: Russian commercial ads of goods of the 2020s;
- Group 7: Spanish social and motivational ads of the 2020s;
- Group 8: Spanish commercial ads of goods of the 2020s.
The data analysis is conducted using stylistic (Shakhovskiy, 2013) and multimodal analyses (Kress & van Leeuwen, 2021) that examines how meaning is constructed through the interconnection of various modes.
Based on this research the table for data analysis is created for a more complete analysis of advertising and poster materials of every analyzed group. These methods explore how verbal elements are combined with multimodal ones to represent a distinct female image within different periods and contexts. By integrating these approaches, this study aims to provide a comprehensive understanding of how meaning is constructed through both linguistic style and visual communication strategies. Key findings from Practical Part
Soviet agitational posters of the 1930s
Most posters utilize conceptual representational processes, depicting women as symbols of progress and ideological beliefs, participating in industrialization, education, and national development. These images often show women with medals, holding grain, or reading, emphasizing strength, literacy, and responsibility. Other posters employ narrative processes, for instance, contrasting traditional roles with modern, symbolizing societal transition and the interaction between saleswomen and customer, highlighting professionalism. In terms of engagement, the posters use direct and indirect gaze to represent authority, hard work, or kindness. The imagery functions as a demand, urging viewers to participate in political or social activities through verbal cues, reinforcing calls to action. Compositionally, central figures (women) are prominently framed, composition is balanced. Bright, saturated colors like red dominate, symbolizing revolution and vitality, while blue conveys trust and progress, and earth tones evoke labor and connection to land. Stylized imagery, with sharp lines and shallow depth of field, enhances focal clarity and emotional impact.
Stylistically, posters employ alliteration, rhythm, and metaphors to reinforce ideological messages. Metonymies symbolize national strength, while epithets describe figures in the way of Soviet ideology. Also, allusion to revolutionary literature connects current efforts to historical narratives, and repetition, parallel structures, and contrasting forms strengthen and emphasize the main slogans. Graphically, punctuation and bold lettering emphasize urgency and importance, making messages clear and striking.
Overall, these posters combine visual and linguistic strategies, such as representational techniques, composition, stylistic devices, and color symbolism to promote Soviet ideals, inspire collective action, and craft a compelling narrative of progress, transformation, and societal unity.
Soviet commercial posters of goods of the 1930s
Representational processes predominantly utilize conceptual associations, linking products with deliciousness, health, beauty protection, and celebration, embodied by a traditionally dressed woman in a particular setting. Interactively, all posters feature women using direct or indirect gaze to engage viewers, fostering connection or recommendation. The images function as offer images, promoting products without demanding action, contributing to a welcoming and quite persuasive tone. Compositionally, women's faces are central and salient, balanced by product images, text, and background context. Modality shows stylized, non-photographic designs with moderate realism. Vibrant colors such as yellow, red and blue evoke cheerfulness, joy and passion, while muted tones convey simplicity and comfort, sharpness and clarity are consistently high.
Expressive and stylistic devices include rhyme and alliteration, enhancing memorability. Epithets emphasize desirable qualities, hyperbole exaggerates the main ideas and attracts the attention, whereas inversion and parallel constructions add rhythm and persuasive messages. Graphically, the posters do not employ excessive use of punctuation marks like exclamation marks, resulting in a more natural tone except where emphasis is specifically needed. Capitalization is used selectively to enhance visual effect. Thus, the posters utilize a range of interconnected visual and textual strategies such as conceptual and narrative processes,  direct engagement, balanced composition, vibrant color palettes, expressive language, and stylistic devices to construct compelling, positive, and culturally resonant advertisements.
So, these features help promote products as symbols of health, beauty, happiness, and a desired sense of modernity, effectively appealing to the target audience’s deepest aspirations and cultural values.
Spanish agitational posters of the 1930s
The analysis of posters shows a focus on social mobilization and support during wartime. All feature women and sometimes children, highlighting nurturing and collective effort against military times. Some posters use narrative styles, depicting actions like women voting or supporting industries, some adopt more symbolic visuals that evoke broader ideas such as safety, sacrifice, and duty through the imagery. The depicted actors such as textile workers, farmers, nurses, soldiers, and children reinforce specific messages aligned with wartime roles. Most posters employ direct address to actively engage viewers, others focus on objects or actions, adding seriousness and concentration to the meaning. They function as demand images, urging support or action through persuasive strategies. Compositions strategically frame figures and text to maximize impact. For example, contrasting colors and close arrangements of the elements emphasize urgency, direct attention and reinforce messages of collective responsibility. Furthermore, the posters employ stylization over realism, employing sharp lines, bold shapes, and saturated colors. Red dominates, symbolizing passion and sacrifice, while other tones convey themes like peace or agriculture. 
Expressively, they employ imperatives, rhetorical devices such as epithets, hyperbole, metonymy, and repetition to evoke emotional responses. Visual cues like exclamation marks and bold fonts demonstrate command, reinforcing calls to action.
In summary, these posters combine symbolic imagery, active engagement, strategic composition, stylized aesthetics, and persuasive language to agitate for support, foster unity, and convey urgent wartime messages effectively.
Spanish commercial posters of goods the 1930s
All five posters mainly use conceptual processes, linking visual elements to ideas like beauty, cleanliness, cultural pride, and modernity. The main figures are stylized, they are attractive women such as the elegant women promoting soap and other cleaning products, the culturally dressed woman, and the "woman of dreams". Some posters engage viewers via averted gaze and textual cues, fostering interpretation and highlighting the independence of female figures, while others directly address viewers through eye contact, creating strong trustworthy connections. Contrast and framing highlight key elements, with backgrounds emphasizing figures and products. Text placement balances visuals and facilitates understanding of the promotion. All posters favor stylized, vintage-inspired imagery with sharp lines and limited depth of field. Most use monochrome palettes, evoking elegance, except one, which employs vibrant colors to express energy and cultural vibrancy.
Stylistically, the posters use hyperbole, metaphors, and epithets to describe the promoted products and main figures, women. Additionally, there is an example of allusion to artisanal heritage, suggesting tradition and craftsmanship. On the phraseological level, repetition and inversion reinforce key ideas, enhancing memorability and persuasiveness. Graphically, capitalization and font variation emphasize key elements like product names and slogans. Punctuation is quite basic, while font size and boldness highlight the essential items.
To conclude, these posters combine representational and stylistic strategies such as conceptual processes, balanced composition, vibrant or muted palettes, and persuasive devices to underscore ideals of beauty, domesticity, cultural pride, and modernity. 
Russian social and motivational ads of the 2020s
The analysis of posters reveals cohesive use of visual and linguistic strategies to convey abstract ideas and evoke emotion. All posters engage viewers with varying strategies, functioning as demands. Some posters use indirect gaze directions like upward or focused to evoke hope, concentration, or connection,  seeking for emotional or reflective responses, urging viewers to consider their health and choices, thus fulfilling a demand. The arrangement of elements reinforces messages by balanced imagery and text, framing centering and vertical split contrasting positive and negative scenes. Central placement of key figures and symbols ensures they are the visual focus, strengthening the message. As for modality, it is created through color, visuals and sharpness that enhance realism. Color schemes encode meaning, for instance, warm gold and brown symbolize hope or pink and white may evoke femininity.
Stylistic devices enrich the messages by utilizing metaphors, hyperbole and epithets to stress the importance and seriousness of the topics. Moreover, there is a quotation that lends authority and antithesis that highlights contrasting choices. Parallel structures create rhythm and inversion draws attention. Graphically, textual elements are framing with a cursive script, bold fonts or omission of punctuation for a direct tone.
Thus, these posters employ cohesive visual, linguistic, compositional, modal, stylistic, and graphical strategies. They use metaphors, symbols, balanced element placement, and stylistic devices to craft compelling messages that prompt viewers to reflect on their behaviors and social choices.
Russian commercial ads of goods of the 2020s
The posters primarily use conceptual representational processes, focusing on evoking emotions, ideals, and associations rather than telling specific stories. They aim to generate positive feelings linked to the products such as relaxation, happiness, luxury, attractiveness, and confidence through a central female figures, through their relaxed facial expressions and elegant poses. Viewers are engaged mainly through models’ direct or inviting eye contact and smile, fostering trust and connection. Overall, the posters serve as “offers”, inviting viewers to try the products rather than demanding immediate action. In composition, most posters place the model or product centrally, with supporting text at the bottom to emphasize benefits. More than that, all of the posters exhibit high realism through professional photography with sharpness and clarity reinforcing a high-quality imagery, enhancing credibility. Color palettes represent bright yellow and red that evoke energy and passion, while muted tones suggest calmness and serenity.
Stylistic devices include phonetic features like alliteration, morphological emphasis, and lexical metaphors, hyperboles, epithets, and personification. Also, contrast through oxymoron highlights the transformative effects of the product. Syntactically, many posters employ parallelism, antithesis, and repetition to reinforce messages. Graphically, bold fonts, capitalization or exclamation marks emphasize the messages. Layouts balance visual elements, with models and products prominently featured and supporting text arranged to guide focus.
Overall, these posters combine conceptual imagery, viewer engagement via gaze and expression, strategic composition, high realism, vivid colors, and stylistic devices to create persuasive advertisements. They aim to showcase product features, positioning them as ways to beauty, confidence, and luxury.
Spanish social and motivational ads of the 2020s
All four posters predominantly employ conceptual and symbolic processes rather than narrative sequences. This choice allows them to raise awareness and provoke reflection on social issues. For instance, a representation of a diverse group of women symbolizes resilience and collective strength, aligning with its goal of promoting gender equality and celebrating International Women’s Day; symbols such as traps and social media icons resist the dangers and manipulative nature of social media platforms; or a representation of the hidden realities of drug addiction. The posters use visual strategies that shape viewer engagement, for example, through women looking away or only slightly at the viewer, or through a representation of the woman with closed eyes that may create distance between the actors and the viewers, but may give a space for reflection about contemporary social problems. functioning as a demand image to evoke empathy and highlight addiction’s seriousness. Many ads rely on metaphoric images and act as implied demands. Element placement and framing contribute to the conveyed messages by balanced text and imagery. However, the posters differ in realism, as one poster employs a symbolic, simplified illustration style, while others use realistic photographs with sharp focus to increase authenticity. Color palettes support emotional tones with purple shades, grey, brown and blue that evoke calmness but hint at deception, while dark colors underscore despair and seriousness.
Stylistically, usually hyperboles, euphemisms, epithets, are demonstrated in the texts, as well as syntactical parallelism and inversion are used for emphasis. Furthemore, some ads omit punctuation and use bold, open-ended text to foster an informal, reflective tone, while others maintain standard punctuation for clarity.
So, these posters employ diverse visual and stylistic strategies like symbolism, composition, color, and rhetorical devices to create convincing messages that challenge perceptions and evoke emotional responses. Their varied approaches aim to engage viewers in reflection, empathy, or critical awareness, promoting social change and understanding. 
Spanish commercial ads of goods of the 2020s
All posters mainly use conceptual imagery rather than literal actions, aiming to evoke emotions and associations related to beauty, strength, shopping, family, and health. For example, some posters feature celebrities or models as aspirational figures embodying qualities like confidence, youthfulness, and advanced beauty technology. Interactions with the viewers is primarily established through gaze, as most posters employ actors making direct eye contact to foster engagement and trust. This gaze strategy positions the posters as offers, inviting viewers to become familiar with these goods. Compositionally, brand logos are placed often at the top, enhancing recognition; сentral figures are emphasized through size and placement. Layouts balance supporting text, images, and visual hierarchy. Most posters feature realistic images achieved through photography combined with graphic elements. As for color saturation, bright colors attract attention, whereas natural tones create a calm and more natural visual aesthetic: warm colors like yellow and pink evoke energy and femininity; white and silver suggest brilliance and technology; red, purple, blue, and green support themes of health, beauty, and lifestyle.
Stylistically, posters employ metaphors, epithets, euphemism and hyperbole to strengthen the products’ effects, while personification attributes human qualities to products, enriching narratives. At the syntactical level, posters often use parallel constructions to reinforce messages by listing qualities or benefits; repetition also emphasizes key ideas, creating rhythm and impact. Graphically, many posters minimize punctuation, using styled fonts and capitalization to set the elegant or credible visual impact.
To sum up, these posters establish emotional connections through gaze engagement and balanced composition, prominently featuring their brands. Realistic photography combined with expressive color palettes and strategic layout effectively evoke desire, trust, and emotional resonance in conceptual imagery with stylistic devices such as metaphors, hyperbole, parallelism, and color symbolism to offer and promote the given products.
4.5. Comparative analysis
The visual communication strategies across different eras and cultural contexts reveal the evolution of how imagery and stylistic devices are employed to shape commercial and non-commercial advertising. This analysis compares posters from the 1930s and 2020s, focusing on Soviet, Spanish, and Russian ads, examining their visual language, engagement techniques, stylistic choices, and thematic focuses.
Firstly, ideological and social functions vary. In the 1930s, Soviet posters functioned as ideological and agitational tools. Agitational posters emphasize progress, industrialization, and societal unity, depicting women as symbols of strength, literacy, and responsibility to reinforce revolutionary ideals and mobilize collective action. Spanish posters from the same era center on wartime social mobilization, emphasizing collective effort, sacrifice, and societal resilience, with imagery of women and children symbolizing nurturing roles and support against external threats. Commercial posters, regardless of time period and culture, focus on individual needs like beauty, health, and household chores. Moving to the 2020s, Russian social and motivational posters aim to evoke emotional reflection on issues like health, self-confidence, social responsibility, and equality, fostering empathy and social consciousness rather than direct ideological mobilization. Spanish posters of the same period focus on gender equality, social awareness, and cultural pride, using symbolic imagery to provoke reflection and empathy. 
Secondly, across eras, gaze direction remains a crucial element. Soviet posters often employ direct or indirect gazes to symbolize authority, strength, or kindness, reinforcing ideological messages. Contemporary posters, whether Russian or Spanish, tend to use indirect gaze or slight eye contact to evoke reflection and hope, encouraging viewers to sympathize. However, the most active engagement with viewers, demanding their attention, is based on commanding visuals, striking symbolic imagery, and verbal cues. Commercial posters across eras primarily serve as offers, using gaze, imagery, and layout to evoke trust and aspiration. As for the composition, most posters across all the aspects highlight the central figures of women by framing, salience, saturation, and size to emphasize their importance, which is also balanced with textual elements. Color aspect also exemplifies evolution: the 1930s utilize bright, saturated reds and bold tones associated with revolution, vitality, and urgency. In contrast, 2020s palettes are more varied: vivid colors evoke energy and optimism, while muted tones suggest seriousness or calmness, reflecting a more emotional approach. 
Thirdly, posters have changed in stylistics as well. Soviet posters of the 1930s employ alliteration, rhythm, metaphors, epithets, and repetition, often complemented by bold punctuation and lettering, to reinforce their messages with urgency, whereas Spanish posters of the same era often use imperatives, epithets, hyperbole, metonymy, and rhetorical devices such as repetition and inversion to evoke emotional responses, reinforced by command-like punctuation and typography that also underscore urgency and team spirit. As time passed, modern posters of both cultures started to incorporate more devices, so the examples of personification, euphemisms, more explicit epithets and hyperbole, contrasting oxymoron, and selective punctuation appeared to create persuasive, emotionally engaging messages that are also emphasized by various types of fonts. Culturally, Soviet posters rely on metonymies and historical allusions, whereas contemporary posters tend to use metaphors and symbols that evoke personal attitude or social values.
Overall, while core principles like central figures, gaze engagement, and color symbolism remain consistent, the thematic focus and stylistic strategies have markedly evolved. Soviet posters of the 1930s harnessed bold, stylized imagery and direct language to promote collective effort and governments’s desires and needs. In contrast, modern posters in Russia and Spain utilize realistic imagery, symbolism, and emotionally layered language to foster reflection, social consciousness, and personal ambitions. This progression reflects broader societal shifts from collective ideological narratives toward individual-centered, emotionally resonant communication, demonstrating both continuity in visual language strategies and adaptability to changing social and cultural contexts.
Conclusion
To sum up, this study has explored the complex ways in which advertising reflects and reinforces cultural, political, and social values and gender role through a comprehensive multimodal comparative analysis of female representation in Russian and Spanish advertising from the 1930s and 2020s. The usage of Kress and van Leeuwen's framework (2021) revealed how representational, interactive, and compositional elements contribute to the construction of female identity, while Shakhovskiy's stylistic analysis (2013) identified the persuasive linguistic strategies employed to evoke particular emotional responses, thoughtprovoking mood or specific actions.
One of the key findings of this study is the sift in ideological and social functions of the advertising. Soviet posters of the 1930s represent women as symbols to promote progress and unity, serving ideological aims, whereas Spanish posters of the same era focus on wartime support, highlighting women's motherhood roles. The application of such stylistic devices as imperatives, metonymy, rhythm, alliteration and their functions are closely related with the political context of that time, specifically with period of inustrialization and collectivization in the Soviet Union in the 1930s and period of pre-military time in Spain when the main focuses were at the motherhood values, unity and worforce. In the 2020s Russian social posters encourage more reflection on issues like health, self-confidence, and social responsibility and Spanish posters promote gender equality, social media awareness and healthy lifestyle. Thus, this change marks a move from collective messaging to fostering individual desires, awareness and social empathy because of the evolution of social factors as norms, beliefs, values, and interests where almost only individualistic approach and authenticity are socially approved. Although, commercial ads throughout the time consistently target individual needs that meets just basic needs of a woman like food, medicine, products for cleaning and casual cosmetics in the 1930s and a broder context of goods as shopping, clothes, various types of cosmetics, and care products in the 2020s.
As for the visuals, posters of the 1930s in general utilize conceptual representational processes, mediam modality and direct interaction with the viewers. Compositionally, central figures are prominently framed within balanced layouts, complemented by bright, saturated colors like red symbolizing revolution and blue evoking trust and progress. However, in the modern times the tecnhiques of representation become more sophisticated, symbolic and authentic: photographic realism appeares, color palletes gain different connotations and imagery and its placement enhance the textual meaning which, in turn, enlarges the list of stylistic devices used in the ads of the 1930s, like direct address to the audience, metaphors, epithets, metonymies, and parallel constructions, with euphemisms, oxymorons, personification and varied graphical elements and styles that is used to create a more explicit, bright and authentic message for a diverse audience of modern times.
Nevertheless, it is important to acknowledge the limitations of this study. The study is based on a sample of 40 pieces, which may not provide enough depth to thoroughly examine all devices and strategies used in the representation of female images. Nonetheless, it appears to be sufficient for identifying the most common practices used in the female ideals demonstration. Furthermore, the subjectivity may introduce bias and affect objectivity and the data interpretation. Finally, focusing on the Soviet Union, Russia, and Spain may restrict the study's scope and do not allow for generalization for other cultural contexts.
To address these limitation, future research should incorporate more analytical frameworks, include additional regions and explore different time periods to provide more knowledge about how different cultures influence female representation across various historical periods.
Overall, this study has contributed to the research on multimodality, advertising, and gender studies, providing a framework for future investigations into the representation of women in media by providing a direct comparative analysis across various cultural and historical contexts and demonstrating the effectiveness of combining multimodal and stylistic analysis for a comprehensive understanding of advertising that is essential for establising a more inclusive society.
