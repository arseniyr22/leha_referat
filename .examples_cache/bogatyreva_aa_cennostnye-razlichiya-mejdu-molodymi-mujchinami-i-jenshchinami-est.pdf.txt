Abstract 

2 

The  ideological  gender  gap,  which  emerged  originally  in  Western  democracies,  has  recently  been 

noticed  in  other  nations  as  well.  This  study  aims  to  explore  whether  this  trend  has  reached  Russia. 

Using data from the R. Inglehart Laboratory for Comparative Social Research (2019-2020), the paper 

analyzes  the  following  set  of  values:  equality  of  opportunities, freedom of choice, tolerance towards 

diversity, and trust in state institutions. According to the developmental theory of the gender gap, the 

shift  towards  post-materialist  values  is  associated  with  generational  change. The younger generation 

holds more progressive views, and young women tend to hold these views more often. Thus, the shift 

towards  post-materialist  values  is  considered  to  be  faster  among  women,  forming  a  gender  gap  in 

ideology.  The  results  of  the  regression  analysis  indicate  that  young  men  and  women  in Russia have 

different views on gender equality, justification of abortion, divorce and homosexuality, and tolerance 

of  outgroups.  At  the  same  time,  the  gap  in  values  related  to  gender  equality  decreases  for  older 

generations, while the gaps in other values do not follow this pattern.

Introduction 

4 

The research field devoted to the gender gap in ideology has been actively developing since the end of 

the twentieth century, when the so-called modern gender gap was discovered, demonstrating the more 

liberal  political  behavior  of  women1.  This  phenomenon  was  first  observed  primarily  through  the 

analysis  of  election  results.  Research  conducted  in  the  US2,  Canada3,  and  European states4 provided 

strong evidence of an ideological gender gap in Western democracies. Along with important structural 

factors  such  as  the  increase  in  women’s  participation  in  the  labor  force  and  the  growing  access  to 

women’s education, the gender gap was also explained by the shift towards post-materialist values that 

occurs  with  generational  change5. Thus, it has been observed that the gender gap in ideology is most 

pronounced among the younger generation, and gradually disappears for older groups.  

Recently,  a  significant  ideological  gap  between  young  men and women was noticed globally6. Thus, 

there are grounds to expect further spread of this trend. In Russia, the situation is rather complex. Over 

the past three decades, certain Western trends became nationwide. These include raising the age of first 

marriage,  normalizing  unregistered  cohabitation7,  and  a  greater  embrace  of  certain  post-materialist 

values among the younger generation8. According to the All-Russian Public Opinion Research Center, 

the  support  of  feminism  is  significantly higher among people aged 18-24 than among older cohorts9. 

However,  the  conservative  turn  of  the  Russian  state10  could  overshadow  this  evolving  dynamic.

and  more  likely  to  favor  equal  responsibilities  in  child-raising11,  there  is  evidence  of  greater

The  goal  of  this  study  is  to  analyze  whether  there is a link between gender and adherence to certain 

values  within  the  cohort  of  young  people  aged 18-24 in Russia, that is whether the Western trend of 

gender  gap  in  ideology  has  reached  Russia.  As  already  mentioned,  it  is  assumed  that  the  younger 

generation  holds  more  progressive  views,  but  the  shift  towards  these  values  is  more  rapid  among 

women.  Thus,  in  order  to  understand  whether  the  trend  of  the ideological gender gap is relevant for 

Russia,  it  is  necessary  to  check  the  following  facets:  1)  the  generational  effect;  2)  the gender effect 

within  the  young  cohort  and  3)  the  intensity  of  the  gender  gap  among  different  age  cohorts.  In this 

regard, the following objectives are set: 1) to provide an overview of the research field, covering both 

global  trends  and  the  Russian  context; 2) to apply the developmental theory of the gender gap to the 

Russian context, constructing the hypotheses; 3) to provide operationalization for values; 4) to validate 

the  measurement  of  values  via  confirmatory  factor  analysis;  5)  to  test  the  proposed  hypotheses  via 

regression analysis and describe the results; 6) to reflect on the results obtained and to place them into 

the general academic discussion. 

The  developmental  theory  of  the  gender  gap14  is  primarily  used  when  proposing  hypotheses.  It  is 

assumed that young people in Russia are more inclined to post-materialist values15, including equality 

of opportunities, freedom of choice and tolerance towards diversity. The shift towards post-materialist 

values  is  faster  among  women,  thus  creating  a  gender  gap16.  At  the  same  time,  adherence  to 

post-materialist  values  excludes  support  for  authoritarian  rule17.  Therefore,  it  is  expected that young 

people  in  Russia  have  less  trust  in  state  institutions,  and  in  the  young  cohort  distrust  is  more 

pronounced  among  women.  Concluding  the  above,  the  gender  gap  in  values  is  expected  to  be most 

pronounced among the young cohort, gradually decreasing as age increases.

labor29. In the late 1970s gender differences in voting ceased to be so pronounced, as is well illustrated 

by the examples of the United States, Canada and the Netherlands30. In particular in the US, this time is 

associated  with  men  moving  to  the Republican Party at a faster rate than women31. Thus, the second 

phase can be outlined, which is called a female party dealignment.  A third phase, observed in most of

were more likely to vote for left-wing parties than male voters32. As for the US, women subsequently 

moved  to  the  Democratic  Party  more  actively  than  men33.  The  revealed  tendencies  were  identified 

across all regions of the United States, despite some variations between the North and the South34. At 

the  same  time,  it  became  clear  that  the  electoral  gender  gap  is  not  the result of specific elections or 

issues, but a reaction to more general societal conditions35. The increased involvement of women in the 

paid workforce was one of them. This has led women to become more economically independent and 

gaining greater autonomy. Thus, traditional gender roles were being eroded and the division of labour 

was changing. In addition, women were at greater risk of working in underpaid sectors, making them 

more  economically  vulnerable.  As  a  result,  women  more  often  supported  left-wing  parties  that 

advocated generous social support36.

the findings of Inglehart and Norris by suggesting that the modern gender gap is indeed a tendency in

traditional  gender  gap  tends  to  decrease  in  these  countries40.  Abendschon  and  Steinmetz enlarge the 

sample  to  the  25  EU  member  states,  including  those  that  were  formerly  communist.  This  study

generally supporting the developmental theory.   For 2008, the modern gender gap spread to countries 

where it had not been observed before, namely Italy, Spain, Luxembourg, and Finland. However, with 

regard to post-Soviet states, the modern gender gap was found only in Bulgaria and Estonia, while in

for  this  is  that  leftist  parties  may  seem  less  appealing  to  women  because  they  may  differ  from  the 

traditional  left-wing  parties  found in established democracies. Also, when studying the electoral gap,

Norrander  and  Wilcox  conducted  one  of  the  seminal  studies  analyzing  the  gender  gap  through

gender gap. The authors attribute the emergence of this phenomenon both to the declining class basis

well-educated and single women throughout the whole period positioned themselves as more liberal50.

1.2. Explaining the gender gap in ideology 

To  summarize,  scholars  provide  both  individual-level  and  country-level  explanations  of  the  modern 

gender  gap  in  ideology.  There  are  also  more complex theories. Individual-level explanations suggest 

that  such  factors  as  age,  education,  race,  ethnicity,  religion,  are  important  as  they  “intersect  with 

gender  in  complex  ways”63.  Also,  a  feminist  orientation  might play an important role. Country-level 

explanations  mostly  focus  on  the  level  of  women’s  participation in the labor force and education, as 

well as on the level of secularization and gender culture64.  

Inglehart and Norris aggregate the explanations introduced earlier, evolving a developmental theory. It 

is  assumed  that  the  process of societal modernization in post-industrial states influenced the political 

preferences of both genders, which led to the ideological divide. Societal modernization includes both 

structural  and  cultural  changes.  Among  important  structural  changes,  the  enhanced  educational 

possibilities for women and the increased female labour participation should be highlighted65. Besides, 

the  secularization  processes  played  an  important  role,  preparing  the  ground  for  a  shift  in  voting 

behaviour.  Regarding  cultural  factors, Inglehart and Norris suggest that the spread of post-materialist 

values,  such  as  values  of  freedom,  self-expression  and  gender  equality,  can  be  associated  with  the 

development  of the modern gender gap. Other scholars may not fully agree on this point and suggest 

that  the  gender  gap  is  associated  with  the  growth  of  “feminist  consciousness”66  rather  than  with 

post-materialist values as a whole. However, there is evidence that women are more likely to support a 

broad  set  of  left-wing  policies,  not  just  those  that  address  explicitly  gendered  issues67.  Also,  the 

developmental  theory  suggests  that  the  gender  gap  varies  across  nations.  It  is  expected  that  in 

traditional  societies,  the  traditional  gender  gap  will  persist,  with  women  being  more  conservative 

politically,  while  the  modern  gender  gap  will  be  more  relevant  in  post-industrial  societies  with 

post-materialist  values.  At  the  same  time,  the  spread  of  post-materialist  values  is  expected  to  vary

There is also an assumption that ideological affiliation is associated with personal traits, a set of which 

is  peculiar  to  each  gender69. Some scholars believe that gender traits have an evolutionary biological 

explanation70.  According  to  this  view,  women  are  more  sensitive  and  empathic  because  of  the 

evolutionary  burden  of  having  and  caring  for  children,  whereas  men  are  more  competitive  and 

risk-taking  because  of  the  need  to  “compete  with  others  to  attract  fertile  women”71.  Other  scholars 

prefer to look at gender personal traits through the lens of social roles. It is suggested that “in early life 

experiences  women  are  encouraged  not  to  compete  or  be  aggressive,  but  to  be  more  social  and 

caring”72.  Women  are  usually  expected to maintain the daily household routine and care for not only 

children  but  also  for  the  sick  and  elderly.  Men  vice  versa  have  more  opportunity  to  “pursue 

individualistic  goals  outside  the  household”73.  These  two  theoretical  approaches  have  contrasting 

views  on  how  differences  in  personality  traits  may  manifest  themselves  at  the  national  level.  The 

evolutionary approach suggests that differences in personality traits should be greater in industrialized 

and  more  prosperous  countries  because  “these societies are psychologically closer to hunter-gatherer 

cultures  than  are  less-developed  agricultural  or  pastoral  cultures”74.  The  social  role  approach argues 

the  opposite,  suggesting  that  as  the  state  becomes  more  prosperous  and  egalitarian,  gender-based 

differences  in  personality  traits  should  disappear  because  men  and  women  have  similar  roles75. 

Apparently, this logic is not supported by the real evidence, since the trends on gender gap in personal 

traits  are  generally  coincident  with  the  modern  ideological gender gap76.   However, the evolutionary 

approach  does  not  seem  very  convincing.  The  emphasis  on  genetic  components of personality traits 

that influence political views could be seen as overly deterministic, potentially downplaying the role of 

individual agency and environmental influences.

theory77. It suggests that adherence to the ideologies by men and women depends on different levels of 

social dominance orientation. It is expected that men more often support militarism, coercion, racism

2.1. Developmental theory applied to the Russian context 

This  part  is  devoted  to  the  theoretical  framework.  In  this  paper,  I  draw  primarily  on  developmental 

theory  by  Inglehart  and  Norris115,  since  it  provides  the  most  comprehensive  approach  to explain the 

modern  gender  gap.  The  theory  aims to explain women’s more progressive voting through structural 

factors  and  a  shift  towards  post-materialist  values.  Considering  the  low  level  of  competitiveness  in 

Russian  politics,  it  makes  little  sense  to  study  the  gender  gap  through  voting.  Thus,  this  study 

examines the gender gap directly through values. Consequently, the importance of structural factors is 

taken  from  the  above theory, as well as the framework of post-materialist values, which is applied to 

the values under study. 

In terms of structural factors, there are rather favorable conditions for the gender gap in ideology at the 

national  level.  First,  Russian  society  is  not  characterized  by  high  religiosity.  Despite  the  important 

symbolic  role  of  Orthodoxy  in  state  narratives,  genuine  religious  activity  in  Russia  remains  low116. 

There is also evidence that younger generations of Russians show lower levels of religiosity, including 

women117. Despite the gender gap in religiosity among older cohorts, where women are more religious, 

this  seems  no  longer  relevant  for  the  younger  generation.  Thus,  it  can  be  stated  that  most  young 

women  in  Russia  are  not  guided  by  religious  mindset.  Second,  according  to  official  state  statistics, 

women’s  employment  in  wage  labor  is  quite  high  in  Russia.  For  2018,  in  the  20-24 age cohort it is 

43% of female employment vs. 53% of male employment, while in the 25-29 age cohort it is 78% of 

female employment vs. 91% of male employment118. Sufficiently high rates of female participation in 

labor  may  indicate  the  possibility  of  many  Russian  women’s  financial  independence.  Also,  the 

majority  of  the  population  with  higher  education  in  Russia  are  women.  According  to  the  2020 

All-Russian  Census,  59%  of  women  have  higher  education,  compared  to  41%  of  men119.  In  2018, 

about  3.1  million female students pursued tertiary education in Russia, while nearly 2.7 million were 

male120. At the same time, there is still a marked tendency for young women to choose fields of study

This  part  is  devoted  to  explaining  the  concept  of  post-materialist  or  emancipatory  values  and  the 

approach  used  to  measure  them.  The  formation  of  post-materialist  values  in  individuals  occurs 

primarily  in  the  process  of  growing  up  and  socialization  in  conditions  of  material  and  existential 

security134.  Individuals  who  feel  secure  regarding  material  conditions,  have  an  opportunity  to  shift 

attention from scarcity-oriented concerns to personal fulfillment and social justice135.   

Inglehart  suggests  measuring  values  on  the  following  scales:  1)  Traditional  values  versus 

Secular-rational  values  and  2)  Survival  values  versus  Self-expression  values136.  Traditional  values 

emphasize  “the importance of religion, parent-child ties, deference to authority and traditional family 

values”137.  Societies  where  traditional  values  prevail,  are  characterized  by  conformity  over 

individualistic striving, honoring authority figures, and high levels of national pride and a nationalistic 

worldview.  Secular-rational  values  are  opposed  to  traditional  values  respectively.  Survival  values 

prioritize economic and physical security, which are associated with “a relatively ethnocentric outlook 

and  low  levels  of  trust  and  tolerance”138.  Self-expression  values,  on  the  contrary,  emphasize 

“environmental protection, growing tolerance of foreigners, gays and lesbians and gender equality”, as 

well  as  “rising  demands  for  participation  in  decision-making  in  economic  and  political  life”139.  The 

Survival  values  versus  Self-expression values scale is important for this work because it substantially 

mirrors  the  materialist  versus  post-materialist  values  dichotomy.  Moreover,  this  scale  most  closely 

resembles  ideological  attitudes.  This  is  supported  by  evidence  that  post-materialist  values  are 

associated  with more left-leaning voting140. Thus, to study the gender gap in ideology self-expression 

values are taken as a basis. 

Welzel  develops  the  concept  of  post-materialist  or  self-expression  values  by  proposing  a  model  of 

emancipatory  values  that  is  claimed  to  be  better  grounded theoretically and has higher measurement 

validity141.  This  model  includes  four  components: autonomy, equality, choice and voice142. Autonomy 

reflects prioritizing more independence and imagination and less importance of obedience in children.

Equality  reflects  gender  equality  with  emphasis  on  education  being  equally  important  for  boys  and 

girls,  women  being  as  equal  as men in political leadership, and women having the same rights when 

there  is  a  shortage  of  jobs.  Choice  is  measured  by  “how  strongly  people  value  freedom  in  their 

reproductive  choices”  based  on  the  level  of  acceptance  of  divorce,  abortion,  and  homosexuality143. 

Voice reflects to what extent individuals value “the voice of the people as a source of influence in their 

society”144. Respondents are asked about the importance of protecting freedom of speech, the ability to 

influence government decisions, and the state of affairs at their workplaces and in their communities145. 

The  component  of  autonomy,  given  its  operationalization,  does  not  fit  well  in  the  context  of 

ideology-based  values.  Voice,  on  the  other  hand,  reflects  important  political  values.  However,  this 

component is not included due to the large number of missing values in data on the required questions. 

Therefore, this study considers equality of opportunities and freedom of choice when investigating the 

gender gap in ideology. 

At  the  same  time,  post-materialist  values  imply  tolerance  of  outgroups,  including  foreigners  and 

ethnically  different people146. Welzel’s model does not provide a component that captures this aspect. 

Therefore,  I  introduce  this component independently: tolerance towards diversity. It reflects attitudes 

toward migrant workers, people who speak a different language, and people of a different ethnicity. In 

addition, trust in state institutions is considered in this paper.  On the one hand, this is a crucial aspect 

when  studying  political  values  in  Russia  given  the authoritarian context. On the other hand, in some 

sense,  it  substitutes  Welzel’s  voice  component.  Thus,  the  development  of  factors  and  indicators  is 

guided  by  both  the  theoretical  framework  and  data  availability.  Below  is  a  full  list  of  factors  and 

indicators included in the analysis.

2.3. Data and methods  

This  paper  uses  data  from  the  R.  Inglehart  Laboratory  for Comparative Social Research collected in 

2019-2020  as  part  of  the  project  “Value  Transformation  and  Subjective  Quality  of Life: A Regional 

Perspective”148.  This  survey  is  based  on  the  revised  modernization  theory149  and  the  evolutionary 

theory  of  emancipation150.  Accordingly,  the  survey  format is based on the World Values Survey. The 

research covers 60 regions of Russia. The sample size is 18,768 respondents, with 500 respondents in 

Moscow and St. Petersburg and 300 in the remaining regions. Respondents living in the region as their 

primary residence for 3 months prior to the survey and aged 18 years or older were interviewed using 

telephone  survey  method.  The  data  obtained  as  a  result  of  the  survey  are  representative  of  the  60 

regions of Russia, as they closely reflect the structure of the population of each region according to the 

main socio-demographic and geographical parameters151.

As  a  preliminary  step,  exploratory data analysis is conducted. Mann-Whitney tests are conducted for 

groups  of  young men and women aged 18-24 in order to check whether the samples, i.e. the average 

responses  of 

these  cohorts  on  certain  questions  reflecting  values,  differ  from  each  other. 

Mann-Whitney  test  is  used  as  the  data  are  non-parametric  according  to  the  Shapiro-Wilk  normality 

test.  Further,  confirmatory  factor  analysis  is  conducted  to test the validity of the proposed model for 

measuring  the  ideological  gender  gap.  The  CFA  model  was  run  in  the  R  program  using  the  lavaan 

package.  The  MLR  estimate  was  used  to  construct  the  model.  Following  this,  factor  scores  were

choice,  Tolerance  towards  diversity  and  Trust  in  state  institutions  as  dependent  variables.  All 

regression  models  were  tested  on  multicollinearity,  heteroscedasticity,  presence  of  outliers  and 

influential  observations  (Appendix  8-29).  All  necessary  transformations  have  been  performed  in the 

case  of  problems  identified.  Also,  Freedom  of  choice,  Tolerance towards diversity and Trust in state 

institutions dependent variables were normalized using the powerTransform function (Appendix 1-7).  

 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
3. Results 

30 

To begin with, exploratory data analysis was conducted by employing Mann-Whitney tests to uncover 

significant differences between groups of young men and women aged 18-24. The results are presented 

in Table 1. The analysis revealed that young men and women differed significantly in their responses 

to  at  least  one  question  in  each  section:  Equality  of  opportunities,  Freedom  of  choice,  Tolerance 

towards  diversity  and  Trust  in  state  institutions.  The  lower  the  p-value,  the  more  significant  the 

difference  between  groups  is  considered  to  be.  At  the  same  time,  the  negative  direction  of  the  R 

indicator  (rank  biserial)  indicates  that  women  have  higher  ranks  on  the  issue,  while  the  positive 

direction demonstrates higher ranks of men. 

In  the Equality of opportunities section, the most significant differences (p-value < 0.01) are evident 

in agreement with the following statements: “Men are becoming better political leaders than women”, 

“Higher  education  is  more  important  for  boys”,  “Women  are  equally  capable  as  men  in  technical 

matters”,  “When  workplaces  are  scarce,  men  should  have  more right to work”. Women, on average, 

agreed  less  with  these statements than men. At the same time, no significant difference was found in 

agreement  with  the  statement  “Housekeeping  is  equally  the  responsibility  of  both  spouses”.  In  the 

section  on  Tolerance  towards  diversity,  the  significance  of  the  differences  was  less  pronounced 

(p-value  <  0.05).  Men,  on  average,  demonstrate  more  positive  attitudes  towards  people  of  other 

nationalities  and  speaking  another  language.  At  the same time, differences in attitudes towards labor 

migrants  from  other  countries  and  trust  in  people  of  other  nationalities  were  not  found  to  be 

significant.  In  the  Freedom  of  choice  section,  differences  in  justification  of  homosexuality  were 

significant at p-value <0.01, demonstrating that women justified homosexuality more often than men. 

At lower significance (p-value <0.05), differences in justification of divorce were also found. Women 

more often justify this action. At the same time, no differences were found in justifying abortion. In the 

Trust in state institutions section, the difference in trust in political parties was revealed. At p-value < 

0.05 it was found that women slightly more often trust political parties. At the same time, trust in the 

President of Russia and trust in elections revealed no differences. 

To  summarize,  men  and  women  aged  18-24  years  old  demonstrate  significant  differences  in  certain 

issues.  Women  are  more  likely  to  reject  gender  biases,  such  as  male  leadership  in politics or higher 

importance  of  tertiary  education  for  boys.  Also,  women  demonstrate  higher  acceptance  of 

homosexuality  and  divorce.  Meanwhile,  men  show  higher  trust  in  people  of  other  nationalities  and 

those who speak a different language. Trust in institutions varies weakly, with the exception of political 

parties with women demonstrating trust more often.

3.2. Regression analysis 

This  part  presents  the  results  of  regression  analysis  (see  Table  3),  where  the  obtained  factor  scores 

were used as dependent variables. Four regression models were built with an interaction effect between 

gender and age. The 18-24 age cohort is used as a reference category. Thus, the estimates for each age 

cohort  show  a  difference  compared  to the reference group. Also, the variable Gender:female reflects 

the  difference  between  women  and  men  only  in  the  reference  group  (18-24  years)  because  it  is 

included in the interaction. 

The gender effect on Equality of opportunities, Freedom of choice and Tolerance towards diversity in 

the  young  cohort  aged  18-24  is  observed.  Young  women  have  on  average  0.122  units  higher  in 

Equality of opportunities and 0.094 units higher in Freedom of choice than men under other conditions 

being  equal,  and  these  differences  are  statistically  significant at the p < 0.01 level. Also,   women on 

average 0.112 units higher Tolerance towards diversity   than men under other conditions being equal, 

and  these  differences  are  statistically  significant  at  the  p  < 0.05 level. However, no gender effect on 

Trust in state institutions is found. Thus, the hypothesis that young women in Russia tend to have more 

post-materialist  values  than  men  of  the  same  age,  more  often  prioritizing  Equality  of  opportunities, 

Freedom  of choice and Tolerance towards diversity (H2) is confirmed. Meanwhile, young women in 

Russia were not found to have less Trust in state institutions than men of the same age (H4). 

The  generational  effect  on  Equality of opportunities and Trust in state institutions is well observed. 

Adherence  to  Equality  of  opportunities  is  lower  in  55-64  and  65+  age  cohorts  with  statistical 

significance  at  the  p  <  0.1  and  p  <  0.05  levels  correspondingly.  Also,  Trust  in  state  institutions 

gradually increases with increasing age, while the average Trust in state institutions among the young 

cohort 18-24 is lower compared to all cohorts. It can also be observed that the adherence to Freedom of 

choice  is  lower  in  all  age  cohorts  compared  to  the  aged  18-24  group.  At  the  same  time,  a  linear 

 
34 

relationship of this component with age cannot be seen. The greatest estimates are in the cohort 25-34 

(-0.119)  and  the  cohort  65+  (-0.155).  That  is,  there  is  a  rather  U-shaped  relationship  of Freedom of 

choice with age. However, the cohort of young people aged 18-24 still is the most progressive in terms 

of Freedom of choice in comparison with all other age groups. In contrast, Tolerance towards diversity 

is unexpectedly higher among older cohorts. Cohorts aged 55-64 and 65+ have on average 0.159 and 

0.048  units  higher  in  Tolerance  towards  diversity  under  other  conditions  being  equal,  and  these 

differences are statistically significant at the p < 0.01 level. Thus, the hypothesis that young people in 

Russia  tend to have more post-materialist values than older generations (H1) is partially confirmed. 

Young  people  are  likely  to  more  often  prioritize  Equality  of  opportunities  (H1.1)  and  Freedom  of 

choice (H1.2). At the same time, contrary to expected results, young people are not likely to be more 

tolerant  towards  diversity  than  older  generations  (H1.3).  Also,  the  hypothesis  that  young  people  in 

Russia tend to have less Trust in state institutions than older generations is confirmed (H3). 

The  intensity  of  the  gender  gap  is  found  to  vary  across  age  cohorts.  In  terms  of  Equality  of 

opportunities, the gender gap is most pronounced for the young cohort aged 18-24. At the same time, 

as age increases, the gender gap gradually diminishes. Also, non-linear dynamics of the gender gap in 

Freedom of choice is noticeable. Although people aged 18-24 are more likely to adhere to Freedom of 

choice  values  in  comparison  with  all  other  age  groups,  the  gender  gap  is  most  pronounced  at  ages 

25-54, decreasing by 55-65+ years, showing no statistically significant difference in the intensity of the 

gender  gap  from  the  reference  group.  As for Tolerance towards diversity, the gender gap completely 

disappears  by  the  age  of  25-34  years,  and  further  the  difference  with  the  reference  group  is  not 

statistically  significant.  In  Trust  in  state  institutions,  the  gender  gap  among  young  people  is  not 

observed, and the same trend remains for all other cohorts. Thus, the hypothesis that the gender gap in 

values  is  most  pronounced  for  the  18-24  age  cohort  and  decreases  with  increase  in  age  is partially 

confirmed (H5). This dynamic is evidenced in Equality of opportunities (H5.1). However, the gender 

gap  in  Freedom  of  choice  (H5.2)  and  Tolerance  towards  diversity  (H5.3)  do  not follow this pattern. 

Meanwhile, the gender gap in Trust in state institutions (H5.4) is not observed across all age cohorts.

Discussion and conclusions  

37 

The so-called ideological gender gap, which originally appeared in Western democracies, has recently 

been  discovered  in  other  countries  as  well.  Therefore,  the  aim  of  this  study was to test whether this 

trend has reached Russia. It is assumed that the younger generation holds more progressive views, but 

the shift towards these values is more rapid among women, thus forming a gender gap in ideology153. 

Accordingly,  in  order  to  assess  whether  this  trend  is  relevant  for  Russia,  the  following  points  were 

tested: the generational effect, the gender effect within the young cohort and the intensity of the gender 

gap among different age cohorts.  

The  study  presents  curious  findings.  First,  exploratory  data  analysis  revealed  significant  differences 

between groups of young men and women aged 18-24 in answers towards at least one question in each 

section:  Equality  of  opportunities,  Freedom  of  choice,  Tolerance towards diversity and Trust in state 

institutions.  Second,  the  results  of  regression  analysis  partially  or  fully  confirmed  the  proposed 

hypotheses. A significant gender effect on Equality of opportunities, Freedom of choice and Tolerance 

towards diversity in the young cohort aged 18-24 was found. Young women in Russia are indeed more 

likely  to  hold  views  that  support  gender  equality  and  justify  divorce, abortion, homosexuality. Also, 

young  women  are  more likely to be tolerant of outgroups such as migrant workers compared to their 

male  counterparts.  At  the  same  time,  no  gender  gap  in  trust  in  state  institutions  was  found.  A 

significant generational effect is also observed. Young people more often give preference to Equality 

of  opportunities  and  Freedom  of  choice  compared  to  the  older  cohort.  Also,  they  tend  to  have  less 

Trust  in  state  institutions  than  older  generations.  In  contrast,  Tolerance  towards  diversity  is 

unexpectedly higher among older generations. Thus, the expected generational effect is noticeable for 

Equality  of  opportunities,  Freedom  of  choice  and  Trust  in  state  institutions.  The  generally  accepted 

dynamics  of  the  gender  gap  in  ideology  suggests  that  the  gap is most pronounced among the young 

cohort  and  that  the  gender  gap  gradually  decreases  with  increasing  age.  As  for  the  intensity  of the 

gender gap in Russia, this trend is relevant for Equality of opportunities. The gender gap in Freedom 

of  choice  and  Tolerance  towards  diversity  does  not  conform  to  this  pattern.  At  the  same  time,  the 

gender gap in Trust in public institutions is not observed in all age cohorts. 

Thus, the gender gap in Equality of opportunities, Freedom of choice and Tolerance towards diversity 

among  people  aged  18-24  is evident in Russia. The gap in Equality of opportunities aligns well with 

the  trend  originated  from  the  West.  The  gap  gradually  decreases  for  older  cohorts  and  thus  is  most 

pronounced  among  the  young  generation. Thus, this is consistent with Inglehart and Norris’ findings

Meanwhile,  Freedom  of  choice  and  Tolerance  towards  diversity  do  not  fit  into  this  logic  or fit with 

some reservations. This is an interesting finding, since all values studied belong to post-materialist or 

emancipatory values, which are commonly assumed to be related to each other. However, this does not 

seem  to  be  the  case  in  Russia.  Two  particularly  curious  findings  challenge  the  existing  theoretical 

foundation. First, people 55-64 years old turned out to be the most tolerant towards diversity, meaning 

people  of  a  different  nationality,  people  speaking  a  different  language  and  labor  migrants.  This  is 

contrary  to  a  number  of  Western  studies  showing  that  post-materialist  values  as  a  whole  are  higher 

among  young  people156.  This  might  be  explained  by  older generations socializing in an environment 

where  Soviet  internationalism  was  promoted.  Another  explanation  could  be  that  tolerance  for 

outgroups is related to loyalty to the state, i.e. supporting migration policy. A similar explanation was 

put forward by Shcherbak, explaining the greater tolerance for migrants among conservative women157. 

Second, women's greater commitment to post-materialist values is not associated with less trust in state 

institutions,  contrary  to  theoretical  expectations.  The  results  are  inconsistent  with  the  Inglehart  and 

Welzel’s suggestion that adherence to post-materialist values is associated with a more critical attitude 

toward  authorities  in  non-democratic  context158.  It  may  be  assumed  that  Equality  of  opportunities, 

Freedom  of  choice  and  Tolerance  towards  diversity  are  perceived  as  a  “personal  matter”  and  in  a 

certain way perceived as non-politicized values. Therefore, supporting these values does not transform 

into distrust towards state institutions. These are the research puzzles that have yet to be solved. 

To conclude, this study makes an important contribution to the research field by introducing the lens of 

the gender gap in the study of values in Russia. It suggests that young men and women in Russia have 

different views on gender equality, the justification of abortion, divorce and homosexuality, as well as 

tolerance towards outgroups. At the same time, in accordance with theoretical implications the gap in 

gender  equality  attitudes  is  narrowing  with  increasing  age.  Future  research  can  address  research 

puzzles identified in this study, as well as test modernization theory for other values, including policy 

preferences.
