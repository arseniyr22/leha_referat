The  first part of  the  research  question of the study  is whether  the  use of  the 

human rights concept in tackling the climate change problem is appropriate, and what 

its limitations would be. The second part of the research question the study is why 

States enforce the judicial decisions of international human rights bodies on the issue 

of climate change.  

The  methodology  of  the  research  is  legal  formalism  (positivism),  a  policy-

oriented approach (the New Haven School) and a rational choice theory (International 

Law  &  Economics  approach).    The  research  is  mainly  based  on  treaty  law  (climate 

change  and  human  rights  treaties)  and  customary  law  (written  statements  of  States, 

resolutions of international fora). In addition to primary sources of international law, 

the jurisprudence of international and national courts is of paramount importance for 

the application of the human rights concept in climate disputes. While legal formalism 

and the New Haven School are used to assess the appropriateness of using the human 

rights concept in addressing climate change, a rational choice theory is used to identify 

the rationale of States in enforcing the judicial decisions of international human rights 

bodies.  A  variety  of  methods  are  used,  including  comparison,  systematisation, 

dogmatic analysis, case studies, and forecasting.  

The  structure  of  the  research  consists  of  an  introduction;  three  chapters, 

containing a total of 7 sections; a conclusion; and a bibliography. The first chapter aims 

to establish a human rights protecting function of the climate change treaties, as well 

as to compare the approaches of domestic and international courts in the field of climate 

change litigation, with a particular focus on the human rights dimension. The second 

chapter  aims  to  identify  potential  and  limitations  of  the  existing  international  legal

I. THE HUMAN RIGHTS DIMENSION OF CLIMATE CHANGE 

The first chapter begins by determining human rights aspects of climate change. 

The chapter aims to establish a human rights protecting function of the climate change 

treaties, as well as to compare the approaches of domestic and international courts in 

the  field  of  climate  change  litigation,  with  a  particular  focus  on  the  human  rights 

dimension. 

§ 1. Fight with climate change in international law 

The climate change problem  is a contemporary challenge for the  international 

community, impacting numerous areas of international law, ranging from international 

environmental  law  to  international  human  rights  law.  For  the  first  time,  the  United 

Nations  General  Assembly  (hereinafter  –  UN  GA)  recognised  climate  change  as  a 

common concern of mankind in its Resolution 43/53 in 1988.22 After 20 years, climate 

change has become “one of the greatest challenges of our time”, as reaffirmed in the 

UN GA Resolution 67/210 of 21 December 2012.23  

Owing to the 2018 Report of the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change 

(hereinafter – IPCC), ‘climate change consensus’ has been reached. In that report, the 

IPCC states that there is a high risk of a much worse outcome if the temperature rise 

exceeds  1.5°C  above  pre-industrial  levels.24  In  its  2023  Synthesis  Report,  the  IPCC

D. Bodansky is describing the development of the climate change regime as a 

play  in  four acts: (i) the negotiation and adoption of the United Nations Framework 

Convention on Climate Change (hereinafter – UNFCCC); (ii) the Kyoto Protocol; (iii) 

the  adoption  of  the  Copenhagen  Accord  in  2009  and  the  Cancun  Agreements  the

One of the core of climate change treaties is the UNFCCC. To date, there are 

198 parties to it, including 196 States, the European Union and the Holy See.27 Despite 

the fact that the UNFCCC established framework provisions with regard to the climate 

change problem, it is binding in nature.28 Thus, the UNFCCC established the objective 

and guiding principles of the UN climate change regime29 as well as the regime’s core 

institutions, for example, the annual Conference of the Parties (hereinafter  – COP)30 

and a system of reporting and review.31

because  it  goes  beyond  the  realms  of  international  climate  and  environmental  law, 

particularly  because  it  refers  to  human  rights,  rights  of  particular  groups,  gender 

equality and intergenerational equity.40 Moreover, the use of the word ‘should’  implies 

a ‘soft’ obligation, which is not usually found in a treaty preamble.41 Regarding travaux 

préparatoires to the Paris Agreement,  an earlier form of recital 11 was more specific, 

as it referred to States’ obligations to promote, protect and respect human rights.42 As 

D.  Bodansky  points  out,  the  Preamble  “addresses  the  human  rights  aspects  only  of 

response measures not of climate change itself.” This is a central point, which concerns 

obligations  “to  take  affirmative  measures  to  prevent  human  rights  harms  caused  by 

climate change, including foreseeable long-term harms.”43

To summarize, the issue of climate change has been addressed in the work of 

almost  all  international  judicial  bodies.  In  terms  of  the  genesis  of  international 

jurisprudence on climate change, three main stages can be distinguished: i) a nascent 

stage, in which certain attempts were made to challenge the actions of the States in the 

field of climate change  (e.g., the Inuit petition of 2005 and the  Athabaskan Council 

petition  of  2013  before  the  IACHR,  Armando  Ferrão  Carvalho  and  Others  v. 

European  Parliament  and  Council  before  the  ECJ);  ii)  a  contentious  stage,  which 

concerns  the  climate  change  disputes  between  individuals  and  governments  (e.g., 

Teitiota v. New Zealand and Billy and others v. Australia before the HR Committee, 

KlimaSeniorinnen  v.  Switzerland  before  the  ECtHR);  iii)  a  phase  of  advisory 

proceedings dedicated to climate change (e.g., the ITLOS advisory opinion, as well as 

forthcoming advisory opinions of the ICJ and the IACtHR). Thus, the climate agenda 

first appeared in international judicial and quasi-judicial human rights bodies, and then 

moved to other international tribunals, such as the ICJ and the ITLOS. 

2.2  Protecting  human  rights  from  climate  change  at  the  national  level:  a  review  of 
national jurisprudence  

Before  moving  to  the  international  level,  the  climate  agenda  was  being 

considered by domestic courts. The most famous case at the national level is Urgenda 

Foundation v. The State of the Netherlands, in which The Hague District Court ruled 

that the State must limit the joint volume of Dutch annual greenhouse gas emissions, 

or have them limited, so that by the end of 2020 this volume will be reduced by at least 

25%  compared  to  the  level  of  the  year  1990.96  M.  Fermeglia  and  R.  Luporini  have

II. THE ROLE OF INTERNATIONAL HUMAN RIGHTS LAW IN 

ADDRESSING CLIMATE CHANGE 

The second chapter is devoted to the analysis of the role of international human 

rights  obligations  of  States  in  the  context  of  climate  change.  The  chapter  aims  to 

identify potential and limitations of the existing international legal norms in the sphere 

of  international  human  rights  law  in  addressing  the  climate  change  problem.  The 

chapter also examines the phenomenon of “climate” refugees and the right to a clean, 

healthy and sustainable environment through the lens of the existing international law.  

§  1.  International  human  rights  obligations  of  States  in  the  context  of  climate 

change  

The  sources  of  international  human  rights  law  are  divided  into  universal  and 

regional instruments. On 10 December of 1948, the Universal Declaration of Human 

Rights (hereinafter – UDHR) was adopted, thus enshrining human rights for the first

emphasised that the affirmations of the UNDR “can bind States on the basis of custom 

within  the  meaning  of  paragraph  1  (b)  of  the  same  Article,  whether  because  they 

constituted a codification of customary law… or because they have acquired the force 

of custom through a general practice accepted as law”.131 As an example, he referred 

to the right to equality.132 There are a lot of States which have not signed and/or ratified

obstacle is an evolutionary interoperation.  

There are two types of treaty interpretation, such as static and evolutionary. The 

former ascribes to the  legal text the normative  meaning that it  had at the time of  its

evolutionary  method  of  interpretation  is  widely  employed  in  jurisprudence  of 

international courts. It was first applied in the Tyrer v. the United Kingdom case, where 

the  ECtHR  held  that  “the  Convention  is  a  living  instrument  which…  must  be 

interpreted  in  the  light  of  present-day  conditions”.152  Then,  in  the  case  of  Christine

§ 2. “Greening” international human rights law: the rights of “climate refugees”

2.1 The international legal protection of “climate refugees” 

In its Synthesis Report, the IPCC highlights that “climate and weather extremes 

are  increasingly  driving  displacement  in  Africa,  Asia,  North  America  (high 

confidence), and Central and South America (medium confidence), with small island 

states in the Caribbean and South Pacific being disproportionately affected relative to 

their small population size (high confidence)”.171 Sea-level rise exacerbates not only 

the  loss  of  habitable  land,  but  also  flooding,  crop  destruction,  and  saltwater 

encroachment, all of which contribute to displacement.172 International Organization 

for Migration (IOM) defines “climate migration” as “the transfer of a person or groups 

of persons who,  owing  mainly to sudden  or progressive changes in the environment 

due to the effects of climate change, are obliged or decide to leave their habitual place 

of  residence  whether  temporarily  or  permanently,  within  a  State  or  crossing  an 

international border”.173 So “climate migration” is a type of forced migration.

2.2 The legal status of the right to a clean, healthy and sustainable environment 

On 18 October 2021, the UN HRC, for the first time, recognised the right to a 

clean, healthy and sustainable environment as “human right that is important for the 

enjoyment of human rights”.193 On 28 July 2022, the UN GA recognised the right to a 

clean, healthy and sustainable environment as a human right by 161 votes  for,  none 

against and 8 abstentions.194 In its recent resolution 52/23 of 4 April 2023, the UN HRC 

called upon States Calls upon to respect, protect and fulfil human rights, including in 

all actions undertaken to address environmental challenges.195 

The right to a healthy environment has been recognised in a number of regional 

human rights instruments. Article 24 of the African Charter on Human and Peoples’ 

Rights (hereinafter – AСHPR or African Charter) explicitly provides for the right of 

peoples to a general satisfactory environment  favorable to their development.196  For 

example, in in SERAC v. Nigeria, the ACHPR clarified that this right “requires the state 

to take reasonable and other measures to prevent pollution and ecological degradation, 

to promote conservation, and to secure an ecologically sustainable development and 

use of natural resources”.197

§  3.  The  relationship  between  States’  climate  obligations  and  human  rights 

obligations  

In general, there are two approaches in addressing the climate change problem: 

mitigation  and  adaptation  measures.  The  purpose  of  climate  change  mitigation  is  to 

enact measures to limit the extent of climate change, meaning reduce GHG emissions. 

While    adaptation  measures  refer  to  preparation  for  the  adverse  effects  of  climate 

change  (e.g.,  constructing  sea-walls).227  The  climate  change  treaties  contain  States’ 

obligations to mitigate and adapt to climate change.228 

As regards a treaty interpretation, a general rule is contained in Article 31 of the 

Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties (hereinafter — VCLT), which establishes 

a cumulative three-tier test (wording, context and object and purpose) accompanied by

climate change, however, using the evolutionary method of treaty interpretation, they 

can encompass protection from the adverse impacts of climate change; secondly, based 

on regional multilateral treaties and instruments and resolutions of international fora, 

there is a deductive international custom — the right to a clean, healthy and sustainable 

environment is a deductive international custom (lex lata); thirdly, from the perspective

there are two approaches to the relationship between States’ human rights and climate 

obligations: a one-dimensional approach and a human rights-integrated approach.

III. APPROPRIATNESS OF THE APPLICATION OF HUMAN RIGHTS TO 

CLIMATE CHANGE AND ENFORCEMENT OF THIS MODEL 

The third chapter aims to assess the appropriateness of the using human rights 

concept in addressing the issue of climate change through two tools of the research, 

namely  legal  formalism (positivism) and a policy-oriented approach to  international 

law (the New Haven School). The rationale for States to enforce the judicial decisions 

of international human rights bodies is analysed from the perspective a rational choice 

theory (International Law & Economics approach).  

§  1.  Appropriateness  of  using  the  human  rights  concept  in  addressing  climate

§ 2. States’ rationale to enforce judicial decisions of international human rights 

bodies  

The  purpose  of  this  section  is  to  identify  the  reasons  why  States  enforce  the

CONCLUSION 

Originally,  the  relationship  between  human  rights  and  climate  change  was 

recognised by the UN GA Resolution 7/23 of 28 March 2008. The  Paris Agreement 

then became ‘a human rights treaty’, with a reminder of States’ respective human rights 

obligations  in  its  non-binding  preamble.  As  a  result,  human  rights-based  arguments 

began to be applied in climate change litigation The relevant provisions of international 

human rights treaties have been interpreted in an evolutionary and systematic manner 

by  domestic  and  international  courts.  Based  on  regional  multilateral  treaties  and 

instruments  and  resolutions  of  international  fora,  there  is  a  deductive  international 

custom  —  the  right  to  a  clean,  healthy  and  sustainable  environment  is  a  deductive 

international  custom  (lex  lata).  From  the  perspective  of  existing  international  legal 

rules, the use of the term “climate migrants” instead of “climate refugees” seems to be 

the most attractive approach (lex ferenda). There are two approaches to the relationship 

between States’ human rights and climate obligations: a one-dimensional approach and 

a human rights-integrated approach.  

Applying a  human-rights-based approach,  the application  of the  human rights 

concept  in  addressing  climate  change  is  appropriate.  In  this  respect,  international 

climate change law and customary international human rights law (e.g., the right to life 

and the right to a clean, healthy and sustainable environment) should be interpreted in 

a harmonious manner, so that the latter would fill the legal gaps in international climate 

change  law.  The  application  of  international  human  rights  law  is  limited  by  certain 

limitations, including  ratione materiae, ratione personae and ratione loci. From the 

standpoint of a policy-oriented approach (the New Haven School), combating climate 

change is a complex social process, involving decision-makers (States) and influencers 

(NGOs or eco-activists). In this regard, it would be appropriate to apply the concept of 

human rights in addressing the problem of climate change, since, firstly, international 

climate change  law and  international  human rights  law share a single common  goal 

(value) — well-being; secondly, non-state actors (NGOs and eco-activists) influence 

existing international law by applying to international human rights bodies. 

 
 
 
61 

As regards the second part of the research question,  it is worth noting that the 

rationale for States to enforce judicial decisions of international human rights bodies is 

analysed from the perspective a rational choice theory (International law & economics 

approach).  As  a  result  of  the  analysis  of  two  landmark  cases  (Billy  and  others  v. 

Australia  and  KlimaSeniorinnen  v.  Switzerland),  it  appears  that  the  rationale  for 

Australia  and  Switzerland  to  enforce  the  judicial  decisions  of  international  human 

rights  bodies  is  rooted  in  their  willingness  to  maintain  a  good  reputation  in  the 

international community. By enforcing the judicial decisions, States have shown that 

they  are  willing  to  refine  their  mitigation  and  adaptation  policies  in  order  to  not  so 

much to fulfill their international obligations, but rather to restore their reputation and 

demonstrate  other  States  that  they  are  reliable  partners  for  further  cooperation  in 

combating climate change.
