ABSTRACT AND KEYWORDS 

This thesis addresses the challenge of uneven flow of incoming / outgoing goods in 

warehouse logistics by developing a ML-based system to predict goods flow volumes. Accurate 

forecasting of goods receipt and shipped enables optimized resource allocation (staff, equipment, 

space), reducing costs from overstaffing and mitigating operational failures caused by 

undercapacity. 

Using historical data from a medium-to-large non-food retail warehouse, sourced via 

Warehouse Management System (WMS), the study implements and compares: 

1.  Statistical models (ARIMA, SARIMA, SARIMAX), 

2.  ML models (Lasso, Random Forest, Gradient Boosting, CatBoost), 

3.  Deep learning architectures (LSTM, GRU, Seq2Seq with attention), and 

4.  Hybrid approaches (e.g., SARIMA + LSTM). 

Forecasts are generated for two horizons: hourly (shift scheduling) and daily (logistic 

optimization). 

Key results: 

  CatBoost achieved the best performance for daily predictions (RMSE: 0.096–0.109). 

  Seq2Seq with attention excelled in hourly shipment forecasts (RMSE: 0.170). 

  Hybrid models improved medium-term shipment accuracy by capturing seasonality and 

nonlinear trends. 

The solution is deployed as a REST API via FastAPI, integrating forecasts into 

warehouse management workflows. Economic analysis estimates potential savings of 5.4 million 

rubles/year through labor optimization alone. 

Keywords: Warehouse logistics, predictive analytics, time series forecasting, machine 

learning, CatBoost, LSTM, resource optimization, operational efficiency.

INTRODUCTION 

Relevance of the work. One of the foundations of modern civilization is the logistics of 

goods. In turn, it is impossible without efficiently operating warehouses.  

The efficient operation of warehouses depends to a large extent on the ability to cope with 

uneven loads. The key processes shaping warehouse workload are receiving incoming goods and 

shipping outgoing goods. Therefor resources allocated for managing warehouse operations have 

to be as “just right” as possible, with flexible adaptation to increase and decrease of workload. 

Overestimating  of  workload  leads  to  inefficient  resources  booking  (staff,  warehouse 

equipment  such  as  forklift  trucks,  space  of  warehouse  racking  etc)  which  obviously  leads  to 

unnecessary costs and decrease of efficiency 

In turn, underestimating of workload leads to operations failures (such as failure to unload 

incoming transport or load outgoing transport, and losses may be high or even extreme (in case 

failure to load or unload leads to spoilage of goods or sales failures). 

Uncertainty may lead to both of the mentioned negative business consequences. 

Purpose  of  the  current  work  is  to  provide  a  first  version  of  a  complex  ML-based  tool 

allowing Warehouses to predict incoming and outgoing goods flow. Such prediction should be 

used by warehouse operational management for: 

-  Avoid  operations  failures  due  to  resource  shortage  during  load  peaks  not  predicted 

timely and 

-  Reduce costs for booking resources not required in the periods of low workloads. 

Business  justifications  of  the  objectives.  In  order  to  convert  predictions  to  cost  saving 

obviously management actions are required, and for different prediction horizons different actions 

are appropriate, i.e.: 

-  On very short-term period (shift) shift leader may concentrate on relocating shift staff 

either on current workload or on long-term supplementary tasks such as stocktaking, 

picking area compaction, maintenance etc.  

-  On short term period (next few shifts) shift leader and warehouse manager is able to 

increase  or  decrease  the  number  of  temporary  staff,  which  is  common  practice  in 

warehousing business. 

-  On  medium  term  period  (next  few  weeks  and  more)  warehouse  manager  is  able  to 

reduce or increase number of total staff and rented equipment available. 

Objectives of the work are: 

- 

to  explore  various  ways  for  short-  and  medium-term  predictions  of  incoming  and 

outgoing goods flows on a warehouse based on historical data and

1.1 Subject Area 

The  object  of  forecasting  in  this  paper  is  the  operational  load  of  the  warehouse, 

quantitatively  expressed  through  the  volumes  of  the  two  main  material  flows:  receiving  and 

shipping.  Receiving  includes  unloading  transportation,  inspection  and  registration  of  incoming 

goods,  their  preparation  and  placement  in  storage.  Shipment  covers  order  picking,  packaging, 

preparation of documents and loading into vehicles. The intensity of these two processes can be 

measured  in  various  metrics.  However most  common  metrics  that  can  be  easily  obtained  from 

WMS are: 

1)  Lines. A line is generally accepted logical unit of processing in WMS. It corresponds 

to 1 line of a document (e.g. delivery note or shipment note), in which all pieces of 

goods  of  one  article  of  one  batch  (or  the  same  goods  without  batch  accounting) 

belonging  to  this  document  are  recorded.  An  incoming  and  outgoing  document  is  a 

group of incoming / outgoing goods combined in one delivery / shipment. Warehouse 

receiving/shipment processes are carried out on these documents respectively. 

2)  Goods weight 

3)  Goods volume 

Why ML? Receipt and shipment volumes to-/from a Warehouse are dynamic and stochastic 

variables shaped by many interrelated factors. Time patterns play a major role: annual and monthly 

seasonality, weekly and daily cyclicity are present. They are also affected by commercial factors 

such as marketing promotions, sales, price and assortment changes. Logistical factors also play a 

role, including supplier delivery schedules and transportation availability, road traffic situations.  

Therefor various ML tools seem to be appropriate for such predictions.

1.4 Possible Approaches to Solve the Stated Purpose of the Work 

Since the load on the warehouse, namely the volumes of incoming and outgoing deliveries, 

is  primarily  distributed  in  time,  the  forecasting  of the load  on  the  warehouse is  reduced  to  the 

forecasting of time series. Below I consider the chronological development of methods for solving 

this problem. 

1.4.1 Traditional Statistical Methods and Corresponding Models: 

Moving average methods: The forecast for the next period is calculated as an average of 

several previous periods. Easy to implement, but do not adapt well to trends and seasonality. 

Exponential smoothing: An improvement on moving average, where more recent data is 

given  more  weight.  These  methods  perform  well  for  data  with  clear  seasonal  and  trend 

components. 

ARIMA/SARIMA  (Autoregressive  Integrated  Moving  Average  /  Seasonal  ARIMA) 

models: A classic and powerful tool for analyzing and forecasting time series. The SARIMA model 

explicitly accounts for seasonal variations, which is often found in warehouse load data (daily, 

weekly,  annual  cycles).  These  models  are  based  on  autocorrelation  of  the  data  and  require 

assumptions  about  the  stationarity  of  the  series  (or  making  it  stationary).  They  provide  good 

interpretability  but  can  be  difficult  to  set  up  and  do  not  always  deal  well  with  non-linear 

dependencies or the influence of external factors without explicitly including them (ARIMAX). 

These  statistical  approaches  often  serve  as  base  models  for  comparison  with  more 

sophisticated  methods.  They  have  the  advantage  of  relative  simplicity  and  interpretability. 

8 

 
 
However, they may not be flexible enough to capture complex patterns, non-linear dependencies, 

and the influence of multiple external factors. 

1.4.2 Machine Learning 

Machine learning methods have become increasingly widespread recently with the development 

of computing power and data availability. Unlike classical statistical models, they often do not 

require strict assumptions about data structure and can automatically detect complex dependencies. 

The  forecasting  task  can  be  viewed  as  a  regression  task,  where  the  target  variable  is  the  load 

volume, and the attributes are time characteristics (hour, day of the week, month), time series lags, 

and external factors. Both linear models and more complex models such as those based on decision 

trees are used [14,15]. 

Decision  Trees  and  Ensembles:  Models  like  Random  Forest  and  especially  Gradient 

Boosting Machines (GBM) such as XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost[16] show high performance 

in prediction problems on tabular data. They are able to model nonlinear dependencies, feature 

interactions  and  often  do  not  require  complex  data  preprocessing  (e.g.,  scaling).  They  can  be 

effectively applied for both one-step-ahead forecasting and for predicting a sequence of values. 

ML methods allow the integration of a large number of heterogeneous features and often 

provide  higher  accuracy  than  traditional  models,  especially  on  data  with  complex  structure. 

However, they can be less interpretable and require careful selection of hyperparameters. 

There are several approaches to time series forecasting: 

Recursive strategy. In this approach, the model only predicts the value of the next time 

step.  To  forecast  the  second  step,  the  previously  obtained  forecast  is  used  as  the  value  of  the 

previous time step in the feature space. Similar actions are performed for the next steps, constantly 

augmenting  the  feature  space  with  predictions  (see  Fig.  1).  This  strategy  allows  us  to  obtain 

forecasts for any horizon, but the disadvantage of this approach is the accumulation of error with 

increasing forecast horizon.

Figure 1. The feature space under recursive strategy. [14] 

Direct strategy. In the direct strategy, it is assumed that each forecast within the forecast 

horizon should be built by a different model. Thus, for forecasting for a horizon of length N, it is 

necessary to train N models with the same feature space but with different target variables (see 

Fig. 2). The forecasts are obtained as follows: the first model makes a forecast for the next step, 

the second model makes a forecast for the next step, the third model makes a forecast for 2 steps 

later, and so on. This approach reduces the accumulation of error, but requires more resources 

for model training. 

Figure 2. The feature space under recursive strategy. [14] 

Hybrid strategy. Hybrid strategy has a similar algorithm to the direct strategy, it also 

trains N models for forecasting for a horizon of size N with the difference that each next model 

uses the forecasts of the previous ones (see Fig. 3).

1.4.3 Deep Learning 

DL models have the potential to provide the highest accuracy, especially on large amounts 

of data with complex, nonlinear and multivariate dependencies. However, they require significant 

computational  resources  for  training  and  large  amounts  of data,  and  are  difficult  to  set  up  and 

interpret.  

Recurrent Neural Networks (RNN). Models such as LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory) 

and GRU (Gated Recurrent Unit) are specifically designed to handle sequential data. They are able 

to  capture  long-term  dependencies  in  time  series,  which  is  important  for  load  forecasting 

dependent on past events. Their advantage is their ability to train on “raw” time series without 

complex feature engineering[9].  

New architectures such as Transformer[17] were originally designed for natural language 

processing but are adapted for time series as well. Thanks to an attention mechanism that allows 

the  model  to  weight  the  importance  of  different  parts  of  the  series  history.  Studies  show  the 

superiority of Transformers in large-window forecasting due to the attention mechanism. At the 

same  time,  GRU  and  LSTM[9]  performed  the  best  when  there  is  a  lack  of  data  and  in  the 

forecasting task with a small window. 

Here are some modern transformers for time series forecasting that are considered best[3]: 

o  Chronos,  an  open  source  family  of  pre-trained  time  series  forecasting  models  from 

Amazon[1] 

o  TimesFM  -  an  open  source  model  from  Google  Research,  pre-trained  on  over  100 

billion real time series points[2,7] 

o  Moirai - a model from Salesforce AI Research, trained on 27 billion observations from 

9 separate domains[8] 

o  TimeGPT  -  a  model  from  Nixtla  that  takes  into  account  the  influence  of  external 

variables[4]

The  current  trend  is  to  use  hybrid  models  that  combine  the  strengths  of  different

1.4.5 Modern Experiments 

Bi-Mamba+  is  a  modern  model  for  Long-Term  Time  Series  Forecasting  (LTSF)  that 

combines  deep  learning  techniques  and  state  model  to  handle  long  dependencies  and  improve 

computational efficiency [5].  

The input time series is divided into patches, which helps to account for both inter-series 

and intra-series dependencies in more detail. The data is processed both forward and backward, 

which improves the capture of relationships in the data. Spearman's correlation coefficient is used 

to choose between independent and mixed tokenization, depending on the data. The model utilizes 

a forgetting mechanism to help retain information about the past, improving predictions over long 

time intervals. Bi-Mamba+ is more efficient compared to transformers, providing less resource 

consumption when processing long sequences. 

Experiments on real datasets (weather, traffic, energy) show that the model outperforms 

other  state-of-the-art  approaches  in  terms  of  forecast  accuracy,  including  transformer-based 

models. 

TIME-LLM is a technique for adapting large language models for time series prediction 

through data reprogramming [6]. In this model, the time series is converted into textual prototypes, 

making  it  more  suitable for  processing  by  language  models.  To  improve  the model's  ability  to 

analyze the data, additional context is introduced through textual cues, which allows for a better 

understanding  of  the  task.  After  the  data  is  processed  by  the  language  model,  the  results  are 

projected back to generate predictions. 

TIME-LLM  consistently  outperformed  specialized  models  on  standard  benchmarks 

(ETTh1/2, ETTm1/2, Weather, Electricity, Traffic, ILI). On average, TIME-LLM showed a 1.4% 

reduction  in  MSE  error  compared  to  PatchTST,  a  12%  reduction  compared  to  DLinear  and 

GPT4TS,  and  a  20%  reduction  compared  to  TimesNet  when  using  Llama-7B.  TIME-LLM 

performed better in 36 of the 40 test cases. TIME-LLM has demonstrated high performance in 

12 

 
situations where limited training data is available, showing excellent results even when trained on 

10% or 5% of the total data [6].

3.2.1 Statistical Models 

All the necessary functionality for working with ARMA family models is provided by the 

python library statsmodel[19]. The conducted Dickey-Fuller test confirmed the stationarity of all 

investigated  series  (p-value  <  0.05),  which  allows  using  ARIMA  models  without  prior 

differentiation. In this paragraph I consider the following models: 

o  ARIMA.  The  basic  ARIMA(p,d,q)  model  was  implemented  with  the parameter  d=0, 

since the series are already stationary. Ranges were used to select optimal values of p 

and q, which is justified by the dependencies revealed in the correlation analysis.  

o  SARIMA. An extension of the basic ARIMA was the SARIMA model, which takes into 

account  seasonal  components  in  the  data,  which  is  an  important  component  of  the 

analyzed series. The seasonal component SARIMA(p,d,q)(P,D,Q)_s with periods s=7 

21 

 
 
 
 
(for daily data) and s=24 (for hourly data) should help the model to correctly describe 

the cycles of warehouse activity. 

o  SARIMAX. This model is an extension of the SARIMA model, providing an opportunity 

to take into account external factors. The time characteristics chosen as external factors 

are: day of the month, quarter, day of the week, month and hour (for hourly data). It is 

also possible to add additional data to the exogenous variables, such as data on orders 

and promotions. The main limitation of using external factors is that they are used not 

only in training but  also in forecasting, which makes it impossible to use data from 

another business process.   

The selection of optimal parameters of each model is realized through the enumeration of 

different  combinations  of  p,  d  and  q  values  in  the  given  ranges  for  each  sampling  level.  The 

Akaike's criterion (AIC) is used as an estimate of model quality, which provides a balance between 

approximation accuracy and model complexity.

3.2.2 Machine Learning Models 

The  main  difference  between  the  approach  to  training  machine  learning  models  and 

statistical models is data preprocessing. If for training of ARIMA family models the investigated 

series was simply used, then for ML models these data should be brought into a “tabular” form.  

All the necessary functionality for working with models is provided in the Scikit-learn[20] library 

in python. 

Feature preprocessing. Lag features and moving averages of the target variable are used 

to convey information about past values of the series. The number of blends and window sizes 

were chosen considering the size of data aggregation (by day and by hour). Since the data revealed 

certain cyclicities related to the specifics of the company's operations, temporal attributes were 

added  to  the  feature  space.  The  created  variables  included  year,  month,  day,  day  of  the  week, 

quarter, as well as hour for hourly data and a binary indicator of weekends. 

Training technology. For convenient training of models and selection of hyperparameters 

the  SklearnTrainer  class  was  implemented,  which  performs  all  necessary  pipelining  -  from 

preprocessing of features to estimation of model quality on a test sample (20%). The implemented 

class  is  a  universal  solution  for  any  ML  models,  requiring  only  passing  the  ranges  of 

hyperparameters for the trained model.  The procedure of selecting the most informative features 

is  performed  using  the  Recursive  Feature Elimination  algorithm,  which  limits  the  final  feature 

space to the 15 most significant variables. This strategy is aimed at preventing model overfitting 

and  reducing  the  computational  complexity  of  forecasting,  which  has  become  very  critical  in 

hourly load forecasting. Feature pre-selection reduced the hyperparameter fitting time by a factor 

of  approximately  5  without  loss  of  accuracy.  The  hyperparameter  tuning  of  the  models  was 

implemented  using  the  Optuna  library,  which  provides  convenient  and  automated  parameter 

selection. The target function of optimization is the root mean square error (RMSE), calculated 

within  time  cross-validation  using  TimeSeriesSplit  specifically  for  time  series,  ensuring  the 

informativeness of the metric under conditions of new data augmentation. 

Models  used.  The  study  examines  four  classes  of  machine  learning  algorithms  that 

demonstrate different approaches to modeling nonlinear dependencies in time series. 

23 

 
o  Lasso-regression  presents  a  linear  baseline  approach  with  L1-regularization  acting  as 

baseline.  

o  Random Forest implements an ensemble method based on multiple decision trees, which 

can efficiently model complex nonlinear interactions between variables without explicit 

assumptions about the functional form of dependencies.  

o  Gradient Boosting presents an alternative ensemble strategy based on sequential learning, 

where each successive tree corrects the errors of the previous ones.  

o  CatBoost, developed by Yandex[16], is  a modern implementation of Gradient Boosting 

with  optimized  processing  of  categorical  features  and  built-in  mechanisms  to  prevent 

overtraining. 

Quality  assessment.  The  generation  of  forecasts  is  carried  out  by  means  of  a  recursive 

strategy in which the predicted values are sequentially included in the feature space to generate 

forecasts for subsequent time horizons. This approach ensures the creation of multi-step forecasts 

while  preserving  the  temporal  structure  of  dependencies,  which  is  critical  for  the  practical 

application of the models. The forecasting quality is assessed at two time horizons: 30 periods for 

daily data and 168 periods (24×7) for hourly time series.

3.2.3 Deep Learning Models 

A fundamental difference between the approach based on recurrent neural networks and 

classical  ML  models  is  the  ability  to  work  with  data  sequences  in  their  natural  representation. 

While traditional algorithms require conversion of time series into a tabular format with lagging 

features,  RNN  architectures  process  sequences  directly,  preserving  the  temporal  structure  of 

25 

 
 
 
 
 
information  through  internal  memory.  All  the  necessary  functionality  for  model  building  and 

training is provided by the PyTorch[21] library in python. 

Data preprocessing. For data processing, the Dataset class was implemented to convert the 

raw  data  into  tensors.  Depending  on  the  model  used,  this  class  creates  tensors  of  the  required 

length, where each element of the sequence describes the configuration of the warehouse at the 

moment. Warehouse configuration here refers to receiving and shipping data, as well as attributes 

describing the temporal component such as month, day, and time (similar to ML processing). One 

sample is information about already known data (input sequence) as well as features corresponding 

to future time steps for prediction. Such attributes are data describing the time component, since 

such data are known in advance without the need for prediction. Also as such attributes can be 

information about orders, weather - the main limitation, as in the case of exogenous variables in 

statistical models, such data should be known for the forecast horizon. 

Training Technology. All models were built in such a way as to use a single dataset format 

without  the  need  for  refinements.  Otherwise,  the  training  format  in  the  case  of  time  series 

forecasting  corresponds  to  the  training  of  models  for  working  with  sequences  (e.g.,  machine 

translation). All models use a teacher forcing strategy with a customizable coefficient that provides 

a  balance  between  training  stability  and  autonomous  prediction  ability.  The  teacher  forcing 

strategy  is  based  on  passing  the  true  sequence  values  when  generating  the  next  step  of  the 

sequence, instead of the previously generated ones. A sheduler was also used to gradually decrease 

the learning rate at each epoch to more accurately adjust the weights at the end of training. All the 

models described below were trained with the same parameters: input sequence length 14 and 48, 

prediction horizon length 7 and 48 for day and hour aggregation, respectively. 

Architectures  used.  Four  classes  of  RNN  models  are  implemented  in  the  study, 

demonstrating different approaches to temporal sequence processing: 

o  Basic  LSTM/GRU  models  represent  fundamental  architectures  with  long  short-

term memory cells. In this case, the input sequence and the predicted sequence must 

have the same dimensionality of one element. 

o  Sequence-to-Sequence 

architecture 

implements 

separate 

encoder-decoder 

components to process historical data and generate predictions. The encoder on the

provides  step-by-step  generation  with  integration  of  temporal  features.  This 

architecture  is  chosen  in  order  to  give  the  model  more  information  about  the 

forecasted  series.  Thus,  several  time  series  are  fed  to  the  model  input  (to  the

26 

SEQ2SEQ_full  accepts  data  from  another  business  process  (both  shipping  and 

receiving). 

o  The attention mechanism model extends the seq2seq architecture with an attention 

mechanism. The decoder at each prediction step obtains weighted information from 

the  hidden  states  of  the  encoder  (input  sequence),  which  can  be  effective  for 

capturing  dependencies  in  warehouse  operations.  This  architecture,  similar  to 

seq2seq, is considered in two configurations: with information about one process 

and about two processes at once. 

Quality Assessment. The evaluation approach is consistent with the ML solution. Prediction 

quality assessment is performed on two time horizons: 30 periods for daily data and 168 periods

The comparative analysis of the received models shows efficiency and easy selection of 

hyperparameters  of  methods  of  machine  learning,  in  particular  CatBoost  which  has  shown  the 

superiority over statistical models of ARMA family and became a strong competitor of recurrent

importance  for  CatBoost  showed  that  in  hourly  forecasting,  calendar  features,  especially  the 

categorical feature of the current hour, have the greatest influence, making the forecasts almost 

independent of past values. Although forecasts of recurrent networks lack such a pattern, and a 

local extremum appears in the case of adding data from a related business process, their results are 

comparable to CatBoost errors, which may indicate a small training sample size. The plateau in 

model training is also indicated by the distribution of errors depending on the horizon, where in 

the results of all models there is a certain pattern of error increase different only by the offset. It 

can also be said, given the low autocorrelation of hourly aggregated series, that there are some 

external factors affecting the workload of the warehouse during the day, such as the number of 

storekeepers or active orders. In the presence of such data encoder-decoder architectures can show 

the best results. All the above-mentioned allows to make a choice that the task of forecasting the 

load of the investigated warehouse is quite solvable and takes place. 

3.3 Solution Integration 

As part of the development of a warehouse load forecasting system, there is a need to create 

a  server  part  that  would  combine  all  trained  models  and  provide  a  convenient  way  to  obtain 

forecasts. To solve this problem in this paper, FastAPI framework was chosen, which allows to 

quickly  create  a  web  service  with  minimal  time  and  provides  high  performance.  FastAPI  is  a 

modern  high-performance  web  framework  for Python,  specifically  designed  to  create  RESTful 

APIs  using  standard  Python  type  annotations.    A  key  feature  of  FastAPI  is  the  auto-generated 

interactive API documentation using the OpenAPI standard and Swagger UI, which simplifies the 

integration process with client applications. [22]  

FastAPI server acts as an intermediary between trained prediction models and end users of 

the  system. The basic idea is that all  models - statistical, machine learning, deep learning, and 

hybrid - are packaged into a single service that can accept queries and return prediction results in 

a standardized format. 

This  approach  has  several  important  advantages.  First,  it  provides  centralized  model 

management: all algorithms are in one place, which simplifies their updating and maintenance. 

31 

 
 
Second, it creates a universal interface for obtaining forecasts, regardless of which model is used 

internally. This means that a web interface or other client applications can work with any of the 

models through the same queries. 

The server is built on the principle of REST API, which means that standard HTTP requests 

are used to interact with clients. When the application is launched, all necessary models are loaded 

into the server memory. This includes loading weights of neural networks and machine learning 

models and parameters of statistical models. 

Using FastAPI to create the server side of the prediction system provides high performance 

and scalability. The framework supports  asynchronous processing of requests, which allows to 

efficiently  serve  multiple  simultaneous  connections.  This  is  especially  important  in  an  active 

warehouse environment, when the system must quickly process requests from different users. 

The standardized interface simplifies integration with corporate information systems and 

allows the forecasting results to be used in various warehouse business processes. This makes the 

developed  system  not  an  isolated  tool,  but  a  full-fledged  component  of  the  enterprise  IT 

infrastructure.
