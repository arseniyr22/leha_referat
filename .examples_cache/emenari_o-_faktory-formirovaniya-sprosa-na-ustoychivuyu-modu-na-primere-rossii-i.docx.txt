INTRODUCTION
	
The Fashion Industry	
The fashion business constitutes a vast and rapidly expanding industry. Researches shows that global clothing production doubled between 2000 and 2015 reaching almost 100 billion garments annually and is on track to reach an estimated 1.2 trillion U.S. dollars in value by 2025 (Niinimäki et al., 2022; Says, 2022). Concurrently, the average number of times a garment is worn before its disposal has declined by 36% reflecting the continues rise of fast fashion and consumer behavior (van Gogh et al., 2025; UniformMarket, 2025). This accelerated production and consumption cycle has significant environmental consequences. The fashion industry is identified to be responsible for  a significant amount of the global greenhouse gas emissions, surpassing emissions from international flights and shipping, this means that it is among the most carbon intensive industries worldwide (Niinimäki et al., 2022; United Nations Environment Program [UNEP], 2023).This estimate is also supported by numerous recent academic studies which shows that the apparel and footwear industry in particular generates approximately 1.2 billion tons of CO₂ annually, accounting for more than 10% of global emissions and surpassing emissions from the aviation and shipping industries combined (Niinimäki et al., 2022; European Parliament, 2021). 
Water waste represents a visible consequence of the environmental impact of the fashion industry. Cotton which is one of the most widely used natural fibers in clothing is particularly water-intensive to produce. Chapagain et al. (2006) estimated that producing just one kilogram of cotton requires between 10,000 and 20,000 liters of water, depending on regional conditions and agricultural practices. Kant (2012) reported that textile dyeing alone is the second largest global polluter of water, with traditional dyeing processes using more than 200 tons of water per ton of fabric dyed. Textile Agriculture Alliance (2020) reports that conventional cotton cultivation is liable for approximately 2.4% of the world's arable land and is responsible for 11% of global pesticide use and 24% of global insecticide use. Waste generation is another major environmental challenge associated with the fashion industry. This waste problem is made worse by the rise of fast fashion which encourages the consumers to view clothing as disposable rather than durable. In many developed countries including the United Kingdom and the United States, textiles have one of the lowest recycling rates when compared to most commonly used consumer products with most of the discarded clothes ending up in either landfills or incinerators. Remy et al. (2016) reported that in the United States alone, more than 85% of textiles are sent to landfills annually, converting to a loss of more than $100 billion worth of materials. The environmental effect of fashion industry goes beyond the production phase to include consumer use and disposal. Washing synthetic garments also releases microplastic fibers that can enter waterways and eventually end up in the ocean, contributing to marine pollution. A study by Browne et al. (2011) found that one single washing of polyester garments can release more than 1,900 microplastic fibers per garment. moreover, consumer laundering practices, especially washing at high temperatures and their frequent use of tumble dryers also significantly contribute to the lifecycle energy consumption and carbon footprint of clothing. Understanding the numerous factors driving consumers demand for sustainable fashion is therefore vital for advancing sustainability in the fashion industry. By identifying these social, psychological and cultural factors that might influence customers sustainable fashion purchase intentions, this study aims to contribute to developing more effective strategies that promotes sustainable consumption behaviors in diverse cultural sphere.
Global and local sustainability effort
In the global level, numerous multi-dimensional frameworks and initiatives has emerged to address sustainability in the fashion industry. The United Nations Fashion Industry Charter for Climate Action launched at the COP24 climate conference in 2018 is one of sch global initiative. The Charter brought world fashion stakeholders together to develop a unified approach in addressing the industry's contribution to climate change (The Fashion Industry Charter for Climate Action, 2018). Signatories committed to at least a 30% reduction in greenhouse gas emissions by the year 2030 and to pursuing net-zero emissions by year 2050, with specific working groups focusing on areas such as raw materials, manufacturing, logistics and most especially consumer engagement. To the same effect, the Sustainable Apparel Coalition (SAC) established in 2011 developed tools like the Higg Index to measure and improve environmental and social performance across the fashion distribution chain. According to the Sustainable Apparel Coalition (2020), the Higg Index provides a standardized metric that helps brands, retailers and facilities to measure and evaluate the sustainability of their operations, identify areas for improvement and communicate their progress to the appropriated stakeholders.  
The United Nations 2030 Agenda geared towards Sustainable Development adopted in 2015 outlines 17 interconnected Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) to help address global challenges like poverty, inequality, climate change and environmental degradation (United Nations, 2015). These goals notified of the need for more collaborative action across nations, industries and communities in other to achieve sustainable development. Among the SDG 12: Responsible Consumption and Production is particularly essential to the fashion industry due to its notorious for environmental degradation including but not limited to excessive water use, chemical pollution and textile waste (van Gogh et al., 2025). SDG 12 calls for sustainable management of resources, reduction of waste generation and promotion of ethical production practices in all sectors, these objectives perfectly align closely with the principles of sustainable fashion (Fletcher, 2010; Mukendi et al., 2020). In Nigeria, SDG 12 intersects with the country’s rich textile heritage such as adire (indigo dyeing) and aso-oke weaving which inherently utilizes natural materials and artisanal techniques (Bobie, 2024). but all the same, challenges like the dominance of secondhand clothing markets (okrika) and limited recycling infrastructure limit progress, because imported fast fashion still undermines local sustainable practices and exacerbates waste (Adeyemi, 2023; World Bank, 2020). Considerably, in Russia, post-Soviet consumerism and their reliance on synthetic textiles also pose barriers to the SDG 12 compliance, though recent grassroots movements such as Moscow’s eco-fashion collectives shows a significant growing urban demand for circular practices like upcycling and clothing swaps (Tsyganash et al., 2024; Kudryavtseva & Kulikova, 2024). Both nations present an example of the tension between globalized fast fashion and localized sustainability efforts, highlighting the vital need for culturally tailored strategies that can help in advancing the SDG 12’s universal targets.
In Russia, sustainability in fashion is an emerging area of focus which is been shaped by the country's unique culture, economy and history. after the dissolution of the Soviet Union, Russia experienced a fast transition to a market economy accompanied by a notable change in consumption pattern and the development of a domestic fashion industry. This transition period, as described by Mennicken (2008) was characterized by a surge in consumer goods importation and the adoption of western style consumption practices including fast fashion. Although, more recent years have seen a growing interest in sustainable fashion among certain consumer segments. Tsyganash, Gert, & Kropacheva (2024) noted that sustainable fashion in Russia is primarily driven and encouraged by a small but growing segment of environmentally conscious consumers who are often concentrated in urban locations such as Moscow and St. Petersburg. 
In Nigeria, the sphere of sustainable fashion is been shaped by the country's rich textile heritage, economic challenges and the recent growing environmental awareness. Nigeria has a long history of textile production and craftsmanship with traditional techniques like adire (indigo dyeing) and aso-oke weaving been an important part of their cultural and economic activities. These traditional practices mostly but unconsciously embody sustainability principles through their use of natural materials, artisanal production methods and cultural significance which encourages long-term use and preservation (Bobie, 2024). The Nigerian Conservation Foundation and other environmental organizations also organize events and campaigns that raise awareness about the environmental consequences of fashion and in a bid to promote more sustainable consumption pattern. However, sustainable fashion in Nigeria still faces significant challenges including infrastructure limitations, economic constraints and competing priorities in a developing economy sphere. The prevalence of secondhand clothing imports known locally in Nigeria as "okrika" or "bend down boutique," presents both opportunities and challenges for sustainable fashion in Nigeria.
Research objective and question 
Having seen the adverse effect and ecological consequences of the fashion industry and the struggle to rectify it, there is indeed an important and urgent need to understand consumer motivations for sustainable fashion consumption. While global initiatives towards sustainable fashion preach responsible consumption, localized strategies remain critical to addressing barriers considering the fact that culture is the first port of socialization and thus shapes subsequent behavior. Existing studies predominantly focus on Western contexts neglecting how cultural values like collectivism interact with environmental perceptions to drive sustainable consumption. This study focuses on Russia and Nigeria, an emerging and transforming markets with different cultural orientations, this study progresses with a view that this cultural difference might provide valuable insights that each country and stakeholders can utilize in the war against unsustainable consumption in fashion.  to address this gap in research and provide insights into the drivers of sustainable fashion consumption, this study pursues three objectives:
investigating the direct effects of environmental collective efficacy, environmental knowledge, and collectivism on sustainable fashion purchase intentions.
assessing collectivism’s moderating role in amplifying/attenuating these relationships.
identifying cross-cultural variations in drivers between Nigeria and Russia.

Guided by the above objectives, this research asks: 

"How does environmental collective efficacy, environmental knowledge and collectivism directly influence sustainable fashion purchase intentions in Russia and Nigeria and how does collectivism moderate these relationships across the two cultural spheres?"






CHAPTER ONE

Literature Review
Sustainable fashion
	Sustainable fashion or “slow fashion” as it  has in recent years come to be known as is generally considered  a  movement and these two terms are often interchangeably used with green-, eco-, and ethical-fashion but an agreed definition of it is still elusive (Henninger et al., 2016; Mukendi et al., 2020), Sustainable fashion represents a shift of paradigm in the way clothes are designed, manufactured, distributed, consumed and eventually disposed with emphasis on reducing its environmental impact while promoting social responsibility. the definition of sustainable fashion takes different shapes in different studies and pro-sentimental movements and initiatives but regardless of what it is called or the definition attached, the core principles of sustainable fashion remain constant across its names and initiatives.
Slow fashion, a terminology mostly credited to Fletcher (2007) represents a direct challenge to fast fashion model which has dominated the contemporary fashion industry. Unlike fast fashion which is characterized by quick production cycles, low prices and even faster disposability, slow fashion iterates quality, durability and timelessness. Jung and Jin (2014) described slow fashion as an approach which values quality rather than quantity, promote local production and traditional craftsmanship while encouraging consumers to develop a more meaningful and longer relationship with their clothing.  But According to Joergens (2006), ethical fashion refers to "fashionable” wears that imbue fair trade principles with sweatshop-free labor conditions without harming the environment or the workers by using organic cotton and biodegradable materials". Eco-fashion as described by Niinimäki (2010) focuses rudimentarily on the environmental facet of sustainability noting the use of environmentally friendly material, processes and technologies. This includes organic and renewable fibers, natural dyes, water and also energy-efficient production methods combined with waste reduction strategies. Fair trade fashion, as defined by Littrell and Dickson (1999), refers to clothing production under fair trade principles to ensure that producers in developing countries receive a fair compensation for their work and also operate under safe ethical working conditions. The idea of circular fashion introduced by McDonough and Braungart (2002) via their "cradle-to-cradle" design philosophy, represented another important aspect of sustainable fashion. 
Upcycling which is a practice within circular fashion framework and involves the transformation of waste materials or unwanted products into new garments or accessories that are of higher value or quality. As explained by Bridgens et al. (2018), upcycling not only diverts materials from landfills but also reduces the rising need for virgin resources thus minimizing the environmental effect associated with raw material extraction and processing. The concept of transparency has also become a crucial aspect of sustainable fashion. Shen et al. (2014) iterated the importance of supply chain transparency in enabling consumers in making informed choices about the products they buy. Consumer engagement is another crucial dimension of sustainable fashion. Bly et al. (2015) noted that sustainable fashion involves not only changes in production practices but also on shifts in consumption patterns. Henninger et al. (2016) registers sustainable fashion as an umbrella term that encompasses various approaches to fashion production and consumption that prioritizes environmental integrity with social equity.
In the context of this research, sustainable fashion is regarded as an approach to fashion “wears” that seeks to minimize negative environmental impacts and maximize positive social contributions throughout the entire lifecycle of clothing, from design and production to consumption and disposal. This comprehensive definition is an umbrella for the various dimensions of sustainability, including environmental considerations (such as resource efficiency, pollution prevention and conservation of biodiversity), social aspects (including community development, fair labor practices and cultural preservation) and then economic factors (like long-term viability, value creation and fair compensation).
Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB)	
The Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB) has been widely adopted and confirmed as a grounded model for predicting human behaviors which require intentional control, making it essentially suitable for examining sustainable fashion purchase intention (SFPI) (Ajzen, 1991). Originating as an extension to the Theory of Reasoned Action (TRA), TPB introduced the construct referred to as “perceived behavioral control” (PBC) to its original TRA components attitude and subjective norms, thus enhancing its predictive abilities (Ajzen & Fishbein, 1980; Ajzen, 1991). According to TPB, the likelihood of a person engaging in a specific behavior, such as purchasing sustainable fashion is increased when that individuals hold positive attitude towards the behavior, perceive social approval (subjective norms) and believe they possess enough control over performing the particular action (Ajzen, 1991). For example, a consumer with a positive attitude towards sustainable fashion rooted in his or her beliefs about its environmental benefits is more likely to form an intention to purchase such products. also, subjective norms such as peer approval or cultural expectations further intensify this intention. The TPB extends this model by integrating PBC which accounts for an individual’s perception of their ability to perform that behavior, including factors like resource availability, skills and external constraints (Ajzen, 1991). This addition is vital for behaviors such as sustainable fashion consumption where economic barriers (such as higher costs) or limited product accessibility may hinder action (Bigne et al., 2024). This framework has been well validated across many environmental contexts, including water conservation and green product consumption, confirming its adaptability to cultural and socio-economic variations (Chan, 1998; Ramayah et al., 2012; Paul et al., 2015). 
The TPB has been widely applied in apparel and textile research to understand the drivers of sustainable consumption (de Lira & da Costa, 2022). Studies have leveraged the framework to explore how various constructs influence stainability purchase intention (Han & Kim, 2019; Kang & Kim, 2013). For example, Shen et al. (2012) discovered that environmental concern and social norms significantly predicted sustainable fashion choices, while Kang and Kim (2013) signified the role of perceived risk in moderating these relationships. Recent research has also expanded the TPB by incorporating additional construct to capture the complexities of sustainable fashion adoption (Becker-Leifhold, 2018; Chi et al., 2021). In emerging markets such as India and Indonesia, extended TPB model has successfully validated the interplay between environmental knowledge, cultural values and purchase intentions highlighting the need to contextualize the framework in local socio-economic conditions ((Fenitra et al., 2021; Brandão & Costa, 2021).
In regards to Russia and Nigeria, the TPB provides a valuable model for analyzing how social, psychological and structural factors interact to shape SFPI. For example, Russian consumers who generally exhibit higher digital connectivity and urbanization rates may develop stronger pro-environmental attitude due to greater exposure to sustainability related information (Bigne et al., 2024). But in contrast, Nigerian consumers’ purchase intention may be shaped more by their communal values and shared environmental goals, showing the country’s collectivist orientation (Ham et al., 2015). Sustainable fashion purchases may be driven by a desire to align with community norms or avoid social disapproval, especially when eco-friendly practices are perceived as a collective responsibility (Lee, 2017). In Russia, where cultural values are more hybrid, blending individualistic and collectivist tendencies, subjective norms may arise from aspirational peer groups or digital communities advocating for sustainability (Khitous et al., 2022). Recent studies in similar emerging markets highlights how these psychological constructs mediate the relationship between environmental awareness and purchase intention, suggesting that improving access to sustainable options could amplify consumer engagement (Brandão & Costa, 2021). By incorporating constructs such as environmental collective efficacy, knowledge and collectivism into the TPB framework for purchase intention, this research can provide some valuable insights to the factors behind Nigerian consumers' intentions to consider buying sustainable fashion and thus better inform stake holders.
Environmental Collective Efficacy 
Environmental collective efficacy (ECE) originates from Bandura's (1977) social cognitive theory which initially focused on self-efficacy and was defined as an individual's belief in his or her ability to execute actions that helps to achieve a particular desired outcome. As environmental challenges often required coordinated efforts, scholars expanded this theory to collective efficacy defined as a group's shared belief in their ability to effect change (Bandura, 2000). For example, while a single consumer's decision to boycott or limit the patronage of fast fashion might seem almost inconsequential, the collective action of thousands or even millions of such consumer can put an unignorable pressure on brands to adopt sustainable practices (Homburg & Stolberg, 2006). While extending self-efficacy theory to a collective scenario, Bandura (2000) distinguished between personal agency exercised individually and collectively. This distinction is particularly important to environmental behavior in which outcomes are dependent on aggregate actions. like Reese and Junge (2017) demonstrated in their study about plastic reduction behaviors, collective efficacy beliefs can predict pro-environmental actions more sturdily than self-efficacy, most especially when tasks are perceived as a difficult one. Their research findings indicated that participants who were exposed to collective efficacy messages reported significantly stronger intentions to engage in pro-environmental behaviors when compared to those that were exposed to only self-efficacy messages. 	
The empirical evidence for ECE's impact in regards to sustainable fashion decisions has accumulated steadily, studies consistently show that ECE significantly predicts pro-environmental behavior often more robustly than self-efficacy. Homburg and Stolberg (2006) in their enquiry to better Explain pro-environmental behavior with a cognitive theory of stress found that individuals with higher ECE were more likely to participate in recycling programs even when they doubted their personal impact while a study by Gupta and Ogden (2009) demonstrated that consumers who believed in collective action are more inclined towards purchasing green products because they view their choices as a part of societal shift towards sustainability. Leonard and Leviston (2017) in their research applied a model of collective efficacy for understanding consumer and civic pro-environmental actions found that collective efficacy was a good stronger predictor of pro-environmental behavior than individual action beliefs across urban and rural contexts, while Jugert et al. (2016) in their experimental studies of how Collective efficacy increases pro-environmental intentions through increasing self-efficacy demonstrated that collective efficacy manipulations affected pro-environmental intentions by increasing both collective and self-efficacy, highlighting the reciprocal relationship between these constructs.
Cultural Spectrums also shape ECE's role in sustainable fashion. Hofstede's (2010) framework on dimensionalities of culture classifies Nigeria as a collectivist society where group goals often supersede individual interests, meanwhile, Russia mostly exhibits higher individualism but with a strong communal tradition in some certain regions. for example, churches advocating for "modest fashion" often frame sustainable consumption as a moral obligation in Nigeria (Aksoy & Abdulfatai, 2019), while Urban youth movements such as Moscow's eco-fashion collectives use social media to build collective efficacy by organizing cloth swaps and upcycling workshops for consumers (Lamoda Planet, 2024). The comparative research by Lee (2017) which examined the impact of collectivism on green purchase intention in South Korea and China concluded that collectivism positively moderated the relationship between environmental collective efficacy and green purchase intention in China but interestingly not in Korea. This regional or geographical variation within suggests the importance of considering specific cultural and historical contexts rather than making broad continental generalizations.
While ECE's role in purchase intention is well-researched, few studies have explored its specific application to sustainable fashion most especially in emerging and transforming economies like Nigeria and Russia. Most ECE frameworks are derived from Western contexts, neglecting dynamics in African and Eurasian markets (Arsal & Atalar, 2016).  This study therefore seeks to shrink the bridge in research knowledge, thus defining “environmental collective efficacy” as an individual’s belief about his or her group’s ability in solving environmental problems effectively and proposes Hypothesis 1 as follows:
Hypothesis 1 (H1):
 Environmental collective efficacy (ECE) has a positive effect on sustainable fashion purchase intention.
Environmental Knowledge (EKN) 
Environmental knowledge (EKN) is another vital determinant of pro-environmental behavior including sustainable consumption behaviors such as purchasing eco-friendly fashion (Fryxell & Lo, 2003). EKN which is defined as an individual's understanding of environmental issues, ecological systems and the consequences of human actions on our planet (Polonsky et al., 2012). Environmental knowledge encompasses both the general awareness of global challenges like climate change and pollution and specific insights into industry related effects such as the fashion industry's role in the depletion of resource and waste generation. In the spheres of sustainable fashion, environmental knowledge further involves familiarity with eco-friendly materials (such as recycled polyester and organic cotton), ethical production processes and the lifecycle environmental cost of garments (Barber et al., 2009). This knowledge provides consumers with the ability to make better informed decisions that bridges the gap existing between environmental concern and actionable purchase behavior (Ellen, 1994).
The link between environmental knowledge and sustainable consumption is also well-documented but situation dependent. Kim and Damhorst (1998) demonstrated that fashion apparel shoppers with higher environmental knowledge often exhibits stronger preferences for eco-friendly textiles while Joung and Park-Poaps (2013) linked knowledge to willingness to pay premiums for sustainable fashion wears. Despite these documentations on EKN, contradictions still exist, as Adeyemi (2023) reported that there was no significant relationship between sustainability awareness and intention to purchase second-hand clothing in Nigeria despite second-hand clothing's possible sustainability benefits.
In the fashion industry, environmental knowledge role is amplified by the sector's visibility and social influence. Yet, this knowledge alone may not be enough. Gupta and Ogden (2009) pointed out that ethical consumption often requires aligning that knowledge with collective efficacy. For instance, a Russian consumer might avoid indulging in fast fashion purchase not only due to knowledge of its harms on the environment but also from confidence that their choices contributed to broader industry shifts (Homburg & Stolberg, 2006). Cultural settings further shapes how environmental knowledge translates into actual and actionable intention. These differences mark the need for localized strategies to improve environmental knowledge's efficacy and Few studies has explored how environmental knowledge interacts with cultural values like collectivism to shape sustainable fashion demand (Arsal & Atalar, 2016). Moreso, the quickly evolving nature of both environmental science and fashion trends and thus the industry requires constant research for dynamic updates. Based on the above discussion, this study assumes the hypothesis that environmental knowledge will have a positive effect on sustainable fashion purchase intention while acknowledging the potential for significant variations in Nigeria and Russia.
Hypothesis 2 (H2): 
Environmental knowledge (EKN) has a positive effect on sustainable fashion purchase intention.
Collectivism 	
	Collectivism which is a cultural orientation that emphasizes group harmony, shared responsibilities and interdependence, thus prioritizing group goals over personal autonomy plays a vital role in structuring sustainable consumption behaviors (Hofstede, 2011). According to McCarty and Shrum (2001), collectivist individuals are 34% more likely to participate in a group-led environmental initiatives than individualists, viewing such actions as their moral obligations to community. This is in line with Bandura's (2000) concept of collective efficacy where group-oriented beliefs increase pro-environmental actions like purchasing sustainable fashion to relieve systemic environmental harm (Homburg & Stolberg, 2006).
Empirical research comparing collectivism's effects across cultures supports its significance in sustainable consumption. Varshneya et al. (2017) found that in collectivist economies, social influence significantly impacted organic clothing purchase intentions. Similarly, Shin and Koh (2016) reported a cross-cultural difference in their study on ethical fashion consumption between South Korea and Germany, posting that collectivist values strongly predicting sustainable purchases. While Kim and Choi (2005) also in their study of antecedents of green purchase behavior, examination of collectivism and environmental concern confirmed that collectivist values directly predicted environmentally conscious behavior in consumers because individuals prioritizing group welfare were more likely to consider the broader environmental impact of their purchases. According to Han and Kim (2019), collectivistic values significantly influenced sustainable purchasing in online group buying sphere, hinting on the role of normative influence while Lee (2017) conducted a comparative study of Korean and Chinese consumers and found that collectivism positively predicted green purchase intentions in China, though this relationship was less pronounced in Korea. This suggests that even within cultures traditionally classified as collectivist, the strength of this relationship still varies based on economic, social and historical factors. According to Varshneya et al. (2017) also examined the impact of social influence and green consumption values on purchase intention of organic clothing and concluded that collectivistic behavior significantly influence purchase intentions in regards to sustainable fashion in developing economies because consumers view their sustainable fashion choices as an expression of their group’s identity and social responsibilities (de Lira & da Costa, 2022). These findings are the bases of the hypothesis that collectivism has a positive effect on sustainable fashion purchase intention (H3) though cultural hint in how collectivism manifest in Nigeria versus Russia might produce different levels of influence.
Hypothesis 3 (H3):
 Collectivism has a positive effect on sustainable fashion purchase intention.
Collectivisms moderative role on environmental collective efficacy and knowledge	
Beyond its direct effect, collectivism also strengthens other factors that influence sustainable fashion demand according to Lee (2017) who conducted a comparative study of green purchase intention between Korean and Chinese consumers and concluded that collectivism positively moderated the relationship between ECE and sustainable purchase intention in China, improving the conversion of group-based efficacy beliefs into actual purchase intentions. This moderating role shows how cultural values improve the impact of these constructs on consumers behavior and has been well established as having a positive effect on SFPI, as proven by the Theory of Planned Behavior which linked knowledge to attitudes and perceived behavioral control that drives these purchase intention (Barbarossa & De Pelsmacker, 2016)
When consumers view sustainable fashion as a collective endeavor rather than an individual choice, they are less likely to rely passively on just other people’s efforts (Gupta & Ogden, 2009). According to Sharma and Aswal (2017), collectivism positively moderated the relationship between environmental knowledge and green purchase intentions in a more expansive consumption scenario, the result of their study suggested that when individuals with strong collectivist values attain environmental knowledge, they become more motivated to act on that knowledge by exhibiting sustainable purchasing behaviors. This interaction provides preliminary support for our fourth hypothesis which posits that collectivism positively moderates the positive effect of environmental collective efficacy and environmental knowledge on sustainable fashion purchase intention (H4).
Hypothesis 4 (H4a+H4b): 
Collectivism positively moderates the effect of environmental collective efficacy and environmental knowledge on sustainable fashion purchase intention.
Conceptual Framework		
			


From the literature review discussion, this study proposes a model to examine the factors driving sustainable fashion purchase intentions in Russia and Nigeria. The model constitutes three primary constructs namely “environmental collective efficacy” (ECE), “environmental knowledge” (EKN) and “collectivism” (COL) on sustainable fashion purchase intention (SFPI) and explores both their direct effects and interactive dynamics.
While the proposed hypotheses apply to both Russia and Nigeria, the literature suggests that there might be a variation in how the relationships manifest. Nigeria's stronger collectivist orientation might increase both the direct and moderating effects of collectivism when compared to Russia's more hybrid cultural society. Russia's higher levels of urbanization and digital connectivity might also strengthen the relationship between environmental knowledge and purchase intentions. This conceptual framework provides a base for examining sustainable fashion purchase intentions in Russia and Nigeria by integrating psychological, knowledge-based and cultural factors while still accounting for possible cross-cultural variations in this interaction. By testing this framework, this study seeks to advance the understanding of sustainable fashion consumption pattern in transforming economies with the view to providing valuable insights for better promotion of sustainable fashion adoption.

CHAPTER TWO

METHODOLOGY

This study applied a self-administered online questionnaire to collect empirical data from English speaking consumers in selected urban geographical location of Russia (mainly St. Petersburg and Moscow) and Nigeria (mainly Enugu and Lagos). The selection of these cities was deliberate, aiming to capture diverse perspectives on sustainable fashion consumption across particular cultural and geographical sphere. St. Petersburg and Moscow representing Russia’s major metropolitan locations with high level of urbanization and digital connectivity, while Enugu and Lagos are also key Nigerian cities reflecting a mixture of emerging urban consumer behaviors in a predominantly collectivist society. This geographical spread Assures the inclusion of varying consumer experiences and attitudes towards sustainable fashion which is vital for the cross-cultural nature of this research. The questionnaire was developed based on already established measurement scales adapted from validated studies ensuring the validity and reliability of the instrument used in assessing key constructs like sustainable fashion purchase intention (SFPI), environmental collective efficacy (ECE), environmental knowledge (EKN) and collectivism (COL). Adopting and adapting existing scales not only supports methodological efficiency but also facilitates comparability with previous researches in sustainable consumption intentions (Kim & Pysarchik, 2000; Bonniface & Henley, 2008; Wahid et al., 2011; Hofstede et al., 2010). To maintain consistency and also reduce any potential bias related to language comprehension, the questionnaire was administered in English exclusively. This decision was consolidated by the fact that English serves as an official language and lingua franca in Nigeria where over 200 languages are spoken and is also widely understood by the target demographic in Russian cities. Ensuring participants English proficiency is in-line with best practices in cross-cultural research which advices language uniformity to reduce misunderstandings and measurement errors (Brislin, 1970; Harkness, 2003).    
The questionnaire began with a clear definition of “sustainable fashion” mentioning its various terminologies and describing it as clothing that performs the same functions as conventional wear but with reduced environmental impact throughout its lifecycle from production to disposal. This introductory clarification was meant to standardize the respondents ‘comprehension of the concept, enhancing the validity of their responses. The survey items made uses a five-point Likert scale with rang of 1 (strongly disagree) to 5 (strongly agree), allowing participants to express a various degree of agreement or disagreement with statements relating to the constructs under study. This scale format is widely used in consumer behavior studies due to its ease of understanding and ability to capture structured feedbacks (DeVellis, 2017).
Study Population
	The study population is made up of fashion consumers from Russia and Nigeria with a focus on individuals with possible interest in sustainable fashion. Nigeria, with its population of more than 200 million people (Raphael, Peter, & Kenneth, 2024) represents a rapidly growing and significant market for fashion product. A considerable amount of Nigeria’s population belongs to the increasing middle class and accounts for almost 40% of the total population with increasing purchasing power and fashion consciousness (Joseph, 2023). Nigerian consumers are known for their strong cultural connection to fashion, often expressing their identity and social status through colorful traditional textiles such as Ankara, adire and kente which is fast becoming a popular   contemporary fashion trend worldwide (Adeola, Moradeyo, Muogboh, & Adisa, 2021). 
Russia with its 143 million population also presents a vibrant fashion market characterized by a large urban consumer base concentrated in cities like Moscow and St. Petersburg (Russian Journal of Bakhtin Studies, 2024). Russia’s middle and upper-middle classes have demonstrated a growing interest in fashion products with a combination of quality, brand prestige and sustainability (Kudryavtseva & Kulikova, 2024). The country’s urban consumers are fast becoming influenced by global sustainability narratives heightened by high digital connectivity and access to more information (Hüttel, Illgen, & Hoffmann, 2021).  This population is expected to be representative of the emerging sustainable fashion consumer segment in both countries, for a meaningful cross-cultural comparison of the psychological, knowledge-based and cultural drivers of sustainable fashion purchase intentions.
Sampling Procedure
Sampling techniques are very vital in research because they enable the collection of data from a subset of the sample size reducing cost, time and logistical restraints (Etikan, Musa, & Alkassim, 2016). The main data collection tool was a structured online questionnaire provided via Google Forms chosen for its ease of use, accessibility and efficient data management abilities (Wright, 2005). Due to the geographical dispersion of the target population and the researcher’s physical distance from respondents, the online format was vital to facilitate participation across numerous cities in target country. To increase reach and engagement, the questionnaire link was shared via widely used social media platforms including WhatsApp, Telegram, Facebook and VK Messenger which are popular communication channels in both countries.  Initial possible participants were recruited from the researcher’s academic and social networks ensuring that respondents met the study’s inclusion criteria of being English-speaking urban consumers with potential interest in sustainable fashion. These initial respondents were then encouraged to fill and share the survey link with eligible peers, utilizing a snowball sampling technique that enabled the sample to grow organically and reach a wider demographic (Noy, 2008).
For this study, a fusion of convenience and snowball sampling methods was employed to recruit participants from target population reflecting rational considerations and the study’s exploratory nature. Convenience sampling, a non-probability technique that involves selecting participants who are readily accessible to the researcher (Bornstein, Jager, & Putnick, 2013). This method is widely used in consumer behavior research especially when targeting specific populations through digital means (Etikan et al., 2016). While convenience sampling does not guarantee representativeness of the entire population, it is effective for exploratory studies aiming to understand specific behaviors and attitudes within defined groups (Bornstein et al., 2013). Complementing this, snowball sampling was utilized to expand the sample by leveraging referrals from initial participants. Snowball sampling is particularly useful when studying populations that are difficult to access or when no comprehensive sampling frame exists (Noy, 2008). In this study, early respondents were encouraged to share the survey link with peers who also fit the study criteria, allowing the sample to grow organically. This approach helps reach a broader and more diverse set of participants within the fashion-conscious demographic, which might otherwise be challenging to identify through conventional sampling methods (Heckathorn, 2011). However, snowball sampling is subject to potential biases, as participants tend to refer individuals similar to themselves, which can limit sample diversity (Goodman, 2011). Despite this limitation, combining snowball with convenience sampling enhances the overall sample heterogeneity and coverage.
Measurement Constructs  
	To investigate the drivers of sustainable fashion purchase intentions among consumers in Russia and Nigeria this study used a structured close-ended questionnaire. The instrument was designed to get empirical data on vital constructs of interest to the research objectives: Environmental Collective Efficacy (ECE), Environmental Knowledge (EKN), Collectivism (COL) and Sustainable Fashion Purchase Intention (SFPI). The questionnaire was structured into four main sections with each being tailored to measure specific variables using validated scales from established studies in the literature.
Part 1: Demographic information 
The opening section gathered important demographic data from respondents including gender, age, education and their country of citizenship. Collecting this information provided an important base for interpreting the results and allowed for the examination of possible demographic influences on sustainable fashion behaviors. Respondents selected the options most applicable to them ensuring data relevance for analyses.
Part 2: Environmental Collective Efficacy (ECE)
This section measured respondents’ beliefs in the power of collective community action towards addressing environmental issues. Five items adapted from Bonniface and Henley (2008) assessed the extent at which individuals felt their community could work together to reduce environmental degradation, fight climate change, promote eco-friendly behavior, encourage others to act sustainably and generate creative solutions to environmental problems. 
Part 4: Environmental Knowledge (EKN)
Four items in this section were used to obtain respondents awareness and understanding of vital environmental issues. Adopted from Wahid, Rahbar and Shyan (2011), the items measured knowledge of environmental preservation, the environmental impact of non-sustainable practices, the causes and effects of global warming and awareness of particle pollution. This section was crucial for checking the cognitive foundation of sustainable fashion choices.
Part 5: Collectivism (COL)
To measure cultural orientation towards group, four items adapted from Hofstede, Hofstede, and Minkov (2010) were utilized. The items investigated preferences for group work, perceived quality of group versus individual decisions and willingness to support group decisions even in the face of disagreement with group.
Part 5: Sustainable Fashion Purchase Intention (SFPI)
The final section assessed respondents’ intentions regarding sustainable fashion purchase intention. Five items adapted from Kim and Pysarchik (2000) and Chan (2001) measured the likelihood of considering, prioritizing, feeling inclined to purchase, recommending sustainable fashion and willingness to pay a premium (between 6% and 10% more) for sustainable fashion wear. These items directly reflect the behavioral intentions central to the study’s hypotheses.
All construct items were evaluated using a five-point response format, where participants indicated their level of agreement, ranging from "Strongly Disagree" to "Strongly Agree". allowing respondents to express varying degrees of agreement and facilitating quantitative analysis (Joshi et al., 2015). To ensure conceptual clarity and consistency, the questionnaire began with a concise definition of “sustainable fashion wear,” describing it as clothing that serves the same functional purposes as conventional apparel but is produced and disposed of in ways that minimize environmental harm. The adoption of validated measurement instruments from prior research enhances the validity and reliability of the data collected and supports the study’s aim of contributing empirical evidence to the literature on sustainable fashion consumption in diverse cultural setting.
Data Processing and Analysis
	The data collected through closed-ended questionnaires were processed and analyzed using the R Project for Statistical Computing, often simply referred to as "R." (version R 4.3.1.) The responses were coded into R and categorized based on the study's objectives. Each objective necessitated different analytical approaches to uncover insights about the drivers of slow fashion purchase intentions among Nigerian fashion consumers. For the demographic features, a descriptive analysis was conducted to provide an understanding of the respondents' gender, age, educational level, and residential area. This initial analysis helped in understanding the composition of the sample population.
The third objective, which focused on evaluating the factors influencing purchase intention, was addressed using regression analysis. This statistical method helped in identifying the impact of various drivers on consumers' slow fashion purchase intention items. Additionally, the relationships between different variables, such as awareness, perceived value, and purchase intentions, were examined using correlation analysis. This approach enabled the study to determine the strength and directional relationships between these variables, providing a comprehensive understanding of the factors driving slow fashion purchase intentions.
Ethical Considerations	
Ethical integrity is the cornerstone of any research that involves human participants and this study seriously adhered to the established ethical principles in other to protect respondents’ rights and ensure the credibility of the research process. All respondents in this study were entirely voluntary, they were provided with a clear introduction outlining the purpose of the research, the nature of the questions and the intended use of their responses. This information allowed participants to make an informed decision about their involvement, this consistency with guidelines improved transparency and respect for autonomy. To protect respondents’ privacy no personally or identifiable information like their names, phone numbers or email addresses was targeted or collected. These measures are important for fostering honest and opinionated responses given the sensitivity surrounding environmental attitudes and ethical consumption (Tourangeau & Yan, 2007). Data handling procedures was in accordance with institutional and international standards for research data protection ensuring that information was used only for academic purposes and would not be shared with any third parties. The questionnaire was carefully designed to avoid language that could offend, distress or encourages responders bias, thus maintaining a neutral and respectful tone all through. Finally, to uphold academic integrity, all sources, scales and frameworks used in the study were properly cited and referenced to prevent plagiarism and ensure transparency of research process and result.
CHAPTER THREE

Results and Discussions
Analysis Model 
	To examine the relationships of these environmental factors, cultural orientation and consumers sustainable fashion purchase intentions, this study utilized a comprehensive multi-step analytical approach implemented in R studio. This analysis included descriptive statistics, correlation analysis, multiple regression modeling and moderation analysis with interaction terms.
First, descriptive statistics and correlation matrices were computed to check the basic relationships between main variables being environmental collective efficacy, environmental knowledge, collectivism and sustainable fashion purchase intention in the entire sample and then separately for each country. Pearson’s correlation coefficients gave the initial insight into the strength and direction of associations between the variables.
Secondly, multiple linear regression analyses were conducted to check the direct effects of the predictor variables ECE, EKN and COL on consumers’ sustainable fashion purchase intentions. These models included standardized coefficients (β) with significance levels (p-values) and model fit indices such as R-squared and adjusted R-squared to determine the explanatory power of predictors.
Finally, to examine the moderating role of collectivism, interaction terms (environmental collective efficacy × collectivism, environmental knowledge × collectivism) were fused into the regression models. The significant interaction effect was interpreted in other to understand how cultural orientation influences strength of relationships between environmental perceptions and purchase intentions.




Scale Refinement Tests

Reliability test
Even though the scales used for this study was adopted from previous research that measured the same constructs, a test of reliability was considered vital to ensure that the constructs are valid in accordance to this studies measurement of interest. The reliability tests indicated strong internal consistency throughout the key constructs under investigation. Environmental Collective Efficacy exhibited high reliability with α = 0.85, showing that the items genuinely measured respondents’ beliefs in collective environmental action. also, Purchase Intention showed very good reliability at α = 0.89, reflecting the stability of the scale in recording behavioral intentions towards sustainable fashion. Environmental Knowledge, though slightly below the conventional threshold at α = 0.69 was retained because of its alignment with exploratory research standards and the theoretical importance of maintain domain-specific items (understanding pollution causes) to preserve the content validity. Collectivism also had an acceptable reliability at α = 0.70, supporting the stability after two items were dropped due to poor correlation to the definition of collectivism measurement. These tests improved the adequacy of measures providing confidence in their use for the hypothesis testing.

Confirmatory factor analysis 
The Confirmatory Factor Analysis (CFA) was utilized to validate the structural integrity of hypothesized measurement model confirming that the observed variables (survey items) appropriately represented their basic latent constructs. The model demonstrated good fit as evident from the correlations between the latent variables, aligning with the theoretical expectations while been discriminately valid. All items loaded significantly in their appropriate constructs including the five items for Environmental Collective Efficacy (such as beliefs in community-driven environmental problem-solving) and four items for Environmental Knowledge (such as awareness of pollution causes). Collectivism and Purchase Intention showed strong factor loading too, with the latter maintaining all five. These results reinforced the durability of the constructs and their theoretical distinctiveness, which supported their use in subsequent analyses. Overall, the CFA test confirms the validity of the measurement tools ensuring that the latent variables surely represent the theoretical concept of research interest.
Harman factor test
Harman’s Single factor Test was also conducted to check the risk of common method bias, a potential research concern in self-reported survey data. The results informed that a single factor explained 44.2% of the total variance which falls below the critical threshold of 50% suggesting that common method bias did not considerable distort the findings. This outcome strengthens confidence in validity of the observed relationships as the variance attributable to a single latent factor was did not dominate. All items across constructs were included in the analysis with no single factor emerging as being disproportionately influential. While the test does not entirely eliminate the possibility of method bias, it still supports the durability of the data by showing that respondents’ answers were not systematically skewed by a uniform response pattern which often arise because respondents didn’t pay attention to questions in survey. These results are in line with the reliability and validity tests that reinforce the integrity of measurement models and the plausibility of hypothesized relationships. The moderate variance explained by a single factor (44.2%) also highlights the multidimensionality of constructs reflecting their theoretical specialty in explaining sustainable fashion demand.
Multi-Group test
To examine cultural differences between the two countries (RQ4) the study applied a subgroup analysis using two methodological approaches. First, country specific regression models were estimated by dividing the dataset into Russian and Nigerian subgroups. These models tested the hypothesized relationships separately for each country, while controlling for demographic variables which are gender, age and educational level. Secondly, interaction terms (efficacy, country, knowledge country) were added to pooled regression models using the full dataset to explicitly evaluate whether the effects of the predictors on purchase intention were different by country. The interaction terms tested moderation effects of cultural context on the focal relationships. While formal multi-group structural equation modeling (SEM) was not conducted, these regression-based strategies provided a pragmatic means to check for cultural variations. The analysis did not include measurement invariance testing which precludes claims about cross-cultural equivalence of constructs but rather focused instead on comparing the effect of sizes and demographic associations across the subgroups. This approach prioritized the identification of context specific patterns in the interplay between variables.

RQ1: How does environmental collective efficacy influence sustainable fashion purchase intentions in Russia and Nigeria?
Environmental collective efficacy has a significant positive effect on sustainable fashion purchase intentions in both countries, this supports H1. But the effect is stronger in Nigeria (β = 0.71, *p* < 0.001) than in Russia (β = 0.63, *p* < 0.001) suggesting that Nigerian consumers are more influenced by their belief in collective environmental action when making sustainable fashion purchase decisions. The stronger effect in Nigeria is ascribed to the country’s more collectivistic culture where belief in community action could be more salient in consumer decision-making. This finding emphasizes the importance of community-based marketing approaches for sustainable fashion in Nigeria, while Russian consumers also show strong reliance on collective efficacy, though slightly less pronounced than Nigeria.
RQ2: What is the relationship between environmental knowledge and sustainable fashion purchase intentions in these two cultural spheres?
Environmental knowledge had a significant positive effect on sustainable fashion purchase intentions in both Russia (β = 0.56, *p* < 0.001) and Nigeria (β = 0.70, *p* < 0.001). This fully supports H2. This result suggests that both Russian and Nigerian consumers translate their environmental knowledge to sustainable fashion purchases, though the effect is stronger in Nigeria. Nigerian consumers' sustainable purchase intentions are now equally driven by knowledge and cultural values, challenging earlier assumptions. For marketers, this means that educational promotions about environmental issues are critical in both markets, while community-based awareness remains vital in Nigeria.
RQ3: How does collectivism moderate the relationship between environmental collective efficacy and sustainable fashion purchase intentions?
Collectivism positively moderated the relationship between environmental collective efficacy and purchase intentions in Nigeria (β = 0.18, *p* < 0.05) but not in Russia (β = 0.08, *p* = 0.371). This partially supports H4a. The significant moderation effect in Nigeria means that Nigerian consumers with stronger collectivist values show even stronger relationship between environmental collective efficacy and purchase intentions. This boosting effect suggests that for highly collectivistic Nigerian consumers, their belief in community environmental action becomes an even stronger driver of sustainable fashion purchases. Such moderation was not seen in Russia, suggesting that collectivism does not strengthen the impact of environmental collective efficacy on sustainable fashion purchase intentions among Russians. H4b was not supported in either country meaning that collectivism does not moderate the relationship between environmental knowledge and purchase intentions.
RQ4: Are there differences in the strength of the relationship between attitudes and purchase intentions between Nigeria and Russia and if so can these differences be attributed to cultural factors such as collectivism?
Notable differences were found in the strength of relationships between attitudes and purchase intentions in the two countries:
Environmental collective efficacy has a stronger effect on purchase intentions in Nigeria (β = 0.71) compared to Russia (β = 0.63).

Environmental knowledge significantly affects purchase intentions in both Russia (β = 0.56) and Nigeria (β = 0.70).

Collectivism directly influences purchase intentions in Nigeria (β = 0.31) but not in Russia (β = 0.12).
These differences are in line with the cultural profiles of the two countries. Nigeria scored higher on collectivism measures (Mean = 3.68) compared to Russia (Mean = 3.25) consistent with the broader cultural characterizations of Nigeria as a more collectivistic society and Russia as a more individualistic one. The stronger effect of collectivism on purchase intentions in Nigeria and its moderation of the environmental collective efficacy-purchase intention relationship suggest that cultural factors explain these cross-national differences in sustainable fashion purchase drivers.
Discussion of Findings 
The results of this study provided vital insights on the sustainable fashion purchase intentions (SFPI) drivers in Nigeria and Russia in respect to with cultural ideology. Environmental collective efficacy (ECE) surfaced as a significant predictor of SFPI in both countries, though its influence was comparatively stronger in Nigeria (β = 0.71, *p* < 0.001) when compared to that of Russia (β = 0.63**, *p* < 0.001). This is confirmation aligns with Bandura’s (2000) collective efficacy theory which proposed that group-oriented belief boosts pro-environmental actions in a collectivist societies. Nigeria’s collectivist culture, according to Hofstede (2010) creates shared responsibility for sustainability where community driven initiatives (such as cloth swaps and eco-fashion collectives) reinforce perceived impact of collective actions (Tsyganash et al., 2024). But In contrast, Russia’s hybrid cultural orientation which balances individualism with a residual of collectivism still reflects strong reliance on collective efficacy, thus reflecting findings reported by Khitous et al. (2022), who noted that urban Russian consumers balance personal agency with collective norms.
Environmental knowledge (EK) exhibited a convergent effect in both countries. In Russia, EK strongly predicted SFPI (β = 0.56, *p* < 0.001), which is consistent with Ajzen’s (1991) Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB) where informed attitudes culminate to intentional behavior. In Nigeria, EK also significantly predicted SFPI (β = 0.70, *p* < 0.001), aligning with Niinimäki’s (2022) observations of knowledge-driven sustainability even in collectivist markets. This dual significance challenges earlier assumptions made by Joung and Park-Poaps (2013) who linked knowledge to willingness to pay premiums primarily in individualistic contexts. The stronger EK effect in Nigeria suggests that structural barriers noted by Adeyemi (2023), such as limited awareness, may be mitigated by rising environmental literacy and access to sustainable products.
Collectivism (COL) directly influenced SFPI in Nigeria (β = 0.31, *p* < 0.001) but not in Russia (β = 0.12, *p* = 0.171), this corroborates Hofstede’s (2010) cultural typology. Nigerian consumers, embedded in communal traditions perceive sustainable purchases as an expression of their social identity and moral duty, and this resonates with Lee’s (2017) findings in collectivist Asian markets. Russia’s weaker collectivist orientation, regardless of their localized communal traditions is a hint on the dominance of individualistic values in shaping consumption which was also observed by Kudryavtseva and Kulikova (2024) in Moscow’s fashion landscape.
The moderating role of collectivism highlighted the cultural divergence further. In Nigeria, collectivism boosted the ECE-SFPI relationship (β = 0.18, *p* < 0.05), confirming Sharma and Aswal’s (2017) conclusion that group-oriented values amplify and strengthen the translation of collective beliefs to action. This effect was absent in Russia, thus aligning Russia with Gupta and Ogden’s (2009) assertion that individualistic societies place more importance on personal efficacy over communal influence. Importantly, collectivism did not moderate EK’s impact in either of the country and this challenges the assumptions by Shin and Koh (2016) who reported cultural moderation in ethical consumption.
CHAPTER FOUR

Conclusion and Discussion

Theoretical Implications of Study
	This study advances the theoretical understanding of sustainable fashion consumption by consolidating environmental collective efficacy, environmental knowledge and cultural values, particularly collectivism within a sturdy cross-cultural framework. The investigation of these constructs in socio-cultural structure of Nigeria and Russia provides insights into the numerous drivers of sustainable fashion purchase intention, enriching the literature on sustainable consumer behavior in emerging and transforming markets.
First, the findings reaffirm the vital role of environmental concern as an important antecedent of sustainable fashion purchase intention, corroborating previous empirical evidences that positions eco-consciousness as a base motivator for pro-environmental consumer behavior (Kim & Lee, 2023; Biswas & Roy, 2015). By empirically validating the relationship in both Nigerian and Russian sphere, this study has extended the generalizability of environmental concern across a heterogeneous population, highlighting its worldwide relevance in shaping sustainable consumption.
Secondly, the addition of collectivism as a moderating cultural dimension offers critical theoretical contributions by demonstrating how societal norms and group-oriented values influence the translation of environmental beliefs into behavioral intentions. In consistency with Hofstede’s cultural dimension theory (Hofstede, Hofstede, & Minkov, 2010), this study revealed that Nigeria’s collectivist orientation increased the influence of social and community expectations on sustainable fashion purchase intentions. This also conform with researches hinting that collectivist cultures prioritize social harmony and conformity, which intensifies the impact of subjective norms on eco-friendly behaviors (Lee, 2017). Russia more individualistic and hybrid cultural domain, personal attitudes and behavioral control came out as stronger predictors of purchase intention, reflecting a greater emphasis on individual agency and self-efficacy (Triandis, 1995). These findings also contribute to the theoretical discourse by demonstrating that cultural constructs not only shapes consumer values but also moderates the strength and pathways via which environmental cognitions influence sustainable fashion consumption.
Thirdly, the study also pointed out environmental knowledge as a vital cognitive antecedent that enhances consumers efficacy and confidence in making sustainable fashion choices. This supports the knowledge-attitude-behavior framework which posits that increased environmental awareness facilitates responsible consumption by impinging on attitudes and empowering action (Hwang, Jo, & Oh, 2020; Smith & Jones; Barbarossa & De Pelsmacker, 2016). Notably, the difference in effects observed between Nigeria and Russia suggests that the efficacy of environmental knowledge is amplified by socio-cultural factors, with collectivist contexts enhancing its impact.
And finally, the findings challenge models that rely solely on individual level predictors such as attitudes or environmental concern, advocating instead for a more holistic approach that factors in the changing relationship between social influence, individual cognition and cultural values (Schwartz, 1994; Ajzen, 1991). By showing that environmental concern, knowledge and collectivism are interrelated drivers whose relativity varies by cultural structure, the study enriches the cross-cultural consumer behavior literature and provides a good foundation for developing culturally structured interventions aimed towards the global adoption of sustainable fashion.
Implication for Stakeholders 
	 The findings of this study offer valuable hints for practitioners, marketers and policymakers seeking to promote sustainable fashion consumption in these countries. These implications highlight the importance of culturally structured strategies that consider the unique social and cultural values of each country and geared towards. Improving quality of health and life by reducing the obvious adverse effects of non-pro-environmental consumption practices.
Tailoring Marketing Strategies to Cultural structure
In Nigeria (mostly collectivist culture): The significant influence of social norms and community-level factors suggests marketing endeavors should utilize social influence and communal engagements. Initiating campaigns that highlights peer endorsements, community leaders’ support and social approval can improve consumers' intention to purchase sustainable fashion. Organizing community events or ambassador programs that feature local influencers can also tap into the collectivist mindset of Nigerian and create a sense of shared responsibility towards environmental sustainability. In Russia (Mostly Individualist): seeing the propensity of personal attitudes and perceived efficacy, marketing efforts could focus on educating the consumers on personal benefits and the impact of sustainable fashion. Strategies such as highlighting individual environmental impact, providing information about the sustainability credentials of products and also showcasing personal values of responsibility can better outcomes. Providing detailed information and transparency can empower Russian consumers to make sentimentally friendly choices that is also in-line with their personal beliefs.
Enhancing Consumers Environmental Knowledge
To further increase consumers’ environmental knowledge and drive sustainable fashion consumption in both Nigeria and Russia stakeholders should implement culturally tailored multiple channel educational strategies that reduce the gap between awareness and actual action. In Nigeria, policymakers and fashion brands could collaborate with local influencers and non-governmental organizations to structure social media campaigns using platforms such as TikTok and Instagram where short videos and infographics illustrate the ecological benefits of traditional practices, stressing water conservation, reduced chemical use and the carbon footprint advantages of traditional practices like adire dyeing compared to fast fashion. Retailers might also inculcate QR codes into their clothing tags, linking to interactive contents that explains the lifecycle environmental impact of fashion wears, while government agencies could partner with vocational schools to develop strategic curricula that teaches sustainable textile techniques, like organic cotton farming and natural dye extraction to artisans in regions like Kano and Abeokuta which is the tie and dye textile hub in Nigeria. In Russia, fashion companies could conceptualize in-store educational hubs with augmented reality (AR) tools that allow customers to visualize the carbon footprint reduction they could by choosing recycled or recyclable materials. Collaborations with eco-bloggers on platforms such as VKontakte could also help demystify sustainable fashion via webinars on topics like circular design while addressing the gap between knowledge and action through gamified challenges. Stakeholders can also lobby the Ministry of Industry and Trade to fund public service announcements featuring local celebrities promoting certified organic textiles alongside partnerships with museums to host exhibitions contrasting the environmental toll of synthetic fabrics. NGOs in both countries could organize community workshops, for example, in Lagos and Moscow where experts teach upcycling techniques for second-hand clothing, changing the perception of second-hand clothes known as okrika in Nigeria and thrifted garments from “discarded” to “sustainable.” Brands can further incentivize knowledge intake by giving loyalty points or discounts to customers who complete sustainability quizzes in their websites, encouraging learning via gamification. By adding environmental literacy into digital, retail and communal spaces and aligning messages with cultural values like Nigeria’s communal heritage and Russia’s tech-driven individualism, stakeholders can disciple misconceptions, build consumers’ confidence and achieve long-term shifts towards responsible consumption that is in line with SDG 12.
Designing Culturally Sensitive Promotions
To design culturally conforming promotional campaigns that is in line with the SDG 12, stakeholders in Nigeria and Russia should structure messages that reflect deeply implanted cultural values and behavioral triggers. In Nigeria, marketers could use communal narratives that frame sustainable fashion as an ingredient for communal well-being. Promotional campaigns could show families wearing aso-oke or adire attire while quantifying environmental benefits (e.g. Adire saves 10,000L of water per garment). Partnerships with local influencers like Nollywood (Nigerian movie industry) celebrities can boost social approval by showing sustainable fashion as a social. Radio jingles in local languages (Yoruba, Hausa etc.) can highlight how eco-friendly choice enriches community health and global ecosystems. In Russia, campaigns could be used to appeal to personal empowerment and individual agency, brands can use personalization apps that calculate customer carbon footprint when buying recycled garments, combined with slogans like “Your Choice, Your Planet”. Campaigns may also highlight individuals’ success stories like choosing capsule wardrobes to reduce waste, using platforms like Yandex.Zen to target tech-savvy urban residents. Retailers can also introduce loyalty programs that reward customers for sharing their sustainable purchases on VKontakte, uniting personal achievement to environmental stewardship while creating more awareness. To navigate the cultural context, Nigerian campaigns can include proverbs or folklore (“A tree does not stand alone in the forest”) to wake communal interest, while Russian messages could add metaphors of personal mastery (“Be the hero of your sustainability journey”) that echoes with individualistic aspirations. By combining promotional language, imagery and channels within these cultural codes, stakeholders can change sustainable fashion from a global mandate to a locally meaningful practice that engagement which surpasses transactional consumption and imbues sustainability into all societal identity.
Product Development and Transparency
To further maximize the effect of campaigns geared towards sustainability in Nigeria and Russia, promotional strategies should inculcate cultural values into their main messages. In Nigeria, marketers should structure anecdotes that tie sustainable fashion to communal identity, campaigns could feature families participating in “community cloth swaps” where exchanging pre-used clothes becomes a ritual of shared responsibility. By using Nigeria’s colorful traditions, radio dramas could structure stories around villagers advocating for fashion sustainable attires as a means to preserve the ancestral lands from chemical pollution, linking eco-conscious choices to acts of cultural preservation. Media influencers can also partner with local organizations to improve visibility of sustainable fashion in major markets such as the famous Onitsha main market where purchasing eco-friendly textiles will be celebrated as a collective achievement.
Campaigns in Russia can inculcate individualism into their promotional initiatives using platforms like Yandex.Direct in delivering personalized adverts that showcases a consumer carbon reduction behavior. Brands can also partner with apps like Strava to incentivize users who pairs sustainable fashion purchases with personal achievements, combining environmental efficacy with self-improvement. limited-edition fashion collections can feature QR-code-linked certificate with items carbon footprint, thus appealing to the Russian value of lichnyy rost (personal growth). Both countries can definitely benefit from cultural relative metaphor initiatives. Nigerian billboards might depict sustainable fashion as a baobab tree, which symbolizes communal strength and endurance, while Moscow subway ads can liken eco-choices to matryoshka dolls, where each sustainable purchase “unlocks” a smaller environmental footprint. 
Limitations and Future Research Directions	
This study provides important insights into the factors driving sustainable fashion purchase intentions in Russia and Nigeria. However, several limitations must be acknowledged and avenues for future research will be highlighted. A primary limitation concerns is the study’s reliance on online data gathering methods which while practical for reaching geographically dispersed populations might have excluded individuals with limited or no internet access or with lower digital literacy. This restrain potentially limits the representativeness of the sample size especially in Nigeria where internet penetration and digital skills vary significantly in regards to regions and socio-economic groups. Future research should consider adding offline data collection methods or utilize a hybrid approaches to get a wider range of consumers including those in rural areas or less digitally connected communities.
Another limitation is the use of self-reported measures of construct which are prone to biases such as recall inaccuracies and social desirability. participants might overstate their environmental knowledge, collective efficacy beliefs or purchase intentions just to align with what they fell is the socially accepted norms, thus, inflating the observed relationships between the variables (Tourangeau & Yan, 2007). To counter this, future studies could utilize longitudinal designs to track the changes in consumer behavior over time to provide a robust understanding of intention-behavior gap often characteristic of sustainability research (Vermeir & Verbeke, 2006).
Though this study’s focus was on environmental collective efficacy, environmental knowledge and collectivism as main predictors of sustainable fashion purchase intention, it did not explore other possibly influential antecedents such as trust in sustainable brands, social identity, perceived value or other moral norms that might also constitute major influence to sustainable fashion purchase decisions. Demographic moderators like income and marital status were not also examined and they could also be a strong driver. Future research should combine these variables to better capture consumer heterogeneity and identify subgroups with distinct motivations and barriers to sustainable fashion consumption (Vermeir & Verbeke, 2006; Joshi & Rahman, 2015).
And finally, comparative cross-cultural study using consistent methodologies not just in Russia and Nigeria but other emerging economies is needed to further the understanding of how cultural constructs such as collectivism interact with socio-economic factors, religion and other social identities in shaping sustainable fashion consumption. Intersectional analyses can also provide major insight into consumers diversity and inform the design of effective and culturally sensitive sustainability strategies (Hofstede et al., 2010; Joshi & Rahman, 2019).
