Abstract 

Drosophila melanogaster serves as an excellent model for studying tissue-

specific gene transcription. In this study, we aimed to identify and characterize 

regulatory DNA elements controlling tissue-specific transcription in Drosophila 

larvae using whole-genome sequencing data. RNA-Seq, ChIP-Seq, FAIRE-Seq, 

and  ATAC-Seq  were  employed  to  identify  tissue-specific  transcripts,  map 

protein-DNA binding profiles, and analyze chromatin accessibility in wandering 

larvae. We focused on two major classes of regulatory elements: enhancers and 

promoters. Tissue-specific enhancers were identified in the salivary glands and 

brain using ATAC-Seq. The presence of enhancer proteins, such as H3K27Ac, 

CBP/p300,  and  EcR,  confirmed  their  enhancer  nature.  Additionally,  these 

markers  exhibited  tissue-specific  enrichment  at  the  defined  sets  of  enhancers, 

indicating that the regulatory elements function only in the target tissue and they 

are not "poised" in non-target tissues.  These enhancers showed enrichment of 

tissue-specific transcription factor motifs. In parallel, hundreds of tissue-specific 

promoters were identified both for salivary glands and brain of wandering larvae. 

Tissue-specific  promoters  were  found  enriched  for  TATA-box  motif.    Unlike 

stable gene promoters, which function consistently across tissues, tissue-specific 

promoters showed selective recruitment of RNA polymerase II subunit Rpb3 in 

the tissue where they are active. Notably, tissue-specific enhancers and promoters 

exhibited distinct transcription factor motif enrichment, indicating independent 

regulatory programs controlling tissue-specific transcription. Furthermore, genes 

regulated  by  tissue-specific  promoters  showed  minimal  overlap  with  those 

controlled  by  tissue-specific  enhancers,  revealing  two  separate  mechanisms 

underlying  tissue-specific  transcription.  The  task  of  future  research  is  to 

determine why different groups of Drosophila genes utilize distinct approaches 

to achieve tissue-specific transcription.

Introduction 

Object of the study: The object of this study is Drosophila melanogaster. 

This  organism  serves  as  an  excellent  model  for  studying  tissue  specific  gene 

transcription.  During  metamorphosis  Drosophila  tissues  undergo  distinct 

developmental  programs:  salivary  glands  experience  autophagy  and  apoptosis 

[1], larval  brain tissue  goes through  neurogenesis,  axon  and  dendritic  pruning, 

arbor outgrowth and cell death that result in the establishment of the adult nervous 

system [2], wing imaginal discs differentiate into adult wings and carcass [3].  

Aim of the study: The aim of this study was to identify regulatory elements 

of  DNA  that  control  the  programs  of  tissue-specific  genes  transcription  in 

Drosophila larvae using whole-genome sequencing data. In addition, we aimed 

to characterize the features of these regulatory elements by analyzing the binding 

levels of transcriptional proteins and motif enrichment.  

Methodology: To achieve these objectives, we utilized the sequencing data 

generated  in  our  laboratory.  RNA-Seq  from  wandering  larvae  and  12-14-hour 

pupae  was  employed  to  identify  target  transcripts,  ChIP-Seq  from  wandering 

larvae  was  used  to  map  profiles  of  protein  binding  to  DNA,  FAIRE-Seq  and 

ATAC-Seq from wandering larvae was utilized to build the profiles of accessible 

chromatin.  The  obtained  sequence  reads  were  preprocessed  using  standard 

pipelines and analyzed with complex bioinformatics approaches to characterize 

features of regulatory elements.  

Since the major regulatory elements controlling transcription are enhancers 

and  promoters,  the  work  was  divided  into  two  major  parts  with  the  aim  of 

identifying and characterizing tissue-specific elements belonging to each of the 

regulatory classes. 

Results: 

(1) Our analysis identified pools of tissue-specific enhancers in the salivary 

glands  and  brain  at  the  wandering  larval  stage.  Contrary  to  previous  findings 

8 

 
suggesting that enhancers are common to all tissues at a particular developmental 

stage [4], our study showed that tissues with distinct developmental fates have a 

number  of  different  enhancers.   Defined  salivary  gland-  and  brain-specific 

enhancers showed tissue-specific enrichment in H3K27Ac, CBP/p300 and EcR 

proving  their  enhancer  nature.  The  activity  of  these  tissue-specific  sites  was 

proposed  to  be  driven  by  selective  transcription  factor  binding.  Distinct 

transcription factor motifs were found enriched within tissue-specific enhancers, 

corresponding to transcription factors predominantly expressed in the respective 

tissues. 

(2)  RNA-Seq  data  from  wandering  larvae  let  us  to  identify  hundreds  of 

salivary  gland-  and  brain-specific  promoters  involved  in  tissue-specific 

regulatory  pathways.  Tissue-specific  promoters  were  found  enriched  with  the 

TATA-box motif, a hallmark of tissue-specific regulation. In contrast, promoters 

of stable genes - active across tissues - exhibited features of housekeeping genes, 

such as enrichment for Dref, Ohler 1, and Ohler 6 motifs. Notably, we observed 

tissue-specific  binding  of  tissue-specific  promoters  with  RNA  polymerase  II 

subunit  Rpb3,  demonstrating  that  these  classes  of  regulatory  elements  are  not 

maintained  in  a  paused  state  in  tissues  where  they  are  inactive.  The  identified 

tissue-specific  promoters  were  found  to  be  enriched  for  transcription  factor 

binding motifs that differ from those found in tissue-specific enhancers. 

Conclusion: This study demonstrates that tissue-specific transcription in 

Drosophila  wandering  larva  can  be  carried  out  by  different  mechanisms  in 

different groups of genes. At one group, tissue-specific transcription is enabled 

by tissue-specific enhancers, which are active only at the target tissue. Notably, 

promoters of this genes group do not work tissue-specifically. They recruit TFIID 

and  RNA  polymerase  II  even  in  tissues  where  they  are  inactive.  In  contrast, 

another  group  of  genes  is  regulated  by  promoters  that  act  in  a  tissue-specific 

manner (demonstrate tissue-specific recruitment of TFIID and RNA polymerase 

II).  Interestingly,  the  identified  tissue-specific  promoters  and  enhancers  were 

9 

 
  
found  to  be  enriched  in  binding  motifs  for  different  transcription  factors, 

demonstrating the existence of completely different programs controlling tissue-

specific transcription in Drosophila larvae.

Literature Review 

1.  Drosophila  melanogaster as  a  Model  to  study  Tissue-Specific 

Transcription 

Drosophila  melanogaster  is  an  excellent  model  to  study  genetics, 

development, and gene regulation. Its compact genome and short  development 

time  make  it  an  ideal  system  for  dissecting  complex  biological  processes, 

including  tissue-specific  transcription.  The  fly’s  developmental  stages  - 

embryonic, larval, pupal, and adult - are well-characterized [5], with each phase 

exhibiting  precise  spatiotemporal  gene  expression  patterns  that  drive  tissue 

differentiation and organogenesis [6] (Figure 1). For instance, larval tissues such 

as salivary glands, brain, and wing imaginal discs are interesting to study because 

they  undergo  distinct  developmental  programs.  For  instance,  salivary  glands 

degrade via autophagy and apoptosis during metamorphosis [7]. Larval neurons 

in CNS remodel synapses during metamorphosis (axon pruning and arborization) 

[2]. Furthermore, two waves of apoptosis (early pupa and adult emergence) sculpt 

the CNS (programmed cell death in CNS) [3]. Finally, adult neurogenesis takes 

place for brain tissue [8, 9]. Wing imaginal discs take part in such developmental 

processes  as  patterning  [10]  and  metamorphic  differentiation  (discs  evert  and 

differentiate into adult wings/body wall during pupation) [11]. These processes 

are controlled by tissue-specific DNA regulatory elements, making Drosophila a 

powerful  model  to  study  how  gene  expression  is  controlled  by  these  tissue-

specific regions.

2. General Mechanisms of Transcriptional Regulation in Eukaryotes 

In  the  current  research  we  investigated  the  fundamental  transcriptional 

mechanisms  underlying  tissue-specific  gene  transcription.  In  eukaryotes,  gene 

expression  is  controlled  by  a  complex  interplay  of  cis-regulatory  elements 

(enhancers, promoters) and trans-acting factors (transcription factors, chromatin 

remodelers). Enhancers are distal regulatory elements that activate transcription 

via transcription factor binding, often in a tissue-specific manner [13]. Promoters 

are proximal regions initiating transcription, containing core motifs (TATA-box, 

Initiator  (Inr)) for  RNA  polymerase II  recruitment  [14].  Pol  II assembles  with 

general  transcription  factors  (GTFs)  at  promoters,  forming  the  pre-initiation 

complex  (PIC).  Critical to  this  process  is TFIID,  a  multi-subunit  complex  that 

includes TATA-binding  protein  (TBP),  which  recognizes  the  TATA-box  and 

bends DNA to facilitate PIC assembly [15].  

Genome wide chromatin accessibility (e.g., via ATAC-Seq) marks active 

regulatory  elements  such  as  promoters  and  enhancers  [16].  Additionally, 

enhancers and promoters are marked by histone modifications and coactivators. 

12 

 
 
For instance, H3K27Ac marks active enhancers and promoters and distinguishes 

poised versus activated elements [17]; CBP/p300 acetylate histones and bridge 

transcription factors to the transcriptional machinery [18]. 

To study the tissue-specific gene regulation in this research, we first needed 

to identify the regions of interest. Dividing drosophila genes into tissue-specific 

and broadly expressed sets was challenging but we managed to do this using the 

previously published scientific concepts.  

The concept of tissue-specific and housekeeping gene regulation in Drosophila 

The  genome  organization  of  Drosophila  melanogaster  provides  a  good 

model for understanding the relationship between chromatin structure and gene 

activity. Drosophila polytene chromosomes in salivary glands exhibit a distinct 

and reproducible banding pattern. Polytene chromosomes have densely packed 

dark bands and less condensed interbands that reflect differences in chromatin 

organization, gene content, and functional activity. It was shown that interbands 

predominantly contain the 5′ ends of housekeeping genes, which are ubiquitously 

expressed  across  various tissues and  developmental  stages [19].  These  regions 

are characterized by open chromatin, enrichment of transcription-related proteins, 

active  histone  modifications,  and  replication  origins.  In  contrast,  dense  black 

bands  are  associated  with  tissue-specific  genes  that  exhibit restricted  temporal 

and spatial expression [20]. Interestingly, that tissue-specific and developmental 

gene regulation is proposed to differ from housekeeping gene regulation.  

The  activity  of  individual  enhancers  and  enhancer  pairs  in  relation  to 

housekeeping  and  developmental  core  promoters  was  investigated  using  the 

STARR-Seq method [21] (Figure 2A). The findings reveal that housekeeping and 

developmental enhancers possess intrinsic properties that operate independently 

of the core promoters they regulate. These enhancers exhibit different modes of 

cooperativity:  housekeeping  enhancers  demonstrate  an  additive  mode,  while 

developmental enhancers function in a super-additive manner (Figure 2B).

enhancers.  Different  types  of  enhancers  were  also  described  using  chromatin 

accessibility profiling.

Chromatin accessibility profiling is well-suited for comparing how transcription 

factors  and  regulatory  proteins  interpret  the  genome  across  different  tissues, 

independent of the specific identity of those factors. For establishing the specific 

binding sites of particular proteins bound to the accessible DNA the ChIP-Seq 

method can be used [26].  

Accessible  chromatin  regions  of  Drosophila  have  been  investigated  by 

many scientists. One of the most remarkable research projects in this field was 

performed  by  scientists  Daniel  J.  McKay  and  Jason  D.  Lieb  in  2013  [4].  For 

mapping ‘open’ chromatin they took Drosophila thoracic appendages (wing, leg 

and haltere imaginal discs) that give rise to different morphological structures in 

adult fly. The main idea of this work was that profiles of accessible chromatin 

that correlate with enhancer activity are nearly identical across the imaginal disc 

16 

 
 
tissues  at  a  given  developmental  stage  except  the  Ubx  locus.  The  changes  in 

‘open’ chromatin landscape appear only over the time of development (Figure 5).

Approximately  the  same  results  were  obtained  in  research  made  on 

Drosophila  cell  lineages  [27].  It  was  asserted  that  the  same  DNA  regions  are 

accessible  in  different  tissues  at  the  larval  developmental  stage.  And  tissue-

specific transcription is realized not through these accessible enhancers, but due 

to  the  fact  that  they  are  associated  with  different  sets  of  transcription  factors 

(which makes them active in one tissue bur not in another tissue). Additionally, 

they discovered that the process of cellular differentiation is primarily driven by 

a transcriptional program that is shared among various tissues. 

Overall, these results underscore that chromatin accessibility profiles are 

shared  across  the  tissues  at  one  developmental  stage  and  change  only  during 

developmental time. That indicates that different tissues possess same enhancers 

in active state at one stage of development. Understanding the interplay between 

enhancers and their associated core promoters is important for understanding the 

17 

 
 
entire mechanism of such regulation. Notably, that promotors of housekeeping, 

tissue-specific and developmental genes are different. What are the specific traits 

of different genes core promoters? 

4. Core Promoters of Tissue-specific and Broadly Expressed Genes 

Housekeeping  and  developmental  gene  transcription  is  released  through 

the activation of distinct types of core promoters. Core promoters in metazoa are 

categorized  into  three  types  based  on  initiation  patterns,  sequence  motifs, 

chromatin configuration, and gene function. Actively transcribed tissue-specific 

genes  have  promoters  with  sharp  initiation  with  imprecisely  positioned 

nucleosomes.  These  promoters  contain  TATA-box  and  initiator  (Inr)  motifs. 

Sharp  initiation  promoters  are  active  in  differentiated  cells  and  marked  by 

H3K4me3 and H3K27ac, which indicate active transcription. Housekeeping gene 

promoters are characterized by dispersed initiation, nucleosome-depleted regions 

flanked by positioned nucleosomes, and marked by H3K4me3 and H3K27ac. In 

flies these promoters include motifs like Ohler 1, Ohler 6, and DRE [28]. The 

third  group  is  developmental  gene  promoters  marked  with  H3K4me3  and 

H3K27me3 in embryonic stem cells. In flies, they contain DPE motif and have 

focused initiation [29] (Figure 6).  

18

Furthermore,  using  in  vitro  DNA  affinity  purification  in  Drosophila  S2 

cells  it  was  shown  that  functionally  distinct  promoters  bind  different  proteins 

[30]. Core promoters that regulate tissue-specific genes and contain TATA-box 

motif were enriched for the canonical RNA Pol II preinitiation complex (PIC), 

including TBP, GTFs and TFIID, most Mediator subunits. DRE-containing core 

promoters  (that  regulate  housekeeping  genes)  were  enriched  for  the  core 

promoter-element binding factor DREF, BEAF-32, and Ibf1/2 (Figure 7). These 

findings highlight the diversity in promoter architecture and the corresponding 

recruitment of distinct regulatory complexes at tissue-specific and housekeeping 

(broadly expressed) genes.

As an example of differential protein recruitment to promoters of tissue-

specific and housekeeping genes, we can describe RNA Polymerase II (Pol II) 

recruitment  and  stalling  that  play  crucial  role  in  gene  transcription  regulation 

during  development.  Pol  II  recruitment  is  traditionally  considered  the  rate-

limiting factor in transcription activation. Recently three distinct Pol II binding 

behaviours  were  identified  using  Drosophila  embryos:  active  binding  across 

transcription units, no binding, and stalled binding at transcription start sites [31]. 

Stalling Pol II is highly enriched only at 10% of genes that are responsible for 

developmental control. That is needed to enable rapid and synchronous activation 

of developmental genes during temporal stages. Further analysis of stalled Pol II 

reveals that during muscle differentiation in Drosophila embryos, stalled Pol II is 

recruited de novo at many genes stage-specifically rather than tissue-specifically, 

whereas  Polycomb  group-mediated  repression  is  highly  tissue-specific  [32] 

(Figure 8).

3.  Proteins  associated  with  Drosophila  regulatory  elements  and  their 
functions 

Different  transcription  factors  are  associated  with  DNA  regulatory 

elements. Their coordinative operating is responsible for activating or repressing 

gene  transcription.  In  this  study  ChIP-Seq  analysis  for  some  of  the  known 

Drosophila  transcription-regulation  proteins  was  performed.  In  this  review 

chapter the description of these proteins is provided.  

Ecdysone receptor EcR 

The ecdysone  receptor  (EcR) is  a  nuclear  hormone  receptor  that  plays  a 

crucial role in Drosophila development by mediating responses to its ligand - the 

steroid hormone ecdysone (20-hydroxyecdysone). EcR forms a heterodimer with 

Ultraspiracle  (USP)  and  binds  to  ecdysone  response  elements  (EcREs)  in 

regulatory regions of target genes, modulating their transcription [34].  EcR is 

essential  for  metamorphosis,  tissue  remodelling,  and  stage-specific  gene 

expression  [35].  Nonetheless,  EcR  role  in  tissue-specific  gene  regulation  is 

controversial. Thus, one group of scientists showed that EcR knockdown did not 

reduce the chromatin accessibility in wing imaginal discs [36], another research 

revealed that in  condition  with  low  ligand  concentration,  binding  of  EcR  with 

salivary  gland’s  regulatory  elements  was  reduced  as  well  as  the  chromatin 

accessibility [37] (Figure 9).

In  this  research,  EcR  was  found  enriched  at tissue-specific  enhancers,

Sage 

Sage is  a  transcription  factor  (TF)  specifically  expressed  in  the salivary 

glands of Drosophila larvae  [38].  It  regulates  genes  involved  in  secretory 

function and gland development. Sage binds to enhancers driving salivary gland-

specific gene expression, often co-localizing with other TFs like Fork head (Fkh) 

23 

 
 
[39].  In  this  study,  Sage  was  identified  as  a  key  regulator  of salivary  gland 

enhancers,  with  its  binding  sites  enriched  near  tissue-specific  open  chromatin 

regions marked by H3K27Ac and CBP/p300. 

Combgap 

Combgap is a zinc finger protein that regulates neuronal development and 

brain-specific gene expression in Drosophila. It acts as a transcriptional repressor 

or  activator  depending  on  the  context  and  is  involved  in  maintaining  neural 

progenitor identity [40].  For instance, Cg functions as a tissue-specific repressor 

of Wg signaling in the visual cortex of CNS, where its downregulation by Wg 

permits  target  gene  activation  [41].  In  this  study, Combgap  binding  sites  were 

enriched  in  brain-specific  enhancers,  suggesting  its  role  in  establishing  brain-

specific  transcriptional  programs.  Its  presence  alongside  H3K27Ac  and  EcR 

marks indicates a combinatorial regulatory mechanism in neural tissues. 

Cropped (Crp) and BEAF-32 

Cropped (Crp) is a transcription factor associated with housekeeping gene 

regulation in Drosophila. It binds to promoters of constitutively expressed genes, 

often co-localizing with general transcription machinery [42, 43]. In our research 

Crp was found at stable promoters (associated with housekeeping genes) rather 

than at tissue-specific ones.  

BEAF-32 (Boundary Element-Associated Factor of 32 kDa) is an essential 

insulator protein in Drosophila that regulates genome organization, transcription, 

and cellular homeostasis [44]. Found near gene promoters, it separates closely 

apposed genes with distinct regulation modes. Mutations in BEAF-32 are lethal, 

causing  developmental  defects  like  epithelial  loss  and  neoplastic  growth  in 

imaginal  discs.  These  mutations  misregulate  genes  involved  in  cell  polarity, 

proliferation, differentiation, and key pathways like Wingless [45]. In this study, 

BEAF-32 was found near constitutively active promoters in brain tissue.

Cooperative  interplay  between  the  cis-regulatory  elements  and  transcription 
factors 

The  regulatory  landscape  of Drosophila is  characterized  by the  dynamic 

binding of thousands of transcription factors (TFs) to genomic elements, where 

their precise combinatorial interactions controll tissue-specific and housekeeping 

gene  expression.  High-throughput  studies  have  revealed  that  TFs  bind 

extensively  across  the  genome,  targeting  both  known  and  novel  enhancers, 

promoters,  and  insulators,  often  in  a  highly  overlapping  manner  [46].  The 

cooperative  binding  of  TFs  facilitates  communication  between  promoters  and 

enhancers,  enabling 

the 

spatiotemporal  gene  expression.  Large-scale 

modENCODE  efforts  elucidated  the  complexity  of  the Drosophila regulatory 

genome, revealing dense TF-binding hubs and extensive co-binding relationships 

that form intricate regulatory networks [47]. These findings underscore that gene 

regulation by enhancers and promoters is not controlled by  individual TF binding 

but emerge from the collective activity of multiple factors working in concert.

Chapter 1: Features of gene regulation by tissue-specific enhancers 

This  chapter  of  the  results  section  focuses  on  the  tissue-specific 

characteristics  of  enhancer  activity  in  Drosophila  melanogaster.  Chromatin 

accessibility  mapping  is  a  widely  used  method  for  identifying  regulatory 

elements. ATAC-Seq, based on integration of adapters by a Tn5 transposase into 

accessible  chromatin,  allows  to  capture  the  accessibility  of    chromatin  at 

regulatory  regions,  including  promoters  and  enhancers  [48].  To  identify 

enhancers this way, promoters must be removed from the dataset. Using ATAC-

Seq data from the salivary gland, brain, and wing imaginal discs at the wandering 

larval stage, generated in our laboratory, we identified a set of salivary gland- and 

brain-specific enhancers. We then analysed their characteristics  to uncover the 

key features of these elements that enable tissue-specific gene regulation.  

Identification of tissue-specific enhancers by ATAC-Seq in Drosophila  

The goal of this study was to describe the mechanisms underlying tissue-

specific  transcription  that  is  realized  through  the  cooperative  work  of  genome 

regulatory  elements.  For  that,  we  needed  to  define  the  pool  of  transcriptional 

regulatory  elements  from  different  tissues  that  could  be  used  for  the  analysis. 

Three tissues that have different fate in Drosophila ontogenesis (salivary glands, 

brain, wing imaginal discs) were chosen.  As it was mentioned in introduction, 

salivary  glands  experience  autophagy  and  apoptosis,  larval  brain  tissue  goes 

through neurogenesis, axon and dendritic pruning, arbor outgrowth and cell death 

that result in the establishment of the adult nervous system, wing imaginal discs 

differentiate into adult wings and carcass. We dissected tissues from wandering 

larvae in two biological replicates and preformed ATAC-Seq procedure (Figure 

1.1).

After defining the pool of tissue-specific enhancers, we sought to identify 

what  proteins  bind  these  regulatory  regions  and  how  they  are  distributed  in 

tissues.  To characterize the landscape of protein binding to salivary gland and 

brain enhancers, we estimated the binding levels of CBP/Nejire acetyltransferase, 

EcR,  H3K27Ac  using  ChIP-Seq  on  salivary  gland and  brain material  obtained 

from wandering larvae. We also assessed the chromatin state of enhancers using 

FAIRE-Seq and ATAC-Seq coverage at these regions (Figure 1.5).  

We  assert  that  selected  by  ATAC-seq  genome  regions  are  enhancers 

because they possess the main enhancer traits: these regions demonstrate a high 

31 

 
 
 
binding level of EcR and CBP/Nejire and are enriched for the H3K27Ac mark 

inherent to active enhancers. It is important to note, that we excluded the promoter 

regions (TSSs) that were detected by ATAC-Seq from analysis.  

We  found  evidence  of  tissue-specific  activity  of  defined  tissue-specific 

enhancers  in  the  salivary  glands  and  brain.  High  signals  of  binding  for 

CBP/Nejire  and  EcR  proteins,  as  detected  through  ChIP-Seq  analysis  of  the 

salivary gland, were observed exclusively at salivary gland-specific enhancers, 

but  not  at the  brain-specific  enhancers.  Conversely,  high  levels  of  CBP/Nejire 

and  EcR  binding  in  the  brain  were  detected  only  at  brain-specific  enhancer 

regions.  Chromatin  accessibility  (FAIRE-Seq  and  ATAC-Seq  signals)  and 

acetylation levels (H3K27Ac) were consistent with the tissue-specific binding of 

CBP/Nejire  and  EcR  in  both  tissues.  Interestingly,  stable  enhancers  did  not 

exhibit  high  levels  of  activity  overall.  This  could  be  attributed  to  the  fact  that 

these  enhancers  are  not 

involved 

in 

tissue-specific  processes  at 

this 

developmental stage, which may explain their reduced activity (reflected by low 

CBP/Nejire  binding,  H3K27Ac  level,  EcR  binding,  and  FAIRE-Seq  signal). 

Nonetheless,  stable  enhancers  serve  as  a  good  control  pool  because  they 

demonstrate  the  binding  signal  in  both  tissues  and  confirm  that  ChIP-Seq 

experiment was valid. The absence of protein binding in ChIP samples obtained 

from salivary gland tissue at brain-specific enhancers (and vise a versa) is not due 

to  the  inaccurate  sample  preparation  but  due  to  the  exclusive  activity  of these 

regulatory elements at a particular tissue.  

Thus, we analysed the molecular states of the 117 salivary gland-specific, 

1131 brain-specific, and 130 stable (control) enhancers by plotting average CBP, 

EcR, H3K27Ac binding and accessible chromatin signal profiles for each tissue. 

Our findings indicate that the active state of enhancers in the salivary glands and 

brain  is  characterized  by  accessible  chromatin  state  and  specific  binding  of 

ecdysone  receptor  (EcR)  and  proteins  that  regulate  chromatin  accessibility 

(CBP/Nejire,  H3K27Ac)  at  a  particular  tissue.  Since  we  did  not  detect  any

enhancers or enhancers with different yet uncovered principles of regulation.

Salivary  gland-specific  and  brain-specific  enhancers are enriched  with  binding 

motifs for distinct transcription factors 

Upon  identifying  the  tissue-specific  activity  of  the  selected  enhancer 

groups, we sought to determine which tissue-specific proteins might initiate the 

formation of these enhancers within the respective tissues. DNA-binding proteins 

are the most likely candidates to act as initiators of enhancer activity. To identify 

the DNA-binding proteins involved in the regulation of tissue-specific enhancers, 

we analyzed the enrichment of DNA-binding protein motifs within these groups 

of  enhancers  using  the  MEME-ChIP  tool  [54].  Salivary-gland  enhancers  were 

significantly enriched for Sage (E-value 1.6e-012), Sidpn (E-value 1.6e-012), and 

Top2 (E-value 1.8e-006) motifs. Brain enhancers showed strong enrichment for 

Klu  (E-value  6.9e-153),  Trl  (E-value  2.1e-094),  Aef1  (E-value  2.2e-079), 

L(3)neo38 (E-value 9.0e-047) and Jim (E-value 1.8e-028) motifs. These findings 

align with the concept that tissue-specific enhancers are bound cooperatively by 

multiple  DNA-binding  proteins  [55].  Notably,  the  motifs  with  the  highest  E-

values correspond to the proteins predominantly expressed in the tissue where the 

enhancer is active, as confirmed by FlyAtlas2 FPKM data [56] (Figure 8A).

high.  The  depicted  model  highlights  the  interplay  between  tissue-specific

In this chapter we obtained the next results: 

1.  Using ATAC-Seq data from wandering larvae, we successfully identified

chromatin  accessibility  underscore  that  promoter  activity  is  tuned  with  high 

specificity in each tissue.

As a result of our findings from the second chapter, which highlight the 

features of tissue-specific and stable gene promoter activity, we propose a model 

where tissue-specific promoters are enriched for RNA Pol II (Rpb3), TBP, EcR, 

and  CBP  protein  binding  exclusively  in  the  tissues  where  the  promoters  are 

active. Furthermore, the model demonstrates that this selective protein binding is 

accompanied by the binding of tissue-specific transcription factors, as evidenced

for Blimp-1 and Adf1 motifs, whereas brain promoters show enrichment for Mad

transcriptional  regulation  within  each  tissue,  with  Adf1  being  notable  for  its 

presence  in  both  tissues.  In  contrast,  stable  promoters  were  characterized  by 

BEAF-32  and  CRP  motifs,  which  are  known  to  coordinate  the  activity  of 

housekeeping  promoters.  Interestingly,  we  demonstrated  that  these  proteins 

specifically bind to stable promoters in the brain but not in the salivary gland.

Conclusion 

In this study, we investigated the mechanisms underlying tissue-specific 

gene regulation in Drosophila melanogaster, focusing on the roles of enhancers 

and  promoters  in  driving  precise  transcriptional  programs.  The  results  were 

presented in two chapters, describing properties of tissue-specific enhancers and 

promoters. 

In Chapter 1, we explored the characteristics of tissue-specific enhancers. 

Using ATAC-Seq data from the salivary gland, brain, and wing imaginal discs of 

at the wandering larval stage, we identified a set of tissue-specific enhancers (117 

salivary gland-specific and 1131 brain-specific enhancers) and their associated 

promoters  (37  SG-specific  and  174  BR-specific  promoters)  within  genes  loci. 

These  enhancers  exhibited  tissue-specific  binding  of  hallmark  proteins 

(CBP/Nejire, H3K27Ac, EcR), with chromatin accessibility patterns consistent 

with  protein  binding.  Promoters  associated  with  these  enhancers  demonstrated 

high  transcription  levels,  as  evidenced  by  RNA-Seq,  but  did  not  show  tissue-

specific enrichment for RNA Pol II, TBP, or CBP binding. This indicates that 

tissue-specific gene activation at these loci is driven by enhancer activity rather 

than through differential recruitment of transcriptional machinery to promoters. 

We  propose  that  these  tissue-specific  enhancers  activate  transcription  at  the 

corresponding promoters in the target tissue by stimulating RNA polymerase II 

“pause” release. 

Chapter 2 focused on the description of gene group demonstrating tissue-

specific  promoters’  activity.  We  identified  a  pool  of  tissue-specific  promoters 

using RNA-Seq analysis (352 SG-specific and 288 BR-specific). Unlike the first 

set of promoters, these were characterized by tissue-specific binding of RNA Pol 

II, TBP, CBP, and EcR, suggesting that their transcription is regulated through 

recruitment of transcription machinery directly to the promoter regions. Notably, 

only a small subset of promoters (14 SG-specific and 19 BR-specific) overlapped 

74 

 
between the two datasets (Figure 10), revealing two distinct regulatory strategies 

for regulating of tissue-specific transcription: (1) a small set of genes is activated 

by tissue-specific enhancers activity within gene loci, and (2) a larger set of genes 

is  regulated  through  tissue-specific  recruitment  of  transcription  machinery  to 

promoters and lack tissue-specific enhancers in their loci. 

Figure 10. Vienn diagrams illustrating the intersection of promoters regulated by tissue

recruitment from the second chapter. 

Furthermore,  we  demonstrated  that  these  distinct  mechanisms  involve 

different transcription factor binding specificities. Salivary gland enhancers were 

enriched for motifs associated with Sage, Sidpn, and Top2, while their promoters 

contained Blimp-1 and Adf1 motifs. In the brain, enhancers were enriched for 

Klu, Trl, Aef1, L(3)neo38, and Jim motifs, whereas promoters were enriched for 

Mad, Brk, and Aef1 motifs (Figure 11).

Figure 11. Table of tissue-specific motif enrichment at enhancer and promoter regions. 
These findings highlight that tissue-specific transcriptional regulation is 
achieved through differential enhancer and promoter activity involving distinct 
sets of transcription factors.  

The  key  finding  of  this  study  is  the  discovery  that  tissue-specific  gene 

activation  can  occur  through  either  tissue-specific  enhancers  or  promoters, 

highlighting  two  distinct  regulatory  mechanisms.  Genes  activated  by  tissue-

specific enhancers within their loci exhibited promoters with non-tissue-specific 

binding of RNA Pol II and TFIID, suggesting that RNA Pol II remains in a paused 

state in tissues where the promoter is inactive. This implies that transcriptional 

activation of these genes is driven primarily by enhancer activity rather than RNA 

Pol  II  recruitment  to  the  promoter.  In  contrast,  at  tissue-specific  promoters 

76 

 
 
identified through RNA-Seq analysis, RNA Pol II and TFIID binding occurred in 

a  tissue-specific  manner,  with  enrichment  observed  exclusively  in  the  tissue 

where the promoter is active. This indicates that these genes are activated through 

the  tissue-specific  recruitment  of  transcriptional  machinery  to  their  promoters, 

underlying critical role of promoters in transcription activation. Together, these 

findings provide valuable insights into the complex interplay between enhancers 

and promoters in regulating tissue-specific gene expression.

Findings 

1.  Distinct tissue-specific enhancers were identified in the salivary glands 

and brain of Drosophila larvae. These enhancers exhibit tissue-specific 

enrichment  of  H3K27Ac,  CBP/p300,  and  EcR,  supporting  their  role  as 

active  regulatory  elements. The  activity  of  these  enhancers  is  driven  by 

selective  transcription  factor  binding,  with  distinct  transcription  factor 

motifs enriched in each tissue. 

2.  Tissue-specific  promoters  were  identified  in  the  salivary  glands  and 

brain,  characterized  by  the  presence  of  the  TATA-box  motif,  a 

hallmark of tissue-specific regulation. These promoters showed tissue-

specific  binding  to  RNA  polymerase  II  subunit  Rpb3  and  were  not 

maintained in a paused state in tissues, where they were inactive. 

3.  Tissue-specific enhancers drive transcription through selective activation 

in  target  tissues,  even  when  associated  promoters  remain  bound  with 

transcriptional  proteins  across 

tissues.  Conversely, 

tissue-specific 

promoters regulate transcription by recruiting TFIID and RNA polymerase 

II  specifically  in  target  tissues  (Figure  1.12).  Surprisingly,  the  loci 

controlled by tissue-specific promoters do not overlap with those activated 

by  tissue-specific  enhancers.  These  findings  highlight  that  tissue-

specific  enhancers  and  promoters  are  parts  of  distinct  mechanisms 

controlling tissue-specific gene expression (possibly acting through the 

utilization of different transcription factors).

Tissue-specific and stable enhancer analysis 

ATAC-Seq  peak  calling  was  made  by  MACS2  callpeak  (separately  for 

replicates) [79] with default parameters (with shifting model). Then differential 

binding  analysis  of  ATAC-Seq  peak  data  was  made  utilizing  DiffBind  [50]. 

Unique tissue-specific ATAC-Seq peaks with differential signal (|fold change|>2, 

FDR<0.05)  were  taken  for  salivary  glands,  brain,  wing  imaginal  discs.  Stable 

enhancers at which ATAC-Seq peak signal does not change across all the tissues 

were selected the next way: 

1. MACS2 callpeak with default settings was performed for merged bam files of 

ATAC-Seq replicates for every tissue (BR1+BR2, SG1+SG2, WD1+WD2).  

2.  Narrow  peaks  of  BR,  SG  and  WD  were  intersected  with  each  other  using 

bedtools. Common ATAC-Seq peaks for all tissues were received.  

3.  At  these  common  for  all  tissues  narrow  peaks,  average  binding  scores  for 

bigwig  files  for 

two  repeats  of  each 

tissue  were  calculated  using 

multiBigwigSummary in BED file mode.  

4. Fold Change and Pvalue for the contrasts BR vs. ID, BR vs. SG, SG vs. ID 

were calculated in R: 

FC = ((BR1+BR2)/2) / ((SG1+SG2)/2) 

Pvalue = TTEST(BR1:BR2, SG1:SG2, 2, 3) 

5. Then all common sites for which Fold Change in each contrast was from 0.8 

to 1.2, and Pvalue<0.05 were selected.  

Transcription  start  sites  ±250  bp  were  excluded  from  all  selected 

enhancers. Finally, we identified 117 SG-specific, 1131 BR-specific, 130 stable 

enhancers.  

Coverage data (bigwig files) was used for average binding plot generation. 

The data was prepared for plotting a heatmap or a binding profile of given regions 

with computeMatrix. plotHeatmap and plotProfile were used to visualize score 

distributions across enhancers. A non-parametric Wilcoxon signed-rank test was 

used  to  test  the  significance  of  difference  in  protein  binding  between  tissues

Differential binding analysis of ChIP-Seq CBP and EcR peaks at enhancers
