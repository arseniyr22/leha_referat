Annotation

The topic of recommender systems (RecSys) is popular nowadays as it allows businesses to

customize the experience of each of their users individually. Realisation with standard approaches

like content- or collaborative-based filtering allows to identify the suggestions appropriate in the

context of relevance. However, such selected proposals are subject to various biases due to the

fact that algorithms overlook the causality effect. A set of techniques that allow to estimate

how impactful recommendations are on user’s actions is called uplift modeling. The current paper

presents a research aimed at the study of topic’s nuances. Through the implemetation of PropCare

and DLCE algorithms the analysis of the relevance factor of formed uplift recommendations is

performed. In particular, how it relates to algorithm’s performance and whether its effect can be

incorporated.

1 Introduction

Recommender systems (RecSys) have become an important part of the modern data-driven

approach to service personalisation enhancement. The general formulation of the task is to recog-

nize the patterns of user behavior and preferences. Then the algorithm selects the most appropriate

user-item pair from the pool of available variants and provides a personalized recommendation.

The interactions are updated through the feedback loop, which enhances the overall quality of

algorithm’s future proposals.

The implementation is usually done through either collaborative filtering or content-based

methods. The former assumes that there exists a positive correlation in interactions between

users with similar features. One of the most popular approaches from this field is the Matrix

Factorization (MF) algorithm [13]. The latter mentioned approach is aimed at the prediction and

maximization of the click-through rate by providing a user with similar items [26, 28].

Each method discussed overlooks an important part that correlation does not imply cau-

sation. The statement refers to the fact that the activity with an item (whether it is click, rating,

purchase, etc.) might have been realised even without the algorithm’s proposal. Therefore, the

introduced potential bias [9] tends to weaken the quality of results produced. Thus, a discus-

sion of uplift modeling, which is a causal approach to RecSys, is needed to estimate the impact

recommendations have on user actions.

Through the incorporation of causality into the process, uplift modeling helps to achieve

several potential benefits. To begin with, the pesonalization is enhanced as the recommendations

are now characterised by greater user dedicated. In addition, the increase in efficiency is featured

as the computational resources are aimed at the results with most impact. Finally, in order to

capture the causality effect of recommendations, the new metrics are introduced to evaluate the

success of different approaches in this field.

The research on the topic was performed as a part of the group work which was to col-

lectively study methods and challenges related to uplift. The current paper is aimed at the

accomplishment of two goals with reference to which further theory and material will be pre-

sented. First and foremost, through the implementation of selected approaches and their various

modifications perform simulations to study the differences and similarities in uplift and relevance

maximisation recommendations. Furhtemore, examine the opportunities to balance the tradeoff

of uplift-relevance relation identified.

2 Literature review

The research on causality-aware RecSys emerged from the ideas of uplift modeling applica-

tions. The key advantage of causal effect account is that it helps to decrease the effect of various

biases (popularity, noise, etc.) [9]. Most important derivation from it in the scope of the current

paper is that it provides with an opportunity to measure the effect produced recommendations

have on users.

2.1 Existing approaches

As stated in the Section (1) one of the most renowned approaches for RecSys is MF [13].

Nevertheless, its most basic version assumes that the observed data is the true preference of a user

and introduces selection bias [25].

The growing interest in causal RecSys [27] has provided with numerous approaches each

based on different assumptions trying to eliminate potential biases. For example, one of the

algorithms called CausE [2] builds upon the ideas of MF but aims to eliminate confounding factors

by introducing two training datasets of large and small. The former contains observations obtained

from some selected recommender policy and the latter from unbiased algorithm (e.g. random).

Leveraging the multi-stage learning the approach leads to the separation of environment’s effects

on user recommendations.

Another approach named CausalNeighborhood [23] provides with an alternative assump-

tions and uses the idea of k-nearest neighbors (kNN) to calculate the similarity of interactions

between users and items. The produced ranked lists are then evaluated in terms of causal effect

and final recommendations are formed.

For the mentioned approaches to work the exposure data is necessary. One of the possible

methods for its estimation is CJBPR [34]. This unbiased approach is aimed at the learning of

user-item relevance and propensity. However, its own training requires the full data collection

containing information about recommendation provision.

2.2 Selected approaches

As stated in the Subsection (2.2) the most prominent challenge to exist is the provision

of algorithms with exposure data and its estimation. For this reason the current paper focuses

on the PropCare approach [16]. Its main advantage is that it estimates the propensity utilising

user-item interactions only. In addition, alike CJBPR it also allows to estimate the probability

of relevance of an item for a user. This information will be used for modifications described in

Section (3) with the motivation and results presented in Section (4).

Considering the causal approach for recommendations generation, the DLCE [24] algo-

rithm was selected. Unlike CausE that requires two datasets and correlation influence present in

CausalNeighborhood, it continues to elevate the ideas of MF calculating the causal effect directly

by leveraging estimation propositions of

[10, 20, 24]. Besides, the comparison of performance of

DLCE to previously discussed approaches is done as a separate part of the group work.

2.3 Related works

With the references to selected approaches described in Subsection (2.2) the existing related

papers were analysed to verify the necessity and uniqueness of the identified goal to study the

relevance factor of produced recommedations.

Considering the methodologies linked to PropCare, several can be discussed. One of the

papers discusses an approach named "CausalX" [14] aimed at the use of LLM for increased trans-

parency and debiasing of the recommendations. Despite the causal consistency enforced by the

incorporation of explanations, this methodology does not directly estimate the exposure data and

requires substantial computational resources. Another technique described in [11] intorduces a

multifactorial bias correction with alternated gradient descent. The handling of more complex

interactions comes with the need for explicit ratings data, which is usually unavailable in many

real-life cases. Last related research discussed in [1] is aimed at stochastic session modeling to

capture temporal dynamics. Nevertheless, its narrow scope causes dependence on time lengths

and limited scalability.

The collection of related researches for DLCE is more extensive and can be classified into

a number of discussed fields. The first one is connected to the topic of causal inference techniques

[17, 30, 31] with various studies in modeling of causal graphs [32] to capture more complex

relationships or exploration of more consistent estimators [21]. The increase in robustness is

obtained with increased complexity in implementation and computation and greater reliance on

data specifity. The goal of alternative debiasing is discussed by [12, 22] or solved through neural

6

networks leverage [7] which both risk overfitting. Studies in unlearning biased user preferences

[8, 33] or increase of algorithm’s fairness in recommendations proposal [4, 6] have narrow causal

scope. Other researches like targeting specific niches (e.g. news [18], cloud services [5]), using

knowledge distillation techniques [3] and simulating counterfactual scenarios [29] are prone to

have dependencies from contextual data, loss of causal interpretability and training instabilities.

All the mentioned works highlight the DLCE benefit as an introductory universal framework for

uplift-specific challenges exploration.

5 Conclusions and future prospects

The current paper presents the idea behind the uplift modeling setting and provides with

an evaluation of potential modifications that can be made for the purpose of attempts to account

for the effect of item’s relevance on user’s interaction with it.

The description of obtained results in both DunnHumby settings suggests that usage of

DLCE (ˆsF

u,i) should be prioritised. First of all, its performance in comparison with the DLCE

(ˆsu,i) is noticeably better and is more stable in various simulation settings. In addition, in the

case of LM od

DLCE implementation, the derived method shows non-worse results almost for all met-

rics for DHoriginal setting compared to initial realisation and substantially enhanced onced for

DHpersonalised. Considering its relationship with relevance, DLCE (ˆsF

u,i) allows one to provide

around the same or even better statistics compared to DLCE (ˆsu,i) and substantially better with

regard to personal popular approach. Furthermore, the discussed method also excels in the pro-

23

vision of more individual recommendations.

Acknowledging the limited scope of FINN.no setting, the analysis performed on it still

allowed to derive some useful considerations. The received metrics indicate more stable long-term

potential performance, suggesting as well that personal popular approach is not the best fit for

all cases.

However, an important assumption is made in the research of the availability of user-item

frequency interaction data. As a result, the derived approach is subject to cold start problem due

to lack of such available information for new users or items.

Considering the future prospects of the work, several ideas could be realised and tested.

Firstly, the research would benefit to be complemented with a large non-synthetic dataset which

would allow to make more definite considerations. Secondly, the solutions for the mitigation of

cold start problem could be tested with the leverage of popularity intial usage for new users and

other items features used. In addition, the new ideas for creation of a more versatile and much

better performing approach that incorporates the item’s relevance should probably be considered.
