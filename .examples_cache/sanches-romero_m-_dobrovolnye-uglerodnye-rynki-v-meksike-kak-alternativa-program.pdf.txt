Abstract

The critical role of ecosystem services in supporting human well-being and
economic stability underscores the urgent challenges posed by their degrada-
tion due to unsustainable development and climate change. Projections such
as a potential annual GDP loss of $2.7 trillion by 2030, primarily impacting
rural and impoverished communities, highlight the risk of deepening social
and economic inequalities. These alarming trends demand a shift toward
more sustainable development models and innovative strategies to address
the current environmental crisis.

This research examines both international and national strategies for
mitigating these issues, with the Paris Agreement serving as a key framework
for climate action. Particular emphasis is placed on financing mechanisms
such as Voluntary Carbon Markets (VCMs). A case study from the state
of Campeche, Mexico, showcases ongoing governmental efforts in forest
conservation through the Payment for Environmental Services program.

The study identifies technical, political, and socioeconomic barriers that
must be jointly addressed by key actors to ensure that VCMs become a viable
solution to conserving forest ecosystem services in Mexico. It presents the
main findings from qualitative research conducted in two ejidos currently
selling carbon credits to international companies and concludes with nine
policy recommendations aimed at strengthening Mexico’s VCM.

This work offers a roadmap for researchers and policymakers seeking to
better understand and enhance the impact of such initiatives, with the goal
of accelerating climate action and empowering local communities.

Keywords: ecosystem services, climate change, Payment for Environmental
Services, Voluntary Carbon Markets, Mexico, Paris Agreement, rural com-
munities.

Introduction

Humanity has always depended on the services provided by the biosphere
and its ecosystems. The benefits we derive from nature, are essential for
human survival and well-being. Food, medicine, raw materials, clean air,
water, fuel, energy, and oxygen are extracted from the environment, we can
not survive without them and the same is true of all other species. Hence,
it is becoming increasingly clear that population growth and economic
development are at the same time diminishing the capability of many
ecosystems to meet these demands.

Many countries decided to increase food supply by converting a forest to
agriculture lands, but in so doing it decreases the supply of services that may
be of equal —or greater importance— such as water supply, flood regulation
or drought control. Therewith, in many parts of the world, this degradation
of ecosystem services is exacerbated by the loss of the knowledge held by
local communities, that are in risk in both sides, the loss of their social and
cultural values and the risk to experiment environmental migration, with all
the social and economic consequences around this phenomena. In this sense,
ecosystem degradation tends to harm rural populations more directly that
urban settlements and has its most direct and severe impact on poor people.
The news about the state of ecosystem services in the world are disturbing
and highlight how urgent it is to shift growing perspectives and look for a
clever and innovative solutions to achieved this scenarios. Some examples
of this world in crisis are:

1. Healthy ecosystems provide 75% of global freshwater resources, with
wetlands playing a key role in water purification. However, since 1970,
35% of wetlands have been lost. (World Health Organization 2025)

1

Introduction

2. World Bank estimates that the collapse of select ecosystem services
provided by nature – such as wild pollination, provision of food from
marine fisheries and timber from native forests – could result in a
decline in global GDP of $2.7 trillion annually by 2030. (World Bank
2021)

3. In the Amazon, around 17% of the forest has been lost in the last 50
years, mainly due to forest conversion to agriculture and livestock.
Forest are carbon sinks but when forest are cut, burned, or otherwise
removed, they emit tons of carbon to the atmosphere instead of
absorbing carbon. In 2022, deforestation in the world, accounted at least
7% of global greenhouse gas emissions. (World Wildlife Fund 2025)

4. In its fifth macro-financial scenario assessment of climate risk, the Net-
work for Greening the Financial System, found that global economic
growth could slow down by 30% by 2100 due to climate change. (Costa
2024)

The impacts of the ecosystem services degradation are not yet well known,
but it is indisputable that every person will experiment this changes, and the
crisis in the more vulnerable regions - countries least responsible will suffer
most - will cause not only negative economics impacts, but also devastating
effects in communities and a problems around the planet.

Climate change presents serious issues, from rising temperatures and
extreme weather events to dwindling resources and threatened ecosystems.
As complex as the issues is, there is hope. In the face of the challenges, there
are climate change actions that offer a road map toward more sustainable
growing and resilient future. Some of these solutions are:

1. Reduce our consumption of fossil fuels

2. Greater energy efficiency

3. Renewable energy

4. Sustainable transportation and buildings

5. Technical and industrial solutions

2

Introduction

6. Better forestry management and sustainable agriculture

7. Conservation-based solutions

Regarding the last solutions, in forest sectors working on all three
fronts—protection, restoration, and sustainable management— can help
combat climate change and foster thriving ecosystems. Some of our strongest
allies in the fight against climate change are the foresty ecosystems (trees,
plants, and soil) store massive amounts of carbon at ground level or
underground. Without these carbon sinks, life on earth would be impossible.
Parties to the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change
(UNFCCC), reached a historic agreement at COP 21 in Paris on December
12, 2015, to fight climate change and to accelerate and intensify the
investments and actions required for a sustainable low-carbon future. The
Paris Agreement brings all countries for the first time in a concerted effort
to combat climate change and prepare for its repercussions, with increased
assistance to assist developing countries do so. Consequently, there was the
beginning of a new direction for the global climate movement.

The Paris Agreement requires all Parties to put forward their best efforts
through “Nationally Determined Contributions” (NDCs) and to strengthen
these efforts in the years ahead. In Article 5, the Paris Agreement encourage
Parties to conserve and enhance sinks and reservoirs of GHGs, including
forest. And in Article 6 its recognized the possibility of voluntary cooperation
among countries to allow for higher environmental ambitions, for any
cooperation that involves international transferal of mitigation outcomes, by
establishing mechanisms to contribute to the mitigation of GHG emissions
and support sustainable development.

These international pathway contributed to innovative solutions and
reinforced another mechanisms to address climate change. It represented
an open window of opportunity for the development on new market- based
instruments as well as providing an global framework for the incorporation
of national and regional carbon crediting schemes. On this new scenario,
countries with a high stock of forest resources can find a route of access to
new ways of financing for the conservation, protection and restoration of

the forest ecosystems that, even if are located under this jurisdiction provide
ecosystem services – like carbon sink – for all the planet.

Mexico is a forest country, 70% of the territory is covered by template
forest, jungles, mangroves, arid and semiarid ecosystems, they are vital for
biodiversity, water supply, food and raw materials, medicine substances,
carbon sequestration an rural livelihoods. However, deforestation, illegal
logging, and the growth of agricultural and livestock areas is undermining
their capacity to provide essential ecosystem services.

This final work sets out to investigate the possibilities that Mexico has to
participate in the initiatives of Voluntary Carbon Markets as an opportunity
to attract international private resources for the conservation of thousand
of hectares that face the high pressure of different deforestation drivers.

The first chapter includes an exploration of ecosystem services, the eco-
nomic efforts to make a economic valuation of them and the international
policies designed to protect this natural capital. These section tries to high-
light the vital role of ecosystem services, while underscore the challenges
of quantifying these services into market and policy frameworks. In this
respect, the Millennium Ecosystem Assessment (MA 2005) and the IPBES
Global Assessment Report (IPBES 2019) emphasize the accelerating degra-
dation of natural ecosystems and the urgent need of transformative actions.
The degradation of ecosystem services has many causes, including excessive
demand for ecosystem services stemming from economic growth, demo-
graphic changes, and individual choices. All initiatives to address this risk
scenario must integrate the local communities into conservation strategies,
as their homelands are under big pressure of degradation despite is recog-
nized biocultural characteristics.

The experience of different economic instruments such as Payments
for Ecosystem Services (PES) had demonstrated both potential and areas
for improvement. Examples like Costa Rica’s PES program, had shown
positive impacts in increased forest cover and the participation of local
communities. In contrast efforts with a lack planning, like PES in Sumatra
could exacerbated deforestation drivers. Both examples illustrate that

effective PES programs require robust institutional frameworks, inter-
institutional cooperation and community engagement.

The second chapter present a brief of the state of the Mexican forest and
biodiversity, considering that the country is one of the five countries with the
highest number of vertebrate and vascular plant species, as well as endemic
species in the world. In the previous arguments it was mentioned that forest
areas are home to many people, including indigenous groups and Mexico
is not exception, almost 10% of the population lives in these areas and 30%
of them are indigenous peoples.

However, these resources are diminished by the increase of processes
of deterioration such as deforestation and forest degradation, increased
erosion and desertification, the expansion of agricultural and livestock lands
and urban infrastructure. The consequences of the rapid transformation
in territories not only affect the ecosystem services, the impacts are also
reflected in the social and economic spheres of communities which lived in
these areas.

The governmental efforts to address these public problems have provided
allowances for the establishment of a legislative framework such as General
Law on Climate Change, the legal instrument that regulate public policies
for climate change adaptation and mitigation and the General Law on
Sustainable Forest Development which aims to regulate and promote the
conservation, protection, restoration, production, management, cultivation,
handling, and use of the country’s forest ecosystems and their resources.

Therefore, in order to properly comprehend how these legislative and
policy tools function in Mexico, its necessary to understand the forms of
land tenure recognized by the Mexican Constitution: ejidos and communities,
both of them consist in social property where decisions about the use of land
such be supported by all inhabitants based on cooperation and economic
democracy. These special configuration gives Mexico unique characteristics.
In this context, the Mexican experience of Payment for Environmental
Services (PSA, by its acronym is Spanish) serves as a watershed for forest
resource conservation. These chapter explains the basic aspects of its
operations as well as present and analysis of budget evolution in the last

fifteen years, to uncover the challenges that represent for conservation
objectives the political decisions of the Mexican government.

An overview is provided at the end of the chapter’s of the current context
of Voluntary Carbon Markets (VCM) in Mexico. The VCM has existed in
Mexico since 2013. This mechanism allows companies to convert their
services and products to carbon neutral. This means that the carbon footprint
of the product or service has been offset by the purchase of carbon credits,
neutralizing its environmental impact. With almost 300 active projects in
Mexico, where 87.5% are related to forestry and land use, these section
analyze the key actors and their main functions in project execution.

1.1 Ecosystem services

The concept of ecosystem services, which appeared in the 1980s, experienced
a research boom, was inserted into political discussions and was considered
on the international agenda starting with the publication of Constanza’s
article (Constanza et al. 1998) about the value of the world’s ecosystem
services. The economic analyses generated from the numerical value that
researchers assigned to a series of good and services (terrestrial and marine)
from common or private use, which generated global or specific benefits
turned into a tool for pay greater attention to issues such as the complex
relationships between ecological and socioeconomic systems, how this
changes affect human welfare, and how to quantifies in monetary terms
the different types of services. These analyses began to include the need
to generate management mechanism and, above all, specific considerations
in case of loss of value or serious alterations on them. The monetary value
of natural resources opened a window to a more concrete conception to
understand what seemed free and infinite.

With roots in the fundamentals of economic theory of value, methods and
techniques for valuing ecosystems and biodiversity have gradually emerged.
The interest for environmentalists to have compelling arguments for more
comprehensive and deeply ingrained policies to safeguard biodiversity than
the ones that are in place now seems to have sparked the interest in such
assessments. The current situation can then be described by a concerning
discrepancy between the theoretical and practical challenges of developing
a consistent and trustworthy analysis of this dependency and the perceived
significance of deepening our understanding of how our economies and
societies depend on the preservation of healthy ecosystems. (Salles 2011)

According to Constanza (Constanza et al. 1998), “ecosystem functions
refer variously to the habitat, biological or system properties or processes
of ecosystems. Ecosystem goods and services represent the benefit human
population derive, directly or indirectly, from ecosystem functions”. In their
research, the authors refer to ecosystem goods and services together as
ecosystem services.

Ecosystem services, which are defined as the benefits that humans derive
from ecosystems, are consistently not "captured" in economic markets
and, for various reasons, have not been sufficiently quantified up to this
point, giving them little weight when making policy decisions. Human
sustainability over the medium and long term is jeopardized by these
undervaluation or omissions.

Ecosystem services consist of flows of materials, energy an information
from natural capital stocks (materials or information that exists at a point in
time) which combine with manufactured and human capital services together
produces human welfare. In these sense, it is meaningful to ask how changes
in the quantity or quality of various types of natural capital and ecosystem
services may have an impact on human welfare.

The Millennium Ecosystem Assessment (MA 2005) was carried out
between 2001 and 2005 to assess the consequences of ecosystem change
for human well-being and to establish the scientific basis for actions needed
to enhance the conservation and sustainable use of ecosystems and their
contributions to human well-being. This international report responds to
government request for information received through the Convention on
Biological Diversity, United Nations Convention to Combat Desertification,
the RAMSAR Convention and the Convention on Migratory Species. One
of the main goals of this report was to meet needs of other stakeholders,
including the business community, non governmental organizations and
indigenous peoples. The assessment focuses on the linkages between
ecosystems and human well-being and, in particular, on “ecosystem services”,
understanding an ecosystem as a dynamic complex of plant, animal, and
microorganism communities and the nonliving environment interacting as
a functional unit.

The conceptual framework for these report, maintains that people are
integral parts of ecosystems and that a dynamic interaction exists between
them and other parts of ecosystems, with the changing human condition
driving, both directly and indirectly, changes in ecosystems and thereby
causing changes in human well-being. These changes could be small at large
scale or large in small scales. For example, changing the gaseous composition
of the global atmosphere by a small amount could represent a large- scale
climate change that will affect the viability of global human populations.
Large change at small scales include, for example, dramatically changing
local forest composition. These changes may alter terrestrial ecosystems,
having several impacts on the benefits and cost of local human activities.
(Constanza et al. 1998).

1.3 Economic valuation of ecosystem services

Based on the previous context and the findings of the different reports, it
became important to make an effort to quantify the value of all the services
and goods generated by natural ecosystems. This issue, the valuation is hard
and is not shying away from all kind of criticism. Actually, the valuation goes
beyond complex mathematical processes and economic theories, its about
trying to objectively integrate concepts that seem relegated to aesthetic or
that are purely economic and such be integrated together. Some arguments
consider that valuation of ecosystem is either impossible, because we cannot
place a value on “intangibles elements” as human life, environmental or long-
term ecological benefits. Another argument is the protection of ecosystems
for purely moral reasons, with out need of valuations, even so, moral and
economic arguments are certainly not mutually exclusive.

Although ecosystem valuation is certainly difficult and fraught with
uncertainties, it is, for the time, one way to help decision-makers consider the
“other” costs of development, in this case, the externalities that are usually
hidden under the economic progress.

In 1997, Constanza and colleagues estimated that at the current margin,
the total global value of the 17 ecosystem services (the major biomes along
with their estimated global surface area), by biome, by service type and
for the entire biosphere, had been estimated at least US$33 trillion dollars
worth of services annually. The majority of the value of services they
identified was currently outside the market system, in services such as
gas regulation (US$1.3trillion/yr), disturbance regulation (US$1.8 trillion/yr),
waste treatment (US$2.3 trillion/yr) and nutrient cycling (US$17trillion/yr).
About 63% of the estimated value is contributed by marine systems (US$20.9
trillion/yr). Most of this comes from coastal systems (US$10.6 trillion/yr).
About 38% of the estimated value comes from terrestrial systems, mainly
from forests (US$4.7 trillion/yr) and wetlands (US$4.9 trillion/yr). (Constanza
et al. 1998).

Economic valuation’s comparative advantage is that it uses a common unit
of account (money) to make it easy and direct to compare values across other
goods, services, investments, and economic impacts. It also communicates

1.4 Commercialization of ecosystem services

1.4.1 Economic instruments

Information on the economic value of ecosystem services can be used
in a variety of decision-making contexts including a) to raise awareness
among all stakeholders regarding the importance of natural capital to
human well-being; b) set priorities across policy targets; c) design policy
instruments for environmental management, such as taxes, transferable
quotas, certification and labelling, and trade restrictions; d) formulate
sustainable financing mechanisms, such as payments for ecosystems services
e) evaluate alternative investments in environmental conservation and
nature based solutions by means of cost-benefit analysis; f) quantify the
distribution of costs and benefits of management decisions among different
stakeholders; g) set compensation for damage to nature; h) and enable the
generation of monetary ecosystem service accounts.

Economic instruments are market based mechanisms, which encompass
a mix of policy instruments designed to influence producers and consumers’
behavior by internalizing environmental or depletion cost trough change in

the incentive structure that these agents face, economic instruments provide
markets signals in the form of a modification of relative prices and/or a
financial transfer. Environmental taxes fall into this category, as do direct

An important policy instruments are the positive incentives for biodiver-
sity, that provide incentives to protect and develop a sustainability use of
biodiversity. They include taxes, fees, environmentally-motivated subsidies,
tradable permit schemes, payments for ecosystem services, and biodiversity

Traditionally ecosystem services have consider as “free” services provided
for nature to our benefit and welfare, therefore the economic values of these
services had been ignored or underestimate. As a result, the depletion and
degradation, continue an alarming rate.

By helping to reflect the true value of ecosystem services in economic
decision-making, positive incentives can play a role in unlocking private
finance and investment in ecosystems. Establishing markets for ecosystem
services can enhance conservation efforts, facilitate restoration, and support
local livelihoods by rewarding landowners and managers for their steward-

1.6 International policies: Paris Agreement

The Paris Agreement is an international climate treaty that aims to limit
global warming to well below 2 degrees Celsius, preferably to 1.5°C
degrees Celsius, compared to pre-industrial levels. To realize this long- term
temperature target, nations aim to reach global peaking of greenhouse gas
emissions worldwide at the earliest opportunity to achieve a climate-neutral
planet by the middle of the century. This agreement was embraced by 196
Parties during COP 21 in Paris on 12 December 2015 and entered into force
on 4 November 2016. (UNFCCC 2015)

The Paris Agreement is an undisputed milestone in the fight against the
climate emergency. It sets out the first-ever framework for global action
against GHG emissions from both developed and developing countries.
It represents elements of previous climate actions plans and agreements
within the UNFCCC. Unlike the Kyoto Protocol, which established legally
binding reduction commitments for developed countries, the core of the
these agreement puts forth a voluntary approach agreed upon by all heads
of state and government in 2009 in Copenhagen, Denmark. Each nation sets
out its own climate action plans for reduction of emissions of greenhouse
gasses. These national plans are called “NDCs,” which stands for Nationally
Determined Contributions. Every nation pledges to create plans for willingly
reducing greenhouse gas emissions instead of enforced reduction targets
imposed from above,as was the case with the Kyoto Protocol. The Paris
Agreement grants national governments the autonomy to determine, based
on their own judgment, how ambitious they wish to be in aiding the battle
against climate change.

Additionally, the agreement aims to increase the ability of countries
to deal with the impacts of climate change, and at making finance flows
consistent with a low GHG emissions and climate-resilient pathway. To reach

36

1 Ecosystem services

these ambitious goals, appropriate mobilization and provision of financial
resources, a new technology framework and enhanced capacity-building is
to be put in place, thus supporting action by developing countries and the
most vulnerable countries, in line with their own national objectives.

The Paris Agreement, addresses crucial areas necessary to combat climate

change. Some of the key aspects of the Agreement are:

1. Long-term temperature goal (Art. 2) – The agreement in seeking to
strengthen the global response to climate change, reaffirms the goal of
limiting global temperature increase to well below 2 degrees Celsius,
while pursuing efforts to limit the increase to 1.5 degrees.

2. Global peaking and ’climate neutrality’ (Art. 4) – To achieve this
temperature goal, Parties aim to reach global peaking of green gas
emissions as soon as possible, recognizing peaking will take longer
for developing country Parties, and to undertake rapid reductions
thereafter in accordance with best available science, as to achieve a
balance between anthropocentric emissions by sources and removals
by sinks of greenhouse gases in the second half of this century, on
the basis of equity, and in the context of sustainable development and
efforts to eradicate poverty.

3. Mitigation (Art. 4) – The Paris Agreement establishes binding commit-
ments by all Parties to prepare, communicate and maintain a nationally
determined contribution (NDC) and to pursue domestic measures to
achieve them. It also prescribes that Parties shall communicate their
NDCs every 5 years and provide information necessary for clarity and
transparency. To set a firm foundation for higher ambition, each suc-
cessive NDC will represent a progression beyond the previous one
and reflect the highest possible ambition. Developed nations ought to
maintain their leadership by committing to comprehensive reductions
across their economies, while developing nations should progressively
strengthen their efforts to mitigate effects, and are urged to move to-
ward economy – wide targets over time in the light of different national
circumstances.

Parties to conserve and enhance, as appropriate, sinks and reservoirs
of GHGs, including forests. The agreement encourage countries to
achieve policy approaches and positive incentives for activities relating
to reducing emissions from deforestation and forest degradation,
and the role of conservation, sustainable management of forest and
enhancement of forest carbon stocks in developing countries, and
alternative policy approaches, such as joint mitigation and adaptation
approaches for the integral and sustainable management of forest.

On this sense, REDD+ can help countries meet their Paris Agreement
targets or NDCs. Under the Paris Agreement, all countries have agreed
to reduce their emissions according to the national targets they’ve

development, and defines a framework for non-market approaches to
sustainable development.

1.6.1 Article 6 of the Paris Agreement

Article 6 of the Paris Agreement sets out how countries can pursue
voluntary cooperation to reach their climate targets. It enables international
cooperation to tackle climate change and unlock financial support for
developing countries. Under Article 6, countries are able to transfer carbon
credits earned from the reduction of greenhouse gas emissions to help one or
more countries meet their climate targets. Despite not mentioning the word
markets, establishes guidance for international trading between domestic
carbon markets, as well as a new offsetting mechanism.

One of the tools which countries can draw upon under Article 6, is
the Paris Agreement Crediting Mechanism (PACM) - the UN’s new high-
integrity carbon crediting mechanism. The carbon crediting mechanism al-
lows countries to raise climate ambition and implement national action plans

1 Ecosystem services

more affordably. It identifies and encourages opportunities for verifiable
emission reductions, attracts funding to implement them, and allows coop-

activities. Developing countries may also be able to access climate money
through the Paris Agreement Crediting Mechanism, with a portion of the
proceeds going toward financing adaptation to increase resilience to the
unavoidable effects of climate change.

Article 6.2 refers to internationally transferred mitigation outcomes,
requesting guidance to be developed by 2020, for parties to be able to trade
their mitigation outcomes to count as progress towards their Nationally
Determined Contributions.

Article 6.4 establishes a sustainable development mechanism (SDM),
which will operate as an offset mechanism, where parties (and private
entities) can sell emissions reductions to another party. Article 6 deal allows
Clean Development Mechanism (CDM) projects to transition to the 6.4

Adjustment in its greenhouse gas inventory when it approves credits
for international usage, also known as Internationally Transferred
Mitigation Outcomes (ITMOs). This change maintains the market’s
environmental integrity by guaranteeing that the buyer using the credit
for compliance or the country selling the credit counts the emissions
reduction, but not both.

Corresponding adjustment requirements may extend beyond compli-

by the private sector’s voluntary commitments to reduce emissions.

system. The international registry guarantees fair access to the global

2.1 State of forest ecosystems in Mexico

Mexico has 138.7 million hectares of forest vegetation cover, it means, around
70.6% of the national territory is covered by temperate forests, jungles,
mangroves and arid and semi-arid ecosystems.

According to the National Commission of Biodiversity, the national
territory contains 12% of the world’s recognized biodiversity, placing Mexico
among the five countries with the highest number of vertebrate and vascular
plant species, as well as endemic species. The country’s forest resources have
a direct impact on the well-being of its populations. In addition to the timber,
food, and medicinal compounds these resources provide, they represent
an important source of vital environmental services for addressing climate
change (including carbon sequestration) and important services associated
with the proper functioning of ecosystem processes: soil conservation,
regulation of the hydrological and nutrient cycle, among others. (CONABIO
2006)

Within Mexico’s forested area, 54 vegetation types can be distinguished,
grouped into six main ecosystems. The three most representative by area
are xerophilous scrublands, with 56.2 million hectares, representing 40.5%
of the total forested area, followed by forests and jungles, with 34.8 and
30.3 million hectares, respectively, representing 25.1% and 21.9% of the total
forested area. The area of other ecosystems, such as mangroves are 0.7% of
the total forested area, actually the most vulnerable ecosystem in Mexico.

Within forest ecosystems, there are components that play an important

one of the main greenhouse gas causing climate change.

2.2 Disturbance factors in forest areas in Mexico

The goods and ecosystem services provided by diverse forest ecosystems,
which sustain the well-being of society, are diminished by increased
emissions from processes of deterioration such as deforestation and forest
degradation, decreased capture of greenhouse gases, river sedimentation,
siltation in hydraulic works and bodies of water, increased erosion and
desertification, loss of fertility in agricultural lands, increased vulnerability
of forests, and loss of biodiversity. These processes lead to the loss of the
capacity to produce food, the loss of economic and productive opportunities,
the loss of jobs, and the intensification of the effects of climate change, while
limiting the capacity to adapt to it. These processes of deterioration of forest

2.2.1 Forest fires

During the period from 1970 to 2022, an average of 7,070 forest fires occurred
each year, affecting 279,725.98 hectares. The years 1998, 2011, and 2024 stand
out as the most critical in terms of the area affected by forest fires. Almost
all forest fires in Mexico (98%) are caused by anthropogenic activities. By
2024, the main possible causes of forest fires were illegal activities (land
use change, hunters, vandalism, illegal logging, etc.) at 35.83%, followed by
agricultural activities at 30.89% (17.83% for agricultural activities and 13.06%
for livestock activities) and unknown causes at 29.87%. In 2024, 8,002 forest
fires were recorded in the country, affecting an area of 1,672,215.7 hectares.
(CONAFOR 2024)

2.2.2 Illegal logging

Illegal logging is the felling and extraction of forest resources without
authorization from the Ministry of Environment (SEMARNAT, by its
acronym in Spanish), whether forests, jungles, or arid zone vegetation. Illegal
logging and illegal trade are related to various factors, such as restrictions on
entering forestry operations, small plots where production is unprofitable,
agrarian litigation and conflicts, and the presence of organized crime in rural
areas.

Regarding the value of the illegal logging business, INTERPOL indicates
that between 15 and 30% of raw materials and wood products traded
worldwide are of illicit origin, generating annual tax losses of between $51
and $152 billion USD. In Mexico, although it has been difficult to accurately
measure the magnitude of the problem, there are estimates that between
6.3 and 7.1 million cubic meters (Mm3) of wood are being traded illegally
annually, representing around 70% of national timber production, which by

million USD. (Loya Ch´avez 2024)

Forest management plays a fundamental role in Mexico’s efforts to
mitigate climate change, as the Land Use, Land-Use Change, and Forestry
(LULUCF) sector currently accounts for 4.9% of the country’s GHG emissions,
while forest ecosystems absorb approximately 26% of total GHG emissions
from all sectors in the country.

Mexico’s NDCs have two components, one for mitigation and the other for
adaptation. Specifically, the country commits to an unconditional reduction
(referring to those that the country can cover with its own resources) of 25%
of its greenhouse gas and Short-Lived Climate Pollutant emissions by 2030.
Additionally, the country assumed a 40% reduction by 2030 as a conditional
measure (that is, they require the establishment of a new international
climate change regime in which Mexico could obtain additional resources
and achieve effective technology transfer mechanisms) (INECC 2022). The
successful implementation of ENAREDD+ will contribute to the fulfillment
of the NDC Commitments that Mexico assumed with the UNFCCC.

Market-based instruments have been used by countries to meet their
emissions reduction commitments, as they represent a cost-effective and
alternative source of resources to achieve their mitigation goals. The effective
integration of regulated and voluntary market instruments, such as local-
scale projects that increase carbon stocks, are alternatives to incentivize and
strengthen the implementation of REDD+ activities and results.

ENAREDD+ recognizes that deforestation and forest degradation pro-
cesses have origins both internal and external to the forestry sector, which
requires inter- and transdisciplinary work to achieve a shared social under-
standing of forest ecosystem management and thus propose adjustments and
harmonization of the set of activities and public policies developed in Mex-
ican territory and that correspond to the activities of all sectors and social
life in general. In Mexico, ENAREDD+ promotes the integrated land man-
agement model, a public management model with a territorial, not sectoral,
approach. It allows for the harmonization and integration of public policies

from the three levels of government and the coordinated implementation
of support programs. It creates regional institutional foundations at a scale
that generates synergies so that these territorial units generate favorable
conditions for their communities to achieve Sustainable Rural Development.
It is also known as Integrated Landscape Management.

2.3.4 Ownership of forest resources in Mexico

Article 27 of the Constitution of the United Mexican States (Congreso de
M´exico 1917) establishes that ownership of lands and waters within the
boundaries of the national territory originally belongs to the Nation, which
has had and has the right to transfer ownership of these lands to private
individuals, constituting private property. It also establishes the right of the
Mexican State to impose on private property the modalities dictated by the
public interest, as well as to regulate, for the benefit of society, the use of
natural elements susceptible to appropriation, for the purposes of, among
others, safeguarding their conservation.

From its inception, carbon market regulation was shaped by international
agreements,
including the United Nations Framework Convention on
Climate Change and the Kyoto Protocol, which introduced the concept of
emission reduction credits and facilitated the emergence of mechanisms
such as the CDM. These initiatives allowed developing countries, including
Mexico, to participate in the issuance and trading of carbon credits based on
emission reduction projects, laying a technical and institutional foundation
that, years later, would influence the development of their own regulatory
frameworks.

In April 2012, the Mexican government approved the General Law on
Climate Change, establishing a robust legal framework aimed at reducing
emissions and consolidating a carbon market that would respond to
ambitious mitigation goals. This law not only laid the groundwork for the
formulation of comprehensive environmental policies, but also granted the
federal government the power to create an emissions trading scheme, which
represented a paradigm shift in addressing the climate issue from a purely
voluntary perspective to a mandatory and regulated regime. Subsequently,

in November 2013, MEXICO2 was launched, a digital platform aimed at
institutionalizing and operationalizing these policies, adapting international
experience to the local context and directing investments toward GHG
reduction projects through the issuance and trading of carbon credits.

2.3.5 Key players: SEMARNAT, CONAFOR, private initiative,

ejidos and communities

The Ministry of Environment and Natural Resources (SEMARNAT, by
its acronym in Spanish) is responsible for promoting the protection,
restoration, and conservation of Mexico’s ecosystems, natural resources,
and environmental goods and services, in order to foster their use and
sustainable development. The fundamental objective of this Ministry is to
formulate and conduct national policy on natural resources, the environment,
and climate change in accordance with the Organic Law of the Federal
Public Administration. Also within SEMARNAT is the General Directorate
of Forest and Soil Management, which establishes the procedures, guidelines,
and criteria for executing forestry-related procedures for forest use and
management in accordance with the General Law of Ecological Balance and
the LGDFS.

The National Forestry Commission (CONAFOR, by its acronym in Span-
ish) is a decentralized public agency of the Federal Public Administration,
subordinate to SEMARNAT, with legal personality and its own assets. It is
the federal institution responsible for developing, promoting, and driving
forestry production, conservation, and restoration activities, as well as par-
ticipating in the formulation of plans and programs and the implementation
of the sustainable forest development policy. Therefore, it serves as the fo-
cal point for the preparation and implementation of REDD+ in Mexico. It
complies with CONAFOR’s obligation to "design strategies, policies, mea-
sures, and actions to transition to a zero percent carbon loss rate in native
ecosystems, for incorporation into forest policy planning instruments for
sustainable development, taking into consideration sustainable development
and community forest management." (Congreso de M´exico 2018).

In the case of land ownership, in Mexico, nearly 70% of forest lands

An ejido is, by definition, a social interest society made up of Mexican–
born peasants, with an initial social patrimony comprising the lands, forests,
and waters granted to them free of charge by the State as inalienable,
non-transferable, non-attachable, and imprescriptible property. Its use and

administration, based on cooperation and economic democracy, and whose
objective is the comprehensive use of its natural and human resources
through the personal labor of its members for their own benefit.

Successful stories of the collective management of natural resources in
ejidos and agrarian communities can be found across Mexico. The most well-
known cases are related to community forestry. Specifically, the communities
of Nuevo San Juan Parangaricutiro in Michoac´an and the Union of Zapotecan
and Chinantecan Forestry Communities (UZACHI) in Oaxaca. In both cases,
the communal ownership of forests has strengthened community cohesion,
encouraged young people to remain in their communities, and improved the
local economy by promoting agriculture, timber production and tourism.

A very interesting fact is that an Ejido property is not private property,
and it cannot be sold to foreigners; it may only be “sold” to Mexicans. A
Mexican citizen wishing to purchase Ejido land must have the agreement
of the whole community that “owns” the land. If an Ejido property is sold
without the consent of all owners, the buyer can risk a legal battle after the
fact, which, in the worst-case scenario means the land will be returned to the
original owner. The owner has rights of possession over the communal land,

2.5 Payment for Environmental Services Program

in Mexico

The Payment for Environmental Services Program (PSA, by its acronym in
Spanish) was created as an economic incentive (instrument) for the owners
of forest lands where these services are generated, with the purpose of
compensating them for conservation costs and the expenses they incur in
implementing good land management practices. PSA schemes are voluntary,
as they are based on the interest and mutual agreement of the parties to
participate. In these schemes, users of environmental services (cities, water
utilities, companies, etc.) are willing to pay to continue enjoying them, while
providers (forest land owners) are willing to adopt the actions and practices
necessary to maintain or improve their provision in exchange for payment.
This program operates under a payment-for-results scheme.

Since 2003, through the National Forestry Commission (CONAFOR),
Mexico has implemented this public policy, which seeks to actively conserve
the country’s forests, jungles, arid zones, and mangroves. For 22 years,
the PSA has promoted comprehensive actions for the conservation of
forest ecosystems, the strengthening of social capital, and sustainable
economic development in rural regions of Mexico. This policy, implemented
through environmentally friendly productive activities such as ecotourism,
beekeeping, eco-technologies, and community nurseries, among others,

68

2 Mexico between PES and VCM

seeks to generate the environmental services necessary for the well-being
of society in the medium and long term, and promotes adaptation to and
mitigation of the effects of climate change.

Initially, CONAFOR undertook two initiatives: the Hydrological Environ-
mental Services Program (PSAH, by its acronym in Spanish) in 2003, and the
Program to Develop the Market for Environmental Services through Carbon
Capture and Biodiversity-Related Aspects of Biodiversity and to Promote
the Establishment and Improvement of Agroforestry Systems (PSA-CABSA,
by its acronym in Spanish) in 2004. Initially, the PSAH launched its activities
with USD20 million derived from water taxes through the Federal Rights
Law.

Beginning in 2006, the two programs were merged under a single concept
called Environmental Services. In 2010, differentiated payments were made
by ecosystem type, seeking to link the amount of support to the opportunity
cost incurred by forest landowners when conducting conservation activities
instead of changing land use for agriculture or livestock. Payment amounts
are calculated based on the type of vegetation actively conserved and the
economic pressure index for deforestation.

Throughout the program’s years of operation and development, several
resources have been secured to finance multi–year projects, which are
allocated through the Mexican Forest Fund (FFM, by its acronym in
Spanish). This Fund acts as an intermediary between the program’s various
funding sources, managing revenue from the Forest Rights Law, CONAFORs
annual budget, as well as contributions and loans from both national
and international organizations, including the World Bank and the Global
Environment Facility.

The FFM was established in 2003 in compliance with the General Law
on Sustainable Forest Development (LGDFS) (Art. 142). This fund was
established "to promote the conservation, increase, sustainable use, and
restoration of forest resources and their associated resources." The FFM is
a financial mechanism for the annual payment to owners and possessors
of forest lands, whom, based on the fulfillment of the committed activities,

can access resources through some of CONAFOR’s programs such as: PSA,
Environmental Compensation, and the Biodiversity Heritage Fund.

The process through which FFM funds are allocated to communities
interested in participating in the PSA is through the publication of an
annual public call containing information on the minimum requirements
for participation, as well as the delimitation of priority geographic areas
(that could change every year) for the provision of the environmental
service. The application is submitted by the interested communities, ejidos, or
individuals, so they must meet the requirements. One of the most important
is demonstrating legitimate ownership of the area and the absence of legal
disputes.

Regarding technical support, this is paid for by CONAFOR, but the
beneficiary has the opportunity to choose their technical advisor. For this
purpose, there is a list of technical providers. From this list, one can be
selected. This provider is the technical manager responsible for the PSA in
the field, as well as the one who supports the communities in preparing field
annually reports.

2.5.1 Support scheme: Payment for Environmental Services

CONAFOR enters into agreements with beneficiaries so that they receive
PSA benefits for five consecutive years (a cycle). The program selects new
beneficiaries each year based on a points system (priority criteria) and
the number of beneficiaries depends on the available annual budget. Each
year, payment is conditioned on the community’s performance, initially
through remote sensing, since the primary payment criterion is that there
are no changes in land use within the polygon recognized for the PSA.
Payments are made annually, and prior to these payments, activity reports
must be submitted to CONAFOR’s regional offices. In some cases, CONAFOR
conducts field verifications. Reviews are conducted based on the program
of activities that beneficiaries submitted at the start of the contract. To
participate in the PSA, owners or possessors of forest land must have
a proposed area located wholly or partially within the eligible area, as
determined by each annual call for proposals.

2.6 PSA Impact Assessment

At the end of 2017, the National Council for the Evaluation of Social
Development Policy (CONEVAL, by its acronym in Spanish), in conjunction
with the World Bank (WB), published the results of the Impact Evaluation
of the Payment for Environmental Services Program (World Bank, 2017),
using data from beneficiaries from 2011 to 2014. The evaluation aimed to
understand the causal relationship between the program and forest cover
management activities and changes in forest cover. The study also measured
the program’s effects on the social capital and socioeconomic indicators of
participating communities and their households. This evaluation, one of the
few impact evaluations of social programs in Mexico, was conducted with
funding from the World Bank, universities in the United States of America,
and CONAFOR itself. (World Bank 2017)

The most notable results (below) ultimately focused on the socioeconomic
aspects of the participants, stemming from multiple difficulties in terms
of quantitative data and program-specific technical data, which largely
depended on access to up-to-date, high-resolution satellite imagery.

Main findings:

1. Participating communities significantly increased their management
activities to protect forest cover, through actions such as monitoring
illegal logging, building firebreaks, and community activities to control
environmental degradation. It was observed that households spent 2.7
days per year on forest management activities.

2. The program has reached remote rural communities in Mexico,
becoming an additional source of income and investment. It was
estimated that school attendance among young people between the
ages of 16 and 18 increased by 30% to 40%.

3. Conditional payments (payments for results) maintained or even
slightly improved community-level employment and social capital
indicators by approximately 8%.

73

2 Mexico between PES and VCM

4. Forest cover was measured using satellite images; however, due to
technical limitations and the short timeframe of the study, it was
not possible to detect changes below 100%. In this regard, the study
suggests that the program "probably" reduced the deforestation rate
by up to 40% (during the years of participation). Due to the limitations
of the satellite information obtained, the impact measurement is not
precise, and the limited variation in forest cover could also be linked
to the implementation of other programs such as REDD+, especially in
the Yucatan Peninsula region.

5. The study’s results are not suitable for measuring the program’s cost-

6. The increase in forest management activities in participating communi-

7. The findings at the social level are innovative for a technical program

8. At the end of the assessment it was suggested that the program be better

2.7 Voluntary carbon markets in Mexico

The voluntary carbon market has existed in Mexico since 2013. This
mechanism allows companies to convert their services and products to
carbon neutral. This means that the carbon footprint of the product or
service has been offset by the purchase of carbon credits, neutralizing its
environmental impact. Mexico was the first country in Latin America to
implement a carbon tax in 2014 and to develop an emissions trading system
in 2019. Since that year, various state carbon taxes have been established,
several of which have flexible enforcement mechanisms in place.

In Mexico, the carbon market operates through two main components:

1. The Regulated Carbon Market: This was established with the creation
of a federal Emissions Trading System (ETS) following the 2018
reform of the General Law on Climate Change, under the direction of
SEMARNAT. The ETS is a market-based instrument designed to reduce
greenhouse gas emissions. This system is based on the cap-and-trade
principle, in which the government sets a cap or limit on the emissions
that one or more sectors of the economy can produce. Companies, for
their part, must have a permit for each ton of emissions they emit, and
they can therefore receive or trade these permits with other companies.

2. The Voluntary Carbon Market: This market refers to the space
in which actors such as private entities, organizations, and others
participate, allowing them to issue and purchase carbon credits by
registering verified carbon reduction projects under independent
verification standards. Carbon credits generated in Mexico are certified

79

2 Mexico between PES and VCM

by recognized international standards, such as the Verified Carbon
Standard (VCS), the Gold Standard, the American Carbon Registry
(ACR), and the Climate Action Reserve (CAR), ensuring their validity
internationally but without being subject to government regulations.
These credits represent GHG emission reductions achieved in other
regions of the world through initiatives such as renewable energy
projects, reforestation programs, or methane capture technologies.

Once issued, carbon offsets are assets that are freely traded on the mar-
ket. The voluntary market is not centralized, and trading is based on the
international supply and demand for credits. The supply of credits depends
on emissions endorsed by international standards based on their protocols.
Demand is heterogeneous, with different groups of buyers with distinct
needs, primarily driven by offsetting carbon pricing instruments or con-
tributing to voluntary decarbonization commitments and carbon neutrality
goals. Credit buyers range from aeronautical, industrial, mining, and oil com-
panies (carbon-intensive) to service and food production companies, private
banks, and even individuals. Therefore, the volumes, prices, and types of
projects are heterogeneous.

2.7.2 Key actors in carbon markets in Mexico

The life cycle of carbon projects involves the participation of various actors
with roles that often work together. From owners, standards developers,
investors, developers, technical advisors, verifiers, brokers, and final buyers,
each actor plays a fundamental role in the project’s success and long-term
survival.

Owners These are the individuals who certify legal ownership of the gen-
erated credits. They may be ejidos, communities, private individuals,
smallholders, companies, or public organizations. This is the case for
projects in the Agriculture, Forestry, and Other Land Use (AFOLU) sec-
tor. In projects focused on other categories, the owner is the project
aggregator (as is the case with agricultural land) or those who own
the technology or infrastructure that enables emissions reduction or
non-emission.

The owners are responsible for selecting a person as the project
coordinator or representative. In the case of an ejido or community,
this person must be a member of the ejido assembly, and in the case
of private or public organizations, this person may be designated by
the project owner. The person designated as coordinator monitors
the project and represents it throughout the entire process before the
standard.

Standards Entities that provide requirements, methodologies, guidelines,
and protocols to guide projects in the design of activities that avoid
GHG emissions from the atmosphere or reduce GHG emissions for
eligible activities while ensuring social and environmental integrity.
These methodologies should guarantee the additionality and integrity
of projects, allowing all emission reductions to be recognized interna-
tionally on a comparable basis. In Mexico its possible to find the active
participation of these standards:

• Climate Action Reserve, better known as CAR, is approved to serve
as a registry of offset projects for the Compliance Offset Program
under the California Emissions Trading System. Therefore, CAR
has developed its California Compliance Offset Program focused
on ensuring the environmental integrity of GHG emission reduc-
tion projects, thereby creating and supporting financial and envi-
ronmental value in the U.S. carbon market. CAR is the only stan-
dard that has protocols adapted exclusively for Mexico in forestry,
livestock, waste management, and industrial processes. This is
due to the proximity and interest of the state of California in hav-
ing Mexican offset credits. Once certified and issued, the credits
obtained from each project can be traded in the international vol-
untary market, under the offset mechanism of the California Air
Resources Board (CARB). The CAR standard accounts for 8.3% of
projects globally, with projects only in Mexico, the United States,
and Canada. The fact that its materials and methodologies are in
Spanish and adapted to the Mexican context (in aspects such as

registering a project.

• Verified Carbon Standard (VCS) is a Verra program that estab-
lishes the rules and requirements for projects seeking certification.
Verra is a non-profit organization recognized for its protocols and
methodologies applicable to a wide range of sectors and activities.
As a regulatory practice, VCS subjects projects to a public com-
ment period, in addition to standardization, auditing, accounting,
and registration systems. VCS has specific methodologies for a
wide range of projects and accepts the registration of proprietary
methodologies. These methodologies are then submitted to an
evaluation process and then opened to the general public; these
processes typically take approximately two years. Additionally,
VCS reports on alignment with the SDGs and holds the CCB (Cli-
mate, Community, and Biodiversity) and SD VISta (Sustainable
Development Verified Impact Standard) certifications, which ac-
credit contributions to biodiversity conservation, governance, and
the care of vulnerable groups. These offer added value to carbon
credits by offering a higher selling price on the market.

The VCS program does not have specific protocols or methodolo-
gies for Mexico. Each sector and activity has a protocol endorsed
by the global standard, and it is through these that the standard
establishes the basic principles and requirements for project devel-
opment and credit generation. The protocols and methodologies
are published in English, and project documentation must also
be submitted in English. Through the VCS program worldwide
(2022), more than 1,806 certified projects were generated, making
it the most widely used standard, representing 68.5% of registered
projects and 76% of credits reported for 2021. 51 In Mexico (2023),
there were only 33 VCS projects, which represent 13% of the total
projects in the country.

Several project developers are currently seeking to participate in

price paid to the projects. In some cases, the commission is a fixed
amount and is established in the purchase agreement when the credits

to flexible compliance mechanisms or for voluntary carbon neutrality
goals. Some act as investors by directly financing projects with the
interest of retaining all or a portion of the issued credits.

Governments In compliance carbon markets (like the ETS), governments

The goal is to create a market-based mechanism for reducing greenhouse

In administrative and financial issues, the Mexican tax authorities have
not yet issued a ruling on the legal nature of carbon credits, nor is there an
express provision establishing it. Article 5th of the Federal Tax Code (CFF, by
its acronym in Spanish) states that, in the absence of an express tax provision,
the provisions of common federal law shall apply subsidiarily when their
application is not contrary to the nature of tax law.

On this regard, Article 759 of the Federal Civil Code establishes that, in
general terms, all assets not specifically classified as real estate are considered
personal property. This principle includes intangible personal property not

93

2 Mexico between PES and VCM

specifically regulated by the Federal Civil Code, such as intellectual property
rights and credit rights. Despite their lack of physical form, these assets
are considered personal property because they can be bought, sold, and
transferred, just like tangible personal property. In this context, in the
absence of an express tax regulation, carbon credits in Mexico must be treated
as intangible personal property for tax purposes.

Therefore, the tax implications of the sale of carbon credits in Mexico must
comply with the provisions of the Income Tax Law (ITL), which states that
"individuals and legal entities resident in Mexico are required to pay said tax
on income obtained in cash, goods, credit, services, or any other type." In the
case of ejidos, which are considered legal entities for administrative purposes,
according to current legislation, Article 9 of the ITL states that legal entities
must calculate the Income Tax (ISR, by its acronym in Spanish) by applying
a 30% rate to the taxable income obtained for the fiscal year. The sale of
carbon credits must be considered as taxable income for the selling legal
entity, considering the total price received upon such sale. Additionally, the
legal entity may deduct, in the fiscal year in which this occurs, the portion not
yet deducted from the asset subject to the sale. For legal entities resident in
Mexico, there is no withholding obligation for purchasers or intermediaries
involved in the purchase and sale of carbon credits.

To verify incomes, participating ejidos are required to issue an online tax
receipt (CFDI, by its acronym in Spanish) for their activities or request a
CFDI for their payments, specifically to determine their accruable income,
support their authorized deductions, and comply with their Value Added Tax
obligations. (L´opez-Velarde P´erez and Salam´an S´anchez 2024)

out this type of procedure, for which they need the support of a trusted
accountant, since many of the ejidatarios need support with reading and

3.1 Research methodology

A scientific approach that enables us to comprehend the social, economic, and
environmental dynamics that define voluntary carbon markets is necessary
to examine how they function in Mexico. Understanding the experiences of
the main players provides a starting point that may lead to future focused
research initiatives.

3.1.1 Techniques employed

This research was structured using an exploratory-descriptive approach. The
exploratory approach was selected due to the limited amount of scientific
research currently available about the functioning of voluntary carbon
markets in the southeast of the country. First, because they are relatively new,
and second, because most studies focus on experiences in the State of Oaxaca,
(Shapiro–Garza 2025) a pioneer in this field with extensive experience in
community forest management.

Nevertheless, the social and economic traits of the southeastern area ex-
hibit distinct trends, primarily because of the diverse population (indigenous
and mestizo) that comprise, and additionally due to the recent government
efforts in building infrastructure, specifically the Mayan Train (Tren Maya)
along with the swift growth of agricultural lands. Regarding the Tren Maya,
which represents a significant investment aimed at fostering economic de-

3 Study case

velopment in the region over the coming years, it is anticipated to stimulate
investments in tourism and services, while also intensifying the demand on
the region’s ecosystem services.

Understanding some of the basic elements of these novel experiences is
the goal of this exploratory study, which may inspire additional research
that will produce suggestions for enhancements for stakeholders engaged
in these mechanisms.

Regarding the descriptive approach, this study was conducted to under-

3.2 Overview of the Mexican State Campeche

3.2.1 Campeche State

Campeche, estado (state), is located in the southeastern of Mexico, on the
western part of the Yucatan Peninsula. It is bounded to the north and east by
the state of Yucatan, to the east by the state of Quintana Roo, to the south
by Guatemala, to the southwest by the state of Tabasco, and to the west by
the Bay of Campeche, an inlet of the Gulf of Mexico. Campeche city is the
state capital and a major port.

In pre-colonial times, the region was called Ah-Kim-Pech, which means
"place of snakes and ticks" in the Mayan language. It was founded in the 3rd
century AD by migratory groups from Guatemala, Honduras, and Chiapas
who populated the state, founding important cities such as Edzna, Santa Rosa
Xtampak, and later Calakmul and Becan, among others.

During the 19th century, Campeche’s main sources of wealth were its
industry and maritime trade, sugarcane cultivation, and the harvesting of

102

3 Study case

dye wood2 in the Isla del Carmen region. Dye wood, destined for the textile
centers of England and France, constituted the peninsula’s main export
product in the 19th century. The boom of dye wood began to decline in
the late 19th century with the development of synthetic dyes, which were
cheaper and easier to produce. This led to an economic crisis in Campeche
that depended on its export. (INAFED 2019)

Manufacturing provides one-third of the state’s income, and services
(including tourism, trade, and government jobs) account for more than
one-fourth. Major manufactures include refined petroleum, extracted from
offshore wells; forest products, made mostly from local hardwoods; and
processed foods. Shrimp trawling and other commercial fishing are also
important. The state is linked to central Mexico by railroad, highway, and
air. Although the great majority of its population is urbanized, Campeche is
sparsely settled and one of the least-populous Mexican states.

Campeche’s relief consists of a low limestone plain broken only by rivers
in the humid south and by the Puuc hills in the arid north, where deep
grottoes hold the main water supply for crops and livestock raising. Stands of
tropical semi-deciduous forest lie east and south of Campeche city; treetops
can reach heights of 65 to 100 feet (20 to 30 metres), notably at Calakmul
Biosphere Reserve. Most of the rivers in the south, including the Golondrinas-
Candelaria system, drain into Terminos Lagoon on the Gulf of Mexico. At
the lagoon’s entrance is Ciudad del Carmen, the chief port and petroleum
depot of the area.

Campeche is a prominent forestry entity, with a land area of 5,759,000
hectares, of which 4.4 million are covered by tropical forest ecosystems,
including 196,000 hectares of mangroves, equivalent to 25% of Mexico’s
mangroves. Campeche’s physical environment is particularly favorable for
agricultural, forestry, and fishing activities. Its natural resources make it one
of the most diverse and abundant states. The climate is warm, sub-humid,
with summer rains and a rainfall of 1,300 to 1,500 mm; the rainy season is

3.5 Project: Use of carbon market schemes in

In both ejidos, the project main objective is to develop a carbon capture
protocol for the conservation and restoration of ecosystem services present
in areas of the Calakmul rainforest. The long-term objective of these projects
is to discourage land-use change by engaging forest owners in market-based
instruments that allow them to receive profitable economic returns from the
sale of carbon offsets. The main purpose of these earnings is to support the
execution of action plans aimed at increasing carbon sinks through territorial
management, active conservation, and climate activities that help mitigate
climate change. The project also aims to make sure that the advantages will
help households’ financial circumstances while promoting social harmony
and community empowerment in relation to decisions about local forest
resources.

4.1 Discussion

The findings derived from the qualitative research conducted through inter-
views, focus groups, and fieldwork light up critical dimensions of voluntary
carbon markets in Mexico, revealing a complex interplay of governance, eco-
nomic viability, regulatory frameworks, and socio-environmental challenges.
Each finding warrants deeper exploration to understand its implications for
both local communities and broader international environmental objectives.
The first significant finding emphasizes the importance of strong commu-
nity governance as a mechanism for ensuring the long-term sustainability
of carbon market initiatives. Effective governance structures are integral for
fostering community engagement and ensuring equitable participation in
carbon projects. This suggests that capacity building must be prioritized to
empower local stakeholders. Initiatives should be designed not only to facil-
itate immediate project outcomes but to enhance the participants’ skills and
confidence, thereby promoting resilience and adaptive capacity in the face
of environmental and economic changes.

The involvement of the Tax Administration Service (SAT, by its acronym
in Spanish) is essential for delineating a clear tax framework for carbon
projects. Without a well-defined tax regime, projects may face financial hur-
dles that could stifle their development or deter participants. Environmental
policymakers must engage in active dialogue with SAT and other stake-
holders to design a tax system that incentivize participation while ensuring
compliance and transparency.

138

4 Proposals to improve VCM projects in Mexico

The bottleneck associated with the verification process poses a signifi-
cant barrier to the effectiveness of carbon markets in Mexico. With response
times stretching up to 12 months, this lag can dissuade landowners from
engaging in carbon projects, thereby undermining potential environmental
benefits. Streamlining verification processes will not only alleviate partic-
ipant frustrations but also foster a more dynamic and responsive carbon
market environment, enhancing overall credibility and appeal for both local
and international stakeholders.

The social and environmental implications of infrastructure projects such
as the Tren Maya cannot be overlooked. While such developments promise
economic opportunities, they also pose risks of ecological degradation and
social displacement. Furthermore, as tourism and urbanization increases,
there is a pressing need for sustainable management strategies to protect
the region’s ecological integrity. Decision makers from different government
agencies and local citizens must carry out joint territorial planning to achieve
orderly, fair and inclusive development.

The substantial upfront

investment required to launch a carbon
project—approximately $70,000,coupled with the expectation of a two-year
recovery period raises questions regarding the financial sustainability of
these initiatives. While green bonds offer an avenue for funding, the mis-
match between the timing of financial disbursements and the operational
needs of forestry initiatives poses a significant challenge. Addressing these
financial barriers is essential to impulse new projects, for example in “blue
carbon” sphere.

Finally, the prospects of international purchasers disengaging from the
Mexican carbon market due to security concerns—exacerbated by organized
crime in forest regions—highlight a critical risk factor. If left unaddressed,
these security issues could lead to a diminished interest from both local ejidos
and foreign investors, undermining the potential for widespread adoption
of sustainable practices in carbon markets. It is imperative that stakeholders
work collaboratively to enhance security measures.

In conclusion, the findings from this research underscore the multifaceted
nature of voluntary carbon markets in Mexico. Addressing the identified

139

4 Proposals to improve VCM projects in Mexico

challenges—ranging from governance and taxation to verification and
security—requires a coordinated approach that engages all stakeholders. By
prioritizing community involvement, refining regulatory frameworks, and
fostering financial viability, Mexico can strengthen its position within the
global carbon market while contributing to sustainable development goals.

4.2 Proposals

1. Targeting PSA for ejidos, communities, or landowners with an area less
than 10,000 hectares or located within arid and semi-arid ecosystems.
One of the lessons learned is that the initial investment in carbon
forestry projects is profitable when working on compact areas larger
than 5,000 hectares. The price offers per ton are better on larger
areas that produce more carbon credits. Therefore, one way to
optimize the PSA resource is by supporting ejidos, communities, and
landowners with relatively small areas, who would face difficulties
accessing these markets. Likewise, the CO2 absorption capacity of
arid ecosystems is limited. Vegetation will not attain the saturation of
carbon sequestration capacity without appropriate climatic conditions,
in that sense lack of rainfall causes physiological stress and limits
vegetation photosynthesis in most arid regions. (Sha et al. 2022) This
characteristic makes them unattractive to buyers.

2. Redesign of the Local Payment Mechanisms of PSA to be a mandatory
instrument and not voluntary actions, with specific restrictions about
the minimum surface and with a minimum term of 5 years. One
of the main objectives of PES –in long-term- is that action for
environmental management should be moved from public to private
hands, building on the assumption that beneficiaries and providers
of a given environmental service will find an agreement based on
their common interest for conservation. (Pirard 2012) Greater business
support for the ecosystem services is urgently required. There is a need
for corporations to move beyond energy efficiency and recycling efforts
performed internally, to supporting ex situ environmental management

4 Proposals to improve VCM projects in Mexico

activities. These “support” have often been limited to one-off and
philanthropic activities that typically seek to engender positive media
attention, reputation enhancement, and brand value, such as tree
planting events. Now, corporations should increase their awareness
and management of the ecosystem services upon which some of their
core business operations may rely. In this sense, a corporation would
pay offsite landowners to change their land management practices in
ways that deliver the ecosystem service that it desires. The ecosystem
service should be a logical and strategic choice and will vary depending
on the type of business profile (Thompson 2021).

3. Green Bonds Program – VCM (with a single-digit interest rate), for
developers who promote carbon projects in ejidos larger than 10,000
ha. Access to finance is a crucial enabler for transforming the forest
sector and achieving sustainable forest management while enhancing
the value derived from forest resources and associated value chains.
Although bonds are commonly used in many sectors, there ares still
barriers to their use in the forest sector. There is a special need
to increase the dialogue and engagement between investors, policy–
makers, and forest stakeholder, like project developers. All sides use
using different language to describe their needs and expectation of
forest finance, and without clear understanding of how forest projects
work and what they are intended to do, suspicion and mistrust can
easily grew up.

4. Development of allometric equations for Mexican endemic species. There
is a great need to develop species-specific carbon stock models which
will produce improved carbon stock estimates. A critical step in the
fight against climate change is the use of allometric equations to
calculate the aboveground biomass of forests. Forest biomass and
carbon storage have been estimated using generalized allometric
equations. However, because of environmental variance, using a
generalized allometric equation to predict the biomass of various
woods creates ambiguity. To precisely measure the biomass, species-
specific allometric equations must be developed. (Daba and Soromessa

4 Proposals to improve VCM projects in Mexico

2019) These initiative must be led by the federal government, to
present official information to the verifiers and standards in order to
be considered as a national request.

5. Promote the participation of Mexican companies in the purchase of
certified carbon credits through tax incentives (limited to 30% of the GHG
emissions that would incur the tax). It’s possible to find international
experiences that suggest this tax incentives can generate good results.
Countries like Singapure and Chile increased their use of domestic
carbon offsets in 2024, taking advantage of the country’s carbon tax
system to meet emissions targets more affordably. Under Chile’s carbon
tax, firms that emit more than 25,000 tonnes of CO2 equivalent per
year must either pay a levy of $5 per tonne or use verified carbon
credits from local projects to offset their emissions. For many, offsets
are becoming the preferred route. This system generates significant
benefits for the country and its inhabitants. (Carbon Units 2025)

6. Strengthen the MRV system in Mexico to support projects with official
data at the country level. “A ton is a tone”. In carbon markets and
NDC national accountability, MRV plays a crucial role because well-
functioning mechanisms require trust in the market. Carbon market
participants need the assurance that a ton reported GHG in one country
is real, an accurate and reliable measurement. MRV systems are key
elements to guarantee transparency, precision and comparability on
climate change information. (GIZ 2017). Innovations in MRV can help
expand climate action worldwide and unleash the potential of climate
finance and the carbon marketplace to combat climate change, these
is an importante reason to ensure results are real before payments are
made. (World Bank 2022b)

7. Organize regional forums for experience sharing with a focus on technical
aspects of active forest ecosystem conservation, voluntary carbon mar-
kets, sustainable agriculture, soil restoration methods, governance, and
financial education. These initiatives must be led by the federal gov-
ernment with the support of local governments and other key stake-

holders. Peer-to-peer learning in a networked community is beneficial
for adult learners, according to the findings. Through sharing stories,
forming identities through discussions, and fostering a sense of "to-
getherness" within the group, these forms of interaction and learning
methodologies can foster the construction of knowledge and aid in the
development of reflective skills and community. (Guldberg 2018)

8. A nationwide registration of transaction platform of carbon bonds, would
make it clear where credits are going in the market – traceability and
transparency-. This would also highlight deficiencies, promote more
resilient markets, and give a national perspective of the supply capacity
of projects. These kind of platform is crucial for several scientific
and technical reasons, promote transparency, efficiency, and a better
accountability in carbon markets. These instruments could prevent
the double counting and ensuring integrity, could provide in real the
records of transactions, ensuring that buyers can verify the legitimacy
of carbon bonds and data helps policymakers analyze trends, assess
the effectiveness of carbon pricing and adjust climate strategies more
dynamically.

5 Conclusions

The Voluntary Carbon Market (VCM) has emerged as a promising mech-
anism to complement existing forest conservation program in Southeast
Mexico, a region that provides important ecosystem services but facing de-
forestation pressures. This research has examined the VCM potential as an
alternative to stade-led Payment for Ecosystem Services (PSA, by its acronym
in Spanish), analyzing its characteristics, limitations, and main challenges in
daily implementation. The findings highlight both opportunities and risks as-
sociated with carbon markets, particularly in the context of Mexico’s unique
socio-environmental dynamics, where ejidos play a central role in forest
governance.

It’s imperative to highlight that this research has ecosystem services
as its starting point, hence, while economic valuation and market-based
mechanisms offer pathways to address the problems of ecosystem services,
the foundation of their success is related to addressing methodological
flaws, ensuring equity, and fostering international cooperation. As Dasgupta
(Dasgupta 2021) argues, a fundamental shift in economic thinking is urgently
to prioritize natural capital and align human welfare with sustainable
development. Safeguarding ecosystem services is not only an economic -
environmental issue, is a cornerstone on long-term of social resilience.

Mexico is a forest country, 70% of the territory is covered by template
forest, jungles, mangroves, arid and semiarid ecosystems, they are vital for
biodiversity, water supply, food and raw materials, medicine substances,
carbon sequestration an rural livelihoods. However, deforestation, illegal
logging, and the growth of agricultural and livestock areas is undermining
their capacity to provide essential ecosystem services. The Payment for
Ecosystem Services (PSA) program, and recently voluntary carbon markets
have emerged as economical instruments to address these challenges, yet

144

5 Conclusions

their effectiveness is constrained by institutional, politic, social and economic
barriers and main challenges.

The PSA program, implemented 22 years ago, has demonstrated positive
impacts, in social sphere and the reduction of deforestation rates in the
supported areas. However, its reach is every year more limited, in 2024, only
1.3% of the eligible forest area was supported by the program, reflecting
budget cuts and competing with other social programs. Meanwhile, since
2013, VCM in Mexico have grown, in 2023 the country had 248 projects,
87.5% focused in forestry, covering around 1.6 million hectares. (SEMARNAT
et al. 2024) Despite these growth, challenges exists, including regulatory
ambiguity, high transaction cost, high taxes, policies with contradictory
objectives in the same territories, and the lack support from government
entities to ejidos and communities.

The case study in two ejidos in Campeche, helped to illustrated how VCMs
can empower marginalized communities while mitigating climate change,
but their success and long- term projects depend on addressing structural
inequities. The ejidos’ ability to reinvest carbon revenues in surveillance
equipment and eco-tourism projects demonstrates the potential for market-
based mechanisms to enhance both environmental and socio-economic
outcomes. However, without systemic adjustments —such as equitable tax
policies, robust MRV systems, and cooperation between local and federal
governments —these projects risk of being actions very short-term effects,
causing distrust in the international markets and with the consequence of
more human pressure on ecosystems.

To enhance these mechanisms, Mexico must strengthen institutional
coordination, streamline regulations, and prioritize equitable benefit-sharing,
particularly for forest-dependent communities. Integrating efforts from
PSA and VCMs with broader rural development strategies could amplify
their impact, ensuring both environmental conservation and socioeconomic
progress. As climate urgency grows, the country’s ability to refine these tools
will be critical for achieving its NDCs and safeguarding its forest ecosystems
for present and future generations.

In the coming years, special attention should be paid to the Tren Maya
project. This infrastructure exemplifies the tension between development
and conservation. While it has already brought short-term economic
benefits (e.g., commerce and tourism), if the second stage of these projects
disorderly development prevails, without inter-institutional coordination
and without integrated management of the territory, the consequence will
be the acceleration of deforestation and habitat fragmentation of this fragile
ecosystems. The ejidatarios expressed concerns about unregulated urban
expansion and its impact on ecosystem services, calling for integrated
territorial planning. These results are consistent with criticisms of "economic
growth” paradigms, which frequently put financial gain ahead of ecological
and social integrity.

Mexico’s VCMs stand at a crossroads. The scientific, empirical, and
institutional knowledge accumulated during these years of PSA and the
moment of innovation that Mexico is experiencing through its active
participation in global climate change strategies constitute a turning point
to redirect efforts, improve processes, and make resources more efficient –
which are always scarce – to transform the carbon markets into real tools for
sustainable development ensuring that conservation benefits are equitably
shared.

As the global demand for carbon offsets grows, Mexico has an opportunity
to been a main actor of these initiatives, demonstrating how community-
centered approaches can reconcile environmental preservation with eco-
nomic and environmental justice.

Finally, the researcher aimed to provide a preliminary understanding of
the reality faced by the Campeche ejidos, who have received little attention
despite their significance to the environment, through qualitative field
research and approximation work with key actors. The goal of this study
was to set the stage for future efforts and, more importantly, to act as a point
of reference for closely observing the social and environmental impacts of
the policies and projects developed in southeast Mexico.
