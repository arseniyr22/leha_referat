Abstract

Numerous studies identify the Russian educational system as one of the most rig-

orous and competitive worldwide. Consequently, Russian adolescents frequently expe-

rience intense stress, often resulting in poorer average psychological well-being. This

study seeks to examine whether sustained stress enhances or impairs academic perfor-

mance when the perceived stakes are exceptionally high. To address this question, I

analyze a positive and negative channels of influence, using an unexpected cancellation

of the 2020 national stage of the All-Russian Economics Olympiad as a natural exper-

iment to compare stress and performance across year cohorts. Using the Difference-in-

Differences approach on self-collected data, I find that affected participants performed

significantly better than previous cohorts. This suggests that the positive effect out-

weighed the negative one. Subsequently, a Two-Stage Least Squares (2SLS) approach

is employed, utilizing pet ownership as a instrumental variable to isolate the distinct

effects of stress. The results suggest that the adverse impact of stress on academic

performance is negligible and statistically insignificant.

Introduction

”Out of life’s school of war — what doesn’t kill me, makes me
stronger...” — Friedrich Nietzsche, ”Twilight of the Idols” (1888).

According to numerous studies, Russian students face a much heavier academic load
than their Western peers. For instance, for Economic Co-operation and Development (2016)
reports that 15-year-olds in Russia spend about 9.7 hours per week doing homework, which
is twice more than the OECD mean. The stakes get so high mainly because of the extreme
competition for university spots. As a result, students become more susceptible to high
levels of anxiety and mental disorders. Chumakov et al. (2021) interviewed St. Petersburg
medical students to find out that almost 75% of them showed burnout on the Oldenburg
Burnout Inventory, —far above 43% burnout rate reported by U.S. medical students.

However, it is still not clear whether such highly competitive atmosphere promote better
results via raising one’s motivation to study or, on the contrary, it causes exhaustion of mental
resource and subsequent academic impairment. In this study, I address the ambiguity of the
long-lasting influence of stress on academic achievement by collecting data on participants of
intellectual competition in economics and using the Covid-19 lockdown as an external shock
that caused an increase of anxiety.

Except for the lack of economic research on the topic, there are also several features
that make my study valuable and unique. First, it uses a natural experiment that speaks in
favor of its internal validity. Second, the reviewed literature indicates that nations with a
highly competitive academic environment, such as China and Russia, might display outcomes
distinct from Western countries (Ye et al. (2019)), where academic pressure tends to be less
intense and stakes are not as high as in China or Russia1. Therefore, the results I expect to
obtain will contribute to the diversity of the context, as the majority of studies in the field
focus on European populations.

2 Literature Review

2.1 Why stress is related to economics?

Although the influence of stress on a human’s body and behavior is widely discussed in
psychological and physician literature, one may doubt why stress is also an economics-

1China and Russia are high-stakes country also because of high social inequality in these countries. Both

in China and Russia education is one of the main social lifts that are available to low-income families.

3

related problem. There are two main clues for that: firstly, stress puts an enormous amount
of pressure on the healthcare system of any modern country in the world. An economical
burden of stress was considered in great detail by Kessler and Greenberg (2002) in the
article ”The economic burden of anxiety and stress disorders”. The authors propose that,
although estimated by Greenberg et al. (1999) as much as $42 billion, economic consequences
of stress are indeed even higher. This is because of the great variety of ways stress can affect
human health. When one is trying to estimate the economic burden of anxiety, it becomes
difficult to intertwine between stress itself and other mental and physical disorders caused
by stress. For instance, chronic stress affects the immune system, hence increases the risks
of infections, cardiovascular disease, depression, drug addiction and other chronic diseases
(Maraksina et al. 2024). This speaks in favor of a considerable underestimation of the
economic burden of distress.

The most important features of stress that make it so costly are its high life-time preva-
lence, chronicity and early age of onset. Following the results of the Midlife Development
in the US (MIDUS) survey, Kessler and Greenberg (2002) argue that distress is the fourth
most prevalent disorder that interferes with one’s daily functioning. In addition, it has a
uniquely early age of onset, with a median age ”in less than 15 years of age”, which means
stress disorders start to intervene in human lives from adolescence, while the rest chronic
physical disorders occur much later.

In addition to all discussed above, stress is well known for its deteriorating effect on
human productivity and performance. Following the National Comorbidity Survey (NCS),
Kessler and Greenberg (2002) found that while stress has a significant effect on employee
work cuts (defined as missing part of a day or working less efficiently than usual), no impact
was established in relation to work loss (defined as missing a full day of work), which implies
that stress indirectly decreases the product of the label, not through fewer working hours but
through decreased efficiency. It makes this effect even harder to compute, as it is revealed
in the form of opportunity costs or neglected profits, but its effects are, indeed, substantial.
Taking $42 billion of the initial estimations of the annual cost of anxiety disorders (Greenberg
et al., 1999) and adding up $100 billion of annual excluded costs of long-term opportunity
costs, Kessler and Greenberg (2002) end up with estimating an economical burden of anxiety
as high as $142 billion per year for the US, 2002. Certainly, reducing these costs is what
economists should do.

2.2 Stress as a performance impairer

The impact of stress on performance (both at work and at school) is the topic that draws
the attention of researchers from different fields. Articles on this theme are published in
psychological, medical journals, journals of human resources and social sciences, journals
of management, and business. Economists may be interested in the consequences for edu-
cational achievements, since it is strictly related to subsequent school dropout, university
acceptance rates, career proficiency, and income level, which are all economic indicators. I
will focus next on the noneconomic articles that use valid research design due to the scarcity
of econometrics analysis in this topic: although surveys in this topic are indeed abundant,
the majority of them use correlation or regression analysis that does not actually lead to
causal link between two variables. Furthermore, since the link between stress and perfor-
mance (either in the workplace or academic settings) might not be straightforward, as we
will explore below, the common approaches employed in most of the examined studies are
not entirely dependable. All of the discussed above shed light on why economical research
with convincing research design is needed in this topic.

In their enlightening paper titled ”Mental Health and Academic Performance: A Study
on Selection and Causation Effects from Childhood to Early Adulthood”, Agnafors et al.
(2020) illustrate the process by which children exhibiting behavioral and emotional issues at
a young age are socially selected into groups with higher risk of academic impairments, such
as achieving lower grades and lacking eligibility for higher education. By so, they challenge
results of numerous physiological surveys which claimed a negative relationship between
emotional distress and subsequent academic failures to be causal. They also confirm results
by Halpern-Manners et al. (2016) that show that education achievements do not influence
mental health.

Halpern-Manners et al. (2016) in their work ”The Relationship between Education and
Mental Health: New Evidence from a Discordant Twin Study” provide a convincing evidence
that the causal relationship between education level and distress, if presence, is one-sided.
Specifically, they refuse any causal effect of educational achievements on mental health (anx-
iety in particular). This contradicts previously obtained results that told us the discussed
causal relationship is positive as ”more education generates additional social and economic re-
sources, leading to fewer stressors, better coping strategies, and more autonomous lifestyles”.
Indeed, Halpern-Manners et al. 2016 proved that this link ’derives from ”preexisting” differ-
ences between individuals (the selection hypothesis)’, thus no causality occurs. The authors’
results are highly robust and trustworthy due to their research design: they use a large

5

dataset of identical adult twins from the US, and get rid of genetic traits and family charac-
teristics bias by regressing within-twin-pair differences in mental health on within-twin-pair
differences in education after controlling for covariates. By so, Halpern-Manners et al. (2016)
partial out any omitted variables (in fact, it is a fixed-effect regression model) to make the
residual orthogonal to schooling, and thus provide an unbiased estimation of the true ed-
ucational effect on mental health. They show that the true causal effect of education on
mental health is zero, conditional on environmental and genetic features of respondents,
which means that all previous results that stood for the presence of such a link were at-
tributed to the preexisting respondents’ characteristics, or in other words were biased. This
important implementation is specifically important for my further research as it enables to
deny potential reverse-causality problems.

2.3 Types of stressors that can affect academic performance

Stress is a concept that implies several types of stressors at the same time, but it is neces-
sary to differentiate them on closer inspection. This is why I will describe the main types of
stressors in relation to educational success next. Afterwards, the isolated effect of academic
stress will be reviewed, as it is the type of stress that causes the most divergent results.
Chinese meta-analysis article named ”A Review on the Relationship Between Chinese Ado-
lescents’ Stress and Academic Achievement” by Ye et al. (2019) proposes several main stres-
sors that disturb highly motivated school students from China. The authors state that
students face tough academic competition since early childhood as they strive to enroll at
a prestigious university, although only a half of them eventually succeed. This context is
especially close to my research field, as I study the effect of stress on a sample of motivated
school students from Russia in a highly competitive atmosphere, therefore it may be reason-
able to assume that a list of the most severe stressors is close to the one Ye et al. (2019)
propose.

To be more precise, Ye et al. (2019) name the three most serious stressors for Chinese
adolescents, they are academic stress, financial stress, and interpersonal stress. The last
one was not shown to have any effect on academic achievements (except for teacher-student
relationship issues), so financial and academic stress is what interests me more. A significant
negative relationship was established between family financial stress and academic achieve-
ments of Chinese school students (Zhang (2010)). However, the link between academic
stress and achievement is not so obvious. While some research stands for negative linear
relationship between academic stress and academic achievement (Kaplan et al. (2005), Ye

6

et al. (2014)), Ye et al. (2019) argue that the relationship may be of an inverted U-shape
(Middlebrooks and Audage (2008)).

2.4 Research based on Russian data

Finally, Fomina et al. (2024) have published a study that highlights Russia-specific aspects
regarding the impact of academic stress on performance. Their findings corroborate earlier
results by Matyushkina (2016), and (2023), who suggested that slight doses of academic
stress have a beneficial impact. Essentially, prior studies depicted an inverted U-shaped cor-
relation concerning academic performance, and this was reaffirmed by Fomina et al. (2024).
Fomina et al. (2024) define academic stress as student’s psychological reaction to various
learning difficulties, which they perceive as not corresponding to the individual resources
(cognitive, personal) to cope with a difficult situation. They claim that exams are the most
severe stressors in this context and they affect one’s performance long before the exam itself
via academical anxiety, especially it is true in the period of late adolescence when a teenager
faces the highest uncertainty about the future (16-23 years old). Fomina et al. (2024) draw
special attention to the higher prevalence of uncertainty stress in the context of influence on
academic performance, especially examining post-Covid data. Despite many foreign studies,
the authors also report that Russian adolescents show a significantly different relationship
between academic stress and achievements: students with higher academic stress show higher
academic performance, but often have a higher probability of suicide risk (Symaniuk et al.
(2019)). The uniqueness of the Russian results is one more reason to conduct such a survey.
In their study Symaniuk et al. (2019), the researchers examine high school students from
a prestigious Russian lyceum. They assert that students attending elite schools are subjected
to increased stress due to heightened academic demands. These ambitious students often
engage in numerous intellectual competitions and Olympiads, which significantly augment
their distress.
Interestingly, they found a positive correlation between stress levels and
academic success of Russian students. Such a result sharply contrasts with international
findings that typically indicate a negative relationship between these factors. It is not clear
whether this positive correlation is attributed to the sampling features, selection bias or
primitive instrumental tools: Spearman’s correlation was chosen for the analysis, though it
was already discussed above that the studied relationship may not be linear. Moreover, the
majority of the respondents (76,1%) reported low or extremely low stress levels that does not
coincide with the authors’ assumption about increased stress levels in elite Russian schools.
In addition, the percent of respondents with high or extremely high stress levels is too small

7

(6,6% or 13 respondents) to draw any unbiased conclusions, thus further research is needed
to examine this topic once again.

Summing-up, there is a whole in the research literature examining causal effect of anxiety
disorders on educational achievements, albeit it is a significant economic issue that deter-
mines one’s productivity, performance and increases healthcare costs dramatically (Kessler,
and Greenberg 2002). Inverse association between stress and academic success was shown
by Agnafors et al. (2020), though it is not causal. The absence of reverse causal influence of
academic achievements on distress has been established by Halpern-Manners et al. (2016),
which eliminates the risk of reverse causality problem in the further economics analysis. Ye
et al. (2019) describe differences between 3 types of stress in the academic context, and
name list of obstacles that mitigate negative effect of stress. Fomina et al. (2024) name key
academic stressors for Russian students, give evidence of an inverted U-shape relationship
between stress and academic achievements, and poses increased importance of uncertainty
stress. Symanyuk et al. (2019) finds unexpected positive correlation between the two studied
variables, even though it contradicts foreign studies. However, their results cause doubts due
to small sample size and primitive research analysis tools.

3 Research question

In the current situation, Russian students find themselves living and studying in the midst of
significant uncertainties: the Covid-19 pandemic, the abrupt outbreak of the Russia-Ukraine
war, and the resulting educational restrictions have significantly impacted their access to
resources and opportunities. These restrictions include limited access to major educational
platforms such as Coursera, LinkedIn, and YouTube, along with diminished chances to study
abroad or win international scholarships.2

Such limitations heighten anxiety about the future and simultaneously raise the stakes of
intellectual competitions. With fewer opportunities available, the path to academic success
becomes more competitive and uncertain. Yet, the effect on performance remains ambiguous:
while long-lasting stress can hinder academic preparation, the higher perceived importance
of each opportunity may also enhance motivation to prepare better. In this study I strive
to answer the question: which effect prevails for highly motivated Russian adolescents when
stakes are high? It is important to note that I concentrate on the continuous consequences

of stress, so that I do not measure anxiety levels during the competition itself. Instead, I
focus on the permanent raise of stress preceding it beforehand.

While a detailed mathematical model elaborating on my research question is provided
in the Appendix, I will now briefly outline the basic ideas. The mechanism that I am
about to incorporate is presented graphically in Figure 1 and it is as follows: first, an
unfavorable shock, or a stressor, occurs3. Stress is a body’s reaction to the stressor, and
this reaction process uses our body’s resources, which may lead to their exhaustion. As a
result, by experiencing stress one has less energy to concentrate on some other tasks such as
preparation to the intellectual competition or solving a non-trivial issue. I name this effect
as an effect of stress. At the same time, unfavorable events are usually unfavorable because
they limit one’s opportunities or make stakes higher. Therefore, higher stakes may motivate
one to enhance the effort and to do better. I name this effect as a motivation effect.

In the frames of this study I also examine how individual differences in stress-resistant
traits of character influence the outcomes. By investigating varying responses to academic
stress among students with different level of stress resilience, this paper seeks to contribute
to a more comprehensive understanding of the relationship between emotional factors and
academic performance. By so, I formulate two main hypothesis:

1. H0 : A deteriorating effect of stress prevails on a motivational effect of stress on Russian

data.

2. H0 : Magnitudes of motivation and stress effects vary across the respondents’ stress-
resistance traits, where a motivation effect prevails for more stress-resistant adolescents,
and is obeyed by the stress effect for the less resistant students.

4.1 Background

In this section I describe the context of data that I am using, and also comment on the
necessary background. The overall idea is to use a cancellation of the final stage of the
All-Russian Olympiad in economics in 2020 as an exogenous shock that triggered a lasting
increase in both distress and motivation among adolescents subjected to it. I compare their
results with the results of their peers not exposed to the shock and claim that the difference
is attributed to the sum of stress and motivation effects. Later I try to distinguish between
them using 2SLS approach.

To start with, the All-Russian Olympiad in Economics is a prestigious academic competi-
tion aimed at high school students across Russia. Participants are engaged in various rounds,
starting from school-level competitions, and culminating in national finals. The complexity
of the tasks increases accordingly.

The Olympiad covers a wide range of topics in economics, such as microeconomics,
macroeconomics, game theory, and applied economics. It serves not only as a platform for
students to showcase their knowledge and skills but it also provides opportunities to obtain
scholarships for further education in economics. For instance, winners of the national final of
the Olympiad receive an opportunity to enroll in any undergraduate program in economics

in Russia absolutely for free.4 It is especially true of talented provincial school students who
are willing to gain a top-ranked bachelor degree from prestigious Russian universities but
cannot afford it.

Like many other academic and competitive events, the final round of All-Russian Olympiad
in Economics was canceled in 2020 due to the Covid-19 pandemic restrictions. Moreover,
dates of the final stage of the Olympiad were repeatedly rescheduled before it was eventually
pronounced that the Olympiad would not be held that year at all. To be more precise,
intra-school, municipal and regional rounds that precede the most serious national stage
were successfully held before the Covid-19 pandemic in Russia, so it was only all-Russian
round that was not held.

Several features of the Olympiad should be taken into account to acknowledge why can-
cellation of the final tours was an issue. As mentioned above, victory in the last national
round provides all winners with a unique opportunity to enroll in any university for free, yet
winning any previous round of the Olympiad gives nothing but the ability to compete in
the subsequent tour. It is also worth noting that the privilege of enrolling in any economics
university remains for three years, which means that if one wins the Olympiad5 in the 9th
grade of school, they are still able to use their privilege at the end of the 11th grade when
enrolling at the University6. Though passing Unified State Exam is a requirement to com-
plete school education, it does not play any significant role if a child has won the All-Russian
Olympiad. That is, a victory in the Olympiad provides unique benefits that can bypass the
usual Unified State Exam requirements.

Considering all of the above, it is clear why the national stage of the Olympiad could
not be quietly canceled: the stakes for the participants were simply too high, therefore all
the 11th-graders who were supposed to participate in the final stage7 received a privilege

4.2 Data Collection

My empirical research is mostly based on the self-collected data as beyond the individual
Olympiad results I also needed personal characteristics and an individual assessment of stress
for each respondent to use them as controls in the subsequent regression analysis. Although
data containing academic results and perceived stress estimations might potentially exist
elsewhere, I chose to gather data myself to incorporate an exogenous cancellation of the
All-Russian Olympiad to increase internal validity of the model.

I have spread my questionnaire via three main channels: first, I published it in Telegram
groups for the Olympiad participants of 2019 and 2021 years respectively. These chats unite
the majority of participants and do not contain any non-participants. Second, I distributed
the link with my Google survey form among NES students via our mailing system. I am very
thankful to Anton Suvorov for his invaluable assistance in spreading my Google form among
BAE NES 2022 - 2027 to cover as many years as possible to track a historical trend. Olympiad
participants of 2019 and 2021 are BAE 2023 and BAE 2025, respectively. Finally, I used my
personal set of network to spread the survey among students from other faculties: I asked
my friends from the HSE Faculty of Economic Sciences to spread my questionnaire among
their surrounding in order to obtain more respondents who have not won the Olympiad.

A list of questions used in the survey can be found in the Appendix. It combines personal
questions about social background and living conditions during the Covid-19 lockdown with

Initially proposed by Cohen et al. (1983), The Perceived Stress

(2016) for a Russian sample. Marakshina et al. (2024) and Zolotareva (2023) proved this
survey set to be efficient for adolescents as well. Implying that one should not recall their
psychological well-being dated 5 years back, I firstly ask a PSS-set of questions about this
February10 and next, I ask the same set of questions about Covid-19 lockdown. By doing
so, I propose to use last-month stress as a reference point to make it easier estimate stress
that occurred 5 years ago.

Following Marakshina et al. (2024) who proved a Russian version of PSS to have a two-
factor structure, I set two sub-scales inside a PSS-questionnaire: a sub-scale of perceived
self-efficacy (questions 4, 5, 7, 8, each question is worth between 0 and 4 points) and a sub-
scale of perceived helplessness (questions 1, 2, 3, 6, 9, 10, each question is worth between 0
and 4 points). For each sub-scale a higher score means worse psychological well-being. A
sub-scale of self-efficacy represents how successfully a respondent tackles issues and varies
from 0 to 16. In other words, it measures a respondent’s sense of control and coping ability
(e.g. ”I felt confident about my ability to handle my personal problems”). A sub-scale of
helplessness is somewhat different: it assesses feelings of being overwhelmed, lost or out of
control, and it ranges from 0 to 24. By adding a helplessness value and a self-efficacy value
altogether, we get a final value of the respondent’s PSS index, therefore the total PSS score
ranges from 0 to 40.

It is important to note that the collected PSS scores are self-reported, thus they may be
biased. Following Kostromina and Pisarev (2017), Russian adolescents provide unreliable
estimates of their distress, and the reported results dramatically converges from the true
indicators of the body measured medically. That is how they explain this phenomenon:
"Возможны различные варианты обобъяснения данных результатов: школьники с тру-
дом оценивалют свое вегетативное состояние, не могут точно идентифицировать его,
либо же склонны давать социально желательные ответы, продемонстрировать свою

Therefore, in my main results (Table 6) I do not use PSS-10 as a proxy of stress. Instead,
I use a natural experiment where a treatment group faced an additional stressor in the form

I also use the PSS-10 in the IV model, so it’s important to keep in mind that the scores may
be underestimated, which could lead to underestimated estimates.

5.1 Difference in Difference

My main underlying assumption is that those school students who were affected by the
cancellation of the All-Russian Olympiad in 2020 faced an additional stressor during Covid-
19 pandemic that the rest of the students who were not about to participate in the Olympiad
in 2020 did not face.

Following this key assumption, I propose to use a Difference in Difference (DiD) approach.
Unfortunately, I cannot test a parallel trend assumption for Treatment and Control groups
in pre-Treatment periods simply because the Treatment group appears only in 2021 in the
result of the cancellation of the Olympiad. Nevertheless, I employ the fact that some students
from the Treatment group took part in the Olympiad before the Covid-19 pandemic expired,
and include D20 dummy to show that the ex-ante students from the Treatment group did
not outperform the average participant.

The main dependent variable is Ri,t which is a proxy for academic achievements. Once
again, I use three measures of it: first, Scorei,t that is a percentage excess of the respondent’s
score obtained during the final stage of the Olympiad in a year t over a bounding score that
determines which participants get the privilege and which do not. I divide this difference by
the total score that could have been potentially obtained. It is important to use a reference-
dependent measurement because of the fact that a passing grade differed from year to year,
as well as a maximum number of points. Second, I use a binary variable W ini,t that equals
to one if a participant won the Olympiad in a year t and is zero otherwise. Finally, I use
P ercentilei,t that is the participant’s percentile inside her grade and year. The general plan
is as follows:

1. Using my Control group to calculate by how much students who are participating
repeatedly in year t are more likely to win the Olympiad this year; I call it ∆experience
and assume it to be above zero. Intuition is that there is an effect from scale in terms
of preparation for the Olympiad, thus those students who participate repeatedly may
have higher odds to win. It is true as such school students are more experienced and
skilled. Variable that stands for the experience is T riedi,t:

5.2 Instrumental variable approach

Since a motivation effect and an effect of stress are tightly intertwined with each other, DiD
approach can only show which effect prevailed but it fails to estimate each effect separately.
In this section I address the potential endogeneity caused by unobserved motivation effect
by employing an instrumental variable approach. Specifically, I use a dummy ”Having a
pet”18 as an instrument for Covid Stress for a male sub-sample. Indeed, there is a field of
studies that prove pets to be an efficient tool to reduce stress (Beetz et al. (2012), Kertes
et al. (2017)), and at the same time, pets are unlikely to affect motivation.

To prove that this IV approach is valid, I discuss the main identification assumptions that
must hold in the next few paragraphs. First, the instrument I am using must be relevant.
According to Figures 2 and 3 from the Appendix, ”Dog dummy” variable has the strongest
negative correlation with Covid PSS metrics for treatment boys (-0.24), while ”Cat dummy”
has the strongest correlation with Covid PSS for control boys. ”Pet Dummy” is a more
aggregated variable which allows for a greater variety of types of pets. I additionally prove
that my instrument is valid by examining a first-stage regression results and F-statistics in
Section 8.3.

Second, the instrument should satisfy the exclusion restriction, which means that having a
pet should affect academic achievements exclusively through the stress reduction mechanism.
Even if owning a pet may affect some adult’s job career prospects through taking time in
the morning and causing frequent delays, this may not be true of adolescents. It is parents
who decide whether to have a new pet, and it is parents who generally take care of a pet,
not a child. It is also highly unlikely that a pet could destroy the preparation process in
any significant scale, since all participants took part in the Olympiad at will. Moreover, if
a child won the Olympiad, it would be beneficial both for them and their parents, therefore
the whole family would strive to eliminate negative consequences of a badly behaved pet.
Besides all the discussed above, it is hard to come up with any other way how having a pet
may affect academic achievements except from reducing distress levels, hence I claim that
exclusion restriction is hold.

Third, the instrument must be exogenous. The main idea of the IV approach is to
eliminate a motivation effect and to estimate a stress effect solely after controlling for the
family wealth and size, region of living, etc. After doing so, a motivation effect should not
be correlated with a pet ownership. Again, it is parents who decide whether to have a pet

In this section I describe the results of the estimations. Firstly, I provide the estimation
results of my DiD regression 3 in order to determine which of the two effects prevailed in
the Russian data. I want to recall that there is a large body of research that claims that
high stress is the main barrier to academic success for young people (Lisnyj et al. (2021),

one also takes a motivation effect into account. Then I allow for heterogeneity in the effects
along stress-coping abilities, and try to isolate the stress effect from the motivation effect
using an estimation of a 2SLS regression equation. Finally, I perform robustness checks on
my results.

6.4 Robustness Checks

To increase the credibility of the results, I conduct some robustness tests for my DID ap-
proach. In addition to using three measures of academic achievement throughout the paper,
I add a set of controls to the main DiD specification, allowing for time varying means in
the Control group, and re-run regressions for the main DiD specification 3 with a full set of
controls, weighting my observations in a way to eliminate selection bias from the collected
data set.

To begin with, I control for Family Wealth and Family Size in Table 9, to show that
the main results do not change much. I choose them as the most credible controls because

29

they can be considered as proxies for motivation to win. Indeed, Family Wealth turns out to
be negatively correlated with girls’ academic achievements in columns 7-9, and one possible
explanation is that students from more wealthy families have lower incentives to prepare so
seriously, since they know that they will be able to enroll in a prestigious university on a
fee-based basis. The same is true of Family Size which might be correlated with wealth as
well as with the how comfortable it is to be at home.

According to Table 9, the main DiD results stay similar in all alternative specifications
but their magnitude slightly decreases (from 8.68 in Table 6 Column 1 to 8.12; from 17.94
in in Table 6 Column 2 to 16.82). The standard errors of the main effect in Columns 7 to
9 increase, which implies that the motivation effect contribution to the total DiD effect was
partialed out by the added controls.

Additionally, I have included interaction between T riedi,t and P ost in my main speci-
fication, presented in Section 5.1, to allow for a different time trend in the Control group

7 Conclusion

To summarize, this paper examines the two-fold impact of negative shocks on the academic
achievement of school students in settings where the stakes are high.
I hypothesize that
in the result of a negative shock students become more exhausted due to the higher stress
as well as more motivated to study hard, since the odds to succeed decrease. I exploit a
unique case of cancellation of a national stage of the All-Russian Olympiad in economics in
2020 to create exogenous variation in both long-lasting stress and perceived stakes among
otherwise comparable cohorts of Russian adolescents. I firstly use the Difference-in-Difference
approach to determine how well students from the Treatment group performed relative to
their peers from the Control group. Next, I switch to the 2SLS regression design to isolate
the deteriorating effect from a motivational one. The study provides the following insights.

1. Motivation can outweigh the psychological burden of stress. Difference-in-
Difference estimations and multiple robustness checks show that the cohort exposed to the

31

2020 cancellation out-performed a comparable group of students who participated in the
Olympiad repeatedly but were not exposed to the negative shock. In the conceptual frame-
work of this study it means that the ”motivation effect” substantially exceeded the ”stress
effect”, when stakes got higher.

In other words, when an adverse shock simultaneously raises the perceived value of suc-
cess, motivated students—especially those accustomed to coping with uncertainty—may
transform anxiety into additional effort.

2. The net effect is heterogeneous along stress-coping abilities. Adding interactions
between treatment status and stress-coping abilities revealed the effects to be heterogeneous.
Among boys, it is only probability to win that the shock has influenced on. Higher odds to
succeed are fully attributed to the least resistant male participants, probably, because they
underestimated their odds to win and studied more. Since there is no premium for scores
and percentiles, I suggest that the attributed effect is the strongest for the medium-skilled
boys, who jump over the boundary score and become winners. For stress-resistant males, I
cannot report any statistically significant differences between them and the Control sample.
Among girls, the premium seems to appear in scores and percentiles, regardless of the type
of stress resistance, but not in higher odds to win. This implies that motivation chiefly
benefited girls already far from the cut-off.

3. Stress per se carries a close-to-zero negative impact. Instrumental-variable ap-
proach enables me to partial out the upward bias from motivation and show small, statisti-
cally insignificant negative coefficients on the Covid Stress for boys. When combined with
the DiD results, this suggests that stress alone does not significantly hinder Olympiad per-
formance unless it is not accompanied by a corresponding increase in perceived importance
of the Olympiad.

It should also be noted that the considered question is not fully answered and requires
further research. First, I conduct my research using small and skewed towards high achievers
data sample, which raise a doubt according to the internal validity of the results. The results
of the weighting procedure have only enhanced such concerns. Self-reported PSS scores are
highly likely to be understated, especially for boys. Though I was able to get around this
problem by using exogenous variation in stress in my DiD approach, 2SLS estimation results
may still be underestimated. Finally, I lacked observations to investigate differences between
the mechanisms for 10th and 11th-graders. Further steps may complement my analysis as
well as highlight the need in tailored educational approach for stress-resistant students and
their peers susceptible to stress.

8.1 Theoretical Model

I explore the research question in greater detail using a modified model by W¨alde (2018).
Although the authors consider a wide range of issues, such as types of stressors that cause
stress, coping mechanisms that have different efficiency, and personal traits that make dif-
ferences in relation to influence of stress on income, I will generally base on the 5th section,
named ”Stress, productivity and income”. So, let me consider a simple model that will
clearly demonstrate the current question of this paper.

The starting point is definitions of the key concepts that I am using below.

• Psychological stressors are ”social and physical environmental circumstances that chal-
lenge the adaptive capabilities and resources of an organism”22; appearance of stressors
is the first step of stress occurrence. Stressors cause a change of stress level.

• Appraisal is ”the mechanism through which stressors increase emotional tension”23.
Generally speaking, appraisal process is responsible for how much stress level is changed
because of one particular stressor. Appraisal processes vary a lot depending on personal
characteristics. For instance, the same stressors can cause different stress surges due
to different appraisal mechanisms.

• Working memory, following Smith and Kosslyn (2007), is ”the system that enables the
short-term storage and mental manipulation of information”. The next definition is
taken from the same source.

• Working memory capacity (working memory span) is ”a measure that describes the

maximum amount of information an individual can store in working memory”.

In other words, every human is endowed with a certain amount of working memory
M24 that is essential for any cognitive actions, from choosing what to eat and what to
put on to solving intellectual problems at university or at work.
In my model that is a
slight modification of W¨alde (2018), working memory is essential for preparation to the All-
Russian Olympiad. From other side, working memory takes part in an appraisal process:

22Definition is taken from Monroe and Slavich (2016)
23From W¨alde (2018)
24Designations are taken from W¨alde (2018)

36

”the appraisal process evoked by stressors is one such example of an automatic, exogenous,
allocation of working memory resources. As stressors are exogenous to the individual, the
amount of working memory used for the appraisal process is exogenous as well”, - W¨alde
(2018) says.

Now we are ready to build a model. Let p be a stressor, Wt(p) be a function of an
appraisal process at certain time point. Then the following equation takes place, since
working memory is spent on coping with stress and preparation to the Olympiad only. M
is set exogenously and is the same for all individuals in the model.

M = M (W (p, t)) + M (a(t))

(5)

Indeed, M (W (p, t)) is the amount of working memory, that is spent on the appraisal process,
that is, how much cognitive energy is needed to experience stress, that was caused by a
stressor p. As well as the authors do, I demand that a higher stress level requires more
working memory to handle it, so M ′(W (p, t) > 0. The rest of the working memory can
be used for everyday activities, but in my model, it is used to pay attention a(t) to the
preparation for the Olympiad. For simplicity I assume M (a(t)) = a(t) as well as M (W (t)) =
kW (t). If so, 5 converges into

M = kW (t) + a(t))

(6)

The main dependent variable in the model is R(t),

it is so-called readiness for the
Olympiad. It is a cumulative measure of how well a child is prepared for the Olympiad,
taking into account her initial ingenuity, mathematical skills, and intuition, marked as h.
The readiness R(t) can also be interpreted as the odds of winning the Olympiad at moment
t. R(t) is also determined by labor amount l(a(t)), it is the amount of hours spent on the
preparation, and by effort e. All the discussed above sum in the following equation:

R(t) = l(a(t))eh = [M − k · W (t)]eh.

(7)

As l(a(t)) rises in attention a(t), the odds of winning rises in attention as well. As by 6
attention falls in stress, readiness falls in stress too.
It is intuitively clear: attention to
studies measures how easy an individual finds it to concentrate on preparation process. If it
is easy, she studies more, so that l raises. Otherwise, if it is hard to focus, one would study
less. How easy it is to focus on studies is a reverse function of stress, so the odds of winning
decrease in stress as well.

However, such a simple model cannot explain the positive impact of academic stress,

37

competitive atmosphere or tight deadlines, therefore I align with W¨alde (2018) by

i.e.
differentiating between two types of stressors.

Stressors on studies, i.e. highly competitive atmosphere or demanding parents, induce
effort e as a response, and as an individual strives to minimize effort by nature, I assume
that effort that she chooses is equal to study stressors. Recalling 7, Readiness R increases in
effort, thus following W¨alde (2018) I will name such stressors productive. Private stressors
do not have any productive nature in average, so they are called ppure. It results in

p = e + ppure

(8)

By substituting 8 into 7 we obtain:

R(t) = l(a(t))eh = [M − k · Wt(p)]eh = [M − k · Wt(e + ppure)]eh.

(9)

While effort has a direct positive effect on Readiness by increasing the efficiency of studies,
higher effort is also a more severe stressor, and, sequently, leads to higher stress Wt that is
a reason of attention’s deterioration. By so, study stressors present a trade-off between a
motivation to put higher effort to studies and limited Working Memory that is available for
studies: the more is used on stress, the less remains for studies.
