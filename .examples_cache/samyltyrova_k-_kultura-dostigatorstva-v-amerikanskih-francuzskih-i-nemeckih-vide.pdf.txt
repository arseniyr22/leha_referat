Introduction 

4 

In recent years, social media has integrated deeply into everyday life, with 

individuals dedicating substantial time to engaging with diverse platforms as both 

consumers and content creators. This constant interaction plays a pivotal role in shaping 

social values, beliefs, and personal identities. Among the most influential trends to 

emerge from this digital environment can be considered a “hustle culture”, or a 

“workaholic culture associated with non-stop busyness, workload, limited time, and 

pressure” (Assariy, Hersari, Sitorus, Arifin, & Faisal, 2024). In the contemporary world, 

it has become a popular phenomenon in response to increasing economic challenges and 

elevated standards of living (Hasanah, Vardhani & Martha, 2024) that force people to 

rush and strive for success often neglecting one’s well-being. This cultural trend has 

been actively propagated through social media tools, particularly on YouTube as one of 

the most popular video sharing platforms. In video blogs, or vlogs, which constitute a 

primary format on YouTube, content creators employ the power of storytelling to 

engage their audiences and convey meaningful messages, including those centered 

around the ideals of constant productivity and success. Despite the popularity of the 

trend on the media resource, while hustle culture has been explored as a force to impact 

mental and physical health (Hasanah et al., 2024; Mardiana et al., 2023; Verma, 2022;  

Wong, Chan, & Ngan, 2019), its verbal and cultural representation in media remains 

underexplored (Jepsen & Eaton, 2025). 

  Thus, this research aims to examine and compare the linguistic and cultural 

features that contribute to the construction of hustle culture in American and Russian 

YouTube vlogs. Accordingly, the paper includes the following research questions: 

1) What is the linguistic representation of hustle culture in American and Russian 

YouTube vlogs? 

2) How are cultural dimensions represented in American and Russian vlogs about 

hustle culture? 

In order to achieve the aim of this study, this paper will employ a mixed-methods 

approach to investigate the hustle culture discourse. Firstly, Norman Fairclough’s 

(1995) Critical Discourse Analysis will be applied to explore the linguistic 

5 

representation of hustle culture. Secondly, Hofstede’s (2010) Cross-Cultural Analysis 

will be utilised to analyse how hustle culture portrayal in American and Russian 

YouTube vlogs corresponds to the cultural dimensions connected with this 

phenomenon. By combining these methodological approaches, the present study 

endeavors to offer a deeper comprehension of hustle culture across cultural settings. 

Overall, this study aims to compare linguistic and cultural representation of the 

hustle trend in American and Russian communities. Firstly, the theoretical aspects 

concerning the impact of hustle culture on health as well as the description of hustle 

culture discourse as discussed in previous studies will be explored. Subsequently, a 

critical discourse analysis will be conducted on a corpus of selected 32 YouTube videos 

that explore the themes of hustle culture, success, and productivity, offering 

perspectives that both endorse and challenge these concepts. The analysis will be 

confined to videos that explicitly invoke the specified concepts within their titles, 

thereby ensuring clarity of focus and minimising any ambiguity or interference from 

unrelated phenomena. Drawing on the findings of the critical discourse analysis, the 

cross cultural analysis may provide new insights into the ways hustle culture operates 

within varied linguistic and cultural contexts. 

Regarding the practical significance of this research, the findings are expected to 

enhance individuals’ awareness of the linguistic mechanisms1 employed within hustle 

culture, thereby enabling a more critical evaluation and resistance to its influence. 

Additionally, this study may contribute meaningfully to the field of intercultural 

communication and serve as a valuable resource in seminars and courses related to 

Intercultural Corporate Communication. By elucidating the processes through which 

cultural phenomena, namely hustle culture, are formed and sustained across various 

societies, the research advances a more comprehensive understanding of the interactions 

between language, culture, and social practices in a global context.

Literature Review 

In recent years, the idea of “hustle culture” has become increasingly prominent 

across digital platforms, influencing societal attitudes toward work, achievement, and 

self-identity. This section explores various sides of hustle culture, including its 

definition, effects on mental and physical health, and its representation on social media. 

Particularly, the vlog as a media genre is considered due to the format serving as a 

powerful instrument for portraying and disseminating hustle ideas. An additional focus 

is placed on the discourse of hustle culture and the attempt to identify its main 

characteristics. Furthermore, culture-specific traits of hustle phenomenon are analysed 

through Hofstede’s cultural dimensions theory (2010) to comprehend how American 

and Russian cultural values shape attitudes toward work, success, and social behavior. 

By investigating these interrelated themes, this review seeks to deepen the 

understanding of hustle culture’s complexity and the influential role of  vlogs together 

with cultural values in both reflecting and reinforcing the trend. 

Hustle Culture Phenomenon 

Hustle Culture: Historical Background 

In order to explore the phenomenon of hustle culture, it seems essential to start 

with its origins. The researchers suggest that the emergence of hustle culture is linked to 

7 

the industrial revolution, which transpired in the 1970s, and marked the era of the 

harshest labor conditions with long hours and low wages (Balkeran, 2020; Bellini & 

Lomazz, 2024; Ulfah & Nurdin, 2022; Peric, 2024; Putri, 2022). The concept of “Time 

is Money”, introduced by mechanised factory work, linked productivity directly to 

working hours (Putri, 2022). Corporate culture, shaped since the 1980s, began to 

emphasise shared values and behaviors reflected in its dress code, business hours, office 

setup, and employee benefits (Tarver, 2021). By the 1990s, technology firms began to 

dominate the market, setting a new standard that encouraged young workers to accept 

overwork as normal (Alp Consulting, 2023). During this time, prominent entrepreneurs 

actively promoted the idea of excessive working hours, framing it as a form of personal 

freedom and empowerment. With the rise of social media in the early 2000s, sharing 

personal experiences became easier, but these platforms quickly evolved into highly 

competitive spaces that highlight only the most successful and polished aspects of 

people’s lives. This environment of constant comparison helped fuel the widespread 

influence of hustle culture, which promises a glorified lifestyle achieved through 

relentless hard work. 

  The sociological foundations of hustle culture can be linked to Max Weber’s 

analysis of Protestant work ethics, which emphasised individual responsibility, 

diligence, and the pursuit of professional perfection as a moral imperative (Weber, 1905 

as cited in Bellini & Lomazz, 2024). This ethic fostered an endless race for success, 

where work became central to personal identity and a pathway to fulfillment as devotion 

to God, which now seems as an alienated version of contemporary hustle culture.  

While Marx’s concept of “value” originally highlighted how capitalism exploited 

workers through long hours and low pay, this notion has transformed (Ulfah & Nurdin, 

2022), the Industrial Revolution 4.0 has progressed into a new phase characterised by a 

stronger focus on human-centered values within the industrial landscape. This latest 

evolution, often referred to as Industry 5.0, sees the rise of companies whose corporate 

culture and values have become crucial in attracting talent, emphasising shared beliefs 

and behaviors. Despite these cultural shifts, fundamental expectations in the modern 

8 

work ethic remain consistent: workers are still required to exercise self-discipline, 

prioritise productivity, and avoid wasteful habits (Faler, 2015). 

Notion of Hustle Culture 

It can be seen that the understanding of the hustle phenomenon has been altered 

from harsh labour with long working hours in the modernist times to the corporate value 

of hard work and productivity in the post-modern society. It has acquired various 

interrelated names such as  “toxic productivity” (Achmad & Lubna, 2023; Assariy et al., 

2024; Hasanah et al., 2024; Perić, 2024), “rise and grind mentality” (Balkeran, 2020; 

Perić, 2024), “toil glamour” (Balkeran, 2020; Perić, 2024), “culture of bustle” (Assariy 

et al., 2024; Chairunnisah & Kurnia, 2023), “rush culture” (Assariy et al., 2024), and 

“workaholic culture” (Assariy et al., 2024; Balkeran, 2020; Hasanah et al., 2024; Kaur 

Arora et al., 2024; Maharani et al., 2024; Mardiana et al., 2023; Perić, 2024). Despite 

the common ground, the notion of hustle culture is not unified and includes a wide 

range of additional meanings.  

First, most authors define “hustle culture” as a culture that encourages non-stop 

work, long working hours, refusal to rest, and a constant drive for productivity to 

achieve success (Assariy et al., 2024; Balkeran, 2020; Bellini & Lomazz, 2024; 

Chairunnisah & Kurnia, 2023; Hasanah et al., 2024; Mardiana et al., 2023; Perić, 2024). 

In this culture, work is considered to be above all health and personal life (Hasanah et 

al., 2024; Mardiana et al., 2023), and being successful – working hard without time 

constraints (Assariy et al., 2024; Hasanah et al., 2024; Perić, 2024).  

Second, hustle culture can be understood as a movement that fosters motivation 

and is accompanied by anticipated rewards (Ulfah & Nurdin, 2022). This phenomenon, 

which implies individuals reducing their available time for work, is often linked to 

particular generations. Several studies (Achmad & Lubna, 2023; Assariy et al., 2024; 

Chairunnisah & Kurnia, 2023; Hasanah et al., 2024; Patri 2022) suggest that Millennials 

(1981-1996) and Generation Z (1997-2012) are the primary demographics associated 

with hustle culture. Internal factors such as desire for self-actualization, independence, 

high ambitions, and external factors, including economic instability, financial demands, 

changing labor markets, are assumed to intensify the pressure on younger generations to 

9 

adopt a "hustle" mindset and push them toward multiple income streams and continuous 

upskilling (Ulfah & Nurdin, 2022). Social media platforms play a crucial role in shaping 

generational attitudes toward hustle culture and amplify the pressure through hashtags, 

reinforcing the idea that success is achieved through constant work and effort 

(Chairunnisah & Kurnia, 2023). 

Third, hustle culture can be perceived as an ideology, a system of beliefs and 

values concerning success, relentless work, and productivity. It is interpreted as a 

product of late capitalism, reviving the myths of the “American Dream” through 

technological optimism (Hill, 2020). Besides, capitalism together with globalisation, has 

affected people’s lifestyle, leading individuals toward consumerist behaviors and, in 

some cases, hedonism (Hennessy, 2017 as cited in Maharani et al., 2024). Hustle culture 

is also assumed to inherit elements of the Protestant work ethic (the value of labor) but 

shifts the focus from spiritual to economic goals (Hill, 2020). 

Overall, hustle culture is characterised by the glorification of relentless work, 

productivity, and success. Formed by social, cultural, and technological forces that 

prioritise professional achievement over work-life balance, it is increasingly recognised 

as a source of workaholism and unsustainable living. 

Additional attention is devoted to the investigation of how hustle culture 

influences individuals’ lives. Evaluating whether this phenomenon reflects a benign 

lifestyle choice popularised via social media or signifies significant threats to well-being 

is pivotal for analysing modern societal norms. Existing evidence underscores negative 

repercussions linked to hustle culture, including detrimental effects on both physical 

and mental health. 

  Specifically, research discovered the correlation between prolonged working 

hours and physiological health concerns such as digestive problems, headaches, muscle 

tension, cardiovascular issues, including heart disease, heart attack, high blood pressure, 

and stroke (Clinic Staff as cited in Perić, 2024), as well as fatigue and sleep disorders 

(Hasanah et al., 2024; Mardiana et al., 2023; Verma, 2022;  Wong, Chan, & Ngan, 

2019).  

10 

Other studies indicate negative impact of hustle culture on mental health, namely 

postponed life syndrome (Najdenova, 2022), imbalance between personal and 

professional life (Putri, 2022; Test Gorilla as cited in Perić, 2024), affecting family 

relationships and social interactions, poor lifestyle choices and neglect of necessary rest 

(Chairunnisah & Kurnia, 2023; Saputra, 2024), chronic stress, resulting in burnout and 

mental exhaustion (Balkeran, 2020; Hasanah et al., 2024) as well as anxiety, depression 

(Assariy et al., 2024; Hasanah et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2019; Mardiana et al., 2023; 

Perić, 2024), and the desire to commit suicide (Assariy et al., 2024; Perić, 2024). 

Thus, hustle culture appears to evoke serious concern regarding individuals’ well-

being. Some researchers suggest prevention efforts to reduce work stress-induced health 

problems, advocating for inclusive company practices prioritising long-term success 

through flexible work arrangements, setting boundaries, skill development, and health 

support comprising psychological safety (e.g., encouraging breaks, accepting mistakes), 

stress management programs, and shifting focus from profit to people (Hasanah et al., 

2024; Perić, 2024). However, little research is dedicated to the role of social media in 

influencing individuals’ work habits and wellness, since most studies focus primarily on 

examining face-to-face interactions among employees within specific companies or 

organisations to assess the effects of participating in “hustle culture,” often overlooking 

the significance of digital influence among these workers. 

Discourse of Hustle Culture 

Definition of Discourse  

In order to examine the linguistic and cultural representation of the hustle 

trend, it seems necessary to analyse the discourse surrounding this phenomenon. 

Discourse, as a notion in general, serves as a foundational framework for 

interdisciplinary research that integrates insights from linguistics, sociology, 

psychology, political science, and cultural studies. It functions as a critical lens through 

which social phenomena, such as power relations, identity construction, and ideological 

formations, are examined across diverse contexts and modes of communication 

(Muxtorova & Abdullaeva, 2024). This broad applicability has given rise to Discourse 

Studies as a transdisciplinary field, characterised by its theoretical orientations and 

 
11 

methodological tools that explore the interplay between language and society, 

particularly focusing on how discourse shapes and is shaped by social structures and 

power dynamics. 

In Discourse Studies, “discourse” is generally understood as a social practice 

whereby participants engage in communication using linguistic and other semiotic 

resources within specific contexts (Angermuller, Maingueneau, & Wodak, 2014). The 

scholars emphasise the constitutive nature of discourse, which can be divided into two 

approaches: one that treats discourse as an intellectual and epistemological issue central 

to social, political, and cultural theory, which can be referred as “discourse theory”, and 

another that addresses the analytical and methodological processes involved in 

examining discourse as a tangible and empirical phenomenon, or, in other words, 

“discourse analysis”. 

In particular, discourse theory encompasses three main approaches: post-

structuralist, normative-deliberative, and critical-realist. First, post-structuralist theory 

highlights the constitutive role of language in shaping social reality, viewing discourse 

as constructing social structures and power relations. Influenced by Foucault (2013) and 

Pêcheux (1975), this perspective focuses on the contingent character of knowledge and 

identity. Second, normative-deliberative theory sees discourse as a means of negotiating 

agreements among participants to resolve conflicts in the public sphere, promoting free 

deliberation and rational consensus. While strong versions, exemplified by Habermas, 

assert that communicative processes are governed by inherent rational norms and 

universal principles that enable participants to reach consensus through reasoned 

argumentation (1985), weaker versions challenge this universalism (Lyoutard, 1994). 

Third, critical-realist theory considers discourse a social practice embedded in power 

structures, producing and reproducing inequalities between social groups. Influenced by 

critical realism, linguists have integrated its philosophical principles into Critical 

Discourse Analysis (CDA) to deepen the examination of social issues through language. 

This approach, notably advanced by Norman Fairclough, treats discourse not merely as 

text or talk but as a social practice embedded within real-world power structures and 

material conditions (1995). 

12 

Thus, these strands share a focus on language, communication, and meaning in 

social theory but differ in their approaches to power, knowledge, and subjectivity. Post-

structuralism decenters identities, normative-deliberative theory highlights tensions 

between ideal and actual discourse, and critical realism reveals power structures hidden 

beneath representations. Together, these theories demonstrate how discourse shapes and 

is shaped by power, knowledge, and the construction of subjectivity. 

Definition of Hustle Culture Discourse 

 Although hustle culture has gained widespread global popularity, its discourse 

remains largely undescribed in academic literature. In order to address this gap, the 

terminology and methodological framework proposed by Norman Fairclough (1995), 

whose critical discourse analysis (CDA) approach has been applied in this study, was 

adopted. Fairclough’s framework conceptualises discourse as both socially shaped and 

socially constitutive, meaning that language use reflects existing social conventions 

while also having the potential to transform social relations and identities (Fairclough, 

1995, p. 131). Applying this to hustle culture, Hustle Culture Discourse can be defined 

as a socially constructed and socially influential way of representing and 

communicating the values, behaviors, and ideologies associated with relentless, 

excessive work, and constant productivity. Thus, the discourse of hustle culture not only 

mirrors prevailing societal norms about work ethic and success but also actively shapes 

individuals’ perceptions and practices related to labor and achievement.

Culture Discourse can be understood as encompassing multiple interconnected 

discourse types, each contributing to its overall communicative function and social 

impact.

Primarily, it functions as an argumentation discourse, where claims about the 

value and necessity of relentless work and productivity are put forward and justified. 

This argumentative dimension constructs a rationale that frames hustle culture as 

essential for success and self-worth in contemporary society.  

Furthermore, Hustle Culture Discourse operates as persuasive discourse, aiming 

to influence and motivate audiences to adopt the values and behaviors associated with 

constant productivity and overwork. Through motivational language, success stories, 

and social validation, this discourse encourages individuals to internalize the hustle 

ethos as a desirable lifestyle choice.  

In addition, it can be classified as transactional discourse because it involves 

exchanges of expectations and commitments between various social roles, such as 

employers, employees, influencers, and followers. These interactions often implicitly or 

explicitly set standards for work ethic and dedication, shaping workplace norms and 

individual behaviors. The transactional nature is evident in the unspoken agreements 

within organisations that equate employee worth with visible commitment and 

productivity, reinforcing hustle culture as a baseline expectation. 

14 

Moreover, Hustle Culture Discourse embodies behavioral discourse, as it not only 

promotes particular values but also prescribes specific actions and habits. It encourages 

individuals to engage in continuous work, sacrifice leisure time, and prioritize career 

advancement, effectively shaping everyday practices and lifestyle choices. This 

behavioral aspect is reinforced through social media narratives that romanticise 

exhaustion and glorify workaholism as a symbol of pride. 

Beyond these types, Hustle Culture Discourse can be attributed to an institutional 

discourse, reflecting and reinforcing the norms, values, and role expectations imposed 

by powerful social institutions, especially media and mass culture. These institutions 

disseminate and legitimise the hustle ideology by promoting narratives that equate 

success with unceasing productivity and self-sacrifice. Media platforms, particularly 

social media, play a pivotal role in normalising hustle culture by showcasing curated 

success stories and productivity performances, often glossing over the detrimental 

effects on well-being. Besides, institutional discourse functions to maintain and 

reproduce social order by embedding these values within organisational cultures and 

societal expectations. For example, workplace communication often perpetuates hustle 

culture norms through implicit demands for constant productivity and exceptional 

dedication, which become internalised by employees as professional standards. 

Finally, Hustle Culture Discourse can be fruitfully analysed as a form of dialogic 

discourse in the sense that it carries two semantic intentions simultaneously: the voice 

promoting hustle culture ideals and the implicit or explicit response to critiques or 

alternative viewpoints. For instance, motivational posts on social media may adopt a 

tone that simultaneously endorses the hustle ethos while subtly responding to societal 

concerns about burnout or work-life balance. Based on Bakhtin’s theoretical framework 

(2010), Hustle Culture Discourse can be called polyphonic, meaning it involves a 

coexistence and interaction of diverse voices and attitudes toward work, productivity, 

and success. For example, within this discourse, while some people glorify relentless 

effort and constant productivity, framing them as moral imperatives, there are counter-

voices, often emerging from mental health advocates, critics of overwork, or alternative 

lifestyle proponents that challenge the sustainability and ethics of such a lifestyle. Thus, 

15 

this form can enrich the discourse, making it a site of negotiation rather than a fixed, 

authoritative narrative.  

According to Hill (2020), hustle culture relies on three main principles, which 

constitute the core of its discourse: entrepreneurialism, psychological and digital tools. 

First, hustle culture promotes the belief that entrepreneurial qualities such as grit, hard 

work, persistence, optimism, and market savvy are essential for modern success. This 

mindset is reflected in popular slogans and the idea that anyone can approach life with 

the drive of a CEO. Second, adherents of hustle culture emphasise the importance of a 

positive, growth-oriented mindset, believing that overcoming a “limited mindset” is 

crucial to success, and so they adopt various productivity hacks, routines, and self-

improvement tactics. This focus on mindset often leads to reframing structural barriers 

as personal challenges to be overcome by attitude and effort, offering a sense of 

empowerment even in the face of inequality. Third, technology platforms provide 

opportunities to monetise skills, products, services or even personality to achieve 

financial freedom. 

With regard to the foregoing, Hustle Culture Discourse has a complex, multi-

layered nature that prescribes behaviors linked to relentless productivity, empowerment, 

and success. Its institutional and dialogical form underscores the powerful role of media 

and organisational structures in shaping and sustaining this discourse, making it a 

pervasive and influential force in contemporary social life. 

Hustle Culture Discourse in Social Media 

  The trend of hustle culture has become increasingly popular in modern society, 

promoting nonstop productivity and ambition that is actively reinforced by the impact 

of social media. In fact, it is distributed through various hashtags that are commonly 

used to tag and search for hustle culture-related content on social media, for instance, 

“#riseandgrind” (Assariy et al., 2024; Chairunnisah & Kurnia, 2023; Griffith as cited in 

Absher, 2020), “#hustle” (Assariy et al., 2024; Griffith as cited in Absher, 2020), 

“#grindtime” (Assariy et al., 2024), “#cantstopwontstop” (Griffith as cited in Absher, 

2020), #ThankGodItsMonday, #NoDaysOff or #EverydayImHustling (Perić, 2024). 

16 

Social media platforms such as Instagram*2,  Twitter*3, and TikTok*4 amplify the 

hustle culture trend through visually appealing content that normalises overwork. For 

example, there have been identified four common types of content observed in posts 

related to hustle culture on social media: 1) “the hustle bustle”, which is depictions of 

constant productivity measured by multitasking and long hours; 2) “achievements and 

income”, posts showcasing salaries or professional milestones; 3) “lifestyle”, 

represented by trends “a day in my life”, glamorising urban work environments; and 4) 

motivational content with inspirational quotes and success stories that equate self-worth 

with productivity (Chairunnisah & Kurnia, 2023). 

Besides, hustle culture popularises slogans and motivational phrases. A study 

examined motivational hustle culture quotes combining text and imagery to construct 

meaning, using Multimodal Discourse Analysis (MDA) (Christina & Shasita, 2022). 

The findings showed that, within 30 motivational quotes, declarative and imperative 

clauses were used at the textual level as well as metaphorical imagery, literal imagery, 

tones, and techniques as visual elements to reinforce the hustle trend. Some other 

examples of quotes in another work suggest that one can approach all aspects of life 

with the same dedication and resilience as a CEO to maximise success: “Be the CEO of 

your life.”; “It’s not about money or connections. It’s the willingness to outwork and 

outlearn everyone when it comes to your business.”; “I’d rather hustle 24/7 than slave 9 

to 5.” (Hill, 2020, p. 9). 

Despite previous research focusing on hustle culture’s presence in social media 

posts and motivational quotes, the trend remains underexplored in video formats, 

particularly in YouTube vlogs. Exploring hustle culture through YouTube vlogs could 

deepen the understanding of how this discourse shapes identities and social expectations 

not only in static posts or quotes, but also in a more dynamic and interactive type of 

content.

Hustle Culture and Vlogs 

Although research has focused on hustle culture short multimedia content, there is 

a growing recognition that longer narratives about hustle culture are better suited to 

video-sharing platforms, most notably YouTube (Holland, 2016). YouTube stands out 

as one of the most popular platforms for both uploading and consuming extended video 

content, providing a space where creators can delve deeper into their experiences and 

perspectives. 

A central format on YouTube is the vlog, or video blog, which merges ongoing 

storytelling with rich audio-visual elements to engage viewers in a more immersive way 

(Kim, 2017, p. 7). Vlogs serve multiple important functions, including self-expression, 

self-presentation, role modeling, and even advertisement, making them powerful tools 

for shaping public perceptions and influencing audiences (Maden & Kalafatoğlu, 2022). 

While Maden and Kalafatoğlu emphasise how vloggers build and influence 

communities through parasocial or one-sided connections where viewers feel personally 

connected to creators, Werner (2012) highlights that vlogging can function both as a 

monologue and as a dynamic dialogue between creators and their audiences. 

This interactive dimension is a defining characteristic of vlogs, alongside their 

intimate tone, relatively short duration, and asynchronous format, which allows viewers 

to engage with content on their own time. Such interactivity is particularly significant in 

shaping how viewers perceive and internalize hustle culture, as it enables creators to 

respond to comments and feedback, fostering a sense of belonging and community 

among followers. This ongoing exchange strengthens viewer loyalty and trust, which 

are essential for effective communication and influence. 

Werner (2012) further describes vlogging as a potent rhetorical practice 

distinguished by its conversational style, emotional expressiveness, and capacity to 

disseminate information of public interest while providing immediate opportunities for 

public expression. Despite the comprehensive vlog typologies proposed by scholars like 

Gercely (2018) (as cited in Maden & Kalafatoğlu, 2022), motivational vlogs remain 

underrecognised as a distinct genre. These motivational vlogs are particularly important 

due to its aim to inspire and encourage viewers to take proactive steps toward achieving 

18 

their personal and professional ambitions, aligning closely with the motivational content 

described by Chairunnisah and Kurnia (2023) (p. 188). 

Given that motivational vlogs on YouTube have not yet been thoroughly studied 

as a medium for the dissemination and popularisation of hustle culture, this research 

seeks to explore how motivational vloggers use linguistic elements to construct 

narratives around hustle culture. As vlogs offer a unique blend of storytelling, emotional 

connection, and interactivity, it can deeply influence viewers’ values, attitudes, and 

behaviors related to work ethic, ambition, and self-identity in the digital era. 

Understanding Cultural Dimensions 

With the aim of deeply analysing cross-cultural differences and peculiarities of 

perception of various social phenomena, researchers often appeal to the theories of 

cultural dimensions. These models allow structuring and quantifying differences 

between cultures based on their values, attitudes, and behaviours. First and foremost, 

Gеrt Hofstede’s model of cultural dimensions, which is one of the most well-known and 

widely used models, is grounded on the investigation of variations in values within 

different sectors of IBM, a global computer manufacturing company (Hofstеde, 1980). 

The framework was further expanded and refined to encompass six dimensions, namely 

Mаsculinity/Fеmininity, Individuаlism/Collеctivism, Shоrt-/Long-Term Orientation, 

Power Distance, Uncertainty Avoidance, and Indulgence (Hofstede et al., 2010). It is 

pointed out that each of the dimensions illustrates the value system intrinsic to a culture 

and, therefore, they have the potential to be used to predict people’s behaviour in 

professional and everyday environments (Hofstede, 2011). 

Although Hofstede’s model is widely used and reasonably popular, it is not 

entirely unique. Another imperative theoretical framework worth mentioning is the 

approach of Fons Trоmpenaаrs and Charles Hampden-Turner, who distinguished seven 

cultural dimensions: Universalism vs. Particularism, Individualism vs. 

Communitarianism, Neutral vs. Emotional, Specific vs. Diffuse, Achievement vs. 

Ascription, Attitudes to Time, and Attitudes to the Environment (Trоmpenaаrs & 

Hampden-Turner, 1997). Unlike Hofstede,  they concentrated on solving cultural 

19 

dilemmas, i.e. how people choose between opposing behavioural strategies in different 

situations. 

Another significant contribution to the study of cultural differences was a study 

by Ronald Inglehart and Christopher Welzel, based on data from the World Values 

Survey (Inglehart & Welzel, 2005). They identified two main axes of cultural change: 

Traditional vs. Secular-Rational values and Survival vs. Self-Expression values. The 

authors present a model of social change that predicts how value systems are likely to 

evolve in the coming decades under conditions of economic development and 

democratisation.  

It is noteworthy that these models are actively utilised in media and 

communication studies. For instance, cultural dimensions have been applied to analyse 

the content of advertising, political discourse, and video content on social media. For 

example, Guftométros and Guerreiro (2021) employ Hofstede’s model to identify 

cultural differences in Facebook* user behaviour, such as the propensity to like, 

comment and repost, in order to predict consumers' online reactions in different 

countries.  

In the context of YouTube, Hofstede's framework has also found application. For 

example, a study by Park et al. (2017) demonstrated that countries’ cultural values 

influence the consumption of popular YouTube videos and limit cultural convergence 

despite global access to content. Besides, in the research by Choi (2021), this 

framework was  used to compare cultural values reflected in popular YouTube 

commercials in the United States and South Korea, which helped to reveal that unlike 

traditional advertising, online video advertising is less reflective of cultural differences 

and more focused on individual and emotional characteristics. 

Hence, cultural measurement theories provide powerful tools for comparative 

analysis, especially in the context of digital globalisation, when media content becomes 

accessible to representatives of different cultures. Hofstede’s Cultural Dimensions 

Theory (2010) was chosen in this study owing to its clarity, broad applicability in cross-

cultural research, and the possibility of a specific numerical expression of the 

characteristics of cultures, which is convenient when analysing YouTube video content.

Data Collection 

Methodology 

For the aim of this research, a purposive sampling method was applied with a 

systematic set of criteria to ensure the relevance of the selected videos. The selection 

criteria included the language of the video, the keywords, the publication date, the 

length of the video, the popularity of the channel as well as the number of views and 

likes. 

Firstly, vlogs centered on hustle culture in the American and Russian languages 

were selected for their pronounced prominence on the YouTube platform compared to 

the relevant content in other languages (i.e. French and German vlogs, initially 

prioritised for the study, proved scarce, with few aligning fully with the established 

criteria). Additionally, selecting the American and Russian languages served to 

facilitate the transcription5 and analysis processes. Secondly, the video search by 

keywords hustle culture, success, productivity, the terms identified as interconnected in 

the literature review, guaranteed that the chosen American vlogs corresponded closely 

with the motivational vlog genre and the theme of hustle culture. However, only a 

limited number of Russian-language vlogs featured the translated versions of the 

corresponding terms – “культура достигаторства” (“hustle culture”) and “успех” 

(“success”) – in their titles. Consequently, the selection process shifted to prioritise 

videos containing the keywords “продуктивность” (“productivity”) and 

“продуктивный” (“productive”) as these more accurately reflected the core themes of 

the phenomenon within the Russian YouTube community. This adjustment ensured the 

inclusion of relevant content that aligns with the study’s focus on the linguistic and 

cultural manifestations of hustle culture. Thirdly, the study focused on videos published 

within the past five years, with a duration not exceeding 25 minutes, accumulating

Data Analysis 

  The data analysis commenced with the transcription of the selected YouTube 

vlogs to compile accurate textual records of the speakers’ monologues. The transcripts 

were carefully reviewed and subjected to a coding process, wherein linguistic choices 

related to hustle culture were identified and categorised. 

Following transcription, a two-step analysis was implemented. The first step 

consisted of Critical Discourse Analysis based on Fairclough’s three-dimensional 

framework (1995) to systematically explore discourse at multiple levels. The analysis of 

each level with the corresponding examples is provided in tables 1 and 2 for American 

and Russian videos respectively. 

The first level, textual analysis, concentrated on the micro-level linguistic features 

present within the vlog transcripts. This phase entailed an examination of vocabulary 

choices, grammatical constructions, and syntactic patterns to uncover how language was 

utilised to construct and convey messages associated with hustle culture. Particular 

attention was paid to identifying lexical fields, metaphorical language, modality, and 

other stylistic devices that contributed to framing success, productivity, and work ethic 

within the discourse.  

The second level, discursive analysis, extended the investigation beyond the text 

itself to encompass the processes involved in the production, distribution, and reception 

of the vlog content. This stage examined how vlog creators crafted their messages, the 

platforms and channels through which these messages were disseminated, and how 

audiences might engage with and interpret the content. By situating each vlog within the 

broader discursive field of motivational and productivity-oriented media, this analysis 

26 

illuminated the intertextual relationships and positioning strategies vloggers employed 

to align their narratives with or differentiate them from prevailing trends in hustle 

culture discourse.  

The third level, social practice analysis, positioned the vlogs within their broader 

socio-cultural context. This involved interpreting how the discourse surrounding hustle 

culture in American and Russian vlogs both reflected and influenced dominant societal 

values, norms, and ideological frameworks. The analysis explored whether and how 

these vlogs reinforced traditional cultural narratives about work ethic and success. It 

also provided critical insights into the role of discourse as a social practice that not only 

mirrors but also actively shapes cultural understandings related to hustle culture in 

diverse linguistic and cultural settings. 

Based on the findings of the Critical Discourse Analysis, the study incorporated 

Cross-Cultural Analysis applying Hofstede’s framework (2010) to reveal how American 

and Russian vloggers interpret hustle culture through three cultural dimensions – 

Masculinity/Femininity, Individualism/Collectivism, and Short-/Long-Term 

Orientation. This methodological choice, outlined in the literature review, was guided 

by the dimensions’ conceptual relevance to the core themes of hustle culture, including 

work ethic, personal gain, and temporal orientations toward success. 

By analysing representations of American and Russian cultures in vlogs through 

Hofstede’s cultural dimensions framework, this study seeks to compare the 

classifications established by Hofstede with their actual manifestations in media 

discourse. Furthermore, the research aims to determine the extent to which each 

community exhibits an orientation toward hustle culture, thereby assessing the 

alignment between theoretical cultural profiles and contemporary media portrayals. 

Overall, given the broad and complex nature of the analysis, this study could not 

encompass all videos related to the hustle culture phenomenon. However, the selected 

sample of 32 videos may provide a representative overview, establishing a basis for 

more detailed future investigations. Moreover, the limitations could be mitigated in 

subsequent research by expanding the scope to include a wider variety of platforms and 

languages. Despite these constraints, the present study offers valuable perspectives into 

27 

the representation of the hustle culture phenomenon within the context of media 

discourse. By combining critical discourse analysis and cross-cultural analysis, the 

study aims to identify linguistic patterns and cultural values that shape the 

representation of hustle culture within the digital community. 

Results and Discussion 

At the end of the data collection, 32 videos, 16 American and 16 Russian videos 

were transcribed and analysed. The American vlogs comprised a total of 38,925 words, 

while the Russian vlogs contained 24,579 words, resulting in an aggregate word count 

of 63,504. The cumulative duration of the American vlogs was 3 hours, 15 minutes, and 

39 seconds, the Russian vlogs totaled 2 hours, 58 minutes, and 53 seconds, amounting 

to an overall duration of 6 hours, 14 minutes, and 32 seconds. 

First and foremost, a Critical Discourse Analysis (CDA) was conducted following 

Fairclough’s three-dimensional model, comprising textual, discursive, and social 

practice analyses. After that, a Cross-Cultural Analysis utilising Hofstede’s cultural 

dimensions helped interpret how each culture’s values shape the representation of hustle 

culture in American and Russian vlogs. As a result, tables for Critical Discourse 

Analysis of American and Russian vlogs were compiled (see Appendix C, Tables С1 

and С2), as well as a table for Cross-Cultural Analysis (see Appendix C, Table С3).

American Vlogs  

Text level 

According to Table C1 (see Appendix C), the linguistic representation of hustle 

culture in American vlogs is characterised by a strong emphasis on personal agency, 

urgency, and self-optimisation. At the morphemic level, modals and quasi-modals 

(Collins, 2009) such as “can” (217), “would” (55), “could” (46), “might” (41), 

“should” (24), “have to” (74), and “need to”  (49) are frequently employed, 

constructing a discourse of obligation and personal responsibility. For instance, 

statements such as “You need to create a non-negotiable routine for yourself” 

28 

(LenaLifts, 2024) or “You must time each of your tasks” (Tam Kaur, 2023) present

Besides, the frequent use of imperatives is vivid in videos, e.g. “Organize your

hand” (Helaine Zhao, 2024), “Stop thinking that you are a lazy person or a 

procrastinator and switch that mindset around” (Helaine Zhao, 2024). Imperative verbs 

could be used to enhance the perception of self-discipline and efficiency. This may 

therefore reflect high masculinity, which values competitiveness and achievement, and 

individualism, which implies that people should be self-reliant.

the idea that success or failure is an individual responsibility, which is consistent with 

the individualistic nature of American society.

The following frequently repeated metaphors that have come to be used as slang 

are “get out of the lazy rut” and “enter “that girl” era” (Leyla Tavas, 2023; Tam Kaur, 

2023). Both metaphors reflect core aspects of hustle culture, namely that unproductivity 

is a disease or a trap to eliminate, whereas development is a transformation of the 

individual, as if one can ‘enter’ a new way of life. Notwithstanding the fact that the 

image of ‘that girl’ is feminine in appearance but metaphorically linked to masculine 

values such as determination, productivity, and inner toughness. 

One more peculiar linguistic means that is bound to be regarded in the research is 

personification. In American videos, hustle culture itself is personified as a powerful 

external force: “It [hustle culture] promises this sort of utopia that we're, I guess, trying 

to reach, in which our whole self is defined by the work we do” (Mayuko, 2021); and 

the “life you want” is depicted as something that “requires to put in work and make 

sacrifices” (LenaLifts, 2024). These constructions depict goals as demanding entities 

that the subject must serve, reinforcing a vision of externalised discipline and self-

sacrifice for the sake of the desired life.

Furthermore,  in the American vlogs, repetition emerges as an affirmative 

strategy. Lexical units such as “productive”, “mindset”, and “maximize” are not merely 

repeated for emphasis, they serve to instil that people must strive for success and 

constantly develop, as required by the hustle culture. For instance, “Maximize your 

productivity…maximize all of the success” (Tam Kaur, 2023); “Mindset is everything,.. 

then the very first step is to change your mindset and the identity that you've created for 

yourself…switch that mindset around” (Helaine Zhao, 2024). From the lens of 

Hofstede’s dimensions, this reflects individualistic values since the self is regarded as 

an autonomous, self-improving agent. 

Finally, at the syntactical level,  American vloggers employ rhetorical questions 

mainly so as to engage the audience in reflection, e.g. “...what is even the point in 

working so hard if you don't have time to be fully present with the people who matter 

most?” (Dr Faye Bate, 2025). American vloggers have been found to utilise several 

more syntactic structures such as inversion structures, which appear to be used to 

highlight personal revelations and emphasise the abnormality of choices reflecting a 

deviation from conventional career expectations, e.g. “Not only have I built my creative 

side hustle, I have a fuller life now, and I'm so grateful that I was brave enough to make 

this decision” (Hippy Highland Living, 2022); “Not only does it feel good, but studies 

show that meaningful social interactions can reduce stress hormones and boost 

oxytocin, also known as the love hormone” (Dr Faye Bate, 2025). Another imperative 

stylistic device that is vivid is antithesis. American vlogs provide examples where the 

device is utilised as a tool for increasing motivation, e.g. “So consider this the last day 

of your lazy lifestyle and the first of your success story” (Tam Kaur, 2023). In this 

context, the syntactic device expresses the orientation towards masculine values and 

short-term motivation as the emphasis is not on the process, but on the moment of 

transformation and immediate results. One more peculiar device is syntactic parallelism 

that is present in both American and Russian videos. Regarding American vlogs, 

content creators most often apply parallelism to personal narratives that display 

behavioral patterns and inner transformation, e.g. “I'm walking downstairs to get a cup 

of water, I'm holding my phone; I go into the bathroom, I'm bringing my phone; go to 

34 

make food, I'm bringing my phone”(Sophie DiLoreto, 2024). This device allows the 

author in this example to question unconscious patterns of behavior (e.g., using a cell 

phone unnecessarily) and motivate a conscious change, which is rather consistent with a 

high degree of individualism and short-term orientation. What that means is that the 

person is responsible for their own performance and needs to reassess his or her current 

patterns. Moreover, syntactic parallelism is utilised in order tо express opposing options 

as equivalent, for instance, “Maybe you really enjoy living a fast-paced or eccentric 

life, and there's nothing wrong with that, but there's also nothing wrong with not 

wanting that” (Malama Life, 2021). What is emphasised here is a sense of acceptance 

and variation in choice, which is characteristic of a more feminine culture where 

diversity and well-being are valued, not just the outcome. It should be also pointed out 

that syntactic parallelism is employed in order to motivate people to change their 

lifestyle, and to keep them engaged until the end of the video, e.g. “I can promise you, 

by the end of this video, you'll know exactly how to stop procrastinating, how to stop 

being lazy, break free from your phone addiction (with some unique tips), how to create 

a simple plan to stay organized, and how to overcome perfectionism so that you can 

actually start making real progress in your life” (fayefilms, 2024).  Besides, cleft 

sentences are frequently used by American vloggers to point out the source of personal 

fulfillment or dissatisfaction, e.g. “More importantly, this ordinary, boring life is what 

fulfills me” (Malama Life, 2021); “Because that means every day you wake up, you're 

dreading the day ahead. And that dread is what leads to procrastination” (Tam Kaur, 

2023); “Building good habits is so important for staying productive and motivated 

because that is what keeps you going, and especially when you're super busy, you'll 

really appreciate having good habits in place so you don't really have to think about 

doing the right thing” (LenaLifs, 2024). This syntactic emphasis reflects a cultural 

orientation toward introspection and emotional well-being, revealing an underlying 

critique of traditional hustle culture from a more feminine and long-term perspective. 

These examples demonstrate a cultural orientation that deviates from Hofstede’s 

cultural dimensions theory, which stresses self-reflection and emotional wellbeing, 

revealing an implicit critique of the conventional idea of hustle culture from a more

feminine perspective.

American vlogs show a strong alignment with the wellness/self-help genre and

Habits, social media influencers, and wellness platforms underscore how ideas about

tanked” (mikayla mags, 2025).

Social Practice Level 

At the level of social practice, American content creators construct hustle culture 

as a lifestyle choice that combines discipline and aesthetic self-care. Productivity is seen 

not simply as a functional goal, but as a representation of identity, especially within a 

feminine aesthetic: “…so that I can be productive but also live my soft girl life, which is 

extremely important” (Tam Kaur, 2023), “My beautiful pre-workout cocktail—in a 

wine glass, of course. Cheers! Fire” (LenaLifts, 2024), “What I love most about this 

planner is that I can make cute little spreads on it” (mikayla mags, 2025). 

These examples highlight how in American culture, and more specifically in 

American vlogs, visualised productivity (complete with planners, vision boards, and 

curated routines) is represented as both individual self-actualisation and public display, 

especially on platforms such as YouTube. This aestheticised approach is in line with the 

feminine code of productivity, where “softness” and “productivity” blend well and 

complement each other. 

37 

At the same time, these vlogs emphasise personal achievement, self-control, and 

discipline as core values: “Discipline is about mastering self-control, and the way you 

do that is you start practicing setting boundaries with yourself—saying no to yourself, 

taking control of your life” (Tam Kaur, 2023), “But motivation can't keep you going 

forever, so discipline is where it's really at” (Helaine Zhao, 2024). It is also worth 

noting the frequent reference to James Clear among American vloggers, many of them 

repeatedly mention his quote, but do not name the author himself, e.g. “So I want you 

guys to remember this quote, okay? “The cost of procrastination is the life that you 

could have lived” (LenaLifts, 2024); “A quote that I heard, and I've not been able to get 

out of my head since, is: “The cost of procrastination is the life you could have lived” 

(Sophie DiLoreto, 2024). In this quote, time is regarded as a finite resource that needs to 

be invested wisely and lost time is equated with lost opportunities, which is in line with 

the capitalist ideology that turns human behaviour and productivity into a commodity. 

This reflects the notion that each individual is responsible for their own success, 

and that being able to manage their time and be disciplined is considered mandatory to 

achieve anything in life. Furthermore, a high achievement orientation is vivid here 

(Masculinity), equating performance with self-esteem and success. 

To conclude, the analysis of American vlogs fully confirms the high rate of 

individualism (60 points) in American culture, according to Hofstede (Country 

Comparison Tool, 2023). The dominance of the pronouns “I” and “you”, the emphasis 

on personal responsibility and personal choice reflect the value of personal autonomy 

characteristic of American society. The use of imperatives in the form of direct 

instructions, which presupposes independent decision-making by the addressee, is 

especially indicative.   

Furthermore, it is worth noting that the high indicator of masculinity (62 points) 

is also vividly reflected in the content. The analysis reveals the predominance of terms 

related to achievements. The metaphors of mechanisation and competitive attitudes 

demonstrate a typically masculine orientation towards results, competition and success. 

However, it is worth noting a certain contradiction: despite the general masculine 

orientation, feminine elements can be traced in the aestheticisation of productivity 

38 

(“soft girl life”), which may indicate the evolution of gender stereotypes in the modern 

digital space. 

As for long-term orientation, the most remarkable divergence from Hofstede’s 

theory is detected here. While the official figures suggest a lack of clear orientation  (50 

points), the analysis of the vlogs reveals a pronounced short-term perspective. The 

emphasis on instant actions and results indicate a desire for immediate effect. Even the 

concept of “routine”, traditionally associated with long-term planning, is presented 

through immediate benefits, consistent with American culture’s tendency toward short-

term orientation. At the discursive practice level, short-term orientation can be seen in 

the emphasis on “life hacks”, which is vivid in the promotion of products, e.g. 

“HelloFresh has truly made my life 10 times easier” (Tam Kaur, 2023). 

However, the study revealed two significant aspects that do not fully fit the 

theory. To start with, the paradox of emotionality, which means that despite 

masculinity, a strong affective component is noted, which is traditionally considered a 

feminine characteristic. This can be explained by the commodification of emotions in 

self-help discourse, where feelings become a tool for productivity. A hybridity of 

temporal perspective is also evident, namely along with the dominance of short-term 

strategies, a narrative of long-term “discipline” is evident, making unambiguous 

categorisation complicated. 

The deviations might be explained by the specificity of digital content, which 

reinforces certain cultural trends, also by the evolution of traditional values under the 

influence of the wellness movement, and by the commercialisation of the self-help 

industry, which creates new discursive hybrids.

In Russian vlogs, hustle culture is portrayed through the prism of external 

pressures. In this context, productivity is considered not as a lifestyle, but rather as a

discourse in Russian vlogs treats procrastination and failure not as something

unchangeable, but as something that can be challenged and replaced with empowering

emotions, behaviour and habits as if they were running their own business. At the same

picture with elements of both conformity and partial divergence from the theory. To 

start with, vlogs strongly supports Hofstede’s cultural dimensions theory, where 

individualism is remarkably low (46 points), indicating a collectivist society (Country 

Comparison Tool, 2023). Next, the frequent usage of such a pronoun as “мы”,  and 

emphasis on common difficulties reflect the collectivist orientation typical of Russian 

culture. Especially indicative is the use of modal constructions “нужно” and 

“должен”, which represent productivity as a social norm rather than a personal choice. 

Сollectivism manifests itself through social pressure, which reflects the prioritisation of 

group standards over individual desires. 

According to Hofstede, Russian culture can be classified as feminine with a score 

of 36 on the masculinity dimension. The linguistic features of the content clearly show 

features of feminine culture, especially in the use of softened imperatives, which are 

presented not as harsh commands but as friendly advice. The frequent use of adjectives 

with an obvious feminine colouring, e.g. “полезный” and “здоровый”, which link 

productivity not so much with achievements as with well-being, is particularly 

revealing, which fully corresponds to the parameters of feminine culture according to 

Hofstede. However, there is a discrepancy between the theory according to Hofstede 

and the vlogs. The discourse of Russian vlogs contains pronounced masculine elements 

that create a certain tension with the theory. First of all, this is manifested in the lexical 

48 

choice, namely words “дисциплина”, “работать” or “контролировать” occur 

much more frequently than in typically feminine cultures, and metaphors of struggle 

and leaving one's comfort zone reflect a rather masculine orientation towards 

achievement and competition. It should be stressed that even the cоncept of 

“самодисциплина” is presented through harsh language, which is typical of masculine 

cultures. Despite the presence of some masculine elements, which might be explained

a feminine discourse of care, imperatives are presented as advice rather than orders, and 

there is an emphasis on emotion and reflection. Even the notion of discipline is often 

associated with self-care rather than external achievement, which is particularly evident 

in constructions such as “рутина и режим это про безопасность” (Kristina 

Laktionova, 2024).  

The analysis of Russian vlogs in the context of long-term orientation (58 points) 

demonstrates full compliance with the theoretical parameters, with a number of 

peculiarities. Lexical analysis reveals a systemic emphasis on processes rather than 

outcomes, for example, the noun “привычка” (49) occurs three times more frequently 

than “достижение” (13), and the verb “развиваться” (10) prevails over 

“оптимизировать” (the latter is virtually absent). This reflects the fundamental 

attitude to gradual improvement characteristic of cultures with a high long-term 

orientation. The reinterpretation of of “routine” is particularly revealing. It is stated that 

“Рутина и режим - это не только про ваши цели и привычки. Это прежде всего 

про безопасность, про стабильность” (Kristina Laktionova, 2024), which 

emphasises its function as a long-term support. Metaphors in Russian vlogs also 

invariably reflect a long-term perspective. It is peculiar that the images of the path and 

gradual movement and development prevail, where the emphasis is on the process 

rather than the point of arrival. At the level of discursive practice, long-term orientation 

is manifested in the integration of educational platforms (e.g. “О смысле”). Regarding 

the social practice level, it is seen in the rethinking of productivity as a lifelong skill (“я 

выбираю” вместо “я должен”), with a focus shifting from external coercion to 

internal transformatiоn. 

49 

Comparative Analysis of American and Russian Vlogs 

The comparison of Russian and American vlogs through a Critical Discourse 

Analysis (CDA) framework and Cross-Cultural Analysis reveals both similarities and 

differences in how linguistic choices reflect broader societal values.  

Text Level 

The analysis of morphemic level reveals that both American and Russian 

vloggers frequently utilise modal verbs. As for American vlogs, they feature modals and 

quasi-modals (Collins, 2009) such as “can” (217), “would” (55), “could” (46), 

“might” (41), “should” (24), “have to” (74), and “need to”  (49), creating a discourse 

imbued with obligation, urgency, and personal responsibility. In contrast, Russian vlogs 

exhibit a different tone in their use. Vloggers utilise modal verbs like “должен” (25), 

“нужно” (87), and negated constructions such as “не надо” (5) which convey a more 

normative and socially framed sense of obligation in Russian culture. These 

formulations often reflect an external necessity or a communal expectation rather than 

an individual decision, which might indicate a collectivist orientation.  

Another linguistic device often detected in bоth cultures is imperative vеrbs. 

While in American vlogs the frequent use of imperatives reinforces a sense of self-

discipline and efficiency, which is consistent with Hofstede’s characterisation of 

American culture as highly individualistic and masculine, i.e. people are expected to be 

confident, success-oriented and competitive, Russian content creators tend to soften 

imperatives with context and frame them as advice or encouragement rather than 

commands, resulting in a more caring and feminine approach. 

In addition, the usage of personal pronouns also differs across the cultures. In 

American-language content, the personal pronouns “you” and “I” are utilised so as to 

stress personal choice and responsibility, implying that each individual determines his 

or her own success or failure. By contrast, Russian vlogs show a stronger tendency 

toward inclusive pronouns, namely“мы”, therefore, creating the effect of a shared path 

to productivity, which helps to erase the boundaries between vlogger and viewer, 

forming an imaginary community of like-minded people. This is in line with the 

50 

collectivist attitude of Russian culture, where individual achievements are often 

understood through the prism of group affiliation. 

At the textual level within Fairclough’s critical discourse analysis, lexical choice 

plays a pivotal role in how hustle culture is linguistically constructed and ideologically 

framed. The findings of the study demonstrate both similarities and differences in what 

American and Russian vloggers associate hustle culture with. In American content, the 

nouns “time” (141), “work”(87), “productivity”(58) and “goal” (54) are more 

frequently used, reflecting an orientation towards efficient use of time, achievement of 

goals and personal productivity. These lexical preferences correspond to the values of 

individualism and masculinity, where success is measured by concrete results and 

personal achievements. On the contrary, in Russian-language vlogs, the words “время” 

(129), “дисциплина” (39), “привычка” (49) and “рутина” (32) are more common, 

emphasising the importance of systematic work and self-control. As a result, the lexical 

choice sticks to a long-term orientation in the perception of productivity due to the fact 

that the emphasis is on process rather than instant results. It is noteworthy that both the 

noun “чувство”  in Russian vlogs and the noun “feeling” in American vlogs are used 

in the context of self-care, which might lead to a more balanced approach to the topic of 

productivity. 

Concerning the description of the attitude towards hustle culture, the majority of 

American and Russian vloggers have a tendency to implement similar adjectives. 

Overall, the adjectives tend to carry a positive evaluation, contributing to the desire of 

becoming like the person being described. The content creators use them to praise the 

idea behind the phenomenon of hustle culture and what it stands for. However, it is 

pivotal to underscore the main differences that manifest themselves in the evaluation 

and connotation, namely American аdjectives (“perfect”, “happy”) reflect the cult of 

the ideal result and positive emotions as indicators of success, while Russian ones 

(“полезный”, “здоровый”) emphasise practical value and balance. This corresponds to 

general cultural trends, that the American emphasis on individual achievements versus 

the Russian focus on usefulness and sustainability. At the same time, both discourses 

51 

retain a common core (“productive”/ “протворенный”), but fill it with different 

cultural content. 

Comparing the frequency of verbs in American and Russian vlogs we can find 

both similarities and differences in the representation of productivity. Both American 

and Russian content creators often use verbs related to emotion and activity 

(“feel”/”чувствовать”, “work”/”работать”), which highlight the generalised nature 

of experiencing hustle culture as an emotionally intense process that requires effort. 

However, some differences also were detected while analysing other verbs. First and 

foremost, American content creators tend to focus on inner transformation and 

effectiveness, using verbs such as “change” and “optimize”. What that means is that 

American vloggers emphasise personal growth and changing oneself for the better, 

which presumably implies a high degree of individualism and masculinity in the culture. 

On the contrary, Russian vlogs more often employ the verbs “контролировать” and 

“развиваться”, which could point out a focus on self-regulation, discipline, and self-

improvement. Unlike “optimize”, the idea of sustainable development within rules and 

norms, characteristic of collectivist and more structurally oriented societies, is 

observable. Besides, it is notable that the verb “изменить” appears to be significant, 

but in the Russian context it is often associated with getting out of a “lazy” state rather 

than with the search for an ideal version of oneself.  

Metaphorical constructions in American and Russian vlogs demonstrate 

fundamentally different approaches to the notion of “productivity”. The American 

content is characterised by predominant metaphors of mechanisation, representing the 

human being as a mechanism that requires optimization: “work machine”, “brain 

dump”, “mental load”. These images reflect an orientation towards maximum 

efficiency, where even rest (“diamonds are made under pressure” (Anna Neubert, 

2025)) is seen as a tool for increasing productivity. It is notable that laziness is 

metaphorically portrayed as a technical malfunction (“get out of the lazy rut” (Leyla 

Tavas, 2023) that needs to be urgently eliminated. Russian vloggers use a 

fundamentally different system of images based on existential and social metaphors. 

The key notion is the path (“путь лени — это путь сожаления” (Alina Marketing, 

52 

2024)), where productivity is conceptualised as a long-term movement rather than an 

instant result. The redefinition of discomfort is particularly revealing: while in 

American vlogs it is an “enemy”, in Russian vlogs it tends to be an “ally” with whom a 

dialogue must be built. The metaphor of the “ведро с крабами” emphasises the 

importance of the social context, which is absent in the individualistic American 

discourse. A striking contrast is observed in the interpretation of routine. American 

authors present it as a tool for quick results, while Russian authors present it as a basis 

for stability (“Рутина и режим – это про безопасность, про стабильность” 

(Kristina Laktionova, 2024)).  

Another linguistic means that is considered in the research is personification.  The 

research reveals fundamental differences in the ways that Аmerican and Russiаn 

vloggers personify the notion of “productivity”. In American vlogs, hustle culture is 

presented as an external force that requires submission and sacrifice. The image of  “life 

you want” (LenaLifts, 2024) is presented as something that can be achieved only 

through constant work on oneself, creating a sense of external pressure and the need to 

meet high standards. Russian vloggers take a completely different approach, 

personifying internal psychological states. In their discourse, abstract notions like 

“goals” and “habits” are endowed with the ability to influence a person's emotional 

state. It is noteworthy that even “discomfort” from new habits is presented as a 

necessary element of growth rather than an obstacle. 

Moreover, some repetitions were detected in both American and Russian videos. 

In American-language content, the repetition of focal words such as “productivity”, 

“maximisation”, “mindset” serves as a tool to affirm a successful lifestyle, creating an 

attitude of continuous self-improvement. This approach reflects individualistic values, 

where the individual is perceived as an independent agent of change. On the contrary, in 

Russian vlogs, repetitions fulfil a different function, which is the emphasis on the 

complexity of the path to productivity. For instance, the frequent use of the word 

“навык”, which highlights the need for gradual mastery of productivity practices 

through hard work. 

53 

It is also worth noting that special attention is paid to the notion of “discipline” by 

both American and Russian content creators. For instance, American vloggers tend not 

to rely merely on motivation, but also on discipline since “it is sustainable long-term”, 

e.g. “I focus on discipline. And everyone wants to know, How can you become more 

disciplined? Discipline is about mastering self-control, and the way you do that is you 

start practicing setting boundaries with yourself—saying no to yourself, taking control 

of your life” (Tam Kaur, 2023); “But motivation can’t keep you going forever, so 

discipline is where it’s really at” (Helaine Zhao, 2024). Although American culture is 

traditionally seen as a culture that seeks quick results, the vlogs demonstrate the 

tendency of American content creators to shift towards long-lasting, gradual changes in 

themselves by sticking to discipline. Russian vloggers also underscore the significance 

of the notion of “discipline” and “self-discipline”: “Когда ты становишься 

дисциплинированным человеком, ты понимаешь, что масштабные и самые 

заветные мечты достигаются путём долгой и последовательной работы. Это 

достигается путём усилий и терпения. Именно поэтому развивать 

самодисциплину очень важно”(Marie Kory, 2025). Moreover, it is stressed that 

discipline is a blessing: “Дисциплина - это одна из высших форм проявления 

уважения к себе”(Kristina Laktionova, 2024). Hence, it can be assumed that control, 

effort, and strictness is seen as a virtue, while gentleness and giving in is a weakness, 

which demonstrates high masculinity. It is also worth emphasising that discipline is 

seen as a discomfort to help heal from the addiction to comfort, namely laziness:“Лень 

— это твоя привычка находиться в комфорте. Ты зависима от комфорта. А 

дисциплина — это зависимость от дискомфорта” (Alina Marketing, 2024). 

At the syntactical level, there are both similarities and differences in the use of 

stylistic techniques between American and Russian vloggers. Rhetorical questions in 

both cases serve to engage the audience, but in Russian vlogs, they are often 

motivational and expository in nature. What is more, American content creators 

extensively use inversion constructions and cleft sentences to point out personal 

revelations and deviations from traditional career paths. These techniques reflect 

individualistic values and a focus on personal choice.  It is noteworthy that in Russian 

54 

vlogs no examples of cleft sentences were detected since there is no equivalent in the 

Russian language. As for antithesis in American vlogs, it tends to serve as a 

motivational tool in order to oppose old and new versions of oneself, which corresponds 

to the masculine orientation towards rapid transformation; whereas in Russian vlogs, 

antithesis often takes the form of enlightening statements, contrasting “дисциплина” 

and “лень”, “система” and “цель”. This reflects collectivist values and a long-term 

orientation towards gradual self-improvement. Syntactic parallelism is also used in 

different ways. While American content creators tend to use it to analyse personal 

behavioural patterns, Russian vloggers frequently employ it to assert “correct” patterns 

of thinking and validate emotions.   

Discursive Practice Level 

It is imperative to underscore that while both cultures engage in a commercialised 

discourse of self-improvement, American vlogs are shaped by a masculine culture 

where success, achievement, and productivity are key values. What is more, the vlogger 

might be positioned as an individual entrepreneur of the self, a person who makes the 

most of time and integrates tools to achieve ever higher personal goals. This 

corresponds to a high level of Individualism where personal autonomy and self-

responsibility is dominant. The strong focus on structured systems such as goal setting, 

habit tracking, stresses a long-term strategic mindset, namely success is seen as the 

result of consistent, intentional investment in one’s future, which tends to deviate from 

Hofstede’s cultural dimensions theory, where American culture is regarded as a culture 

where there is no dominant preference to Short- or Long-Term Orientation.  

By contrast, Russian content creators have a tendency to express a more feminine 

approach, where empathy, emotional openness, and psychological wellbeing are 

dominant. The vloggers allow viewers to explore their inner world of doubt, trauma, 

and reflection. Additionally, the collectivist overtones expressed through shared 

emotional experiences and the use of informal, intimate language indicate a decrease in 

individualism and a greater emphasis on relatedness and reciprocal support. While 

many vloggers are not as explicitly focused on long-term effectiveness, they advocate 

55 

personal development practices that support sustained mental and emotional resilience, 

indicating Long-Term Orientation based on psychological resilience. 

Social Practice Level 

At the level of social practice, vlogs by both American and Russian authors 

integrate hustle culture into broader cultural and societal discourses, reflecting social 

expectations and gender norms. While both cultures tend to endorse productivity and 

self-discipline, the ways in which these are articulated and justified differ significantly, 

reflecting their corresponding cultural contexts and ideologies. 

American authors present hustle culture as a conscious lifestyle where 

productivity becomes part of personal branding. Visual aesthetics (“beautiful planners” 

and “vision boards”) are combined with rigid attitudes to self-control, creating a 

paradoxical image of “productive gentleness”. The core message is individual 

responsibility for success, where discipline and time management become a personal 

moral imperative, which corresponds to high Masculinity and Individualism. 

Furthermore, quotes from popular authors on self-development are used as universal 

mantras, emphasising the commodified nature of productivity under capitalism. Time in 

this discourse is presented as a limited resource, and its loss is equated with missed 

opportunities, reflecting the values of capitalist society, e.g. “The cost of 

procrastination is the life you could have lived”” (Sophie DiLoreto, 2024). 

By contrast, Russian-speaking vloggers view productivity often as a forced 

necessity in a competitive environment. Harsh formulations create an atmosphere of 

constant social pressure, where productivity becomes not so much a personal choice as 

a moral obligation to society. As a consequence, this reflects collectivist attitudes where 

comparison with others and the fear of being “replaceable” play an important role. 

Nevertheless, there is also discourse that elevates this rigidity through an aestheticised, 

feminine environment, as in American vlogs (“доска на Pinterest”, “благоприятная 

атмосфера”). It is also worth noting that Russian vlogs are developing a 

counterintuitive trend, namely the “psychologisation” of productivity discourse. 

Vloggers increasingly emphasise intrinsic motivation, suggesting that obligations (“я 

должен”) should be reformulated into conscious choices (“я выбираю”). Special 

56 

attention is paid to the emotional state and the creation of a comfortable working 

atmosphere, which softens the initially harsh rhetoric, which might represent the feature 

of feminine culture. 

Conclusion 

This research aimed to explore and compare the linguistic and cultural 

representation of the hustle trend in American and Russian YouTube vlogs. Through the 

Critical Discourse Analysis, the study examined the linguistic representation of hustle 

culture in American and Russian YouTube vlogs, while the Cross-Cultural Analysis 

allowed to assess the cultural dimensions represented in American and Russian vlogs 

about hustle culture. 

Primarily, the notion of hustle culture was analysed, which can be understood as a 

culture, as a movement or a trend, and as an ideology, or system of beliefs fostering the 

idea of hard work, productivity, and success. Besides, the negative impact of the hustle 

trend on health was considered, which highlights the importance of recognising and 

addressing the potential harms associated with the glorification of relentless work and 

the need for sustainable approaches to productivity and self-care.  

Furthermore, the hustle culture discourse was derived as a socially constructed 

and socially constructing way of representing and communicating the values and 

ideologies of excessive work and constant productivity. It was further defined as 

argumentation, persuasive, transactional, behavioural, institutional, and dialogic 

discourse based on various classifications of discourse types. The core principles of the 

hustle culture discourse, as also identified through the analysis of American and Russian 

vlogs, were found to encompass entrepreneurialism or entrepreneurial qualities, 

psychological strategies such as life hacks, tactics, routines, and mindset, as well as 

digital tools that facilitate the monetization of content. 

The findings of the Critical Discourse Analysis and Cross-Cultural Analysis 

reveal that the language employed in American and Russian YouTube vlogs discussing 

hustle culture is characterised by the use of modal verbs, imperative forms, personal 

pronouns, metaphors, personification, rhetorical questions, and antithesis. However, 

these linguistic features serve to construct distinct cultural representations of the hustle 

57 

phenomenon. American vlogs depict hustle culture through a predominantly masculine, 

individualistic, and short-term oriented framework, partially diverge from Hofstede’s 

traditional classification of American culture. In contrast, Russian vlogs present a 

feminine with some inclination towards masculine, collectivist, and long-term oriented 

perspective on the trend, reflecting differing cultural values and interpretations. 

Regarding the theoretical value, this study contributes to the existing research by 

critically examining the linguistic construction and cultural distinction of hustle culture. 

The comprehensive analytical framework for understanding the discourse surrounding 

hustle culture may be further developed and utilised in future discourse studies, 

particularly those examining digital content. This framework can facilitate deeper 

investigations into how multimodal elements, combining verbal, nonverbal language, 

visual imagery, construct and perpetuate the values, norms, and ideologies embedded in 

hustle culture. By extending the approach, researchers can better analyse the interplay 

between language, culture, and media in shaping contemporary social phenomena 

across diverse platforms and cultural contexts. 

From a practical point of view, the research is expected to raise awareness among 

individuals regarding the linguistic strategies employed within hustle culture discourse, 

fostering critical engagement and enabling resistance to its potentially adverse 

influences. Furthemore, such research provides organisations, managers, and employees 

with a clearer understanding of how hustle culture shapes workplace expectations, 

behaviors, and professional relationships. Moreover, the study holds relevance for the 

field of intercultural communication, providing material for academic seminars and 

courses to illuminate the mechanisms through which cultural phenomena such as hustle 

culture are constructed and sustained in diverse societies.
