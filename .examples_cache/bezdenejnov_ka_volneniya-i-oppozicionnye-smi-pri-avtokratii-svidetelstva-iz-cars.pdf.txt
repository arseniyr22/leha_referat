Abstract

The last 20 years of the Russian Empire were characterized by social and political

internal crises. Radical leftist movements were gaining popularity, while peasant

uprisings and worker strikes were becoming much more frequent. In my thesis, I

analyze the relationship between radical political leftist periodicals and the popular

unrest of the period. Using gravity model, I found a statistically and economically

significant positive correlation between worker strikes and the leftist press, while

there was no such correlation for peasant revolts. I also found evidence in favor of

the hypothesis that the presence of correlation in the case of workers was due to

their higher literacy rate compared to peasants. Thus, the propaganda of the leftist

parties either had no effect on the illiterate peasants or, thinking strategically, the

leftist parties did not distribute their periodicals among peasants at all.

Introduction

At the beginning of the 20th century the Russian Empire was in a state of social turmoil.
In its last years, the country experienced three revolutions; peasant uprisings and worker
strikes became much more frequent. Left-wing party agitators took an active part in these
events by distributing their periodicals. The agitators got more leeway after 1905, when
censorship was relaxed during the First Russian Revolution and left-wing parties were able
to legally print their publications. The question arises here: what was the association be-
tween the popular unrest and the leftist periodicals? It is important to emphasize that
the issue will be examined within the framework of an authoritarian semi-agrarian state
of the early 20th century. The regime of the Russian Empire can indeed be described as
tsarist autocracy (samoderzhaviye), in which the entirety of actual power belonged to the
emperor. The emperor appointed the highest officials, commanded the army, controlled
all three branches of government and had the exclusive right to pardon. I also consider
the Russian Empire a semi-agrarian country, as the main economic sector at that time
was still agriculture, although the country had embarked on the path of industrialization
of its economy. In my opinion the study of the correlation between left media and popular
unrest of the time is important, since it will help researchers better understand the social
processes taking place not only in the Russian Empire, but also in other authoritarian
semi-agrarian countries of the time: Bulgaria, Serbia, Romania, Portugal, etc. Moreover,
this question is of interest within the framework of media economics as well. The major
motivation for my research is the new autocratic setting. Many modern media economic
studies are conducted on the examples of democratic states, such as the United States, due
to the greater availability of data. Thus, with my research, I will try to expand the scope
of media economic analysis to include autocratic regimes and their unique challenges. In
addition, the object of my research is traditional media, particularly newspapers, so my
results may be useful for analyzing the media of contemporary impoverished countries
with low internet and telephone coverage, where newspapers may still be a key source of
information. In my work, I examine this correlation for two types of popular discontent:
peasant revolts and worker strikes. As a leftist press, I will consider publications affiliated
with labor parties: Russian Social Democratic Labor Party (RSDLP), Socialist Revolu-
tionary Party (SRs), Trudoviks, Anarchists, etc. The Constitutional Democratic Party,
for example, though moderately leftist, were not a labor party, so I don’t consider their
press leftist. It should be noted that not all the newspapers in my source had a clear party
affiliation: a large proportion of them were labeled as either “bourgeois” publications or
“labor” publications. Therefore, I do the analysis specifically for aggregate press metrics
of all left-wing labor parties together to avoid biased estimations.

2 Literature review

The main focus of my research is to study the correlation of the politically leftist press
in the gubernias of the Russian Empire with the number of peasant revolts and worker
strikes. To begin with, a few articles on media economics should be mentioned to under-
stand how media can influence its audience. In Political Talk Radio and Public Opinion
(Barker & Knight, 2000) the researchers found that listening to political Limbaugh radio
talk show had a significant effect on listeners’ political views. The magnitude of the effect
was directly related to the amount of time devoted in the radio broadcast to a particular
topic. Interestingly, radio was much more effective in creating opposition than in build-
ing support for particular views. Thus, people were more willing to listen to negative
statements about groups, people and ideas than to positive ones. This reinforces the mo-
tivation for my research, for if the mechanisms of media influence on public consciousness
are indeed arranged in this way, then leftist labor parties openly criticizing the tsarist
regime may have been successful in inciting popular discontent. In addition, in Surprise
me if you can: The influence of newspaper endorsements in U.S. presidential elections
(Casas & Fawaz, 2016) the authors of the article evaluated the effect of endorsement of
presidential candidates in 2008 and 2012 by print newspapers on public opinion. It was
shown that print media had a significant effect on readers’ opinion. The effect was greater
when the candidate endorsed by the newspaper was both consistent and unexpected. In
other words, a candidate received the most promotion if his or her figure did not con-
tradict the political views of the newspaper’ audience, while the candidate’s party itself
had not been previously endorsed by the newspaper. The authors used prediction-market
data (the pricing of future contracts on a candidate’s victory) to analyze the probabilities
of candidates winning. As a part of their identification strategy, they made an assump-
tion that endorsements of newspapers and their timing didn’t depend on the candidate’s
winning probability. Thus, they aren’t concerned with the endogeneity problem, saying
that they have daily observations. Unfortunately, this approach does not work for my
research, as I operate with annual data. Considering the topic of media economics, the
paper Media Bias and Influence: Evidence from Newspaper Endorsements (Chiang and
Knight, 2011) is important for my research as well. The researches found similar effect
that voters rely on the media during election campaigns but that the extent of this re-
liance depends on the credibility of the endorsement. Moreover, the group most heavily
exposed to the media were moderate voters with no strict political stance. According to
the researchers, people understand the political bias of periodicals and tend to discount
the information they receive on it. Thus, endorsement of a Democratic candidate from
a left-wing newspaper will be less credible than endorsement of a Republican candidate
from the same newspaper. In my data, there is no such precise differentiation of news-
papers by partisanship and endorsement of candidates from other parties. Because of

a particular party, as well as the use of panel data with annual observations. However,
together with the paper of Casas & Fawaz researchers prove that traditional media, like
newspapers, do have an impact on behavior of people even these days, let alone in the
early 20th century when it was practically the only media resource available, enhancing
my motivation. Also, it is important to note that both of these studies were conducted
within the framework of a democratic state (the USA). Thus, my study will well com-
plement the findings of these authors, considering the fact that Russian Empire was an
autocratic state. Similarly, Enikolopov, Petrova, and Zhuravskaya in Media and Political
Persuasion: Evidence from Russia (2010) showed that the access of audience to the inde-
pendent media (TV channels) decreased the aggregate vote for the government party in
Russian 1999 Parliamentary Election. As in previous researches, the degree of the influ-
ence depended on the credibility of the media source. Complementing these studies, the
researcher in News and Public Opinion: Which Comes First? (Wlezein, 2023) showed the
inverse effect of audience on media. The author concluded that public opinion influences
news covers. Reverse effect is also present, but the media effects were less frequent and
relatively smaller than those that flow from the public to the news. The magnitude of
both the effect of the media on the behavior of its audience and the audience on news
covers is directly related to whether new information about the agent was disclosed. For
example, news with exclusive political information had a comparatively greater impact
on the audience. Thus, from these studies it can be concluded that media can indeed
influence the actions of its audience, but there may be a reverse effect where the audience
shapes media trends. This is why the potential finding of a statistically significant cor-
relation between popular unrest and the left-wing press should not be taken as a causal
relationship, because the effect could be in the other direction.

It is crucial for my research to consider the literature related to popular discontent as well.
There are several scientific researches directly touching upon the problematic of the causes
of popular discontent in the Russian Empire of the early 20th century. Among them it
is necessary to highlight the study Career incentives in political hierarchy: evidence from
Imperial Russia (Gokmen and Kofanov, 2020). One of the main results obtained by the
authors is that there is no effect of career incentives on governors’ performance in the
gubernias. A statistically significant effect was found only for oblasts: regions which were
distant from the center and poorly developed. Thus, the state had no mechanism of

using fixed effects in my regressions, to clear the variation in popular discontent from
historically and geographically determined causes.
In addition, it’s worth mentioning
the study Collective Action and Representation in Autocracies: Evidence from Russia’s
Great Reforms (Castaneda Dower, Finkel, Gehlbach, and Nafziger, 2017), in which the
authors analyzed the consistency of the results of the Acemoglu-Robinson model on the
example of peasant uprisings in the Russian Empire under Alexander 2. The researchers
found that peasants had relatively lower representation in regions with a high historical
probability of revolts. Presumably, this may have been because the authorities either
wanted to punish peasants for disobedience or were unwilling to conduct liberalization in
dangerous regions, fearing only to give the rioters more opportunities. The results of the
study Famine, Inequality, and Conflict (Meril¨ainen & Mitrunen, 2022) are also important
for my paper. The researchers showed that the 1866-1868 famine in Finland was a sig-
nificant determinant of the redistribution of wealth to large landowners. This led to the
regions most affected by the famine being the most actively involved in the Finnish Civil
War. Complementing the results of this study, I should mention the paper “Let Them Eat
Cake”: Drought, Peasant Uprisings, and Demand for Institutional Change in the French
Revolution (Waldinger, 2023). This study demonstrated that peasants in areas of France
most severely affected by drought in 1788 exhibited comparatively higher levels of popular
discontent (rebellion) during the Great French Revolution of 1789. It was found that the
demand for change in social and economic institutions was comparatively higher in the
most drought-affected areas. This was measured through complaints filed by peasants.
Thus, the level of agricultural yields have a significant impact on violent uprisings. These
researches on French and Finnish data emphasize the importance of controlling for yield
in my specifications. In my paper, I will control for the productivity of the agricultural
sector and worker wages, which reflect people’s standard of living, as one of the most
important time-varying variables that can affect popular discontent.

3 Data

In my work I analyze 49 gubernias located in European part of Russian Empire. I use data
on periodicals from 1901 to 1913 using the reference book by Cherepakhov and Ingerit
(1957), namely information on the number of unique left-wing periodicals in the gubernia
per year, the dates of the beginning and end of the publication, the party affiliation of
the publication and its circulation. It’s important to note that these data are new and
they were classified and digitised for the very first time by me manually. A periodical was
considered left-wing if it was affiliated with labor parties such as the RSDLP, Left SRs,
Trudoviks, Anarchists, etc. As it was already mentioned, moderately leftist non labor
parties, like the Constitutional Democratic Party, aren’t considered leftist in my work.
The source did not always accurately indicate the newspaper’s affiliation with a particu-
lar party. Often researchers noted that periodical was “bourgeois”, “labor”, or controlled
by the “tsarist government”.
It
is worth nothing that this source was compiled in the USSR, which creates grounds for
political bias. The Soviet government could influence the scientists gathering this data
not to include some periodicals in the collection. I assume that Soviet scholars tried to
document leftist labor parties periodicals in the most complete way possible (which is
partially confirmed by the presence in the source of a large amount of illegal leftist press
printed on hectographs). Hence, when studying the correlation between the leftist labor
press and popular discontent, the under-registration issue is unlikely.

In such cases, I considered only ”labor” periodicals.

The main dependent variables in my study are peasant revolts and worker strikes. The
data on peasant revolts from 1901 to 1913 were taken from the database of Dmitriy Ko-
fanov used in the research Career incentives in political hierarchy: evidence from Imperial
Russia (Gokmen & Kofanov, 2020). I took data on number of workers, worker strikes,
the number of workers participated in strikes, and workers wages from the Reports of
factory inspectors. All these data were also collected, classified and digitised manually by
me from the scans of the reports of factory inspections. It is important to note that the
inspectors’ reports do not include data on the strikes of 1905-1908, since during the First
Russian Revolution it was difficult to count strikes and number of workers participated in
them due to the mass scale nature of those strikes. Thus, in this research I used the data
on worker strikes from 1901-1904 and 1909-1913. Also, in the calculation of number of
workers per gubernia the inspection took into account only the workers who were imposed
to pay fines. Thus, the parameter of average wage per worker, obtained by dividing the
total of all workers’ incomes by their number, is noisy. This is a limitation of my dataset
that could potentially bias the estimate of the correlation of interest, but there is no better
quality data for worker wages.

4 Hypothesis and main results

4.1 Hypothesis

In my research, I am going to explore the correlation between labor leftist media (pe-
riodical print media) and popular unrest (peasant uprisings and worker strikes). There
are several theoretically possible directions of the link between these variables. Positive
direction: agitators, by distributing the leftist press, might intensify the emerging social
conflict between workers (or peasants) and the state. Also, as it was already discussed,
there might be a reverse causality. For example, agitators could spread the leftist press
in gubernias with high levels of discontent in order to destabilize the situation even more.
These two situations under consideration may be the reason for the positive relation-
ship between the above variables. On the other hand, the link might be negative: if the
left press adopted an extremely confrontational tone towards the tsarist regime, it may
have alienated more moderate or cautious peasants and workers. In the case of reverse
causality, the leftist parties could spread their media in the relatively peaceful gubernias
to provide the initial impetus for the beginning of popular discontent. These options
may explain the negative relationship between the variables. I hypothesize that the dis-
tribution of politically left-wing periodicals took place near the place of printing, since
the movement of agitators over long distances leads to more costs: increased risk to be
caught by the police, costs on train tickets, time costs, etc. Therefore, I assume that the
correlation between the left press and popular unrest will be stronger the closer it is to the
analyzed gubernia. Further, I will apply the gravity model method, which will discount
the correlation between left-wing press and popular unrest by the distance between the
analyzed region and the print region of the periodical.

Before addressing the issue of literacy, I try to consider the causal relationship between the
left-wing press and popular unrest. For this, I’m using the instrumental variable method.
As an instrumental variable, I use the number of printing houses in the gubernia in 1904,
following the logic from master thesis of Andrey Kolyadenko5. Due to the First Russian

press. The variable proposed by Kolyadenko6 represents the number of printing houses,
which is taken to be zero until 1905, and then take as a constant value thereafter. This
variable construction is motivated by the fact that it is very difficult to change printing
capacity quickly and the figure for 1904 may well approximate the value for subsequent
years. The value equal to 0 before 1905 since left parties could not use these printing
capacities because of censorship. I think that this variable may be used as an instrument,
because is should be correlated with leftist press, while at the same time the number of
printing houses in each gubernia should be exogenous towards peasant revolts and strikes.

6 Testing the literacy hypothesis

Based on the 1897 census data, I calculated the number of people who can read for
each gubernia in rural and urban areas. In order to test the hypothesis that left-wing
publications had an insignificant correlation with peasant revolts and statistically signif-
icant correlation with worker strikes because of different literacy levels, I added to the
basic gravity model the interaction of the left-wing press variables (Gravity volumeit and
Local volumeit) with the share of the literate population in rural and urban areas, respec-
tively.

As it can be seen, with the addition of the interaction on the share of the literate urban
population, the statistical significance of Local volume urbanit for worker strikes (2) and
worker strikes participants (3) disappeared. It is important to note that in the case of
worker strikes (2) the interaction of urban literacy level with the Local volume variable is
positive and statistically significant at 10% level. This evidence supports the hypothesis
of literacy as the channel that provided the significance of the relationship between the
leftist press and popular unrest. In the case of worker strikes participants (3) this interac-
tion itself is not statistically significant. However, the sign of the coefficient of the variable
Local volume urban decreased but still remained positive. The sign of the interaction of
this variable with the share of literate urban population is also positive. Despite the fact
that the interaction turned out to be statistically insignificant, jointly the variables of
left volume with their interactions on literacy are statistically significant at the 1% level
(F = 7.16). This result also partially supports the hypothesis put forward about literacy
rates. Although my specifications include fixed effects and linear trends, so the literacy
rate variation across provinces and its growth are already implicitly accounted for in my
models, for a more rigorous confirmation of this hypothesis it is necessary to obtain an-
nual data on the literacy of the population in the Russian Empire. Unfortunately, no
such data is available at this time.

The results obtained support the idea that leftist press did have a statistically significant
positive correlation with workers’ strikes because workers were literate people and could
read the propaganda disseminated by the leftist parties. For peasants we do not observe
such an significance, probably because they were mostly illiterate. Therefore, there might
be two options: either left-wing press had no effect on peasants because of that or left
parties thinking strategically didn’t distribute left press among peasants, knowing it would
have no effect on their behaviour.

7 Conclusion

The goal of my work is to examine the relationship between the leftist press in early
20th century Tsarist Russia and popular unrest.
In this paper, I found a statistically
significant positive correlation between the number of worker strikes (as well as for strikes
participants) and left-wing political periodicals. In the case of peasant revolts, no such
correlation was observed. I hypothesized that the presence of the correlation in the case
of workers and its absence in the case of peasants is due to the different literacy levels of
these social groups. Analyzing this hypothesis by adding the interaction of the left press
variables with the shares of literate population in rural and urban areas, it was found that
statistical significance of correlation in the case of worker strikes disappears. Interaction
with literacy rate in case of worker strike was also found to be positive and statistically
significant. This fact partially supports the hypothesis. Workers were much more likely to
be able to read than peasants, so the leftist press either had no effect on peasant revolts,
or the leftist parties did not distribute the press in the villages.

This finding is an important historical result in the context of Tsarist Russia. Workers, be-
ing more literate people, were more easily influenced by printed propaganda and therefore
were (or at least were seen by leftist parties) the main driving class of revolutionary-leftist
movements. Peasants, on the other hand, were not exposed to propaganda to the same
extent, due to their illiteracy. In addition, this fact is important for historical economics
in general, as these results give us deeper understanding of semi-agrarian autocratic soci-
eties of the time (Portugal, Bulgaria, Serbia, Romania, etc.) and the political processes
that took place in them. Also, finding a statistically significant correlation in the case
of worker strikes is an important result within the field of media economic studies. As
mentioned earlier, most studies in this area are conducted on data from democratic states,
due to their greater availability. In turn, my research is conducted on data from authori-
tarian state. I found that, even with state censorship, there is some connection between
opposition media and their audiences.

Of course, my paper does not cover the topic of leftist media in Tsarist Russia in its
entirety.
I see several possible ways to further develop this research. First of all, the
resolution of causality problem is crucial for the understanding whether the opposition
leftist media had the effect on the action of people or not. Secondly, the database I use
has serious limitations: the number of worker strikes and their participants from 1907
to 1908 is unknown, since the factory inspectors did not keep records, and there is no
decomposition of data at the uezd level. Also, there is no data on Siberian and Central
Asian gubernias of the Russian Empire, the sampling is limited to the early 20th century,
and literacy levels are known only for 1897. In addition, it might be interesting to analyze

23

other media resources, such as public speeches of agitators, possibly based on the data on
political crimes. This line of analysis is of additional interest because in the case of public
speeches, the literacy of the people the propaganda is trying to influence is not required.
