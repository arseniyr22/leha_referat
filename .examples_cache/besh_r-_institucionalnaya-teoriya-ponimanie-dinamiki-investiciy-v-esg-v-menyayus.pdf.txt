Abstract 

Institutional factors have the power to shape an industry and influence business operations 

of  all  included  organizations.  Coercive,  normative,  and  cultural-cognitive  elements  can 

implement trends within the economy. The financial industry is experiencing significant change 

over the last years. Main topic hereby is  the trend of environmental, social, and governance 

(ESG) investing. The growth of ESG investing was steadily increasing in the past five years, 

but in 2023, the growth rate decreased. There is a strong correlation between the development 

of institutional frameworks and ESG in the financial industry.  

This dissertation is focusing on analysing the intertwining between ESG investing and 

institutional  factors. The development  of ESG is  being  investigated. Furthermore,  this  work 

looks  at  what  role  institutional  factors  played  and  how  the  current  state  of  the  institutional 

framework  and  ESG  landscape  is  shaped.  The  three  main  pillars  of  institutional  theory, 

regulations and rules, industry standards and norms, and social pressures get researched to find 

out  the  connection  and  influence  of  them  regarding  the  development  of  ESG  investing. 

Furthermore, this work discusses macroeconomic and global trends which influence investment 

behaviour,  challenges  and  barriers  of  implementation,  strategies  and  adaptions,  and  future 

directions of ESG development in the financial industry. Goal of this work is to find out what 

the  predominant  institutional  factors  are  that  contribute  to  the  recent  slowdown  of  the  ESG 

investing  growth  rate.  First,  a  literature  review  and  theoretical  foundation  give  a  deep 

understanding  of  the  topic.  To  gather  relevant  and  reliable  data,  qualitative  interviews  with 

industry experts from the financial industry in Switzerland, Liechtenstein, Germany, and India 

are  being  carried  out.  After  that,  the  empirical  results  get  compared  with  the  literature  and 

conclusions are drawn. 

Macroeconomic  trends,  such  as  the  current  global  economic  instability  and  shifts  in 

market dynamics, have led to a cautious investment approach. Many investors favour traditional 

investment options due to their perceived stability and reliability. Less social pressure on the 

development  of ESG  cause a slowdown of implemented  regulations and industry standards. 

These institutional factors are now less forceful, which contributes to the observed slowdown. 

Keywords: Institutional Theory; ESG Investing; Institutional Landscape; Financial Industry

Introduction 

In the first chapter of this master dissertation follows a systematic introduction into the 

sphere of the topic of institutional theory and ESG investments. This starts with analysis of 

the research background. Thereafter, the relevance of this research gets explained. This shall 

give a general overview of the importance of this sphere and the significance of the research. 

The following research goal and objectives give exact guidance on what this research aims 

to generate. The description of distinctive features and the chapter about the structure of the 

dissertation show the given framework which helps to better understand the guidance through 

this paper. 

1.1 

 Research background 

In understanding the realms of organizational  analysis, social sciences, and external 

influences, institutional theory has long stood as a major pillar of these relations (Furusten, 

2023). It provides a structured framework with which it is possible to deeply dive into the 

practices of organizational life and its relations with the business world. The interconnection 

of this theory and all  business  spheres makes it an indispensable construct  of the modern 

economic  world.  Since  the  beginning  of  organizational  development  and  the  modern 

economic landscape, institutional factors always shaped the way of how organizations are 

operating  (DiMaggio  &  Powell,  1983).  It  offers  a  framework  of  understanding  how 

institutional factors, such as comprising norms, regulations and laws, rules, and social and 

cultural factors shape the behaviour of individuals and organizations. It is generally proven 

that institutions can influence social actors, organizations, and individuals in a significant 

way, and this happens not only via formal regulations, but also via informal cultural, social, 

and  cognitive  ways.  This  perspective  is  crucial  to  understand  the  interplay  between 

institutional structures and individual or organizational behaviour (Furusten, 2023).  

This also applies to the context of financial markets, its organizations, and individuals. 

Institutional  theory  shows  how  institutional  frameworks  influence  general  investment 

behaviours and trends. It acts as a guide that drives the decision-making process of investors 

through  a  variety  of  regulatory  mechanisms,  ethical  standards  and  norms,  and  social 

influences. The financial market is very complex and there are many influencing factors on 

how it operates  (Lewellyn &  Muller-Kahle, 2023). The application of institutional theory 

helps to understand these complexities of investment decisions and the underlying factors 

that lead to certain business operations. Institutional factors contain a distinctive number of

drivers. They focus on influencing business operations to a certain pattern (Risi et al., 2023). 

There are different intentions behind the implementation of factors and drivers. To a certain 

extent, trends in organizational, social, and economical behaviour play a significant role. For 

the past two decades, the trend of sustainability gained importance in all economic and social 

spheres. This also affected the financial industry tremendously. The keyword here is ESG, 

which stands for Environmental, Social, and Governance (Wen et al., 2022). This represents 

a notable shift in the financial world towards long-term sustainable development, which also 

includes  investment  behaviour.  Sustainability,  ethical  considerations,  and  social  parts  got 

increasingly integrated into investment strategies and are currently a fixed component of the 

financial  industry.  The  main  idea  behind  the  trend  of  ESG  investing  is  to  develop  more 

sustainable business practices, which should then lead to long-term value creation not only 

in financial terms, but also in the development of a sustainable environment where ethics and 

social responsibilities play a crucial role. This shift towards sustainable business practices is 

a  result  of  the  general  societal  movement  towards  sustainability,  driven  by  growing 

awareness  of  global  environmental  challenges,  social  inequalities  and  injustices,  and 

governance issues within the business world (Dmuchowski et al., 2023). 

 The interplay between institutional theory and ESG investing offers a broad area of 

research. Institutional factors play a critical role in shaping the ESG investment landscape. 

The investment decisions by organizations and private investors depend significantly on the 

influencing norms and the regulatory frameworks of the financial industry. Cognitive factors 

and social pressures, such as the social movement towards sustainability, have a tremendous 

impact on investment decisions (Kerber et al., 2023). Banks, wealth management companies, 

and  other  financial  institutions,  invest  the  money  according  to  the  given  regulatory 

framework and desires of investors. Therefore, the connection between institutional theory 

and how it affects the investment landscape is clearly given, which underpins the importance 

of  analysis  of  this  relation.  Besides  that,  the  institutional  theory  is  providing  a  valuable 

framework for understanding the general dynamics of current ESG investing, including its 

main  drivers  and  barriers  for  ESG  adaptation  and  overall  development  (Avramov  et  al., 

2022).

1.2 

 Research relevance 

The global economy is becoming more intertwined than ever, and the different industries 

have tremendous influence on each other. The influencing institutional factors therefore have 

increasingly high impact on the business operations within the economy. Where there is money, 

there is power, which is a fundamental rule of business (Furusten, 2023). This is also applicable 

to our economy with its main driver, the financial industry. It has an immense influence on the 

current state of the economy and also its future developments. The financial industry has the 

power to steer the economy in a certain direction (Lewellyn & Muller-Kahle, 2023). And that 

is also the main reason why the institutional factors that are influencing the financial sector, are 

of such importance. They can change the economy in a way that affects all industries, and all 

people. Because of that, the movement of sustainability affects the whole economy. Traditional 

investment paradigms, which primarily focused on financial returns without always taking the 

broader effect of corporate operations into account, have been significantly replaced by ESG 

investments (Pedersen et al., 2021). Therefore, ESG investments promote a more reasonable 

and  sustainable  global  economy  by  matching  financial  rewards  with  larger  social  goals 

(Andonov et al., 2021). 

This trend increased in importance over the last 25 years and is currently one of the most 

important factors of modern finance and our economy. When we talk about the starting point 

of ESG investing itself, it is difficult to point out an exact spot. But many refer to the year 1990 

where the first socially responsible investment index was established. This brought ESG on the 

map in the financial world and since then it grew in popularity (Masters & Temple-West, 2023). 

In the beginning the development  was modest,  but  over time, the encouragement of society 

helped ESG to establish itself within the investment landscape.  

The biggest boom of ESG in modern finance happened in the year 2018. Larry Douglas 

Fink, CEO of the biggest wealth management company in the world, BlackRock, made ESG 

one of the main pillars of their investment strategy. This was the step that made ESG the centre 

of  modern-day  financial  investment  decisions.  Besides  that,  this  step  of  Larry  D.  Fink  has 

triggered  another,  even  stronger  wave  of  support  and  advancement  of  sustainable  business 

practices and regulations. Many other financial institutions followed the lead of BlackRock and 

increased  their  investments  in  ESG  portfolios  and  investment  opportunities  (Zickus,  2024). 

Despite the fact  that non-ESG investments  are still popular, since they bring steady returns, 

ESG  investments  grew  exponentially.  This  led  to  a  global  ESG  Asset  under  Management 

(AUM) of 35 trillion US-Dollars in the year 2023 (Kerber et al., 2023). This is remarkable since

this corresponds to 27.5 percent of the total global Asset under Management. Between the years 

2016 and 2022 the average growth rate of ESG AUM was 11.7 percent (Zickus, 2024). But 

after the year 2022, something changed. ESG investments were still growing, but the growth 

rate declined to 3.5 percent in the year 2023. That represents a 70 percent decline in the growth 

rate, which is surprising. The whole movement of sustainability, ESG, and sustainable investing 

is developing even further and therefore the decrease is surprising not only financial investors, 

but all people (Masters & Temple-West, 2023). It raises questions about the factors influencing 

the growth or decline of ESG investments and the role of institutional factors in shaping the 

landscape of sustainable finance. It is essential to understand the reasons behind this slowdown 

and how the influencing factors intertwine with each other. It can be part of the foundation of 

understanding  the  current  ESG  landscape,  the  current  state  of  institutional  factors,  and  the 

reasoning  behind  investment  decisions.  It  has  implications  for  the  future  of  sustainable 

investing and the overall goal of a sustainable global economy, and therefore demands critical 

research. 

1.3 

 Research goal and objectives 

The research question for this paper is: 

What are the predominant institutional factors contributing to the recent slowdown in 

the growth of ESG investments? 

The main goal of this research is to explore the ESG landscape in the financial industry 

and analyse how institutional factors influence investment operations and the decision-making 

process. This paper will find out what are the institutional reasons for the recent slowdown of 

the ESG growth rate and how this influences the market. It is a multi-faceted analysis of not 

only  the  investment  landscape  of  ESG,  but  it  also  gives  an  understanding  of  the  broader 

regulatory,  societal,  and  market  conditions  that  have  a  large-scale  impact  on  investment 

decisions.  This  research  will  gather  information  about  the  background  and  context  of 

institutional  theory  and  how  it  is  applicable  to  the  ESG  landscape.  It  will  examine  what 

influence  market  and  macroeconomic  dynamics  have  and  how  they  interface  within  the 

institutional  framework.  Besides  that,  it  will  give  explanations  on  how  and  why  investment 

strategies  are  adapting.  This  study  aims  to  analyse  these  complexities,  providing  a  detailed 

explanation of the interactions between investor behaviour, regulatory impacts, transparency, 

and  market  dynamics  within  the  ESG  investing  field.  This  study  tends  to  fill  the  crucial 

information gaps found in the body of current literature by identifying the main causes of the

2  Literature review

2.1 

 Institutional Theory 

Institutional  theory  provides  an  important  framework  to  understand  organizational 

behaviour,  especially  the  immense  influence  of  institutions  on  the  actions  and  structures  of 

organizations. The theory has evolved over time and is mesmerizing different perspectives and 

addressing  a  variety  of  subjects  such  as  finance,  societal  norms,  governance,  and  others. 

Institutional theory has  been researched by many people in  academia, due to  the fact  that it 

affects  all  business  industries,  and  that  this  theory  is  already  part  of  the  organizational  and 

business environment since over 40 years. One of the most famous academic research in this 

field was done by DiMaggio & Powell (1983). They are considered as the main founders of 

institutional  theory  and  this  research  is  about  institutional  isomorphism.  It  explains  how 

organizations conform to the expectations of specific parts of their environment, which in the 

end leads to increased homogeneity within this environment. The authors underline different 

parts  of  isomorphism,  such  as  coercive,  mimetic,  and  normative  pressures.  They  claim  that 

these factors contribute as mechanisms to make companies within an industry more similar over 

time. 

But even before DiMaggio & Powell, there was another research that is considered one 

of the first academic research papers in the field of institutional theory. Meyer & Rowan (1977) 

explore  how  organizations  adopt  formal  structure.  They  claim  that  organizations  do  this 

primarily to gain legitimacy and conformity to societal expectations and not to increase their 

efficiency. Another discovery was that organizations often engage in separating their formal 

structures from their actual business practices. The reason behind is, is to maintain a certain 

flexibility  while  appearing  to  the  outside  as  an  organization  that  is  compliant  with  the 

institutional norms. Glynn & D’Aunno (2023) provide a systematic analysis of the history of 

institutional theory and the evolution over time. This research serves as a fundament for the 

reflection of the development of this theory and helps to understand the overall areas where it 

is applicable. The authors even state that the theory has its origins in the early 1900s. In this 

time, it was not described as institutional theory, but it included several  parts of the today’s 

state of the theory. The scope of institutional theory increased and got addressed in different

business spheres.  

Many research papers were conducted in all different spheres. A research done by Jiang 

et  al.  (2024)  describes  the  impact  of  foreign  ownership  on  bribery  practices  in  Chinese 

companies.  They  use  institutional  theory  to  argue  that  foreign-owned  firms  have  a  lower 

probability to engage in any form of bribery or fraud due to institutional norms in their home 

countries. Another study focused on the impact  of executive cognitive characteristics on the 

ESG performance of a firm. It gets analysed from the perspective of institutional theory and 

reveals  that  top-level  executives  who  have  a  high  awareness  of  social  norms  significantly 

influence their companies ESG outcome (M. Liu et al., 2024). This supports the integration of 

cognitive  and  institutional  perspectives  and  underlines  the  importance  of  understanding  the 

surrounding norms and guidelines in the respective business fields. 

Besides  that,  the  increased  scope  included  new  terms,  such  as  corporate  social 

responsibility (CSR). This sphere was researched by Panicker et al. (2024) and they explain the 

differential impact of CSR investment on financial gains in multinational companies (MNCs) 

in  the  Indian  market.  This  emphasises  the  important  role  of  institutional  factors,  such  as 

institutional  and  geographical  distance.  It  is  a  reflection  of  the  connection  between  CSR 

practices and institutional factors. It outlines the institutional theory in the context of emerging 

markets and their overall financial performance. The authors point out that institutional forces 

have a significant influence on shaping the corporate sustainability strategy of an organization. 

The importance of aligning with the SDGs to develop long-term benefits is also highlighted 

(Galeazzo et al., 2024). 

In  a  study  by  Arduino  et  al.  (2024)  the  impact  of  institutional  investors  on  the  ESG 

transparency of family companies and their approach to it got researched. They unveiled how 

institutional  pressures  can  shape  disclosure  practices  of  companies.  They  offer  insights  into 

various  dynamics  between  institutional  investors  and  the  transparency  approach  of 

organizations.  Another  interesting  research  was  published  by  Elango  (2024),  where  he 

addresses the impact of institutional disruptions on MNC subsidiary control systems. This is 

about  how  institutional  changes  influence  the  options  of  control  mechanisms  within  an 

organization while navigating through the complex institutional framework.  

Lei & Yu (2024) explain in their research what influence institutional investors have on 

green  financial  policy  and  corporate  ESG  performance.  The  outcome  of  their  research 

underlines  the  importance  of  green  financial  policies  in  the  enhancement  of  corporate  ESG 

outcomes. They claim that without the active promotion of institutional investors about green

financial policies, the overall ESG development and performance will not enhance as much. 

Another important aspect of institutional theory is institutional isomorphism. For a long time 

this was not a topic of research, but due to its increasing significance, Posadas et al.  (2023)

2.2 

 ESG Investing 

As Starks (2023) claims in her article, the interplay between financial value and ethical 

values is the most important aspect of sustainable finance and ESG investing. She focuses on 

the  navigation  of  investors  and  corporations  in  the  complex  dynamics  of  integrating 

environmental,  social,  and  governance  considerations  into  their  financial  decision-making 

processes.  The  outcome  was  the  clear  underlining  of  the  shift  that  is  happening  towards  a 

growing recognition of ESG factors as critical components in evaluating long-term value of a 

firm. This shift is also confirmed by Hartzmark & Sussman (2019) in their research paper. They 

are  using  a  natural  experiment  in  which  funds  are  reclassified  according  to  sustainability 

criteria,  to  explore  whether  investors  give  higher  capital  allocations  to  funds  with  high 

sustainability  ratings.  According  to  their  research,  funds  which  have  higher  sustainability 

rankings experience a higher inflow of funds, which indicates that investors indeed place a high 

value on sustainability and focus on funds with an overall positive environmental, social, and 

governance performance.  

Bauer et al. (2021) also claim in their research article that many investors have a clear 

preference of investing in sustainable companies, even when they show slightly worse financial 

performance then other companies. It is also researched that these investment decisions based 

on ESG indicators is a rather emotional decision for many investors. The clear target of meeting 

financial performance indicators is not anymore the only important thing. It also attracts new 

investors who have the perception of investing in long-term sustainable companies (Heeb et al., 

2023). It became also more common to reveal environmental information about the company 

with  a  climate  risk  disclosure.  According  to  a  study,  enhanced  transparency  through  such 

disclosures leads to a significant reallocation of capital by private and institutional investors 

towards companies with  lower climate  risks. This  highlights the important  role of clear  and 

comprehensive  climate  risk  information  to  investors  and  other  stakeholders  for  reshaping 

institutional investment strategies (Ilhan et al., 2023).

Another  important  factor  to  take  into  consideration  in  sustainable  investments  is  the 

uncertainty of ESG rating factors. Due to the high complexity of these factors, it is difficult to 

evaluate which firms align with the sustainable targets of investors. This also reconnects with 

the issue of greenwashing, which is a not uncommon practice, especially in global companies. 

Avramov  et  al.  (2022)  investigate  this  issue  and  address  how  the  variability  and  potential 

discrepancies in ESG ratings can impact investment decisions and the portfolio construction of 

investors. They demonstrate that investors can still build portfolios that successfully match their 

risk tolerance and sustainability goals with the difficulties presented by ESG rating ambiguity, 

underscoring the adaptability and resilience of sustainable investing strategies. 

It  is  also  of  importance  to  understand  the  influence  of  ESG  criteria  into  investment 

portfolios. Since these criteria are often difficult to accurately measure, the influence can be 

difficult to measure. The most important outcome is always the profit. Therefore, the influence 

of ESG criteria on profit of the investment is the focus. In a study about responsible investing, 

Pedersen et al. (Pedersen et al., 2021) analysed the influence. They put out a concept known as 

the ESG-efficient frontier, which enables investors to attain their preferred risk-return profiles 

and maximize their ESG ratings. The study shows that investors can strike an ideal  balance 

between sustainability concerns and financial success, demonstrating that ethical investing does 

not always equate to lower profits. How these green policies imposed by countries catalyses 

sustainable investment is researched by Zhang et al. (Zhang et al., 2021). The impact of China's 

environmental  legislation  on  domestic  ESG  investment  practices  is  investigated  by  the 

academics.  The  study  demonstrates  the  critical  role  that  policy  plays  in  influencing  the 

landscape of responsible investing by showing how supportive green policies in China greatly 

enhance  ESG  investing.  It  also  shows  that  carefully  designed  regulatory  frameworks  and

 Institutional Theory 

The following chapters give a deep understanding of the fundamental theory, including 

important aspects to give an overview of the broad variety and complexity of this topic. First 

the definition of institutional theory is mentioned, after that follows a general introduction into 

the topic, including subdivisions for the different aspects of institutional factors. At the end the

3.1.1  Definition of Institutional Theory 

Institutional  theory  provides  a  comprehensive  framework  of  certain  structures  that 

influence  organizational  behaviour,  even  beyond  economic  factors.  This  theory  claims  that 

organizations are strongly influenced by internal and external factors, such as norms, cultures, 

regulations, and social pressures  that drive an organization in  a certain  way of behaviour or 

operations. The following statement provides a fundamental definition of institutional theory: 

„It considers  the processes by which structures,  including  schemes, rules, norms, and 

routines, become established as authoritative guidelines for behaviour“ (DiMaggio & Powell, 

1983). 

DiMaggio and Powell are considered the main developers of this theory, and they shaped 

the development significantly. They helped to understand the relationship between institutional 

factors and organizations and created a foundation of common ground to serve as a base for 

academia and business. According to Scott (2013), it is important to highlight that institutions 

dictate what is considered legitimate and rational within a given context in an organization or 

industry. It affects the way how businesses are operating, how cultural norms are adapting, and 

how overall trends interact with regulations and industry norms.

3.1.2 

Introduction to Institutional Theory 

The roots of institutional theory are reaching back in the early 20th century, evolving out 

of contributions of sociologists and organizational theorists. The main fundament comes from 

organizational theory and one important researcher is Max Weber. His works in the early 1920’s 

highlight  the  importance  of  rational  and  legal  authority  and  their  significant  influence  on 

shaping structures of organizations (Weber, 2013). Other milestones of this theory were set by 

Meyer & Rowan  (1977). They researched the concepts of institutional isomorphism and the 

role of institutional factors and environments on general organizational behaviour. Research in 

the same aspects was done by DiMaggio & Powell (1983) who to this day, are described as the 

main developers of modern institutional theory. Their basic concepts and ideas are applied to 

all  modern  organizations  and  have  a  tremendous  impact  on  how  organizational  culture  is 

applying institutional factors into their operations.  

These  foundational  ideas  initiated  a  remarkable  shift  from  traditional  economic  and 

numerical  explanations  of  organizational  behaviour  to  a  broader  focus  which  also  includes 

cultural, social, political, and regulatory aspects. During the following years the research in this 

area has increased and the broad scope officiates as an interesting and relevant research field. 

Over time institutional theory embraced in complexity and many researchers explored different 

ways of institutional logics, impact areas, trigger points, and influencing aspects (Jepperson & 

Meyer,  2021).  The  theory  changed  in  terms  of  what  it  includes  and  today  there  are  also 

important parts newly introduced. Research today includes the analysis of aspects such as global 

trends and challenges, technological development, sustainability, and other factors that relate 

to institutional theory. These aspects underline the broad impact field of this theory. It shows 

that it is not limited to regulations or norms (Posadas et al., 2023). 

Everything that is happening in the world on a global scale has a significant impact on 

what people want and need. That shapes their demands and what they expect of companies and 

their operations (Risi et al., 2023). These factors can change the perception of people on how 

organizations should behave. Besides that, they impact other institutions and how they perceive 

organizational  behaviour.  Institutional  theory  helps  to  understand  the  deeper  meaning  and 

intertwining of social reality that influences structures, strategies, and business practices of an 

organization (Guy Peters, 2022).  

Throughout  time  and  development  of  institutional  theory,  three  main  pillars  of 

institutional factors have emerged and are established as cornerstones. This includes regulative, 

normative, and cultural-cognitive pillars. Each one of them plays a crucial role for shaping the 

19 

 
 
 
ESG Investing 

institutional landscape that organizations are operating in (Greenwood et al., 2017). Firstly, the 

regulative  pillar  is  about  the  importance  of  rules,  regulations,  and  laws  that  influence  the 

landscape.  It  is  an  important  part  of  defining  how  organizations  are  able  to  shape  their 

organizational  behaviour  and  their  management  decisions.  Secondly,  the  normative  pillar 

describes  how  norms  and  values  in  society  but  also  within  institutions  affects  organizations 

(Guy Peters, 2022). It serves as a guideline on which current views of society and institutions 

are important to cohere to. Thirdly, the cultural-cognitive pillar emphasizes the importance of 

shared beliefs and general understandings which make desirable actions. These pillars influence 

and  are  dependent  on  each  other.  For  organizations  it  is  important  to  try  to  be  in  overall 

alignment  with  them  to  improve  their  widespread  acceptance  and  increased  efficiency  and 

success of business practices  (Andonov et al., 2021).  

Another perspective of institutional theory is brought up and analysed by DiMaggio & 

Powell  (1983).  They  address  the  mechanisms  through  which  institutions  exercise  their 

influences  and  how  organizations  develop  over  time  when  they  face  constant  institutional 

factors.  This  is  described  as  isomorphism.  The  authors  identify  three  main  mechanisms  – 

coercive, mimetic, and normative isomorphism (Elango, 2024). The main concept behind it is 

that organizations get pushed to homogeneity over time and develop similar practices. Coercive 

isomorphism results from formal and informal pressures (Marano & Kostova, 2016). Mimetic 

isomorphism comes from imitation of models that are perceived as best-practice or successful, 

whereas  normative  isomorphism  arises  from  professionalization  of  norms  within  the 

organizational landscape (Posadas et al., 2023). 

3.1.3 

Legal regulations and compliance (coercive) 

In this chapter, the context of legal regulations and compliance within institutional theory 

is being given. Legal frameworks and regulatory compliance build the regulatory part of this 

theory and are a cornerstone of understanding how landscapes are shaped. There is a dynamic 

interplay  between  regulatory  environments  and  organizational  strategies.  The  regulatory 

landscape  has  a  nature  of  evolving  due  to  changing  regulations  and  it  guides  institutional 

practices in these aspects. It is a fundamental pillar of organizational behaviour and builds the 

frame of the environment in which organizations are doing their business. They might interpret 

these regulations as dictated rules that hinder their operations, but just as Edelman (Edelman, 

1992) describes it in  his fundamental study, it should  be understood as  active  elements  that 

shape organizational structures within the companies. Regulations can offer a broad variety of 

20 

 
 
 
 
ESG Investing 

positive  influences,  since  the  provide  a  structure  that  is  applicable  to  all  organizations  and 

companies within this field and therefore everybody has to apply to the same rules. In the end 

it is up to the organizations on how to accept and interpret these regulations (Uchida, 2023). 

They have to navigate through the regulatory framework to align with institutional expectations 

and to gain overall legitimacy of their organization. This part of the theory does not only create 

boundaries for organizations, but it also offers a landscape in which they can create and develop 

their business strategies. 

Compliance mechanisms, regulatory sanctions, and the role of law in organizational life 

are critical aspects to emphasize within the regulative pillar. Scott (2013) highlights that legal 

regulations  serve  as  more  than  simple  constrains.  They  are  indispensable  parts  for 

understanding organizational dynamics and the overall institutional theory (Guy Peters, 2022). 

Without regulations, the system would be based on assumptions, feelings, and opinions, which 

are  not  optimal  fundaments  for  a  framework  that  should  provide  longevity  and  quality  of 

business practices (Ilhan et al., 2023). Rules and compliance aspects have a significant impact 

on organizational practices. Other influencing factors include global trends digitalization and 

newly set sustainability goals. Organizations find themselves in a situation where they need to 

understand and implement traditional legal compliance, but also create innovative responses to 

new regulatory changes (Jepperson & Meyer, 2021). These imposed formal rules can be used 

to control behavioural compliance and institutional order within a landscape (Furusten, 2023)..  

The rules are created by governmental actors or state institutions which possess the power 

to  set  laws.  Therefore,  political  interest  interface  with  institutional  aspects  and  can  create 

tension between organizations and rule makers. Big influencing factors in this area are power 

of  diplomacy,  negotiation  and  lobbying  of  interest  parties  and  overall  political  strategies 

imposed by governmental bodies (Glynn & D’Aunno, 2023). The intertwined connections of 

legal regulations and compliance within the context of institutional theory reveals a landscape 

where laws and organizations are in constant relation and dialogue (Furusten, 2023).  

3.1.4 

Industry standards and norms (normative) 

Another pivotal role in institutional theory plays the normative pillar, including industry 

standards and norms. Unlike the regulatory pressures, these standard and norms are not a result 

of implemented formal rules. They derive from shares values, norms, and expectations within 

the  industry.  As  explained  by  Brunsson  &  Jacobsson  (2002),  these  normative  pressures  can 

reach the same extent of influence like formal rules. Organizations will implement these norms

and adapt according to them to gain legitimacy and acceptance within their business fields. It 

is of significant importance to find yourself in line with industry standards, because for external 

institutions  and  people  it  is  crucial  that  organizations  where  they  want  to  invest  in  or  make 

operations together, they have to fulfil certain standards (Lammers & Garcia, 2017). Besides 

that, the adherence to certain established norms and standards can be seen as a key driver for 

organizations  to  maintain  order  and  consistency  in  their  business  operations,  which  enables 

them to face simpler and less complicated interactions between different  interest groups and 

stakeholders.  

These norms define how organizations within the field should behave and operate (Li & 

Haleblian, 2022). That happens in an informal way, but it is still critical to try to implement it 

to  establish  long-term  stability  and  success.  These  standards  and  norms  cover  a  wide  range 

(Massi  et  al.,  2021).  Usually,  they  are  concerning  about  technical  specifications,  quality 

benchmarks, ethical guidelines, and organizational codes of conduct. Due to this broad range, 

it  affects  the  whole  organizational  operations,  including  product  or  service  development, 

operational  processes,  strategies,  innovations,  and  overall  organizational  culture.  Given  an 

example by the financial industry, standards and norms play a significant role in operations. 

During the last 15 years, the industry created explicit financial reporting standards to advance 

inter-organizational value (Sahin & Mert, 2023).  

Very often the focus is about being in line with established norms and standards, since

in  the  meantime  there  is  the  risk  of  losing  needed  resources  for  business  innovation  and 

improvement,  which  is  substantial  for  success.  That  is  a  main  challenge  that  needs  to  be 

addressed by organizations and requires a tremendous amount of skills and experience (Li & 

Haleblian, 2022). And exactly in this area big organizations that are also market leaders within 

their  industry  have  an  advantage.  Due  to  their  high  influence  and  size,  they  can  shape  new 

industry standards  and norms and align them  to  their own goals  and primary  organizational 

guidelines. This can elevate their market leading position and that is why smaller organizations 

have  it  more  difficult  to  keep  up  with  the  fast-paced  changes  and  dynamics  of  normative 

frameworks. 

The normative pillar of institutional theory offers a deep understanding of the complex 

landscape  of  industry  standards  and  norms  which  impact  organizational  behaviour  and 

operations  significantly  (Sahin  &  Mert,  2023).  It  becomes  clear  that  industry  standards  and 

norms are crucial in guiding organizations in their strategies and operations while demanding

3.1.5 

Social expectations and pressures (cultural-cognitive) 

The  cultural-cognitive  pillar  of  institutional  theory  emphasizes  the  influence  of  social 

expectations  and  pressures  on  organizational  behaviour.  This  goes  beyond  formal  rules  and 

industry standards, it is more about  unspoken or on the contrary  distinct societal  norms and 

opinions. Organizations have to deeply understand the opinions and wishes of society to adapt 

their operations and gain the eligibility of the people. These expectations ad pressures shape the 

identity of organizations and their actions. Meyer & Rowan (1977) and Zucker (1987) were the 

first  researchers  who  explained  that  organizations  adopt  their  practices  not  just  to  certain 

efficiency  aspects,  but  also  to  align  with  societal  expectations.  The  influence  of  society  is 

tremendous,  because  at  the  end  of  the  day  the  people  are  the  customers,  investors,  and 

employees and all organizations are dependent on them. Therefore, it is deeply embedded that 

social norms and cognitive influences guide organizational actions (Chen et al., 2023).  

In recent  years, social  expectations increased significantly and shaped the institutional 

landscape of various industries. One of the main drivers to mention is the growing awareness 

of sustainability. This trend already exists for more than 30 years, but within the last 10 to 15 

years it reached new dimensions. Societal expectations in terms of sustainability have changed 

and now society demands more sustainable business practices of organizations (Skandera et al., 

2023). They pressure whole industries to adopt to climate change and implement sustainability 

as  one  of  the  central  factors.  But  the  changing  social  expectations  are  not  limited  to 

sustainability  and  the  environment.  They  extend  to  issues  such  as  diversity  and  inclusion, 

sustainable  business  management,  corporate  social  responsibility,  and  many  more.  Society 

grew their demands for overall sustainable business practices. They wish for a corporate culture 

that is inclusive to all genders, nationalities, and operates in an open way while following a 

certain code of conduct. The challenge hereby for organizations are that to be successful in the 

market, they need to find a balance between implementing these societal pressures and keeping 

their own corporate identity and competitive advantage (Bitektine & Song, 2023). Besides that, 

over time these pressures also created challenges.  

One of the main issues to mention is greenwashing. This describes the process of when 

organizations are operating not sustainable, ethical, or inclusive, but try to improve their public 

image by taking part in certain corporate social responsibility actions. This shows to society 

that they are a sustainable organization, but in reality, it is just a facade. This is something that

3.1.6  Macroeconomic and global trends 

In  the  development  of  institutional  factors,  macroeconomic  and  global  trends  play  a 

pivotal role. While it is not part of the theory itself, due to the dynamic elements they impact 

the  regulatory,  normative,  and  cultural-cognitive  dimensions.  Organizational  behaviour  and

3.2.3 

Social considerations 

The social dimension of ESG evaluates how a company is managing their relationships 

with  customers, suppliers, employees  and other involved stakeholders  (Wen et  al.,  2022).  It 

includes  diversity  and  inclusion,  human  rights  treatment,  social  customer  satisfaction  and

stakeholders with respect, fairness and social inclusion tend to have a more sustainable long-

3.2.4  Governance considerations 

The  way  of  leadership,  internal  controls,  managing  shareholder  rights  and  audits  of  a 

company builds the governance aspect.  It shows the company’s quality and fairness of their 

administration,  their  board  structure,  compensation  of  executives  and  top-tier  managers, 

interactions with stakeholders and most importantly, transparency of operations. The main idea 

behind a positive corporate governance is to build trust among stakeholders and to attract more 

investments. With these influences, there is a high chance of increased operational performance 

and long-term success (Sinha & Juneja, 2022). For investors, this builds an important part in 

their investment-decision. The governance of a company mirrors their decision-making process, 

risk  management  strategies  and  the  attitude  and  relationship  management  with  stakeholders 

(Bauer et al., 2021). 

3.2.5 

The rise of ESG Investing 

The concept of ESG (Environmental, Social, and Governance) investing has come a long 

way, changing our perception of investments and business practices. This strategy has origins 

in the socially conscious investing movements of the 1960s and 1970s; it did not emerge out of 

nothing. Investors in the past would frequently resist from funding companies that disagreed 

with their moral convictions, like those that produced tobacco products or firearms. Modern 

ESG investing, on the other hand, is more proactive, prioritizing the support of businesses that 

actively exhibit good environmental, social, and governance policies (Kaminskyi, 2022). 

The early 2000s experienced a significant increase in the popularity of ESG investment. 

Global concerns like inequality in society, climate change, and the value of moral leadership in 

businesses  came  to  the  attention  of  more  people  (Zeidan,  2022).  The  United  Nations'

introduction of the Principles for Responsible Investment (PRI) in 2006 was a major driver. 

This  gave investors a precise set  of rules for taking  ESG factors into account  when making 

investing decisions. Around the same time, a number of instruments and services that assessed 

a  company's  performance  according  to  ESG  standards  also  gained  popularity  (Bell  &  Van 

Vuuren, 2022). The largest increase in ESG investment took place in 2018 because of Larry 

Fink, the CEO of Blackrock, the largest wealth management company in the world. ESG gained 

popularity after it was shown to the public that it will influence the nature of investments going 

forward. 

It  became  obvious  to  many  that  businesses  which  do  well  in  ESG  areas  are  not  only 

benefiting the world but are also frequently more profitable and effective in the long run. There 

is a variety of studies to substantiate this link. People's expectations have shifted at the same 

time. Businesses are increasingly expected by society as a whole, employees, and customers to 

be more transparent and ethical (Houston & Shan, 2022). In an effort to promote sustainable 

business practices, governments and international organizations have also taken the initiative 

and  introduced  a  variety  of  laws  and  regulations  to  sustain  the  ground  base  for  the 

implementation  (Rahman  &  Lau,  2023).  ESG  investing  is  becoming  more  popular  than  an 

investment trend. It is an essential component in modern investment strategies, indicating an 

important shift in the way that investors and businesses perceive the interplay of the financial 

system, society, and environment. It demonstrates that achieving financial success and making 

a positive impact on the world are not mutually exclusive but rather may coexist (Bansal et al., 

2022).

the  significance  of  external  factors  in  shaping  the  ESG  investment  landscape.  Since  this 

decrease  in  growth  for  ESG  investments  is  a  new  phenomenon,  the  amount  of  research  is 

limited. The existing research describes a variety of aspects of ESG in the financial landscape 

and the intertwines of sustainability  and investment  behaviour. But  there is  no research that 

outlines the influencing factors on the current decrease in the growth rate.

4.1 

 Qualitative research 

In this work, qualitative research is used to answer the research question and fulfil the 

objectives of the thesis. The qualitative approach was chosen because the topic of this work is 

complex, and the investigation requires a lot of in-depth-analysis to obtain the desired results. 

In research, a general distinction is made between qualitative and quantitative research. 

In quantitative research, hypotheses are tested (Xiao & Watson, 2017). As the name suggests, 

quantitative  research  is  about  the  quantity  of  results.  The  goal  is  to  generate  as  many 

examination  units  as  possible  in  order  to  obtain  a  reliable  and  meaningful  result.  The 

quantitative  method  is  advantageous  if  there  is  already  a  lot  of  literature  on  the  topic  being 

discussed and you want to test an existing theory (Mayring, 2010). 

In  comparison,  qualitative  research  is  about  the  detailed  and  extensive  collection  of 

information, which is evaluated interpretively (Aspers & Corte, 2019). Qualitative research is 

about context specific analysis, while quantitative research puts a focus on the quantity of data. 

This is important to gain the necessary depth of the topic and to be able to analyse the various 

issues precisely. The number of people surveyed is therefore smaller than with the quantitative 

method,  but  the  respondents  are  examined  more  closely  (Mayring,  2010).  This  is  achieved 

thanks to the greater scope for answers that the people surveyed have. The qualitative methods 

include qualitative content analysis, case study, group discussion, utility analysis and interview 

(Xiao & Watson, 2017). 

Selecting the appropriate interview format is crucial for this purpose. Rowley and Slack 

(2004)  distinguish  between  three  types  of  interviews:  semi-structured,  unstructured,  and 

structured. In a structured interview, a guide helps you through the process. All the questions 

in  this  interview  guide  are  closed-ended.  Although  a  guide  is  still  used  throughout  the 

29 

 
 
 
 
ESG Investing 

procedure, open-ended questions are asked in a semi-structured interview. There are no rules 

in  an  unstructured  interview;  only  open-ended  questions  are  posed.  The  most  flexibility  is 

provided by this  type of interview,  although data processing may become  more  challenging 

(Knopf,  2006).  The  condition  of  this  thesis  requires  qualitative  method  and  semi-structured 

interviews. This is the best way to gather high-quality results. This is due to the complexity of 

influencing  factors  of  ESG,  the  interpretive  nature  of  qualitative  research  which  suits 

understanding  of  motivations,  perceptions,  and  decision-making  processes.  Besides  that,  the 

appropriateness  for  the  research  aim  of  influencing  factors  of  ESG  investments  makes  this 

approach suitable. Also the depth of information and understanding and the flexibility that this 

approach brings with it is the perfect method for this research. As Sciarelly et al. (2021) and 

Harnett  (2018)  claim,  qualitative  research  helps  to  gather  information  about  complex  issues 

were there is an in-depth analysis needed. 

4.2 

 Inductive research 

Separation into deductive and inductive research approaches comes after the division into 

qualitative and quantitative research. The investigative method involves independent research 

that may lead to an understanding of novel theories. When there is little to no literature on the 

subject and one wishes to contribute oneself to knowledge gain, investigative research is used 

(Wild  &  Schiefele,  1993).  Existing  theories  are  attempted  to  be  verified  using  deductive 

research, which is an overview of previous research. Therefore, the deductive method is applied 

when there is already an extensive amount of literature on the subject and a researcher seeks to 

validate established hypotheses through their own research. Given that, it can be concluded that 

the inductive approach is future-focused because it presents a new theory on its own (Meinhold, 

2001). The steps of leading observation, data collection, formulation of one's own hypothesis, 

and generalization make up inductive argumentation. First, one establishes a certain context. 

The gathering of data on this incident and the analysis of the information gathered come next. 

From there, a hypothesis that is based on the recently acquired knowledge may be formed. This 

verified hypothesis allows for the statement to be generalized (Carl, 2017). 

The processes of developing a theory, coming up with a hypothesis, gathering evidence, 

and  deciding  whether  the  hypothesis  can  be  proven  are  all  included  in  deductive  reasoning. 

Initially, an observation is derived from the current hypothesis. On the basis of this finding, a 

hypothesis  is  proposed.  This  aims  to  verify  current  theories.  The  following  phase  of  data 

collecting involves doing this. Following the completion of the survey, the results either back

up  or  dispute  the  previously  stated  premise  (Meinhold,  2001).  Because  there  is  not  much 

previous study on the subject, this work focuses on the inductive research technique in an effort 

to generate new information.

4.3 

 Multiple case study research 

A case study is a comprehensive investigation of an individual, a group of individuals, 

businesses, sectors, or other research subjects. Research papers apply case studies to investigate 

research questions and research objectives. It is divided into several case studies alongside a 

single case study. A single case study includes a detailed examination of a single instance. This 

is often used in situations where a single example is adequate to address the research topic of 

the study and there is a specific case for which exact information is sought of (Yin, 2012). 

Hunziker and Blankenagel (2021) state that the multiple case study consists of several 

cases  that  are  examined.  The  topic  of  the  paper  determines  how  many  case  studies  are 

appropriate. A multiple case study is more appropriate when the subject matter and the related 

research issue are complicated and offer the possibility of numerous points of view. Several 

instances will be looked at to gather the various viewpoints of the interviewees on the complex 

subject. It is intended that this would generate more useful and accurate data to meet the goals 

of  the  study.  The  data  from  several  case  studies  may  be  compared  to  each  other  to  identify 

discrepancies  and  similarities.  With  the  single  case  study,  this  is  not  feasible  (Yin,  2012). 

Because of that, for this work the approach of multiple case study is reasonable since the topic 

of institutional theory and ESG investing is very diverse and data generation requires different 

opinions and perspectives.

General Background and context of institutional theory

(CIO) for Deutsche Bank  India.  The  experience  includes many investment  decisions, which

There  are  regulations  that  have  been  implemented,  especially  in  Europe,  about  how 

people should invest. For example, many ESG funds and banks have the regulation that they 

should not invest in investment options that have an ESG score of less than 4 or 3, depending 

on the industry. This also applies to Deutsche Bank, but more on the Europe operations. Mr. 

Khemka highlights that investors in the US also do not care that much about ESG as it may be 

illustrated. In discussions with private and institutional investors, they ask if ESG investments 

give them higher returns than non-ESG investments. If they do, great, if not, then they prefer 

non-ESG investment options. In India, the governance is important. In developing countries,

An example is Tata Steel, a subsidiary of the Tata corporation, the biggest corporation in

There are certain pollution control standards, but there is not a specific regulatory framework

6  Discussion 

This chapter discusses the gathered results with the theoretical foundation. Through this 

comparison,  similarities and differences  of the data gathered in  interviews and the literature 

review are being highlighted. 

6.1 

General background and context of institutional theory 

The interview partners have a lot of experience in the financial industry and with ESG 

investing. This includes positions of the managing director of the largest German bank in India, 

a partner of a private equity fund in Switzerland, the CEO of an Indian investment company, a 

customer relationship manager of the second largest bank in Liechtenstein, the CEO of an ESG 

consulting agency in Liechtenstein, and head of equities of one of the largest German private 

banks.  

Institutional theory applies to the ESG investment landscape in a way that regulations and 

laws  according  to  ESG  standards  get  implemented,  as  well  as  social  pressures  and  industry 

standards and norms. All these factors influence investment decisions. Organizations have to 

take these pressures into consideration to further develop their strategies and business success 

(Aksom & Tymchenko, 2020). This is also stated in the literature, but the empirical data shows 

that this development depends on the geographical area. In India, the application of institutional 

theory to the ESG landscape is only developing slowly. In Europe it is developed the furthest 

in global comparison.  Rating agencies are being implemented to analyse the ESG development 

in organizations. In many large organizations, ESG reporting became a standard practice. There 

are  certain  KPIs  that  have  to  be  fulfilled,  such  as  the  average  ESG-score  in  the  investment 

portfolio.  Due  to  all  these  institutional  factors  the  financial  industry  is  moving  towards 

sustainable business practices. This is also the outcome of the empirical data.  

The institutional frameworks shapes organizations in different ways (Jepperson & Meyer, 

2021). Large banks in Europe are adhered to not invest in companies that have an ESG score 

of  less  than  3  or  4,  depending  on  the  industry.  In  terms  of  governance,  the  track  record  of 

companies gets analysed. Investment organizations are now following ESG ratings closely and 

some of them are excluding companies from industries such as tobacco, betting, dirty energy, 

or  weapons.  New  sustainable  ESG  funds  get  imposed,  which  changes  the  direction  of 

organizations a lot. They get pushed towards active ESG implementation, otherwise there will 

arise difficulties in attracting capital. Code of conducts, compliance managers, and risk matrix 

are new tools that organizations implemented according to institutional pressures.

6.2 

Legal regulations and compliance 

Institutions impose regulations for ESG investments. The goal is to create a framework 

which drives organizations and investors towards higher investments in ESG projects. It should 

be  the  cornerstone  of  a  sustainable  development  of  the  financial  industry  and  the  overall 

economy. The literature states that this is happening on a global scope (Aw et al., 2022). But 

this is also depending on the region. Outside Europe and the US, the development of regulatory 

frameworks  is  almost  not  existing.  This  applies  especially  for  developing  countries  such  as 

India.  In  Europe  many  new  regulations  are  being  implemented.  There  are  referendums  to 

include society into ESG regulatory decisions. In Switzerland, all listed companies have to do 

a yearly risk analysis about ESG. The financial market supervision is controlling the adherence 

to these laws (Andonov et al., 2021).  

Europe  implemented  the  laws  of  Corporate  Sustainable  Reporting  Directive  (CSRD), 

MiFID 1 and 2, the EU-taxonomy, Sustainable Financial Disclosure Regulation (SFDR), and 

other imposed rules are influencing the ESG strategies of organizations in a way that they have 

to be compliant,  they need to  ask  customers about their sustainability  preferences, and ESG 

reporting is obligatory after a certain company size. This drives organizations towards stronger 

implementation of ESG standards and options in their funds and portfolios. For certain banks 

there are strict rules on the lending side. Complete industries get cut out of investments, such 

as oil and gas, coal, tobacco, and others. The EU imposed the EU Green Deal and new fund-

classifications into Article 8 and 9. Organizations now have to classify their funds which gives 

information  about  how  ESG-conform  their  portfolios  are.  Moreover,  certain  regions  in  the 

world get cut out of investments due to being not in alignment with ESG standards, such as the 

Middle East or South-East Asia.  

6.3 

Industry standards and norms 

Over time, industry standards and norms have been established in the financial industry 

when it comes to ESG. They guide organizations and their decisions in the same direction of 

sustainable practices (Avramov et al., 2022).   

The interviewees agree that industry standards especially impact governance. They act 

like unwritten rules or guidelines which brings organizations to increasing their product offering 

of sustainable investment options. Besides that, emission targets of organizations have been set. 

This  affects  the  business  operations  itself  and  the  investment  products.  According  to  the 

literature,  this  leads  to  more  sustainable  investments,  which  is  confirmed  by  the  empirical 

66 

 
 
 
 
ESG Investing 

results. Through these standards, ESG just becomes more important in the financial industry. 

In India, foreign institutional investors bring these standards with them when investing in the 

Indian  market.  Investors  strive  for  sustainable  investment  decisions.  Diversity  is  being 

increased in companies since this attracts ESG investments.  

Isomorphism  is  becoming  a  crucial  factor  within  the  industry  and  overall  investments 

(Posadas et al., 2023). The interviewees state that this adheres more to larger organizations. In 

Europe  they  are  becoming  more  similar  in  terms  of  ESG  practices.  Organizations  look  at 

business-best-practices  and  try  to  implement  them.  They  follow  the  same  guidelines  and 

therefore isomorphism is definitely applying and developing in the financial sector.  

6.4 

Social expectations and pressures 

In  the  existing  literature  it  is  mentioned  that  the  social  movement  towards  more 

sustainability  influences  the  financial  industry  (Dmuchowski  et  al.,  2023).  Movements  like 

Fridays for future, climate change, and social diversity aspects influence the financial sector 

(Zickus, 2024). This is confirmed by the interviewed persons. They explain that the root for all 

institutional factors is society. All pressures arise from a social perspective and therefore this is 

the most crucial institutional factor for most of the interviewees. It influences politics and rule 

makers. But also, here we diversify geographically. In India the social pressure regarding ESG 

is still very low. In Europe society is changing towards sustainability in a massive way, but still 

the investment decisions show a slightly different picture. Due to the pressure, organizations 

are increasing their ESG portfolio and creating ESG funds, but the most important criteria for 

the majority of investors is return.  

But  still  organizations  have  to  align  to  the  social  sustainability  pressure,  otherwise 

institutional investors would leave. For private investors, return is the crucial aspect, not ESG. 

From their own experience of the interviewees, examples of social movements are that people 

are protesting for more  sustainable business  practices,  institutional  investors shy away  from 

non-ESG  funds,  and  “Fridays  for  future”  from  Greta  Thunberg.  All  these  aspects  push 

organizations  towards  increasing  their  strivings  for  sustainable  business  practices.  This  is 

shown in the increasing ESG portfolios and general organizational behaviour in environmental, 

governance, and social topics. When the interview partners look at investment options, ESG 

criteria are now in full focus due to social pressures.

investor behaviour and the financial landscape, which is also proven by data and experience 

(Rubin,  2023).  They  play  a  crucial  role  in  shaping  the  economy  in  every  way  (Bell  &  Van 

Vuuren, 2022). All interviewees agree that the current conflicts in Ukraine and Palestine have 

a tremendous influence on the investment landscape. The resulting inflation, money shortage, 

and resource scarcity have a huge impact and are important to consider. These aspects influence 

institutional factors and therefore go hand in hand. The conflicts even took the spotlight away 

from climate change and the sustainability trend. People are now considering other factors, such 

as security, financial stability, and peace, instead of green investments.  

6.6 

Impact of institutional factors on ESG investment 

The  combination  of  social  factors,  industry  norms,  and  regulations  shape  the  ESG 

landscape.  The  literature  mentions  that  it  is  the  mixture  of  these  factors  which  makes  the 

influence  significant.  There  is  no  clear  factor  that  stands  out  (Rahman  &  Lau,  2023).  The 

interviewees agree partially. They claim that the interplay between those aspects is crucial and 

that they influence each other. Social pressures make regulatory institutions impose rules, which 

then leads to business standards and norms. But all interview partners agree that the starting 

point is social pressure from society. Since these social pressures decreased in the last year, 

because of the conflicts and inflation, the pressure on rule makers decreased as well. This is 

how institutional factors interplay which is part of the reasoning for the current reduction in 

ESG growth.  

6.7 

Challenges and barriers 

Changes of a scale that influence a whole industry take time and a lot of effort (Jepperson 

&  Meyer, 2021). It  requires a structured long-term  approach and high  commitment from  all 

stakeholders (Heeb et al., 2023). The empirical results show that besides structural barriers, the 

main  challenge  is  the  on  average  lower  return  rate  of  ESG  funds.  In  the  financial  world, 

everything is about money. And when investors see an option with higher returns, they usually 

take  it,  even  though  it  is  not  as  compliant  with  ESG  standards  as  others.  In  Europe  this  is 

declining,  but  in  developing  economies  like  India  this  is  the  standard  behaviour.  The 

interviewees  also  claim  that  the  measuring  of  ESG  criteria  and  adherence  is  difficult.  It  is 

lacking comparability and accuracy. Besides that, ESG development demands high CAPEX. In 

68 

 
 
 
 
 
the governance aspect, there is also a lack of female candidates for leadership positions. Overall, 

this change towards sustainability and ESG needs time and society should bring the demand. 

Currently, the focus is not on ESG in the world, therefore it needs time to evolve again.

6.8 

Strategies and adaptations 

Throughout the financial industry, strategies are adapting and ESG factors get into focus 

(Masters & Temple-West, 2023). To implement such a strategy is crucial to succeed in such a 

drastic change. The environment needs to be given and a structural approach is demanded to 

sustain long-term success (Marsh, 2023). The empirical results show that institutional factors 

already caused many organizations to adapt their strategies and to include ESG criteria much 

more.  This  includes  the  stopping  of  funding  dirty  power  or  industries,  strategic  changes  in 

lending money, and new implementation of only ESG funds. Now there is a clear separation 

between green and non-green investment options. But the interviewees also claim that changing 

the  business  strategy  will  take  more  time  to  be  fully  adherend  with  current  and  future  ESG 

criteria, which will become stricter.  

6.9 

Perceptions and experiences 

Market trends are changing over time and there are phases of incline and decline. This is 

natural behaviour (Furusten, 2023). One interviewee explains this with the example of the S-

curve.  This  is  currently  applying  to  the  ESG  investment  trend  as  well.  The  other  interview 

partners agree that one of the main reasons for the current decline in the ESG growth rate is the 

usually lower return rate on ESG investments in comparison to non-ESG investments. This was 

not  applicable  in  that  scale  some  years  ago,  but  not  it  shows  more  significantly.  The  social 

pressures  are  currently  not  in  focus  of  society.  The  two  main  conflicts  are  now  the  most 

important topics, and this causes the society to decrease the pressure for ESG. This results in 

lower pressure on regulatory institutions, which then impose less rules for ESG investing.  

The fast development of ESG frameworks two or 3 years ago slowed down. But this is 

also not something unusual when you try to restructure a whole industry (Aw et al., 2022). The 

recent  money shortage in  the global financial market  is  another factor of why there are less 

investments in ESG funds. Interest rates went up, which brings people to put their money in the 

bank, rather than investing it. Inflation is very high globally, but especially in Europe. Investors 

need quick returns, and they should be as high as possible, which brings them towards non-

ESG funds. The demand for ESG funds is simply not there and the market is in a volatile state.

The overall growth of the economy is slowing down, which leads to less new investments.

6.10 

Future directions 

The existing literature states that ESG is not only a trend, but it will shape the financial 

industry tremendously and will become a core element (Peterdy, 2023). All interviewees agree 

on that fact.  Climate change will not  disappear  and therefore it needs  a restructuring of our 

economy, which includes the financial industry (Bell & Van Vuuren, 2022). When the current 

conflicts will find an end, ESG will again become more popular for investors, according to the 

empirical results. The capital amount in the financial market will increase and social pressures 

will start to rise again. To what extent and how fast ESG will rise in the next years is unsure to 

say, but the results clearly show that this is just a current downward development, and it will 

strive again. First in Europe and the US, and then in the rest of the world.  

The society is  already developed  enough in  ESG  aspects,  so the  foundation for future 

development is given (Bauer et al., 2021). All interviewees believe that the issue with the lower 

returns will disappear, because the economy will change in such a way that non-ESG funds will 

decrease over the years due to climate and other restrictions. 

6.11 

Recommendations and insights 

Companies  need  to  develop  an  environment  which  aligns  with  ESG  criteria.  To 

successfully implement  ESG in business strategy, it is important to  include all stakeholders. 

The focus needs to switch to longevity and ESG needs to be developed sustainably (Bansal et 

al., 2022). The results show that this will need time and will lead to high costs, but it is crucial 

to implement ESG into business operations. Therefore, the empirical data shows that companies 

should make that step now, because it will be an advantage for them in the future. It will bring 

them into a promising market position, when ESG investments will increase strongly again. All 

interview partners agree that the area which requires the further research the most is governance. 

It is a crucial part of sustainable business development and requires more focus. 

6.12 

Final thoughts 

There  are  difficulties  with  the  implementation  of  ESG.  This  is  not  only  shown  by  the 

current growth decline, but in problems that companies faced in recent years. Measuring the 

alignment with ESG standards is difficult. Certain standards have been implemented, but the

All interviewees mention that ESG is here to stay, and with further institutional development,

7  Conclusion 

At the beginning of this study, a detailed introduction provided an overview of the topic. 

The focus of the introduction is on the research background, research relevance, research goal 

and objectives, description of distinctive features, and structure of the dissertation. After that 

followed a systematic literature review to give an understanding of what research in this area 

already has been done. After the literature review, the theoretical foundation about institutional 

theory and ESG established the necessary knowledge of the topic. The part about method and 

data explains the used empirical approach of this work. The empirical part showed the results 

which were conducted with six qualitative expert interviews from six different organizations 

and four different countries. The findings were discussed in Chapter 6 in comparison with the 

existing literature. This  chapter concludes the findings of this  thesis, including implications, 

limitations, and possible future research. 

The  goal  of  this  thesis  is  to  answer  the  following  research  question:  “What  are  the 

predominant  institutional  factors contributing to  the recent slowdown in the growth of ESG 

investments?” 

Within the last years, society has changed. The current geopolitical conflicts, particularly 

those  in  Ukraine  and  Palestine,  have  shifted  priorities.  The  resulting  unstable  economic 

situation and high inflation contribute to the change in demand when it comes to investments. 

Financial instability and money shortage lead investors to rethink their investment decisions 

and put their focus on short-term high returns, rather than long-term sustainable impact. Due to 

this change of social demand, social pressures on implementing ESG regulations  decreased. 

The ESG framework is developing, but at a slower pace. Regulatory bodies are taking action 

when  they  feel  the  pressure  from  stakeholders.  When  this  pressure  is  weakening,  the 

implementation of new ESG rules and control mechanisms of ESG criteria is declining. This 

regulatory  slowdown  reflects  the  broader  trend  of  reduced  focus  on  sustainability  and  ESG 

under the current economic and geopolitical situation. The main drivers of implementing ESG 

frameworks, are cultural-cognitive pressures. The current social shift weakened the momentum 

of ESG investing, even though the awareness of its importance remains high. This also affects 

the  development  of  industry  standards  and  norms  regarding  ESG.  Due  to  the  intertwined 

relationship  of  coercive,  normative,  and  cultural-cognitive  aspects,  industry  standards  are 

developing at a much slower pace than two or 3 years ago. Therefore, the reason for the growth 

decline is not a combination of certain specific institutional factors, but their general decrease 

of  development  and  implementation.  While  ESG  remains  a  critical  area  of  interest  in  the 

72 

 
 
 
investment landscape, the overall reduction of institutional factors has significantly contributes 

to the recent slowdown in the ESG growth rate.

7.1 

Implications 

The  contribution  of  this  dissertation  is  to  provide  a  reference  framework  for  both 

academia  and  practice.  It  illustrates  the  extent  to  which  ESG  frameworks  progressed  in  the 

financial industry, particularly institutions, organizations, and investors. This work contributes 

knowledge to existing literature about the current state of development of institutional factors 

and ESG in the financial industry. It highlights the reasons for the development in recent years 

and  now.  Industry  experts  with  much  experience  explain  the  complex  intertwining  of 

institutional factors and ESG frameworks, both from an organizational and investor perspective. 

Furthermore, clarity is provided about the implemented ESG regulations and how social and 

industry pressures lead to it. This work highlights the reasoning behind the current slowdown 

of ESG growth and takes multiple perspectives into consideration. All six interviewed persons 

emphasize that ESG should not only be seen as a challenge but also  as an opportunity. The 

opportunity lies in developing the organization in a sustainable way which provides long-term 

success and stability. This work and its empirical data serve as a guidance for organizations 

about  strategic  alignment  with  institutional  trends  in  the  financial  industry.  It  gives  a  deep 

understanding  of  the  current  market  situation  and  how  to  structure  business  operations 

accordingly. 

7.2 

Limitations and outlook for future research 

Firstly,  it  should  be  noted  that  this  thesis  refers  to  organizations  from  the  DACH 

(Germany,  Austria,  Switzerland,  Liechtenstein)  region  and  India.  Therefore,  a  general 

conclusion cannot be drawn for all global financial organizations, even though it is applicable 

to many regions in the world. Besides that, this work focuses on financial organizations, such 

as public and private banks, financial advisors, and investor companies. Financial institutions 

could have a different perspective of the topic. A qualitative approach in the form of interviews 

was used to achieve in-depth results. It is assumed that all respondents answer conscientiously. 

Moreover,  the  qualitative  approach  could  limit  to  capture  the  full  spectrum  of  this  global 

subject, due to the small number of interview partners. Non-institutional factors are not covered 

in this work, and they could lead to different results. 

The mentioned limitations enable further research opportunities in this area. By selecting 

73 

 
 
 
 
 
ESG Investing 

different countries or regions or expanding the geographic scope further, it is possible to gather 

different  results.  Moreover,  taking  financial  institutions  into  consideration  can  lead  to  a 

different perspective on the topic and the reasoning behind the ESG growth decrease. Choosing 

a quantitative approach, a larger number of respondents could be surveyed, potentially leading 

to a broader acknowledgeable result. Furthermore, analysing non-institutional factors such as 

return  rate  or  profit  margin  could  be  examined  to  deepen  the  understanding  of  financial 

indicators.
