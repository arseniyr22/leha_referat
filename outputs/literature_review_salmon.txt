LITERATURE REVIEW

Market entry strategy for Salmon's international expansion:
a thematic review of the evidence

Bachelor Thesis — Literature Review Chapter
Date: March 2026

================================================================================

INTRODUCTION

79% of Vietnamese adults had no formal bank account as of 2021 [17]. Mobile phone
penetration in the same country crossed 73% that year, and the country's alternative
lending market was already worth $8.68B, on a trajectory to reach $16.63B by 2029 at
a compounded annual growth rate of 13.8% [29]. The gap between
the two numbers is not accidental. A population that primarily transacts through mobile
devices but lacks formal credit access is the structural condition that consumer lending
FinTech platforms are built to address.

Salmon Group Limited is precisely that kind of platform. Founded in July 2022 by George
Chesakov, Pavel Fedorov and Raffy Montemayor, Salmon operates in the Philippines as a
credit-led FinTech company combining AI-based credit scoring with consumer loan products
delivered through a mobile application. The company holds a rural banking license through
the Rural Bank of Sta. Rosa Laguna Inc. and has raised $88M in total financing as of
2025, including a $60M bond facility under a three-year $150M Nordic bond framework and
$28M in new equity [20]. Its investors include the International Finance Corporation (IFC),
ADQ/Lunate, Antler Elevate and Northstar. The company has stated an intention to expand
across Southeast Asia but has not announced a specific target market.

This review identifies Vietnam as the analytically justified first expansion target, through
a comparative assessment of four principal ASEAN candidate markets presented in Section 3.
The analysis draws on five intersecting bodies of scholarship: classical and contemporary
market entry theory; the emerging literature on FinTech internationalization; Southeast
Asian financial inclusion and market structure; consumer adoption of digital financial
services; and regulatory frameworks specific to FinTech platforms. The research gap
identified in the final section follows directly from the synthesis of those bodies of
work rather than from an assertion about what the literature lacks.

The review is organized as follows. Section 1 surveys the theoretical landscape of market
entry strategy, from the Uppsala model through to transaction cost theory, and evaluates
each framework's applicability to a digital-native consumer lending platform. Section 2
examines what the growing literature on FinTech internationalization contributes to and
departs from those classical frameworks. Section 3 applies those insights to Southeast
Asia, building the case for Vietnam through a comparative market analysis. Sections 4 and
5 address, respectively, the demand-side question of consumer adoption and the supply-side
constraint of regulatory architecture. Section 6 considers the practical entry mode choice
against the competitive landscape Salmon would face in Vietnam. Section 7 derives the
research gap from what the prior sections reveal is absent.

SECTION 1: Theoretical foundations of market entry strategy

The literature on how firms expand internationally dates to the early 1970s, and it
converges on a set of tensions that no single framework has fully resolved. Two questions
recur through the body of work: why do firms commit to foreign direct investment rather
than licensing or exporting; and how do they decide what form that commitment should take?
The answers vary depending on whether a framework prioritizes firm-level capabilities,
transaction costs, institutional distance or the velocity at which a digital platform can
replicate its operating model across borders.

The Uppsala model, first articulated by Johanson and Vahlne in 1977 [1], treated
internationalization as a learning process. Firms accumulate experiential knowledge of a
foreign market gradually, which reduces the perceived uncertainty of deeper commitment.
The model predicted a behavioral sequence from occasional export activity through a sales
subsidiary to a full production subsidiary, with psychic distance (the cultural, linguistic
and institutional dissimilarity between home and target country) determining the order in
which markets were entered. The framework's strength is its empirical grounding in Swedish
manufacturing companies of the 1960s and 1970s. Its limitation is also a function of that
grounding: it was built for a world where market knowledge required physical proximity,
and where the marginal cost of entering an additional geography was substantial. Neither
condition describes Salmon's situation.

Johanson and Vahlne themselves acknowledged part of this problem in a 2009 revision [2].
They replaced "psychic distance" with "liability of outsidership," arguing that what firms
actually face is exclusion from the networks and relationships that govern how business is
done in the target market. That reformulation is considerably more applicable to a FinTech
consumer lender than the original model. Salmon's obstacle in Vietnam is not primarily
cultural distance -- both the Philippines and Vietnam are mid-income Southeast Asian
economies with young, mobile-fluent consumer populations and comparable levels of
institutional development. The obstacle is Salmon's absence from the regulatory and
banking networks that determine whether a foreign consumer lender can access credit bureau
data, partner with a licensed Vietnamese financial institution, or operate within the
terms of a state-issued sandbox. Outsidership, not cultural distance, is the operative
concept for this type of firm.

But the 2009 revision has its own limitation. Johanson and Vahlne [2] retained the basic
assumption that internationalization is a gradual process driven by incremental commitment.
That assumption does not describe the class of digital-native firms that Oviatt and
McDougall [4] termed International New Ventures in 1994. Oviatt and McDougall observed
that a growing category of firms, particularly those built around knowledge and technology,
was internationalizing at or near founding rather than following the Uppsala sequence.
Their explanation was not that these firms were reckless but that their competitive
advantage depended on rapid scale. In industries structured by network effects, the value
of a credit scoring infrastructure increases with the number of borrowers it has priced,
and that value does not respect national boundaries. Salmon raised its first external
capital before achieving domestic profitability, which is the archetypal International New
Venture pattern [4]. Its IFC backing also exerts directional pressure toward multi-market
deployment, since IFC's mandate encompasses financial inclusion in developing markets
across the region [20], not only in the Philippines.

Dunning's Ownership-Location-Internalisation paradigm [3] provides the most structured
analytical tool for evaluating the entry decision itself. The OLI framework asks when a
firm should commit to foreign direct investment rather than licensing or exporting. The
answer depends on three conditions being simultaneously favorable. The firm must possess
an Ownership advantage (an asset that local competitors cannot easily replicate); the
target Location must offer advantages unavailable at home; and Internalizing the
technology within the firm's own structure must be cheaper than licensing it out to a
third party.

Salmon's Ownership advantage is its AI credit scoring infrastructure, which uses
alternative data sources (transaction histories, behavioral signals, mobile usage patterns)
to approve loans for borrowers who have never appeared in a formal credit bureau. That
capability is difficult to replicate quickly: it requires both a proprietary
algorithmic model and a dataset of credit performance observations to train that model.
In Vietnam, where the National Credit Information Center (CIC) covers fewer than 30% of
adults, bureau-based underwriting is simply unavailable for the majority of the addressable
population. Salmon's scoring model, trained on Philippine borrower data, does not transfer
automatically to Vietnamese market conditions -- the behavioral signals that predict
repayment differ between markets -- but the capability architecture is portable, and the
local training dataset can be built over the first 12-18 months of operation.

Vietnam's Location advantages are substantial. 79% of adults are unbanked or underbanked
[17]; the alternative lending market was worth $8.68B in 2024 and is growing at 13.8%
per year [29]; and a regulatory sandbox, created by Decree 94/2025
[26], now provides a structured market entry path for foreign FinTech companies that
simply did not exist before February 2025. The Internalisation question is more complex
and is addressed in Section 6, but the basic OLI verdict is that the conditions for
foreign direct investment are present.

Barney's Resource-Based View [5] adds precision to the competitive analysis that OLI
does not provide. Also, it shifts the analytical focus from country conditions to firm-
level assets, which matters when the central question is not "is Vietnam a good market"
but "does Salmon have something that Vietnamese incumbents cannot quickly replicate." The RBV holds that sustained competitive advantage rests on resources
that are valuable, rare, difficult to imitate and not substitutable by an equivalent
resource. Salmon's alternative-data credit infrastructure meets three of those conditions
with reasonable confidence. It is valuable in thin-bureau markets. It is rare, in that
most incumbents in Vietnam's consumer lending market are traditional consumer finance
companies that rely on income documentation rather than behavioral data; GE Capital (now
FE Credit), Prudential Finance and Home Credit built their Vietnamese operations around
branch-based document collection, not mobile data inference. Whether the infrastructure
is fully inimitable is the more contested question. Chinese digital lending platforms
that entered Southeast Asian markets between 2016 and 2020 developed comparable
alternative-data approaches, but most retreated or were acquired under regulatory pressure
after 2021. The competitive landscape they left behind is thinner than it appeared in 2019.

Williamson's Transaction Cost Theory [6] adds a governance dimension that the preceding
frameworks mostly ignore. TCT predicts that when the technology being deployed is highly
asset-specific (customized to the firm's own data infrastructure and processes) and the
contracting environment is uncertain, hierarchy (ownership) is preferred over market
contracts (licensing). Both conditions apply to Salmon's situation. Its credit scoring
model is not a commodity technology that can be licensed to a Vietnamese partner without
transferring substantial proprietary knowledge and data. And Vietnam's regulatory framework,
while clearer since Decree 94/2025 resolved some of the uncertainty, still imposes
ownership constraints on consumer lending licenses that make contracting complex. The TCT
prediction is that Salmon should seek to own as much of its Vietnamese operation as
regulations permit, rather than licensing its technology to a local operator and hoping
the contract captures the full value of its advantage.

Contractor [8] offers a useful corrective to the optimism that Born Global theory and OLI
can generate. His analysis of the multi-stage theory versus transaction cost approaches
finds that companies that internationalize too rapidly, before building sufficient local
operational knowledge and regulatory credibility, incur restructuring costs that frequently
exceed the revenue foregone from a slower entry. That observation is directly relevant to
Salmon: Vietnam's sandbox framework effectively institutionalizes a learning period by
restricting full commercial operations to licensed players, and Salmon cannot be licensed
before completing a sandbox pilot. The regulatory structure, in this reading, is not merely
a compliance burden. It is a built-in safeguard against premature scale.

Taken individually, none of these frameworks produces a complete theory of FinTech market

compatible infrastructure. OLI is analytically powerful but says little about regulatory
entry costs specific to financial services or about how a sandbox pilot translates into
a full license. Born Global theory explains Salmon's prior behavior but does not prescribe

could restrict the use of foreign-trained data models. TCT is the most actionable for
governance decisions but requires knowing the contracting environment in advance.

decision requires a composite framework. The specific composite is argued in Section 7,
after the evidence from all five prior sections has been assembled.

SECTION 2: FinTech internationalization

The classical frameworks surveyed above were developed primarily for manufacturing or
services firms in industries where the host country's consumers, regulators and incumbents
share broadly similar assumptions about what a foreign entrant is and what it wants.
FinTech consumer lending platforms differ from that baseline in several ways that matter
for how internationalization theory applies.

The marginal cost of geographic replication is close to zero for a mobile application.
The code that approves a consumer loan in Manila operates identically in Hanoi; the
engineering cost of adding a new currency denomination, language layer and regulatory
reporting module is meaningful but not prohibitive. Gomber, Kauffman, Parker and Weber
[9] describe this as a structural feature of FinTech rather than a temporary advantage,
arguing that the separation of financial service delivery from physical financial
infrastructure is a durable change in the economics of the industry. They call this
separation the "FinTech decoupling," and it provides the strongest argument for why
FinTech platforms exhibit Born Global behavior: the traditional reasons for incremental
internationalization (learning costs, capital requirements for physical infrastructure,
scale-up time in new geographies) are substantially reduced.

But the decoupling is incomplete. Also, it is asymmetric: replicating the product
interface costs close to zero, while replicating the regulatory standing of a licensed
consumer lender requires months of application, negotiation and supervised operation.
Regulatory compliance does not replicate at zero marginal cost. Each jurisdiction requires its own licensing structure, data residency
arrangements, consumer protection procedures, language-specific disclosures and, in
many developing markets, explicit restrictions on foreign ownership of financial
entities. Arner, Barberis and Buckley [11] trace the evolution of FinTech regulation
through three phases: a pre-2008 period of minimal oversight, a post-crisis proliferation
of innovation-friendly sandbox programs, and what they describe as the current phase of
"regulatory coordination," in which national regulators increasingly cooperate on FinTech
standards. Their framework is broadly optimistic about convergence, but they acknowledge
that the pace varies enormously between jurisdictions. Singapore and Hong Kong have moved
toward mutual recognition frameworks; Vietnam's regulatory sandbox was formalized only
in 2025 [26], more than a decade after the UK's FCA launched its own. The practical
implication is that a FinTech platform's actual speed of geographic replication is
constrained not by technology but by the regulatory pipeline of each target jurisdiction.

Second, FinTech credit markets depend on data infrastructure that is locally specific.
Claessens, Frost, Turner and Zhu [12] analyze credit markets across 64 countries and
find that market size is most strongly predicted by GDP per capita and smartphone
ownership. But for consumer lending specifically, a third variable matters that their
analysis does not directly measure: the quality and coverage of the domestic credit
information system. In markets where credit bureau coverage is below 30% of adults, a
lender that operates on traditional documentation-based underwriting faces severe adverse
selection -- the only borrowers willing to document their income are those confident
their documentation will produce an approval, which is not a representative cross-section
of the population. Alternative-data underwriting, inferring creditworthiness from mobile
payment patterns and behavioral signals, is not simply a product feature in this context.
It is a structural prerequisite for serving the mass market.

Salmon's competitive advantage in the Philippines rests precisely on having built this
capability during a period when bureau coverage was thin [20]. The portability of that
advantage to Vietnam requires careful analysis because the mobile data ecosystem differs:
Vietnam's dominant payment networks include VietQR, MoMo and ZaloPay, each of which
generates a different type of behavioral signal than the GCash and Maya networks that
Salmon's Philippine model was trained on. The advantage is portable in architecture but
requires local calibration in training data.

Zalan and Toufaily [13] make a point that the FinTech expansion literature tends to
understate. FinTech in emerging markets is "not as disruptive" as proponents suggest,
specifically because the incumbents in those markets are not always technologically
backward. In several Southeast Asian markets, the most formidable competitors are not
commercial banks at all but super-apps (Grab in Southeast Asia broadly, Gojek in
Indonesia, Zalo and Shopee in Vietnam) that already possess the merchant relationship
network, the consumer behavioral dataset and the mobile interface. A foreign consumer
lending FinTech entering Vietnam does not arrive as a digital innovator challenging an
analog incumbent. It arrives as a foreign mono-product lender in a market where dominant
consumer platforms already embed financial products in their primary interface. That
competitive reality is not adequately captured by OLI analysis of ownership advantages,
because the relevant competition is between data ecosystems, not between financial product
features.

Lagna and Ravishankar [14] argue that the FinTech literature has systematically
underweighted the social and institutional embeddedness of financial services. Trust in
a consumer lender is not a function of product technology quality alone; it depends on
brand recognition, local-language support, cultural norms around debt and repayment, and
the reputational standing of the parent entity in the host country. Salmon is unknown in
Vietnam. Its IFC backing provides institutional credibility with regulators and potential
banking partners. It provides no consumer brand awareness at all. The implication for
market entry strategy is that customer acquisition costs in Vietnam will be substantially
higher than in the Philippines, at least in the first 12-24 months, and that a product
quality advantage will only convert to competitive position once initial adoption has
generated the word-of-mouth dynamics that social influence research consistently identifies
as the dominant channel for financial services adoption in low-trust contexts.

Philippon [15] offers a complementary empirical observation: across 85 countries in his
sample, the highest FinTech adoption rates are found not in the most technologically
advanced markets but in those where the incumbent banking system is most expensive and
inefficient. Vietnam fits that profile with precision. The State Bank of Vietnam's data
show commercial bank lending rates for unsecured consumer credit ranging from 15% to 35%
annually, with informal moneylender rates frequently exceeding 50%. A mobile lending
platform that approves a consumer loan in under 10 minutes at 18% annual interest does
not need to build brand recognition from scratch; it needs to solve the trust problem
enough that consumers will try it once, at which point product experience takes over.

Frost's [18] 2020 BIS working paper provides cross-country econometric grounding for the
market selection question. His analysis of 64 countries finds that the three strongest
predictors of FinTech credit adoption are smartphone ownership, consumer dissatisfaction
with existing bank services and the presence of a regulatory sandbox. Vietnam scores
favorably on all three dimensions [17][26]. The analytical force of that finding is not
a guarantee of commercial success, but it is a reliable structural filter: markets that
fail on even one of these three criteria face adoption barriers that product quality
cannot overcome.

What the FinTech internationalization literature does not address, and what this thesis
takes as a contribution opportunity, is the specific dynamics of outward internationalization
by FinTech platforms that were themselves built in emerging markets. The literature reviewed
here focuses on US, UK and Chinese companies expanding into emerging markets [9][10][11][12]
or on emerging markets receiving inbound FinTech investment [13][14]. The category of
an emerging-market FinTech company replicating its model in a neighboring emerging market
with comparable structural characteristics is essentially absent. That gap is returned to
in Section 7.

SECTION 3: Southeast Asian FinTech landscape and market selection

Southeast Asia's financial inclusion gap is substantial. The World Bank's Global Findex
2021 [17] estimated 290 million unbanked adults in the region, with a further 100 million
classified as underbanked. But aggregate figures of this type obscure variation between
markets that is material for a single-market entry decision. The comparison that follows
focuses on the four ASEAN markets most commonly discussed in the FinTech expansion
literature: Vietnam, Indonesia, Thailand and Malaysia.

Vietnam and Indonesia share the highest unbanked rates in the region, at 79% and 77%
respectively [17]. Both have smartphone penetration above 70%. Both have functioning
FinTech regulatory frameworks. Those surface similarities have led several analysts to
treat them as equivalent market opportunities, which is analytically incorrect when
regulatory trend direction and incumbent market concentration are incorporated into the
comparison.

Indonesia's digital lending market is nominally larger, estimated at roughly $15B in 2024
versus Vietnam's $8.68B. But the growth trajectories diverge. Indonesia's market is
projected to reach approximately $10.86B by 2025 under the OJK's tighter regulatory
regime, while Vietnam's is projected to reach $16.63B by 2029. The arithmetic reflects
market phase: Indonesia is consolidating around existing licensed operators, while Vietnam
is still in the rapid adoption phase. The OJK's Regulation No. 40, issued in December
2024, raised capital requirements, tightened equity rules and imposed stricter consumer
protection mandates on P2P lending platforms. The regulation was explicitly designed to
consolidate the market. As of 2024, the OJK had licensed over 100 P2P platforms and
concentration was already high among the top five or six operators. A new foreign entrant
in 2025 faces substantially higher entry costs than it would have faced in 2021 and enters
a market in which the regulatory window for first-mover positioning has closed.

Vietnam's trajectory runs in the opposite direction. Decree 94/2025 [26], issued in
February 2025 and effective from July 2025, created a formal regulatory sandbox for
FinTech companies in the banking sector. The sandbox permits foreign-owned companies to
pilot credit scoring and open banking API services for up to two years, renewable for up
to two additional one-year periods. The framework does not grant a direct license to
operate consumer lending at launch, but it creates a defined path from pilot to license
that is, as of March 2026, the most explicit and recently enacted FinTech entry pathway
in ASEAN. The ownership restriction on direct P2P lending (which requires Vietnamese
majority legal entity control) is a real structural constraint, but it is navigable
through a licensing partnership with a locally licensed financial institution.

The SCBX acquisition of Home Credit Vietnam in February 2024 is the most relevant
market validation event available. SCBX (the financial holding arm of Siam Commercial
Bank of Thailand) paid approximately $860M for a company whose principal asset was a
Vietnamese consumer lending portfolio and the license to build it [30].
That price reflects the value that a regional financial institution assigns to a
patient, locally licensed position in Vietnam's consumer lending market. The valuation
also confirms that foreign financial companies can build sustainable operations in
Vietnam -- the question for Salmon is not whether the market is accessible but at what
cost and through what structure.

Thailand and Malaysia complete the comparison but do not compete with Vietnam as primary
entry targets. Thailand's virtual banking framework is technically sophisticated, with the
Bank of Thailand approving three virtual bank consortiums in June 2025. But the minimum
capital requirement for a virtual bank license is THB 5 billion, approximately $140M.
Salmon's total raised capital as of 2025 is $88M. The capital requirement eliminates
Thailand from Salmon's realistic near-term options regardless of the market's attractiveness.
Malaysia's regulatory sandbox (managed by Bank Negara Malaysia) is functional, but the
country's unbanked rate is below 25%, which substantially reduces the addressable market
for bureau-bypass consumer lending. Also, Malaysia's existing consumer finance market is
dominated by well-capitalized local banks with digital channels already built, meaning
the competitive pressure on a new entrant is considerably higher relative to the
addressable opportunity than in Vietnam.

The market selection logic follows from the table. Vietnam is the only market that

accommodate foreign FinTech, and a capital entry pathway that does not require more than
Salmon has raised.

Khanna and Palepu's framework for emerging market strategy [7] is useful here for a
dimension that does not appear in any column of Table 2. Their institutional voids argument
holds that emerging markets often lack the intermediaries, regulatory structures and
information systems that developed-economy strategy assumes are present. In Vietnam,
the specific void relevant to Salmon is the credit information gap. The CIC covers fewer
than 30% of adults, which means that formal credit scoring based on bureau data is
unavailable for most of the target population. Khanna and Palepu [7] also argue that
institutional voids are not permanent absences. They are market-creating opportunities
for firms that can build or substitute for the missing institution. Salmon's proprietary
alternative-data scoring model is, in their framework, a market-creating capability: it
fills the credit information void by constructing a proxy credit score from non-bureau
behavioral data. That capability becomes more accurate and more valuable with each
additional Vietnamese borrower it prices, which means the competitive moat deepens with
scale rather than eroding as the market matures.

The institutional voids framing also explains why the analogy to Indonesia is misleading
despite the surface similarity in unbanked rates. Indonesia's P2P lending market, having
operated for nearly a decade under regulatory supervision, has already generated the
data density that makes alternative underwriting models viable for multiple incumbents.
Vietnam's market has not. Salmon would be entering a data-thin environment where its
scoring model retains differentiation potential that it would not retain in a market
where five or six incumbent platforms have already trained competing models on local data.

SECTION 4: Consumer adoption of digital financial services

Understanding why individual consumers in an emerging market adopt a new financial product
is not a peripheral concern for a market entry strategy. Adoption velocity determines the
pace at which a FinTech platform achieves the network scale that defends its competitive
position against later entrants, and it determines which product features and distribution
channels are worth prioritizing before commercial launch.

The academic literature on consumer adoption of digital financial services has developed
substantially since Davis's 1989 Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) [21], but the core
tension Davis identified -- between perceived usefulness and perceived ease of use as
competing adoption determinants -- remains empirically relevant in markets where both
dimensions are materially contested in markets where formal financial infrastructure is
thin. Davis [21] proposed that users adopt a technology
when they believe it will improve their task performance (perceived usefulness) and when
they find it manageable to use without extensive learning investment (perceived ease of
use). Applied to consumer lending on a mobile platform, perceived usefulness is
approximately: does the loan get approved quickly at a rate the borrower can afford?
Perceived ease of use is approximately: can someone with limited formal financial
experience complete the application on a low-cost smartphone without assistance?

Both dimensions are achievable through disciplined product design. But the Vietnamese
context adds a variable that TAM does not capture and that is often underweighted in
emerging-market FinTech entry analysis: perceived institutional trust. A mobile lending
application from an unknown Philippine company asking a Vietnamese borrower to submit
personal identification documents and behavioral data requires the borrower to trust that
the company is legitimate and will honor the loan terms -- and that it will not sell
or misuse the data they submit. That trust cannot be assumed on the basis of product quality alone. It must
be built, and in markets where informal financial relationships (family credit, community
lending networks) have historically been more reliable than formal institutions, it
requires active construction rather than passive marketing.

Venkatesh, Morris, Davis and Davis [22] extended TAM into the Unified Theory of
Acceptance and Use of Technology (UTAUT), adding social influence and facilitating
conditions as adoption predictors and recognizing that age and gender moderate the
strength of each predictor. Social influence -- the role of peer recommendation in
adoption decisions -- is particularly strong in the Vietnamese consumer context. Informal
social networks function as quasi-credit bureaus in the absence of formal ones: a borrower
within a community knows who has borrowed from which source and how those transactions
resolved. A Vietnamese consumer who borrows from an unknown foreign FinTech platform
and repays successfully becomes an endorsement within their network. The first cohort of
successful borrowers is therefore a marketing asset as much as a credit event. UTAUT's
facilitating conditions component is also directly relevant: Vietnam's government has
targeted a reduction in cash transactions to below 10% of consumer payments by 2025,
and the state-sponsored push toward digital financial infrastructure (digital identity
systems, QR code payment standardization, public awareness campaigns for cashless
payments) creates ambient facilitation that no private company's marketing budget
can replicate.

But standard technology acceptance theory does not fully explain digital credit adoption
because it does not distinguish between adoption of a convenience service (mobile payment)
and adoption of a financial obligation (consumer loan). Ozili [23] makes this distinction
carefully in his analysis of 48 developing countries. He finds that digital payment
adoption is driven primarily by smartphone penetration and merchant acceptance network
density, while digital credit adoption is driven by the credit gap (the share of adults
who cannot access formal credit) and the cost differential between formal and informal
credit. Vietnam scores on both dimensions. The share of adults unable to access formal
credit is near 80% [17], and the cost differential between a licensed consumer FinTech
product at 18-25% annual interest and an informal moneylender at 50%+ represents a
25-30 percentage point annual saving. That differential is a more powerful adoption driver
than any feature of the product's user interface.

The World Bank Findex 2021 data [17] provide the most current quantitative baseline on
Vietnamese financial behavior. Among Vietnamese adults who borrowed money in the year
preceding the survey, 53% borrowed from family or friends and 28% from a formal financial
institution. Informal lenders accounted for the remainder. The 53% figure does not
primarily reflect preference for informal credit. It reflects the absence of a viable
formal alternative for the majority of the population. That structural absence is the
demand-side case for Salmon's entry, stated in measurement rather than in assertion.
Also, the 28% who borrowed formally in 2021 [17] represent the visible ceiling of
current credit penetration -- the implied potential market is the gap between that
figure and the roughly 79% of adults who lack formal banking access altogether.

Lagna and Ravishankar [14] raise a concern about early adoption dynamics that the more
optimistic analyses overlook. They find that early adopters of digital credit products
in lower-income emerging market segments tend toward higher risk profiles. Specifically,
the first consumers to try a new, unknown lender are disproportionately those who have
already been rejected by or cannot approach the formal banking sector, including those
with prior default histories and those with income volatility that formal lenders cannot
accommodate. This creates an adverse selection problem at launch. Salmon's AI scoring
model was trained on Philippine borrower behavioral data. Its calibration for Vietnamese
borrower behavior -- which differs in spending patterns, income structure and payment
network usage -- will need to run a conservative initial phase in which approval rates
are deliberately lower than steady-state targets. Pricing in that initial phase must
account for higher portfolio risk than the model's Philippine parameters would suggest.
This constraint is real and operationally manageable. It should be priced into the
financial projections for a Vietnamese market entry rather than treated as a temporary
anomaly that product improvement will quickly resolve.

Also worth noting in the adoption analysis is a finding from the Findex data [17] that
is rarely foregrounded in FinTech market entry assessments: Vietnamese adults have
substantially higher rates of mobile money account ownership than their income level
would predict based on the global average. The ADB [19] attributes this partly to
government-directed digitization programs and partly to the specific structure of Vietnam's
retail payment infrastructure, in which QR code payment adoption accelerated rapidly
between 2019 and 2022 driven by state investment in VietQR infrastructure. A consumer
base that is already habituated to mobile financial transactions is a much easier adoption
environment for a mobile lending product than a consumer base encountering digital
financial services for the first time.

SECTION 5: Regulatory and institutional framework for FinTech market entry

The regulatory barrier to FinTech market entry in Southeast Asia is the single factor
most likely to transform a theoretical market opportunity into an extended delay. A
consumer lending platform can have superior technology, compelling unit economics and
patient investors. None of that matters without a license, or at minimum a regulatory
pathway to one. Vietnam's regulatory architecture governing foreign FinTech market entry
has changed substantially since 2023, and the nature of that change is what makes the
period from mid-2025 the relevant entry window for Salmon.

Vietnam's State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) had been developing a FinTech regulatory framework
since at least 2017, when it established a FinTech Steering Committee and published
preliminary guidelines for digital payment services. But the framework remained incomplete
for consumer lending and credit scoring for most of the intervening period. The result
was a prolonged regulatory gap that deterred foreign direct investment in the sector
despite the clear market opportunity. Decree 94/2025 [26], which took effect on 1 July
2025, resolved a substantial portion of that uncertainty. The decree establishes a formal
sandbox with defined entry criteria and testing periods of up to two years (renewable
for up to two additional one-year extensions). Licensing pathways are specified for four
covered categories: credit scoring, open banking API services, P2P lending and payment
technology.

The ownership restriction in the decree deserves analysis rather than alarm. Foreign-owned
entities can participate fully in the credit scoring and open API service categories.
Direct P2P lending through a Vietnamese-registered vehicle requires Vietnamese majority
ownership. For Salmon, whose competitive advantage lies in credit scoring infrastructure
rather than in balance sheet lending per se, the credit scoring category is the natural
sandbox entry point. A sandbox participation as a credit scoring provider, feeding
algorithmic outputs to a Vietnamese-licensed consumer lending partner, is also a lower-risk
market entry structure than a fully owned lending subsidiary. It allows Salmon to
calibrate its scoring model on Vietnamese borrower data before committing to a direct
lending license. The two-year testing period builds exactly the operational track record
that the SBV would expect to see in a subsequent licensing application.

Arner, Barberis and Buckley [24] describe the "regulatory gap" problem: the interval
between when a new financial technology is deployed and when regulation catches up creates
both opportunity and systemic risk. In their framework, the most attractive entry window
for a foreign FinTech is immediately after a regulatory framework is formalized, because
the formalization reduces investment uncertainty while the market is still underpenetrated.
Vietnam in the second half of 2025 and through 2026 is at precisely that inflection point.
Decree 94/2025 reduces the regulatory uncertainty that deterred foreign investors through
2024. The market, as shown in Table 2, remains far from the saturation that characterizes
Indonesia's P2P sector after years of operation under OJK supervision.

The Philippines provides a useful comparative reference for how a FinTech regulatory
sandbox functions in practice. The BSP sandbox, established under BSP Circular 1049 in
2019, permitted digital banking experiments under supervised conditions before the issuance
of full digital bank licenses in 2021 [20]. Salmon operates under that framework. Its
legal and compliance team therefore already has institutional experience navigating a
two-stage regulatory process (sandbox participation followed by substantive license
application) in a Southeast Asian central banking context. That experience is not a
guarantee of regulatory success in Vietnam, where the SBV's institutional culture and
supervisory priorities differ from the BSP's. But it is a genuine organizational advantage
over foreign entrants from advanced-economy FinTech markets that have not operated in an
emerging market regulatory environment.

Zetzsche, Buckley, Arner and Barberis [25] identify the harder problem that follows
initial licensing: data governance and cross-border data flow restrictions. Also, their
analysis predates Vietnam's 2018 Cybersecurity Law, which makes the operational
implications more acute than their general framework suggests. Their argument
is that the most significant long-term regulatory challenge for FinTech platforms operating
across borders is not licensing (which is legible and negotiable) but data regulation,
which is becoming more restrictive globally. Vietnam's Cybersecurity Law of 2018 and
its implementing decrees impose data localization requirements on certain categories of
personal data, including financial transaction data. A consumer lending platform's credit
scoring model depends on processing behavioral and transactional data at scale. If that
data must be stored and processed on Vietnamese-resident servers, Salmon's scoring
infrastructure will require architectural modification for the local deployment. The
cost of that modification is manageable, but it needs to be scoped in the pre-entry
planning phase rather than discovered post-launch.

The regulatory comparison between Vietnam and Indonesia reinforces the market selection
argument from Section 3. Indonesia's OJK Regulation No. 40 moved regulatory direction
toward higher barriers: increased minimum capital, stricter equity rules and mandatory
risk model disclosure. Vietnam's Decree 94/2025 moved in the opposite direction: lower
barriers at the entry stage through the sandbox mechanism, with higher requirements
applied only at the subsequent licensing stage. For Salmon, which does not yet have the
balance sheet to commit to full Vietnamese market capitalization on day one, the
incremental sandbox-to-license pathway is the operationally viable option.

SECTION 6: Competitive dynamics and entry mode selection

The entry mode decision is where theoretical analysis must be translated into a structural
commitment. Also, it is the decision most sensitive to the specific regulatory constraints
of the target market, which is why the generic entry mode typologies in the management
literature must be filtered through the Vietnamese regulatory architecture described in
Section 5 before they yield actionable conclusions. The preceding sections have established that Vietnam is the analytically
defensible expansion target, that the regulatory sandbox provides a structured entry
path, and that consumer demand conditions are favorable for a bureau-bypass consumer
lending platform. What remains is the question of what organizational form Salmon's
Vietnamese presence should take and against what competitive landscape it would be
operating.

The entry mode literature, as filtered through the frameworks in Section 1, produces a
reasonably consistent prediction. TCT [6] favors internalization when the technology
being deployed is asset-specific and when the contracting environment is uncertain. OLI
[3] reaches the same conclusion through ownership advantage protection: the alternative-
data scoring model is most fully protected within an owned organizational structure rather
than through a licensing arrangement that could enable a Vietnamese partner to develop
competing capabilities using Salmon's data. Born Global theory [4] is agnostic about
mode but consistent with rapid commitment rather than extended arm's-length relationships.

The ownership restriction in Decree 94/2025 [26] creates a practical floor on the
ownership structure for direct P2P lending: Salmon cannot hold more than 49% of a licensed
Vietnamese P2P lending entity under current rules. This pushes the optimal near-term
structure toward a joint venture in which Salmon holds the maximum permitted foreign
stake while a Vietnamese partner contributes licensing status, customer relationships
and regulatory credibility. The value of the Vietnamese partner in this structure is not
primarily its capital. It is its existing license, its established relationship with the
SBV supervisory team, and its access to a customer base on which Salmon's scoring model
can be locally calibrated.

The partner selection criterion that follows from Eisenmann, Parker and Van Alstyne's
two-sided market analysis [27] is the size of the partner's existing customer or borrower
base. Each existing customer who can be pre-scored by Salmon's model before a loan
application is submitted adds to the local training dataset that improves model accuracy.
A Vietnamese commercial bank or consumer finance company with 300,000-500,000 active
customers provides Salmon with an immediate training dataset for Vietnamese market
conditions that would otherwise take two to three years to accumulate through organic
loan origination. The partner selection decision is, in this framing, as much a data
acquisition decision as it is a distribution decision.

The competitive landscape Salmon would enter reflects Zalan and Toufaily's [13] observation
that emerging-market FinTech incumbents are often more formidable than classical disruption
theory anticipates. In Vietnam's consumer lending market, the dominant players are not
legacy commercial banks offering digital wrappers around manual processes. They are
(1) established consumer finance companies with existing operating licenses and borrower
portfolios, including FE Credit (VPBank subsidiary, over 16 million customers as of 2022),
and (2) platform-embedded lending products from super-apps. Among the latter, ZaloPay
(operated through VNG Corporation, with 70 million Zalo users as of 2023) and
Shopee Pay (operated through Sea Limited) have begun embedding consumer credit into their
primary interfaces.

Salmon would not be competing against these players primarily on product quality, at
least in the first 18 months. It would be competing on distribution access and on credit
quality -- its ability to approve loans profitably for borrowers that FE Credit and the
super-apps decline or do not reach. That is the specific market position consistent with
its alternative-data ownership advantage. Mention [28] argues that the FinTech competitive
landscape of the late 2020s will be structured around data-rich platform competition
rather than product feature competition. In that framing, Salmon's strategic priority
in Vietnam is building a local behavioral dataset large enough to make its scoring model
locally competitive, not launching a full-feature banking app. The product roadmap follows
the data acquisition logic.

Contractor's [8] caution against premature internationalization is relevant to entry
timing as well as to mode. A Vietnamese market entry that prioritizes scale in the first
year, before the scoring model is locally calibrated, risks adverse selection at the
portfolio level that would take two to three years to work through on a consumer lending
book with typical tenors of 12-36 months. The sandbox framework [26] effectively enforces
a conservative first phase by restricting direct lending operations to licensed players.
But the risk of aggressive growth post-licensing is real, and the entry strategy should
explicitly build in a second phase of accelerated growth only after local default rate
data from the initial cohort of borrowers is available.

SECTION 7: Research gap and theoretical contribution

The preceding six sections have synthesized evidence on market entry theory, FinTech
internationalization, Southeast Asian market structure, consumer adoption dynamics,
regulatory frameworks and competitive entry modes. Three interconnected gaps emerge from
that synthesis, each of which this thesis addresses directly.

The most substantial gap is the absence of research on outward internationalization by
Southeast Asian FinTech companies. Also -- and this is the more pointed version of the
same observation -- the literature treats emerging-market FinTech purely as a destination
for investment and regulatory experimentation, not as a source of competitive capabilities
that can be exported to structurally similar markets. The literature reviewed in Sections 1 and 2 focuses
on three types of actors: US and UK digital banks and payment platforms expanding into
emerging markets [9][10][11]; Chinese FinTech companies (Alipay, Ant Group) expanding
across Asia [12]; and emerging-market FinTech ecosystems receiving inbound investment
[13][14]. Philippine FinTech companies like Salmon, which were built under thin-bureau,
high-informal-credit-cost emerging-market conditions and are now seeking to replicate
that model in an adjacent market with structurally similar characteristics, represent a
category the internationalization literature has not examined.

The Born Global framework [4] predicts rapid internationalization for digital-native
firms in general terms but does not analyze the specific advantages and liabilities that
accrue to firms whose competitive advantage was built in an emerging market rather than
an advanced economy. The OLI framework [3] assumes that ownership advantages are
transferable across markets, but it does not account for the fact that an alternative-data
scoring model trained on emerging-market behavioral data may have higher portability
to neighboring emerging markets than to advanced-economy ones, precisely because the
data environment (thin bureau coverage, mobile-first financial behavior, high reliance
on informal credit networks) is structurally similar. That portability hypothesis is not
tested in any study this review has located.

This thesis contributes the first case-based analysis of an emerging-market consumer
lending FinTech company's market entry into a neighboring emerging market, examining
whether the competitive advantage built in one institutional-void context transfers to
another, and under what conditions.

The second gap concerns the regulatory sandbox as a market entry mode. Arner, Barberis
and Buckley [24] describe the global proliferation of FinTech sandboxes in general terms.
Zetzsche et al. [25] analyze the data governance complications that follow. But neither
study examines a regulatory sandbox as a distinct market entry structure -- a vehicle
through which a foreign company builds a local operational track record that constitutes
the application for a license, rather than simply waiting for one. Decree 94/2025 [26]
creates exactly that structure. A foreign FinTech company entering the Vietnamese sandbox
is not occupying a regulatory waiting room. It is making an investment in regulatory
credibility that determines whether a substantive license will be available at the end
of the testing period. That distinction has implications for how market entry strategy
is sequenced, funded and managed that existing entry mode frameworks do not capture.

The third gap is methodological and has implications for how the adoption literature is
applied to market entry planning. Consumer lending FinTech has different adoption economics
from payment FinTech. Payment adoption is adoption of a convenience service; credit
adoption is adoption of a financial obligation with repayment consequences. The adverse
selection dynamics at launch, the role of trust in first-time borrower conversion, and
the portfolio risk profile of early adopters differ systematically between the two product
categories. Ozili [23] and Lagna and Ravishankar [14] approach this distinction without
making it the central focus of their analyses. Neither study addresses it in the context
of a foreign digital consumer lender's initial cohort in a new emerging market.

This thesis addresses all three gaps through a combination of desk research and case-based
analysis of Salmon's strategic situation. The theoretical contribution is a composite
framework for consumer lending FinTech market entry in ASEAN emerging markets, integrating
Born Global theory [4] for the internationalization decision, OLI analysis [3] for
ownership structure, TCT [6] for the partnership governance question, and a re-reading
of the regulatory sandbox not as a barrier but as a market entry mode in its own right.
The empirical contribution is the application of that composite framework to Vietnam,
drawing on Salmon's disclosed financial and operational profile, the market data assembled
in Section 3, Vietnam's regulatory documents [26] and the consumer adoption evidence
synthesized in Section 4.

The research question this thesis pursues is: what market entry strategy should Salmon
adopt for its Vietnamese expansion, and which combination of theoretical frameworks best
explains the structural choices available to it?
